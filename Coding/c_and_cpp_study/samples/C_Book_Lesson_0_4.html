Thinking Like a Programmer

You've been a software user your whole life. Becoming a programmer means flipping the way you think about a few specific things. Let's name them.

1 Why this matters

Two brains, same person 🧠

Here's a thing nobody tells you up front: becoming a programmer isn't about learning syntax. Syntax is the easy part — you can pick up C's 30 keywords in a week. The hard part is rewiring how you approach problems.

Every day of your life, you've been a software user. You click, you tap, you swipe. When something doesn't work, you complain, restart, or look for a different button. That's a perfectly fine way to be a user.

A programmer faces the same screen with a different brain. When something doesn't work, the programmer asks "why?" and starts taking it apart. When something doesn't exist, the programmer asks "could I build it?" and starts sketching. That's the shift we're naming today.

2 User vs programmer — side by side

How the same situation looks through two different brains 👀

Imagine an app crashed when you tried to upload a photo. Here's how a user reacts vs how a programmer reacts. Both reactions are valid — they're just different jobs.

The user brain

  • "Ugh, this app is broken again."
  • "Let me try restarting it."
  • "Maybe the photo's too big — I'll pick a smaller one."
  • "If it still doesn't work, I'll give up and use a different app."
  • "That's the developer's problem, not mine."

The programmer brain

  • "Why did it crash exactly there?"
  • "What was different about that photo? File size? Format? Permissions?"
  • "What does the code do just before the crash?"
  • "Can I reproduce it on demand?"
  • "If I were writing this, how would I handle this case?"

Notice the second column is mostly questions. The programmer brain isn't smarter — it's just trained to keep asking questions instead of giving up.

3 The three big shifts

The mindset upgrades that change everything 🔄

These are the three transitions that turn confused beginners into capable programmers. Once you've internalized them, the rest is just practice.
From "I assume the computer will figure it out."
→
To "The computer is brutally literal. I have to spell out everything."
From "Works on my machine = it works."
→
To "It works when I've thought about every case — including the weird ones."
From "This problem is too big — I'll never solve it."
→
To "I'll break it into pieces small enough to solve one at a time."

That last one — decomposition — is the most important skill you'll ever learn. Let's practice it next.

4 The superpower: decomposition

Big problems are just small problems stacked up 🧩

Programmers don't solve big problems. They solve tiny problems, hundreds of them, that combined turn out to be big. The trick is splitting the big one into the tiny ones — that's called decomposition.

The pattern: take any task, ask "what are the 3 or 4 sub-steps?", then take each sub-step and ask the same question. Repeat until each sub-step is so simple you could just do it.

You already do this all the time without noticing. "Make tea" is a single thought to you — but if you had to teach an alien how to make tea, you'd find yourself listing steps: get water, boil water, put tea in a cup, pour the water, wait, drink. Each of those can be broken down further. Get water is really: find the kettle, open the tap, fill the kettle, close the tap.

Programming is exactly this pattern, applied to a computer instead of an alien. Try the interactive decomposition tree below — see how deep "make tea" actually goes.

7 Common mistakes

Traps new programmers fall into 🚧

🙅
"I should be able to just write it from the start." Almost nobody does. Experienced programmers spend most of their time thinking, sketching, and decomposing — actual typing is a smaller fraction than you'd guess. If you're stuck staring at a blank screen, the problem usually isn't your typing skill; it's that you haven't broken the problem down enough yet.
🙅
"If I don't know the answer, I'm not cut out for this." Wrong. Programming is mostly not knowing the answer yet. The skill is in tolerating that feeling long enough to break the problem into pieces small enough that you do know the answer for each one.
🙅
"It worked once — it's done." The user brain says "ran successfully = it works." The programmer brain says "ran successfully this time, on this input, on my machine." Until you've thought about edge cases — empty input, huge input, weird input, broken network, missing file — you're not done. You're just lucky.
8 Your turn

Practice the shift 💪

Easy Decompose three everyday tasks. Pick "brush your teeth," "send a text," and "make a sandwich." For each one, write 4–6 sub-steps. Then for one of those sub-steps, write 3 sub-sub-steps. Notice how it never quite bottoms out.
Medium Edge-case hunt. Pick the calculator on your phone. Spend 5 minutes trying to break it. Divide by zero, type an empty expression, paste a 10,000-character string, switch orientation mid-calculation. Note what happens. This is the kind of thinking a programmer does before shipping a feature, not after a user complains.
Stretch Teach the alien. Imagine you have to teach a being from another planet (who has no common sense, only follows literal instructions) how to tie shoelaces. Write the instructions. Now hand them to a human friend and ask them to follow them exactly. Watch them fail. Rewrite. This is the actual job.

🎓 Quick recap

  • Becoming a programmer is mostly a mindset shift, not a syntax-learning exercise.
  • The user brain assumes things work. The programmer brain asks why, when, and what if.
  • Three big shifts: assume the computer is literal · think in cases, not single answers · break big problems into tiny ones.
  • Decomposition is the superpower. It's how programmers turn "impossible" into "long list of doable."
  • Being stuck or not knowing the answer is normal — it's most of the job. The skill is staying with the discomfort long enough to break the problem down further.
