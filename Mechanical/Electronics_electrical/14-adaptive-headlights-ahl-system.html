Adaptive Headlights — AHL System

Source images: IMG20250515003703, IMG20250515003711, IMG20250515003720

Note: These three images were originally misfiled in Steering_Wheels_tyres/ and moved to Electronics_electrical/ in Phase 4.

Overview

Adaptive Headlight (AHL) is BMW's system that steers the headlight beam left/right with the steering wheel — so on a winding road, the beam follows the bend instead of pointing straight ahead at the verge. Combined with Bi-xenon (xenon-discharge lamps) and automatic vertical levelling, it dramatically improves night-driving visibility. The page covers the 3 advantages of AHL, the prerequisites for activation (rain/light sensor must detect darkness), the system architecture (multiple control units on PT-CAN + LIN), and the zero-position sensor / beam-pattern geometry that aims the beam correctly relative to the steering input and road geometry.

Working Principle

What AHL does

Notebook (IMG 003703 top): "[geometric sketch: a + b = c, showing a circle, square, and angle relationship] ★ Adaptive headlights: – Bi-xenon — headlights – Can be swivelled vertically"

AHL physically rotates the Bi-xenon headlight reflectors to: 1. Swivel horizontally with steering input → beam aims into corners. 2. Tilt vertically with vehicle pitch and loading → maintains correct dazzle-line height.

So instead of beams pointing fixed forward, they actively track where the car is going to be in a few seconds.

3 advantages of AHL

Notebook: "Advantages: ① Curves are better illuminated ② Obstacles are detected earlier ③ Oncoming traffic not dazzled to same extent"

# Advantage Why it matters
1 Curves illuminated On a dark winding road, fixed headlights point at the verge; AHL points into the road = sees obstacles sooner
2 Obstacles detected earlier Earlier illumination + driver eye position gives critical reaction time
3 Oncoming traffic not dazzled The beam pattern is aimed away from the opposing lane during cornering, reducing glare for oncoming drivers

Bi-xenon — what the bulbs are

Bi-xenon = a xenon-discharge bulb that produces BOTH low beam AND high beam by mechanically blocking/unblocking part of the reflector aperture. Compared to halogen: - 3× brighter for the same wattage. - Whiter colour (~ 4200 K vs halogen's ~ 3000 K) — closer to daylight. - Longer life (~ 2000 hr vs halogen's 500 hr). - Needs a high-voltage ballast (~ 25 kV to strike, ~ 80 V to run) — drives complexity.

Activation prerequisites

Notebook (IMG 003703 bottom): "Operation of adaptive headlight: ★ Prerequisites: ① Driving light control (ALC) switched on ② Rain & light sensor detects darkness"

AHL doesn't engage all the time: - ALC (Automatic Light Control) must be in AUTO or ON position. - RLS (Rain & Light Sensor) must report darkness (i.e., it's actually night or in a tunnel). - (Speed thresholds apply too — usually disabled below walking pace where it's distracting.)

In daylight or with ALC off, AHL stays in the home position.

The AHL system involves multiple ECUs on a layered bus:

   PT-CAN (powertrain bus, 500 kbit/s)
        │
        ├──── DME ◄── reports vehicle speed
        │
        ├──── DSC ◄── reports lateral G (cornering)
        │
        ├──── SMC ◄── reports steering angle
        │
        └──── AHL master control unit
                  │
                  │ LIN bus (slow, 19.2 kbit/s)
                  │
                  ├──── LM (Light Module) ── front bulb power switching
                  │
                  ├──── RLS (Rain & Light Sensor) ── ambient light + wiper trigger
                  │
                  └──── Xenon slave modules (left + right headlight)
                            │
                            ▼
                        Stepper motors that swivel reflectors

The AHL ECU is the master; the bulb-specific Xenon modules are LIN slaves receiving target angles.

Coding after replacement

Notebook annotations: "After replacement of AHL control unit: ─ VIN, type of vehicle [must be re-coded] After replacement of Xenon: ─ VIN, Headlight type, Polly attention"

A swapped AHL control unit needs dealer coding to the specific VIN + vehicle type — no "plug and play." Same for Xenon modules. This is the standard BMW practice for any electronically-coded module (see DIS diagnostic process chapter).

Beam pattern + zero-position geometry

Notebook (IMG 003720): Geometric diagram showing: - Steering angle input (angle indicator on car nose) - Axle distance (a) — wheelbase - Head-light range (s) — typically ~ 100 m design distance - Headlight inside aperture (5° inside angle = inboard limit of swivel) - Headlight outside (28° outside angle = outboard limit) - Driver side less lobe (asymmetric pattern — beam wider on the passenger side to illuminate the verge without dazzling oncoming traffic on the driver side) - Zero position sensor label at top

The system needs to know: - Where the steering wheel is now (from SMC). - Vehicle yaw rate / speed (from DSC). - What angle the headlight reflector is at right now (from a zero-position sensor at each headlight that confirms the actual swivel angle).

The AHL ECU runs the kinematic equation: given current steering angle + speed + axle distance, where will the car be in ~1 second → swivel the beam to that future location.

Asymmetric beam — driver-side dim

Notebook (IMG 003711 top, partly visible): "Asymmetric dipped beam — light-dark boundary of left-hand curve"

Headlight dipped (low) beams are designed asymmetric — they project further and higher on the passenger side (kerb-side) and are cut off lower on the driver side (centreline side). This is so the driver sees road signs and pedestrians on the verge clearly without dazzling oncoming traffic that's on the centreline side.

For right-hand-drive countries (UK, India, Japan), beam pattern is mirrored: passenger side is now the centreline, so the dim boundary shifts. Re-coded headlight units are required when importing across the divide.

Why "zero position" sensor

The AHL stepper motor moves the reflector by counting steps from a reference position. The zero position sensor confirms where exactly that home position is at startup (the stepper itself can lose position if power glitches). The system "centres" each startup → all subsequent swivel commands are relative to that calibration.

Force Path / Process Flow

   ADAPTIVE HEADLIGHT CONTROL LOOP:
   ─────────────────────────────────

   1. Driver turns wheel into corner
            │
            ▼
   2. SMC reads steering angle → publishes on PT-CAN
            │
            ▼
   3. DSC reads yaw rate, lat-G, vehicle speed → publishes on PT-CAN
            │
            ▼
   4. AHL ECU reads PT-CAN + checks: RLS = dark? ALC = on?
            │
            ▼
   5. AHL ECU computes target swivel angle:
            ─ axle distance a (vehicle constant)
            ─ steering angle θ (current input)
            ─ vehicle speed v
            ─ predicted curve radius R = a / sin(θ)
            ─ where will the car be in ~ 1 second
            ─ target swivel = arctan(displacement / s)
            │
            ▼
   6. AHL ECU sends LIN command to each Xenon slave:
            ─ target angle for left lamp
            ─ target angle for right lamp
            │
            ▼
   7. Xenon slave drives stepper motor; reads position sensor
            ─ moves to target angle
            ─ confirms via position feedback
            │
            ▼
   8. Reflector swivels → beam now illuminates inside of curve
            │
            ▼
   Loop repeats at ~ 50 Hz (every 20 ms) → smooth tracking

Detailed Component Notes

Why use Bi-xenon and not separate low/high-beam bulbs

Traditional cars: separate H1 (high) and H7 (low) bulbs + filaments. Bi-xenon: one xenon arc-lamp + a mechanical shutter that reveals/blocks the upper half of the beam → low or high beam from one bulb. Advantages: - Half the bulbs to manufacture, light, and maintain. - Single light source for AHL to swivel (instead of two parallel mechanisms). - More compact headlight unit.

LED headlights (post-AHL era)

By 2013, LED headlights were starting to replace Bi-xenon. LED has even longer life, faster startup, smaller heat output. Modern LED-Matrix headlights take adaptive lighting further: individual LED segments can be turned off to "punch holes" in the beam where oncoming traffic is detected — no swivelling needed, full high-beam usable while preserving other drivers' eyes. The trainer's notebook is from the Bi-xenon AHL era (pre-LED-matrix).

Why "Polly attention" annotation

Notebook margin: "Polly attention (pix and seal)"

Interpreted as "Pay attention" (typo). After replacing a Xenon module, the technician must verify the lens (pix?) and weather seal are correctly fitted — otherwise water ingress will destroy the ballast electronics.

"100 metres installed height of headlight sign"

Notebook (IMG 003711): "light-dark boundary of left-hand curve of 100 metres installed height of headlight sign"

Refers to the headlight aim test target: a vertical line drawn on the test wall at a distance of 5 m or 10 m from the car, marking where the light-dark boundary should fall when the headlight is correctly aimed. For a typical headlight installed at ~75 cm above the ground, the boundary on a wall 5 m ahead should be at a specific calculated height. The "100 m" wording is uncertain — could be related to design beam range (the distance at which the cutoff projects in real driving).

Swivel angle limits

Notebook (IMG 003720): "Headlight inside aperture 5°", "Headlight outside 28°"

The AHL beam can swivel: - 5° inboard (toward the centreline, minimum) — prevents pointing into oncoming lane. - 28° outboard (toward the kerb, maximum) — limit set by reflector housing geometry.

These limits prevent the system from creating a dangerous beam direction even if commanded incorrectly.

Key Facts

  • AHL (Adaptive Headlight) swivels Bi-xenon beams with steering input.
  • 3 advantages: curves illuminated, obstacles seen earlier, oncoming not dazzled.
  • Prerequisites: ALC (Auto Light Control) switched on + RLS (Rain & Light Sensor) detects darkness.
  • Bi-xenon = one xenon arc lamp with mechanical low/high-beam shutter; 3× brighter and longer-life than halogen.
  • System modules: AHL master + LM (Light Module) + RLS + SMC (Steering) + SGM (gateway).
  • Buses: PT-CAN for sensor inputs + LIN for Xenon slave control.
  • Zero position sensor at each headlight confirms reflector home position.
  • Swivel range: 5° inboard to 28° outboard.
  • Asymmetric dipped beam = wider/higher on kerb side; lower on centreline side (to spare oncoming traffic).
  • AHL/Xenon replacement requires VIN-coding via DIS/ISTA.
  • Modern successor: LED Matrix headlights (individual segment control, no mechanical swivel).
