Design of shafts p.163–164

Shafts are rotating members mounted in suitable bearings that carry power-transmitting elements like gears, pulleys, sprockets and flywheels. They are subjected to bending, torsion, buckling or shear, and are checked for strength, stiffness, torsional stiffness, buckling and critical speed depending on the particular requirement.

Why machine-tool shafts run large

In machine tools, stiffness requirements are normally more stringent than strength requirements. This invariably leads to a much larger shaft than strength alone would need. Because of this, the strength formulae below are deliberately simplified — the dynamic character of the load and stress-concentration factors are not included, though some allowance is built into the recommended allowable stresses.

i) Combined bending and twisting. The required diameter follows from the equivalent bending moment:

σbe = √(M² + 0.45·T²) / Z √(M² + 0.45·T²) is the equivalent bending moment; M = √(Mx² + My²) is the resultant of the bending moments in the X and Y planes (at right angles). Allowable σbe → Table 1.

Calculation for stiffness p.166–168

Heavy loads deflect the shaft, making gear-tooth and bearing loading non-uniform and concentrating pressure at the edges. Stiffness must therefore be checked for both bending deflection and torsional twist.

Deflection of a stepped shaft is found by Macaulay's method, the area-moment method, the conjugate-beam method, Castigliano's method, or graphical integration. The area-moment method (used in the worked example, Fig 1) takes the deflection of a point P as the moment, about an ordinate through P, of the area of the M/I diagram between P and the tangent point.

Torsional stiffness (quick sizing)

Solid: d ≥ 128·⁴√(P/n) for θ ≤ 0.25°/m  ·  d ≥ 118·⁴√(P/n) for θ ≤ 0.35°/m Hollow: (d⁴ − di⁴) ≥ 1.959×10⁸·(P/n) for 0.25°/m; ≥ 2.742×10⁸·(P/n) for 0.35°/m.
Permissible values (machine-tool practice)
  • Max deflection ≤ 2×10⁻⁴ × bearing span
  • Deflection under a gear ≤ 10 × module (in µm)
  • Slope where a gear is mounted ≤ 1×10⁻³ rad
  • Angle of twist ≤ 0.25°–0.35° per metre
  • Slope at bearings ≤ the bearing's permissible misalignment (Table 3)

Critical speed p.169–170

At its critical (whirling) speed a shaft resonates and deflection runs away; the running speed should be kept at least ±20% away from it. If the shaft carries a distributed load or several point loads it has many critical speeds, the first being the important one. Formulae apply to steel shafts: E = 2.1×10⁴ kgf/mm², specific weight 7.85×10⁻⁶ kgf/mm³. Self-aligning or very short bearings → treat as simply supported; long stiff bearings → treat as fixed.

Shaft weight is replaced by concentrated loads added to the loads already acting; static deflections y are then used:

nc = 946.48 · √(ΣWy / ΣWy²)  rpm

Stability in buckling p.171

Long, slender, axially-loaded shafts must be checked for buckling. The choice of formula depends on the slenderness ratio S = l/r.

Rankine (S ≈ 20–100): Pcr = (π·d²·σc/4) / [ 1 + K·(l/r)² ] K = 1/25000 (both ends fixed) · 1/12500 (one fixed, one hinged) · 1/6250 (both ends hinged).

Keys p.172–179

Keys are checked for bearing pressure on the critical bearing area. The contact area depends on key depth in shaft vs hub and on round- vs square-ended keys.

p = F / A,   F = 2T/d Round-ended key A = t1(l − b); square-ended A = t1·l (parallel keys for general engineering: A = (l−b)/2 or l/2 × shaft/hub depth).
Allowable bearing pressure

Soft steel keys (σu ≥ 58 kgf/mm²): 8–10 kgf/mm² for medium conditions, higher for good working conditions. Hardened keys: up to 10–12 kgf/mm². Keys need not be checked in shear — the shear area is more than double the crushing area.

Splines p.173–174, 180–182

Splines carry higher torque than keys and allow axial sliding. They are checked for bearing pressure on the flank contact surfaces between shaft and hub.

p = F / (h′·l),   F = 4T/(D+d),   h′ = (D−d)/2 − 2c Effective contact surfaces n′ ≈ (2/3 to 3/4)·n; D major dia, d minor dia, c chamfer.

Rotary shaft seals p.182–185

Rotary lip seals retain lubricant and exclude dirt where a shaft passes through the housing. Three constructions: Type A rubber-cased, Type B metal-cased, Type C built-up. Selection and fit follow shaft diameter, housing bore and surface speed (Table 12).

Mounting details (p.185)
  • Shaft dia tolerance h8–h11; housing bore H8.
  • Surface finish by speed: Ra 0.8 up to 2 m/s · 0.4 at 2–4 m/s · 0.2–0.4 at 4–12 m/s.
  • Harden shaft seat to HRC 45–60 (ground) for 4–12 m/s; hard-chrome plate 0.02–0.03 mm for 8–12 m/s.
  • Chamfer width k by shaft dia: ≤30 → 3 · 30–60 → 4 · 60–120 → 6 · 120–250 → 8 · >250 → 10. Round/chamfer the lead-in edge so the lip is not damaged on assembly.
