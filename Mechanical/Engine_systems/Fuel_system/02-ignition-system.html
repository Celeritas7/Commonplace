Overview

The ignition system fires the spark plugs at the right crank angle for each cylinder. The page lists the classic distributor-based ignition components, the modern Distributor-less ignition module (DI / coil-per-cylinder) that replaced them, the role of the crank position sensor / magnetic triggering device, and the warning that "ignition coil burns if ignition switch kept on" (a real failure mode in points-style systems).

Working Principle

Classic distributor ignition (top of the page)

A 12 V supply from the battery drives current through the ignition coil's primary winding. The contact breaker (points) interrupts that current periodically; the collapsing magnetic field induces a high voltage (~ 15–30 kV) in the secondary winding. A distributor rotor sends that high voltage to the correct spark plug for the current firing-order position. The condenser (capacitor) sits across the points to absorb the inductive spike and stop them arcing.

Modern coil-per-cylinder / coil-pack ignition (red sketch, bottom)

The distributor is replaced by an ignition module under ECU control. Each cylinder gets its own ignition coil stacked over the spark plug. A crank position sensor (magnetic triggering device) tells the ECM where the crank is; the ECM then commands the module to fire each coil at the right moment.

Why ignition-coil burnout when switch left on

Notebook ★: "Ignition coil gets burn[t] if Ignition switch is kept on."

If the points happen to be closed (or in a hall-sensor system, the dwell happens to be high) when the engine is not running, full battery current flows continuously through the coil primary → coil overheats → insulation breaks down → coil dies. Modern ECU-controlled systems cut dwell to zero after a few seconds of no rpm signal to prevent this; old points systems didn't.

Components

Component Function
Starter Initial crank — uses battery to spin the engine until it fires.
Battery 12 V DC supply for ignition primary current.
Ignition coil Step-up transformer: 12 V primary → ~25 kV secondary spike when primary current is interrupted.
Cables (HT leads) Carry high voltage from coil / distributor to plugs (now mostly obsolete with coil-on-plug).
Condenser Capacitor across the points — absorbs inductive spike; prevents arcing/erosion of points.
Contact breakers (points) Mechanical switch driven off the camshaft; interrupts primary current at the right crank angle.
Distributor Rotating rotor that routes high-voltage pulse from the coil to the correct spark plug.
Ignition module Electronic replacement for points + distributor. Switches coil primary under ECM command.
Spark plug Electrode pair in each combustion chamber where the spark jumps and ignites the mixture.
Magnetic triggering device / Crank position sensor Hall or VR sensor reading a toothed wheel on the crank — feeds ECM with precise crank angle / rpm.
ECM Engine Control Module — decides when each coil fires.

Detailed Component Notes

Why points were replaced (with the ignition module)

  • Points wear out (mechanical contact, arcs every revolution).
  • Distributor caps and rotors crack/erode.
  • Spark timing fixed by mechanical advance — can't adapt to load, knock, fuel quality.
  • ECU-controlled coil-per-cylinder gives independently optimised timing per cylinder, no moving HV parts, and only needs servicing at plug-change intervals.

Coil-on-plug vs coil-pack

  • Coil pack — one coil firing two cylinders 360° apart ("wasted spark" — one spark fires in compressed mixture, the other in exhaust where it does nothing).
  • Coil-on-plug — one coil per cylinder, mounted directly on the plug. Each fires only on its own cylinder's compression. No HV leads, no wasted spark.

Magnetic triggering device — how the ECM finds TDC

A toothed reluctor wheel on the crank passes a Hall (or variable reluctance) sensor. The wheel has a missing tooth (or a unique pattern) that gives the ECM an absolute reference once per revolution. Combined with a cam sensor, the ECM can identify which cylinder is on which stroke.

Key Facts

  • Classic ignition components: battery → coil → contact breakers + condenser → distributor → spark plug.
  • Modern: ECM + ignition module + coil-per-cylinder + crank position sensor — no distributor, no points.
  • Coil burnout happens if the points close with the engine off and the key on — current flows continuously through the primary.
  • The magnetic triggering device is the crank position sensor — provides the ECM with absolute crank angle to schedule sparks.
  • Condenser = capacitor protecting the points from arcing/erosion.
  • "Distributor → Ignition module" arrow on the page summarises the entire transition from mechanical to electronic ignition.
