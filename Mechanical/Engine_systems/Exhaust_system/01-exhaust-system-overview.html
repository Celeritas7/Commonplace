Exhaust System

Exhaust System — Overview

Source images: IMG20250514112257, IMG20250514112305

Overview

The exhaust system carries burnt combustion gases from the cylinder out to the atmosphere, cleans them (catalytic converter, oxygen-sensor closed-loop feedback), quiets them (silencer), and on a turbocharged engine harvests energy from them (turbocharger) to compress fresh intake air. The page lists the components in order and gives the block-flow diagram including sensor and camshaft references.

Working Principle

What happens to the burnt gas

  1. Combustion is over; piston is now on the exhaust stroke.
  2. Exhaust valve opens (in head; driven by camshaft).
  3. Gas flows through the exhaust manifold that collects from all cylinders.
  4. Exhaust manifold temperature sensor reports gas temp to ECU (used for turbo / cat protection).
  5. Oxygen sensor (lambda probe) sits on the manifold or just downstream — reports A/F ratio.
  6. Gas passes through the catalytic converter (chemistry: HC/CO/NOx → H₂O/CO₂/N₂).
  7. (Turbo engines) Gas drives the turbocharger turbine wheel.
  8. Pressure ductwork continues via the exhaust pipe.
  9. Silencer (muffler) cancels noise via internal chambers / absorption.
  10. Gas exits the tailpipe.

Turbocharger vs supercharger (notebook callout)

Notebook: "Turbocharger — operated by exhaust gases." "Supercharger — operated by engine." (Compressed air → so more power developed.)

Turbocharger Supercharger
Driven by Exhaust gas (waste energy) Engine crank (belt)
Lag Yes — turbo has to spool up None — instant boost
Net efficiency Better — uses energy that would otherwise be wasted Worse — parasitic load on crank
Where it sits In the exhaust path before the silencer On the intake side, belt-driven
Typical fitment Modern turbo-petrol & all car diesels Roots / centrifugal — high-output petrols, AMG, classic muscle

Both compress intake air → more air mass per stroke → more fuel can be injected → more power developed from the same displacement.

Components

Component Function
Exhaust valve Cylinder-head poppet valve that opens during the exhaust stroke (camshaft-driven).
Exhaust manifold Casting that collects exhaust from each cylinder's port.
Manifold temp sensor Thermocouple/NTC that protects the turbo and catalyst from over-temp.
Oxygen (lambda) sensor Reports A/F ratio to ECU — closes the fuel-control loop. See O2 sensor & lambda.
Catalytic converter Honeycomb of Pt/Pd/Rh that converts CO, HC, NOx to CO₂, H₂O, N₂. See pollutants & catalytic converter.
Turbocharger Turbine + compressor on one shaft, driven by exhaust pressure; compresses intake air.
Intercooler Air-to-air (or air-to-water) heat exchanger that cools compressed intake air before it enters the cylinder. (On the intake side, but always paired with a turbo/SC.)
Air preheater Conducts exhaust heat into the intake air on cold-start to help cold-running emissions; mostly historical.
Exhaust pipe Carries gas from turbo / cat to silencer.
Silencer (muffler) Resonant chamber that cancels noise pulses; reduces back-pressure via internal baffles. See pollutants & catalytic converter — muffler note.
Tailpipe Final exit duct; directs gas away from the cabin.
Supercharger (alternative) Crank-driven air compressor on the intake side; not strictly part of the exhaust system but referenced on this page.

Detailed Component Notes

Why a manifold temp sensor

The catalytic converter is destroyed above about 900 °C; turbochargers can warp turbine wheels above 950–1000 °C. The manifold temp sensor lets the ECU enrichen the mixture (excess fuel cools the exhaust by absorbing latent heat) when load gets too high — a survival strategy.

Where lambda lives

The notebook draws the oxygen sensor reading at the manifold. Real-world: pre-cat O2 sensor (closed-loop A/F) plus a post-cat O2 sensor (catalyst efficiency monitor — OBD-II requirement). The pre-cat sensor must sit close to the exhaust valves for fast response.

Turbo placement

The notebook puts the turbo after the cat in the flow diagram (catalytic converter → turbocharger). On real engines it's usually the other way around — the turbo sits close to the manifold so it gets hot, fast-moving gas. The cat is downstream. The notebook drawing should be read as a schematic block list, not a literal layout. See Open Questions.

Intercooler vs Air preheater

  • Intercooler = cools compressed intake air (post-turbo) → denser charge → more power.
  • Air preheater = warms intake air using exhaust heat during cold-start → faster combustion stabilisation → cleaner cold emissions. Largely obsolete on modern cars (replaced by close-coupled catalyst + heated O2 sensors).

Key Facts

  • Exhaust flow order (notebook): chamber → exhaust valve → manifold (+temp sensor +O2) → catalytic converter → turbocharger → exhaust pipe → silencer → tailpipe.
  • Turbocharger driven by exhaust gas; supercharger driven by crank. Both compress intake air → more power per stroke.
  • Intercooler cools boosted intake air; air preheater (historical) warms intake on cold-start.
  • Manifold temp sensor protects cat + turbo from over-temp by triggering enrichment.
  • Oxygen sensor closes the A/F control loop — feeds ECU.
  • Silencer cuts noise; back-pressure is the unwanted side effect.
