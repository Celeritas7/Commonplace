Clutch

Overview

The clutch sits between the engine flywheel and the gearbox input shaft, letting the driver decouple the engine from the transmission for gear changes and smoothly re-engage it without stalling. The notebook page lays out the actuation chain — pedal → linkages → fork → release bearing → diaphragm springs — and a cross-section sketch showing how the diaphragm pulls the pressure plate away from the clutch plate to break the friction-driven connection.

Working Principle

When the driver presses the pedal, force travels through cables / linkages (mechanical or hydraulic — the trainer's opening question highlights the distinction), to a fork that pushes the release bearing axially against the centre of the diaphragm springs. The diaphragm pivots and lifts the pressure plate off the clutch plate, breaking the clamp force between flywheel and clutch plate. The engine spins freely; the gearbox input shaft is now disconnected.

When the driver releases the pedal, the diaphragm springs reload, the pressure plate clamps the clutch plate against the flywheel, and friction transmits engine torque through the clutch-plate hub into the gearbox input shaft.

Components

Component Function
Clutch pedal Driver input lever.
Cables / linkages Transmit pedal force. Either mechanical cable+rod or hydraulic (master cylinder + slave cylinder).
Fork Pivoted lever in the bell-housing; converts cable/slave-cylinder pull into axial push on the release bearing.
Release bearing (throwout bearing) Bearing assembly that lets the rotating diaphragm centre be pushed by the non-rotating fork.
Diaphragm spring Belleville-style spring pivoting on the cover; provides the clamp load that holds the pressure plate against the clutch plate. Pushing its centre with the release bearing flips it → releases clamp load.
Pressure plate The plate that the diaphragm pushes against the clutch plate. Bolted to the cover, which is bolted to the flywheel — so it rotates with the engine.
Clutch plate Friction-lined disc with a splined hub. Hub connects to the gearbox input shaft. Friction faces grip flywheel + pressure plate.
Torsion springs (in clutch-plate hub) Small coil springs around the hub that absorb engine pulsations + smooth torque delivery on engagement.
Friction lining High-coefficient material on both faces of the clutch plate. Historically asbestos; now cerametallic / organic / Kevlar.
Flywheel Large rotating disc bolted to the crankshaft. Provides smooth rotation + the friction surface for the clutch plate.

Detailed Component Notes

Hydraulic vs cable actuation

Notebook header: "Which vehicle have hydraulic clutch?"

Cable / mechanical Hydraulic
How it transmits force Cable in a sheath, pulled by pedal Master cylinder at pedal → fluid line → slave cylinder at bell-housing
Feel Heavier; cable stretch + friction Lighter pedal; less hysteresis
Maintenance Cable wears, stretches Fluid leaks possible; needs bleeding
Used on Small cars, older vehicles Performance cars, most modern manuals, all BMWs

BMW E46 / E60-era cars (the notebook's main subject) all use hydraulic clutches — the answer to the trainer's opening question.

Why a diaphragm spring (vs coil springs)

Older clutches used multiple coil springs arranged in a circle around the pressure plate. The diaphragm spring replaced them because: - One spring instead of 8–12 → easier to manufacture, balance. - Self-centring force as the friction lining wears (diaphragm geometry). - Lower pedal effort for the same clamp force. - Higher max rpm before centrifugal force distorts the spring.

Torsion springs in the clutch-plate hub

The "Torsion spring" arrow in the diagram points to small coil springs arranged tangentially around the clutch-plate hub. They: - Absorb the impulse when the clutch first grabs (smoother engagement). - Damp engine-torque pulses (4-cyl engines pulse at 2 events/rev — would feel rough through the driveline without damping).

Friction lines

Notebook label: "friction lines" pointing at the clutch plate

The friction faces are the actual frictional interfaces — flywheel face ↔ clutch plate front face, and clutch plate back face ↔ pressure plate. Two friction surfaces total, hence the term "single-plate clutch." Multi-plate clutches stack more interfaces for higher torque capacity (see multi-plate brake/clutch).

Key Facts

  • Clutch chain: pedal → linkage → fork → release bearing → diaphragm → pressure plate → clutch plate → flywheel.
  • BMW E46 / E60 = hydraulic clutch (master cylinder + slave cylinder).
  • Diaphragm spring replaced earlier coil-spring designs — lighter pedal, smoother wear compensation, higher rpm.
  • Torsion springs in the clutch-plate hub absorb engagement shock + engine pulsations.
  • Pressed pedal = clutch disengaged; released pedal = clutch engaged (clamped).
  • "Friction lines" = the two clamp interfaces (flywheel ↔ clutch plate ↔ pressure plate).
  • Hub of clutch plate is splined onto the gearbox input shaft — that's the torque exit.
