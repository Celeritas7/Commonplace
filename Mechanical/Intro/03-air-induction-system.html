Air Induction System

Source images: IMG20250514111934

Margin note on the page: "ZEN — Zero Engine Noise" (BMW marketing/abbreviation for noise-suppressed intakes [?]).

Overview

The air induction system is the airflow path from the outside atmosphere all the way to the combustion chamber. The page lists every component the air passes through (or that meters it) in order, then re-lists them in flow order with sensor positions marked. The Engine Control Unit (ECU/DME) reads the sensors on this path to compute injection mass and ignition timing.

Working Principle

Outside air is drawn through a filter and ductwork, then metered for mass (HFM), regulated by the throttle, distributed to each cylinder via the intake manifold, and admitted to the combustion chamber by the intake valve on the intake stroke. Sensors along the way (air-temperature, MAP, throttle position) give the engine controller the data it needs to set the air–fuel ratio and ignition timing. A resonator on the intake pipe cancels low-frequency pulsations for the "zero engine noise" target.

Components

Component Function

Air filter Removes dust/grit before air reaches the engine.
Air pipes Connect filter housing to throttle body.
Resonator Pipe-side chamber that cancels intake pulsation noise (ZEN goal).
Throttle valve Butterfly plate that meters air flow rate to the engine.
Throttle body Housing carrying the throttle valve plus TPS and (often) IAC.

Air temperature sensor NTC thermistor — supplies intake-air temp to ECU.

MAP sensor Reads manifold vacuum/pressure — used for load calculation.
Throttle Position Sensor (TPS) Potentiometer or Hall sensor reporting throttle angle.
HFM (Hot Film Air-Mass sensor) Heated-film anemometer reporting air mass flow (and velocity).

Detailed Component Notes

HFM (Hot Film Air-Mass sensor)

A heated film is held at a fixed temperature above ambient. Air flowing past it cools it; the current needed to keep it hot is proportional to air mass flow. Better than a vane meter because it directly measures mass (the quantity needed for stoichiometric combustion calculation) instead of volume.

MAP vs HFM

On BMW DME systems of this era, either an HFM (mass-flow) or a MAP sensor (speed-density) can be used as the primary load signal — the notebook's two listings suggest the trainer pointed out that both are present and one is "main" while the other is plausibility / backup.

IAC valve (ICV)

With the throttle blade fully closed at idle, the engine still needs some air. The IAC opens a small bypass passage around the throttle to supply that air, and the ECU varies its opening to hold idle rpm against load changes (AC compressor kicking in, headlights on, etc.).

Resonator (ZEN reference)

A Helmholtz-type side chamber tuned to cancel specific intake-noise frequencies — BMW marketed quiet intakes under the "ZEN — Zero Engine Noise" label [?].

Key Facts

  • Flow order: atmosphere → duct → filter → pipe/resonator → throttle → manifold → intake valve → chamber.
  • 5 sensors live on this path: air-temp, IAC, MAP, TPS, HFM.
  • HFM measures air mass (not volume) — directly used for fuel-quantity calculation.
  • Resonator is on the intake pipe for the "ZEN — Zero Engine Noise" target.
  • MAP and HFM may coexist on one engine for plausibility checking.
