Steering, Wheels & Tyres

Tyre Footprint, Aquaplaning & Typical Damage

Source images: IMG20250514181610

Overview

The tyre footprint (contact patch) is the small area of rubber actually touching the road at any moment — typically the size of one human palm per tyre. Everything the car does to the road — acceleration, braking, steering — happens through these four small patches. The page covers what determines the contact-patch size (tread, pressure, speed, load), what decreases it (over-inflation, centrifugal force at high speed), the catastrophic case where the contact patch is destroyed by water (aquaplaning), and the typical causes of tyre damage seen on service intake.

Working Principle

What contact-patch size depends on

Notebook (IMG 181610): "★ Wheels & tyres – Contact surface between vehicle & road is called as footprint. – Depends on: ① Tyre tread ② Tyre inflation pressure ③ Speed ④ Tyre's load."

The four factors that set how big the contact patch is:

Factor More of it → footprint...
Tread design Block tread (large flat blocks) = bigger contact; aggressive lug tread (knobs) = smaller contact (rubber spread across small contact points)
Inflation pressure More pressure → tyre bulges less under load → smaller contact patch
Speed Higher speed → more centrifugal effect tries to lift the tread off the road → smaller contact at speed (especially > 200 km/h)
Load More load (passengers, cargo) → tyre squashes more → larger contact patch (until pressure rises from heating and balances out)

What decreases contact-patch size

Notebook: "★ Decreased contact surface: ① Increased inflation pressure ② Centrifugal force at high speed."

The two specific cases that make the contact patch shrink: - Over-inflation → tyre bulges only slightly under car weight → less rubber on the road → less grip. - High speed → centrifugal force on the tread tries to fling it outward → less rubber stays pressed against the road → grip drops at high speed (one reason 200+ km/h driving demands very specific tyres).

Aquaplaning

Notebook: "During aquaplaning complete loss of wheel control properties is possible."

Aquaplaning (US: hydroplaning) — when the tyre tread can't displace water from under the contact patch fast enough, a layer of water gets between rubber and road. The tyre is floating on water, not gripping the road. Steering inputs do nothing. Brakes do nothing. Lateral and longitudinal grip both = zero.

Three contributing factors (not all on the page): 1. Speed — higher speed = less time for water to be displaced. 2. Water depth — deeper standing water = more to displace. 3. Tyre condition — worn tread (less depth in grooves) can't channel water away.

Typical aquaplaning threshold: 70–90 km/h on standing water with worn or wide tyres. Modern tyres with deep block-tread + V-cut grooves dramatically push this threshold up — but no tyre is immune at high enough speed.

Typical tyre damage

Notebook (IMG 181610, bottom): "★ Typical tyre damage: – Hitting a kerb – Driving over obstacle – Locking the brakes (patches) – Due to heating of tyre material tyre treads worn out."

The four main causes of premature tyre damage:

Cause Damage type Visual signature
Kerb strike Sidewall pinch / bulge / cord cut Bulge on sidewall (impact rupture); deep scrape on rim flange
Driving over obstacle Sidewall puncture, tread cut Cut in tread, visible cord, slow leak
Locked brakes (skid) Flat-spotted tread, "patches" One flat area on the tread; ka-thump every revolution at low speed
Heat-related wear Tread compound breakdown, accelerated wear Smooth, glossy tread surface; chunks missing; over-the-tread cracks

Aquaplaning sequence

   Dry road:
       tyre tread blocks compress water film thin → rubber-road contact
       ► Full grip

   Wet road, moderate speed:
       tread grooves channel water sideways → some water remains
       ► Reduced grip; tyre still rolls

   Wet road, HIGH speed (above threshold):
       water doesn't have time to be displaced
       water gets WEDGED under leading edge of contact patch
       hydrodynamic lift wedge spreads BACKWARD
       eventually entire contact patch floats on water
       ► AQUAPLANING — zero grip
       ► Driver: steering = nothing, brakes = nothing, accel = nothing

   Recovery:
       Driver: lift off accelerator gradually
       Do NOT brake hard
       Do NOT steer suddenly
       As speed drops below threshold + tread re-bites,
       grip returns suddenly

Components / Vocabulary

Term Meaning

Groove / sipe Channels in the tread that displace water and add edges for grip
Sidewall The flexible rubber side of the tyre between bead and tread

Flat spot Damaged tread area from a locked-wheel skid

Detailed Component Notes

Why over-inflation kills grip

A tyre at design pressure flexes slightly under load → the tread sits flat on the road across the full width of the contact patch. Over-inflated, the centre of the tread bulges out → only the centre touches the road → less rubber-on-road area → less grip + uneven tread wear (centre wears, shoulders stay fresh).

Under-inflation causes the opposite problem: tread edges bulge down + carry the load → shoulders wear, centre stays fresh + high rolling resistance → fuel waste + heat buildup → potential blowout.

Why centrifugal force at high speed shrinks the contact patch

At 200 km/h a tyre rotates at ~ 30 rev/s → tread blocks experience hundreds of g of centrifugal acceleration. The tread tries to fling outward away from the rim → only the part of the tread instantaneously in contact with the road stays compressed → effective contact area shrinks vs the same tyre at 50 km/h.

This is why high-speed-rated tyres (V, W, Y, ZR) have reinforced tread plies and shoulder belts to resist this centrifugal expansion. Cheap tyres rated H (210 km/h) or below will start to degrade visibly at speeds past their rating.

Why locked brakes create a flat spot

A skidding tyre is not rotating but the car is still moving forward. The rubber under the contact patch drags across the road, generating heat. After even a few seconds: - A chord of tread is worn flat where it dragged. - The rubber compound is glazed (heat-damaged). - On the next rotation, that flat-spot hits the road as a bump → vibration, "thump-thump-thump" at low speed.

Modern ABS prevents wheel lockup → eliminates flat spots from emergency braking. But hard old-school braking on a non-ABS car can ruin a brand-new tyre in one panic stop.

Why heat ruins tyres

Rubber is a viscoelastic polymer. Heat from continuous flexing: - Reduces tread depth faster (worn surface gets stickier and sloughs off). - Hardens the compound over time (oxidation of rubber bonds). - Causes "ply separation" where tread comes apart from the casing → blowout risk.

Under-inflated tyres run hotter (more flexing). High speed runs them hotter. Heavy loads run them hotter. All three together = ruined tyre quickly.

Key Facts

  • Contact patch (footprint) = small area where tyre meets road (~ palm-sized per tyre).
  • Depends on: tyre tread, inflation pressure, speed, load.
  • Decreases with: over-inflation, centrifugal force at high speed.
  • Aquaplaning = water film between tyre and road = total grip loss. Recovery: lift throttle, don't brake hard.
  • Typical tyre damage causes: kerb strikes, obstacles, locked brakes (flat spots), heat (tread breakdown).
  • Under-inflation → shoulder wear, heat buildup, blowout risk.
  • Over-inflation → centre wear, less grip.
  • Locked brakes (no ABS) can flat-spot a new tyre in one stop.
  • Friction circle: each tyre has a finite grip budget — split between accel/brake/cornering. Exceed it → tyre slides.
