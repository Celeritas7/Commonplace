Steering, Wheels & Tyres

Runflat Tyres & RPA/RPC Pressure Monitoring

Source images: IMG20250514181627

Overview

Runflat (BMW: RSC = Runflat System Component) tyres let the car keep driving safely after a puncture — typically up to 150 km at 80 km/h with zero air inside the tyre. They achieve this through a combination of stronger sidewalls (so the tyre doesn't collapse under the car's weight when deflated), an extended-hump rim (EH2) to retain the bead, and an electronic tyre-pressure monitoring system (RPA — Reifen-Pannen-Anzeige) that warns the driver as soon as a tyre loses pressure. The page covers the system components, advantages, the difference between the two BMW monitoring methods (RPA indirect / RPC direct), and what re-initialisation procedure is needed after a tyre change.

Working Principle

Why runflats exist

Notebook (IMG 181627, top): "★ Runflat tyres: ① Emergency conditions in case of puncture. ② Controllability is not lost when pressure suddenly drops." Margin: "stronger side-wall"

Conventional tyres go from "fine" to "useless flat tube wrapped around a rim" within seconds of a serious puncture — the tyre collapses, the bead comes off the rim, and the driver typically loses control as the rim grinds on the road. Runflats prevent this by having sidewalls so reinforced that the tyre supports the car's weight even with no air inside.

Runflat performance specs

Notebook (IMG 181623, bottom — actually visible here as well): "P = 0 bar speed — max 80 km/hr Light load: > 250 km (155 miles) Medium load: > 150 km (93 miles) Full load: > 50 km (31 miles)"

When pressure drops to zero (full puncture), the driver can keep moving safely up to these limits:

Load Maximum range at 80 km/h with 0 bar
Light load > 250 km (155 mi)
Medium load > 150 km (93 mi)
Full load > 50 km (31 mi)

Speed must be reduced to 80 km/h max — above that, sidewall flex generates too much heat too fast → tyre fails completely.

Why no spare wheel needed

The two big advantages over carrying a spare: - No need to stop on a motorway hard shoulder in dangerous conditions to change a tyre. - No 15–25 kg spare wheel taking up boot space → more cargo room + slightly lighter car.

This is why runflats are standard on BMWs from roughly 2003 (E60, X5, etc.) where a spare wheel was deleted from the spec entirely.

Foam ring on the rim

Notebook margin (right side): "Foam ring is fitted on rim on which run-flat tyre connected. So we can't remove this tyre without cutting it."

Some runflats have a noise-damping foam ring inserted in the tyre cavity (between rim well and inner tyre surface). It: - Reduces road-noise resonance inside the tyre cavity (cabin quietness). - Bonds permanently to the rim or to the tyre inner surface. - Means the tyre cannot be removed for repair without cutting it — fitting and removal is one-way only.

Runflat system components

Notebook (IMG 181627, top): "★ Runflat system: Components: ① Runflat tyre (RSC) ② Extended hump rim (EH2) ③ Tyre pressure monitoring system."

The three-part system:

# Component Role
1 Runflat tyre (RSC) Reinforced-sidewall tyre — carries car weight even with no air
2 Extended Hump (EH2) rim Special rim with double-height hump — keeps the deflated tyre bead retained against the rim flange
3 Tyre pressure monitoring Electronic system that warns the driver of pressure loss (otherwise they wouldn't know — the car still drives normally)

Why pressure monitoring is mandatory

On a runflat-equipped car, the driver cannot feel a puncture — the car keeps driving normally. Without electronic monitoring, the driver could easily exceed the 250 km / 150 km / 50 km limit without realising the tyre was deflated → catastrophic tyre failure + accident.

So the law (and BMW spec) requires automatic detection to warn the driver. Two implementation methods:

RPA vs RPC — the two monitoring methods

Notebook (IMG 181627, middle): "The change in pressure of tyre is recognised by ECU & hence the driver by: – RPA — tyre pressure control – RPC — tyre pressure check (measures circumference of wheel)

★ If there is pressure loss in one tyre the circumference of tyre decreases, so 'tyre defect indicator' compares the speed of tyre with other tyre speeds & sends message.

– RPA detects loss of approx 30% pressure. – We should have to re-initialise whenever the air [is] refilled or tyre [is] changed."

Type How it works Pros Cons
RPA (Reifen-Pannen-Anzeige) — indirect Compares wheel rotational speed across all four wheels. Deflated tyre has slightly smaller rolling circumference → spins faster than the others → ABS wheel-speed sensors detect the mismatch → DSC ECU warns No extra hardware; uses existing ABS sensors. Low cost. Only detects ~30% pressure loss before triggering. Slow detection. Must be re-initialised after every tyre change or pressure adjustment.
RPC (Reifen-Druck-Control) — direct Pressure sensor mounted in each rim's wheel electronics, transmits the actual pressure reading to the receiver via radio Detects small pressure changes (0.2-0.4 bar). Reports actual pressure values. Faster + more accurate. Extra hardware in each wheel. Sensor battery life ~5 years. Sensors must be re-paired after wheel swap.

The notebook page is mostly about RPA but introduces RPC. Modern BMWs (F-chassis and later) use RPC (the more accurate direct system).

Re-initialisation

Notebook: "We should have to re-initialise whenever the air [is] refilled or tyre [is] changed."

After any of: - Tyre inflation pressure changed (even just topping up) - Tyre replaced - Wheels swapped between positions - New season tyres fitted

...the system needs to be re-initialised so it learns the new "normal" baseline. Procedure detailed in RDC chapter.

Detailed Component Notes

Runflat tyre construction

The sidewall is the magic — a runflat has: - Thicker rubber in the sidewall. - Internal reinforcement plies (Kevlar/nylon braid) that act as load-bearing structure. - Cup-shaped reinforcement at the bead that locks against the EH2 hump.

When deflated, the sidewall doesn't fold flat — it stays roughly cylindrical, with the tyre tread continuing to contact the road. Driving feel is much firmer than a normal tyre (less compliance) but the car remains controllable.

Why EH2 (Extended Hump 2)

A normal rim hump retains the tyre bead under normal pressure. A runflat needs to retain the bead at zero pressure plus dynamic side loads (cornering pushes the bead inward). EH2 = a double-height hump that physically blocks the bead from sliding inward → critical for keeping the deflated tyre on the rim during a cornering manoeuvre.

If a runflat tyre is fitted to a non-EH2 rim, the system is invalid → driving on a puncture would unseat the bead anyway.

RPA — indirect sensing limitation

The 30% pressure loss threshold (the trainer's "RPA detects loss of approx 30%") is a fundamental limit of the indirect-method physics: - A tyre at 2.2 bar vs 2.0 bar (9% loss) has a circumference change of < 1 mm — below the ABS sensor resolution. - A tyre at 2.2 bar vs 1.5 bar (~32% loss) has noticeable circumference difference → detectable.

So RPA cannot warn of slow leaks — only of acute deflation. RPC (direct sensors) catches slow leaks immediately.

RPC — Direct pressure sensing

Each wheel has a small electronics module mounted behind the air-filling valve, containing: - Pressure sensor (silicon diaphragm) - Temperature sensor (to compensate for temperature-induced pressure changes) - Transmitter (~ 433 MHz UHF radio) - Battery (lithium coin cell — ~ 5 years life)

The module transmits a pressure value every few seconds (more often when pressure changes rapidly). A receiver in the car body picks up the transmission and forwards it to the DSC ECU.

Why RPC needs re-pairing after wheel swap

Each pressure sensor has a unique ID. The car knows that "ID 12345 = front-left position." If wheels are swapped (e.g., front-to-back rotation), the IDs no longer match positions → either the car must be told the new positions (re-pairing), or the system auto-learns over a few drive cycles by correlating with wheel-speed signals.

Key Facts

  • Runflat (RSC) tyre: reinforced sidewall supports car weight at 0 bar.
  • Specs at 0 bar: max 80 km/h for > 250 km (light) / > 150 km (medium) / > 50 km (full load).
  • 3 components: Runflat tyre + Extended Hump (EH2) rim + tyre-pressure monitoring.
  • No spare wheel carried — runflat replaces it.
  • Foam ring (in some variants) damps noise but one-use — tyre can't be removed without cutting.
  • RPA (indirect): uses ABS wheel speeds; detects ~30% pressure loss. Re-init after any change.
  • RPC (direct): pressure sensor in each wheel; detects 0.2 bar loss. Re-pair after wheel swap.
  • Driver feels nothing when runflat deflates → electronic warning is mandatory.
  • Modern BMW (F-chassis +) uses RPC for direct sensing.

  • [?] "Foam ring" annotation — applies to some specific runflat tyre models (BMW used these on certain X5 / 7-series variants). Not all runflats have foam.
  • [?] "30%" pressure-loss threshold for RPA is the dealer-training number; real BMW DTC trigger is somewhere between 20–35% depending on the implementation.
  • [?] RPA = German "Reifen-Pannen-Anzeige" (tyre puncture indicator) — interpretation confirmed by context.
  • [?] RPC = German "Reifen-Druck-Control" (tyre pressure control) — interpretation confirmed by context; modern terminology is TPMS (Tyre Pressure Monitoring System) — internationally standardised.
  • [?] "Tyre defect indicator" mentioned in the notebook = the RPA implementation. Older BMW term.
  • [?] The list "RPA detects loss of approx 30%" + "We should have to re-initialise whenever air [is] refilled or tyre [is] changed" is specific to RPA only; RPC has different re-pair protocols.
