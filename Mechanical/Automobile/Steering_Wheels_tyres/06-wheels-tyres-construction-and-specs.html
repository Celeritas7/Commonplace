Wheels & Tyres — Construction, Types, Specs

Source images: IMG20250514181614, IMG20250514181623, IMG20250515002222

Notebook date: 25/7/2013

Overview

The everyday-service reference page on tyres: the three seasonal types (summer, winter, all-season) and what makes them different; the two reasons to fill tyres with nitrogen instead of plain air; the anatomy of the rim cross-section (well, shoulder, lip, offset); the rim sizing nomenclature (e.g., "3½J × 18 H2 IS25") that lets you read a tyre size off the sidewall; the function of the hump moulded into the rim well; and how a typical tyre sidewall code like "165/70 R 13" decodes into width, aspect ratio, construction type, and rim diameter.

Working Principle

Three seasonal tyre types

Notebook (IMG 181614): "① Summer tyres — for wet & dry roads; temperature from 7 °C (45 °F); very high speeds. ② Winter tyres — M&S tyres. Margin: 'M&S — Mud & Snow, to classify as winter tyres.' ③ All-season tyres — there should not be extreme gap between summer & winter season otherwise it can damage these tyres."

Type Compound + tread When to use
Summer Hard rubber compound, optimised for grip above ~ 7 °C; tread blocks with continuous ribs for wet/dry road Wet AND dry roads. Cold-temperature performance drops sharply (rubber goes hard).
Winter / M&S Softer compound stays flexible below 7 °C; deep aggressive tread with many sipes (small slits) for snow/slush traction Mud, snow, cold weather. Wears fast above 10–15 °C — must be swapped off in summer.
All-season Compromise compound + intermediate tread Mild climates with no extremes. Worse than summer in summer, worse than winter in winter — but no swap needed.

M&S marking

Margin: "M&S — Mud & Snow, to classify as winter tyres."

"M&S" (or "M+S") on the sidewall is a manufacturer's claim that the tyre meets the mud-and-snow tread pattern requirement — it's necessary to legally classify as a winter tyre in many European countries. Stricter classification = the 3-peak-mountain-snowflake (3PMSF) symbol introduced later.

Why fill tyres with nitrogen

Notebook (IMG 181614, middle): "Nitrogen filling of tyres — Adv: ① N₂ molecules are bigger than O₂ molecules so it's less easily [leaks] than the other constituents. ② Also N₂ doesn't assist combustion, in the event of accident."

Advantage Explanation
Slower leakage N₂ molecule diameter ≈ 3.0 Å; O₂ ≈ 2.9 Å plus polar O₂ permeates rubber more easily than non-polar N₂. So pure N₂ holds inflation pressure longer (~50% slower loss rate).
Non-flammable N₂ doesn't oxidise → in a tyre fire or wreck, no combustion-supporting gas inside. Trivial advantage on road cars; more relevant on aircraft tyres + racing.

Plain air is already ~78% N₂. Pure-N₂ filling is a modest gain — most useful on cars driven infrequently (lower pressure-loss rate over time).

Rim sizing — the code on every wheel

Notebook (IMG 181623, top): "3½ J × 18 H2 IS25 ↳ Rim width (3½ inches) ↳ Rim edge design (J) ↳ Rim type (×) ↳ Rim diameter (18 inches) ↳ Hump design (H2) ↳ Rim offset (IS25 = 25 mm inset)"

Decoding the standard rim code:

Code piece Meaning
3½ (width) Rim width in inches (from inside edge to inside edge of the lips)
J (rim edge design) Lip profile shape — J, JJ, K, etc. determine where the tyre bead seats
× (rim type separator) "Cross" symbol — indicates a one-piece deep-well rim
18 (diameter) Rim diameter in inches
H2 (hump design) Type of hump moulded into the well (see below). H = one hump; H2 = double hump
IS25 (offset) "Inset" = +25 mm — the mounting face sits 25 mm inboard of the wheel centreline

Function of the hump in the rim

Notebook (IMG 181623, middle): "★ Function/purpose of hump: ① Positioned at outer side of rim, to sustain high stresses occurred on rim while cornering. ② Doesn't [allow] tyre to escape & holds it. ③ Upto certain inflation range it prevents air leakage & jumping of rim. ④ H, H1, H2, EH1, EH2 types of rims to hold tyres during under-inflation conditions."

The hump is a small bump moulded into the rim well, just inboard of the bead seat. It acts like a physical lip that the tyre bead has to climb over to come off the rim. Functions: - Keeps the tyre bead seated against the rim flange during cornering when lateral force pushes the bead inward. - Prevents bead unseating on a sudden deflation (run-flat behaviour). - Maintains air seal during rapid pressure changes.

The EH (Extended Hump) designations were developed for run-flat tyres — provide stronger bead retention so the tyre doesn't peel off the rim when pressure drops to zero.

Tyre sidewall code — 165/70 R 13

Notebook (IMG 002222, central diagram): Tyre cross-section labelled with "165/70 R 13" at the centre + radiating arrows to: - 165 → Width (in mm) - 70 → Aspect ratio = Height / Width - R → Radial (type of construction) - 13 → Rim diameter (in inches)

Plus annotations: "Speed symbol (e.g. 180 km/h max)", "load index", "Tyre defect indicator", "Department of transport approved", "12 — week — last week of March (date of manufacture)", "36 — year — year of manufacturing (2036?? actually 36 = 1996 or '06)", "5 years from days of manufacture tyre becomes useless, irrespective of use of it. Even not [used] it will seem to be new. Because rubber loses its property", "Winter tyres become hard in summer, life become less of tyres"

Decoding 165/70 R 13:

Field Meaning
165 Section width in millimetres (165 mm wide tyre)
70 Aspect ratio = sidewall height / section width × 100 (so sidewall height = 165 × 0.70 = 115.5 mm)
R Radial construction (vs bias-ply); R is now universal
13 Rim diameter in inches (fits 13-inch rim)

Other sidewall codes typically present: - Load index (e.g., 84 = 500 kg per tyre at max pressure) - Speed symbol (H = 210 km/h, V = 240, ZR = > 240, etc.) - DOT code (date of manufacture: last 4 digits = week + year, e.g., "1213" = 12th week of 2013) - Department-of-transport approval marks

Tyre lifespan

Notebook: "5 years from days of manufacture, tyre becomes useless irrespective of use. Even [if] not [used], it will seem to be new. Because rubber loses its property."

Rubber ages chemically — UV, ozone, temperature cycling all degrade the polymer chains. A spare tyre stored for 5+ years is structurally compromised even if it looks brand new and has full tread depth. Check the DOT date code on any used tyre purchase.

Detailed Component Notes

Why 7 °C as the summer-winter cutoff

  • Above 7 °C: summer rubber stays in its design hardness range. Winter rubber is too soft → squirms, wears fast, less precise.
  • Below 7 °C: summer rubber hardens → less grip, especially on wet surfaces. Winter rubber stays in its design range → grip retained.

This is why some European countries mandate winter tyres below a certain calendar date — the cutoff isn't about snow being on the ground, it's about ambient temperature crossing the rubber's transition point.

What "tubeless" actually requires

A tubeless tyre seals directly against the rim — no inner tube. This requires: - A well-and-hump rim design that holds the bead in place under all conditions. - Continuous rim (no spoke holes through to the well) so air can't escape. - A valve mounted directly through the rim with an O-ring seal.

All modern car rims are tubeless. Inner-tube rims survive on motorcycles, vintage cars, and some bicycles.

Rim offset (IS / ET) — why it matters

  • Positive offset (IS25 or ET+25) — mounting face is inboard of the rim centreline by 25 mm. Tucks the wheel in toward the body. Typical for FWD cars.
  • Negative offset (ET−25) — mounting face is outboard of the centreline. Wheel pokes out. Typical for off-road and certain performance setups.
  • Zero offset — mounting face coincides with centreline.

Wrong offset = wheel rubs body/suspension, wrecks bearings, fails MOT/inspection.

"IS" vs "ET" abbreviation

"IS" (German "Innen-Senkung" / "inset") and "ET" (German "Einpresstiefe") are the same offset measurement, just different national conventions. The number is the offset in mm; positive = inset.

Why "rim diameter calculated from rim's offset"

The trainer's note: rim diameter is measured at the bead-seat diameter (where the tyre bead sits), not at the outer edge of the rim. The offset reference is to the mounting face which is fixed → diameter is calculated from this geometric reference.

Key Facts

  • 3 tyre types: summer (>7 °C), winter/M&S (<7 °C), all-season (compromise).
  • M&S marking = mud-and-snow tread pattern for winter classification.
  • Nitrogen fill advantages: slower leakage (bigger molecule), non-combustion-supporting.
  • Rim cross-section: well + shoulder + lip + hump + attachment face. Well lets bead drop during mounting.
  • Rim code format: "3½J × 18 H2 IS25" = width × type-diameter × hump-design + offset.
  • Hump types: H, H1, H2, EH1, EH2. Extended hump (EH) for run-flat tyres.
  • Tyre sidewall code: 165/70 R 13 = width/aspect-ratio + Radial + rim diameter in inches.
  • DOT code on sidewall = week + year of manufacture (4 digits).
  • Tyre lifespan: 5 years from manufacture regardless of use. Rubber chemistry degrades.
  • Tubeless requires hump-well rim + valve sealed through the rim.
  • Offset (IS/ET) sets where the wheel sits laterally relative to the hub centreline.

Open Questions / Uncertainty

  • [?] Rim code shown as "3½ J × 18 H2 IS25" in notebook. The "3½ inches" is unusually narrow for a passenger-car rim — typical car rims are 6"–9" wide. The trainer may have written 3½ as a generic placeholder or for a motorcycle example.
  • [?] "IS" for offset is BMW/European service-literature usage. The same measurement is called "ET" in German specs and just "offset" in US service literature.
  • [?] Tyre date code annotations on IMG 002222 read "12 — week — last week of March" + "36 — year — year of manufacturing." A 4-digit DOT code like "1236" = 12th week of 2036 (future-dated nonsense) so this is a generic teaching example, not a real tyre.
  • [?] "5 years" as useful tyre lifespan — manufacturers' guidance varies between 5–10 years. 5 is conservative; 6 is common. Tread depth + visual cracking + bulge inspection also matter.
  • [?] No mention of load index numbers or speed symbol letters as specific tables — just acknowledged as sidewall markings.
  • [?] "Rim diameter (calculated from rim's offset)" — phrasing slightly off. The diameter is measured at the bead seat; the offset is a separate measurement from the mounting face. Both reference points are needed to fit the wheel correctly to a hub.
