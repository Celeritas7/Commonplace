Steering, Wheels & Tyres

Wheel Balancing — Static, Dynamic, Imbalance Forces

Source images: IMG20250514181651, IMG20250514181707

Overview

A rotating wheel that isn't perfectly balanced creates forces that grow quadratically with speed — a tiny imbalance at parking speed becomes a destructive force at highway speed. Static imbalance = heavy spot on the wheel's perimeter; dynamic imbalance = mass uneven across the wheel's width. Both are corrected by adding counter-weights to the rim. The page gives a numerical force table showing how a few grams of imbalance generates hundreds of newtons at highway speeds, the 3 effects of imbalance, the 3 causes, the balancing procedure (3 steps), and the types of counter-weights used.

Working Principle

Why imbalance matters

Notebook (IMG 181651, top): "★ Balancing the wheel: ① One side of the area is heavier. ② Vehicle tends to jump. – Imbalances are: ① Unpleasant while driving ② Affect safety ③ Lead to increased wear"

Three consequences of imbalance: 1. Unpleasant driving feel — vibration through steering wheel, seat, body. 2. Safety risk — vibration disturbs steering precision; severe imbalance can wear bearings to failure. 3. Accelerated wear — wheel bearings, tie-rod ends, ball joints, suspension bushings all see oscillating loads → fatigue + premature failure.

Two types of imbalance

Notebook (IMG 181651): "static ... dynamic ..."

Type What it looks like What it feels like
Static (single-plane) Heavy spot on the wheel's perimeter — like a wobbly Ferris wheel Vehicle bounces up-and-down (vertical vibration) — sometimes called "hop"
Dynamic (two-plane) Heavy spot on one side of the wheel's width but not the other (or unevenly distributed across width) Vehicle shakes side-to-side (lateral/yaw vibration) — sometimes called "wobble"

Notebook (IMG 181651, middle): "But if we balance imbalances by using counter weights, uneven distribution of mass on front & back halves of wheels → WHEEL WOBBLES WHEN IT TURNS."

This is the trainer's key insight: simply adding a single counter-weight to fix static imbalance can introduce dynamic imbalance if the counter-weight is placed on only one side of the rim. Correct balancing requires weights on both faces of the rim — usually one on the outer rim flange and one on the inner rim flange.

Imbalance force grows with speed

Notebook (IMG 181651, lower half — force table): "Forces acting on tyre at: – Speed 0–30 km/h (rotation speed B) – 80 km/h (weight at C: ~ 0) – 120 km/h (weight at C: shown)"

Table: A (grams) | B (~80 km/h) | C (~120 km/h) ────────────────────────────────────────────── 10 | 16 N | 35 N 20 | 32 N | 71 N 50 | 79 N | 177 N 100 | 157 N | 354 N "Due to imbalance, weight increase by 4 folds means, 10 N force becomes 2500 N at 100 km/hr."

The fundamental physics: centrifugal force scales with the square of speed.

$$F = m \cdot \omega^2 \cdot r$$

Where: - $m$ = imbalance mass - $\omega$ = angular velocity (proportional to road speed) - $r$ = radius (rim distance)

So doubling the speed quadruples the force. The notebook's "weight increase by 4 folds" claim corresponds to this: from 80 km/h to 120 km/h is a 1.5× speed increase, so force grows by ~ 2.25×. From 0 to 100 km/h is a much bigger jump; tiny imbalances become very large forces.

Imbalance At 80 km/h At 120 km/h
10 g 16 N (~ 1.6 kg-force) 35 N
20 g 32 N 71 N
50 g 79 N (significant) 177 N
100 g 157 N (steering vibrates noticeably) 354 N (~ 36 kg-force!)

The trainer's "10 N becomes 2500 N at 100 km/h" claim — interpretation: a small static imbalance at low speed translates into enormous dynamic force at highway speed. This is why balancing within 5–10 grams matters.

Causes of imbalance

Notebook (IMG 181707, top): "Causes of imbalance: ① Damage to rim resulting in deformation ② Balancing weights coming off ③ One sided wear e.g. because of slamming of brakes without ABS."

Cause Where it comes from
Rim deformation Kerb strike, pothole hit. Even small bend in rim → asymmetric mass distribution → imbalance
Balancing weights coming off Old adhesive failing, weights getting knocked off by stones/snow chains
One-sided wear (from locked brakes without ABS) Flat spot on the tread = significant mass missing from one part of the tread = static imbalance. ABS prevents this on modern cars

Balancing procedure

Notebook (IMG 181707, middle): "Balancing — Procedure: ① Clamp wheel with the valve to the bottom ② Measure with balancing m/c ③ Locate the [place] where to clamp weights."

The 3-step workflow on a balancing machine:

  1. Clamp wheel onto balancer spindle — orient with the valve stem at the bottom (a known reference point so all measurements are relative to this position).
  2. Spin and measure — the balancer rotates the wheel at high speed and detects vibration in two planes (inner + outer rim flange). It reports where weight needs to be added and how much.
  3. Locate and clamp weights — based on the measurement, weights are clipped, glued, or hammered to specific positions on the rim flange.

Types of balancing weights

Notebook (IMG 181707, lower): "★ Balancing weights: ① Clamping in case of steel rim ② J-shape profile on alloy wheels to fix the weights ③ Some alloy steel rims have adhesive weights which can stick to rim

Adhesive weights: These types of weights can sustain till temp of 200 °C"

Type Where used Attachment method
Clamp-on (steel rim) Old-style steel rims Spring-clamp grips the rim flange — hammered on with a small hammer
J-profile (alloy wheel clip) Modern alloy rims with a J-shaped flange profile Specially shaped weight that clips over the J-flange
Adhesive (alloy or chrome rims) Premium alloy rims, chrome-plated rims Self-adhesive strip — stuck inside the rim where it won't be visible. Sustains up to 200 °C before adhesive fails

Detailed Component Notes

Why static and dynamic are both important

  • Static balance alone is what an old-style "bubble balancer" measures — wheel hung on a vertical spindle, gravity locates the heavy spot. Works for narrow tyres (motorcycle, older skinny car tyres).
  • Dynamic balance requires the wheel to spin — the imbalance manifests differently across the wheel's width.
  • A wheel can have zero static imbalance (no heavy spot when stationary) but bad dynamic imbalance (heavy on inner, light on outer, with the masses cancelling vertically but creating a yaw moment when spinning).

Modern wide tyres need dynamic (spin-)balancing. Bubble-balanced tyres on wide alloy wheels = noticeable highway vibration even though the wheel appears balanced at rest.

Why "valve at bottom" for the reference position

The valve stem is a small but real mass that's always at the same angular position on the wheel. By putting the valve at the bottom for the initial measurement, the balancer takes the valve's mass into account in its imbalance calculation — so the weights it recommends are correct for the wheel as assembled.

If you spin the wheel with valve at any random angle, the machine might think the valve mass is part of the imbalance and tell you to add a weight to cancel it — which would then become an imbalance once the valve is back at a random position.

Adhesive weight 200 °C rating

Adhesive (stick-on) weights are made with acrylic adhesive that survives brake-rotor radiated heat in normal driving but degrades above ~ 200 °C. Hard track use or repeated emergency braking can heat the inboard side of the rim above this → adhesive fails → weight falls off → instant imbalance.

Race-car wheels rarely use adhesive weights for this reason — clamp-on, drilled-and-bolted, or even nitrided into the rim are used.

Why ABS reduces wheel-balance maintenance

Notebook causes: "One sided wear e.g. because of slamming of brakes without ABS."

A locked wheel during emergency braking creates a flat spot on the tread — visible as one area where the tread is suddenly thinner. This is mass missing from one part of the wheel = static imbalance instantly. Modern ABS prevents lockup → tyres don't develop flat spots from panic stops → balancing maintains better over the tyre's life.

Key Facts

  • Wheel imbalance = uneven mass distribution → vibration, safety risk, premature wear.
  • Static imbalance = heavy spot on perimeter → vertical bounce.
  • Dynamic imbalance = mass uneven across the wheel's width → lateral wobble.
  • Force grows quadratically with speed: doubling speed = 4× the imbalance force.
  • 100 g imbalance at 120 km/h = 354 N (~36 kg-force) shaking the steering.
  • 3 causes: rim deformation (kerb hits), weights coming off, one-sided wear (locked brakes without ABS).
  • 3-step balancing procedure: clamp valve-down, measure on machine, clamp weights.
  • 3 weight types: clamp-on (steel rims), J-profile (alloy wheels), adhesive (alloy / chrome). Adhesive rated to 200 °C.
  • Both faces must be weighted in dynamic balancing — single-side weight can cure static but cause dynamic imbalance.
  • Valve-at-bottom mounting on the balancer ensures the valve mass is accounted for in the calculation.
  • Modern ABS has reduced one-sided wear cases by preventing brake-lock flat spots.
