Steering — Components Overview, Power & Electronic Steering

Source images: IMG20250515002135, IMG20250515002146

Notebook dates: 26/7/2013 (components) and 24/7/2013 (power steering)

Overview

The steering system converts the driver's wheel input into a left/right angle at the front wheels. The page lists the standard mechanical components (steering wheel, column, universal joint, rack & pinion, tie rods, knuckle/hub) and shows the force-flow chain from driver to wheel. A second page then layers in hydraulic power steering (mechanical effort assisted by an engine-driven pump) and electronic (electric) power steering (an electric motor on the rack assisted by a steering-angle sensor) — the two assist technologies that took over from purely-mechanical steering in the era.

Working Principle

Component list (notebook)

Notebook (IMG 002135): "Steering wheel, Tie rod, steering arm, rack & pinion, steering column, steering wheel hub, universal joint, ball joint"

Force-flow chain

Notebook flow: steering wheel ──► steering column ──► universal joint ↓ Rack & pinion ↓ wheel ◄── knuckle/hub ◄── ball joint ◄── steering arm ◄── tie rods

Reading the chain end-to-end: 1. Driver turns steering wheel. 2. Steering column transmits rotation downward through the dashboard. 3. Universal joint lets the column kink around obstructions (firewall, exhaust manifold) without binding. 4. Rack & pinion converts the rotary motion to linear motion — the pinion is the small driving gear, the rack is the long toothed bar. 5. Tie rods (left and right) push/pull from the rack ends. 6. Steering arms extend from each front wheel hub; the tie rods attach to them. 7. Ball joints at the steering-arm and tie-rod ends let the wheel pivot freely while transmitting steering force. 8. Knuckle/hub rotates about its kingpin axis; carries the wheel bearing → wheel turns.

The rack & pinion sketch

Notebook (top of IMG 002135): Pinion drawn as a small star meshing with a long horizontal toothed Rack.

The pinion meshes with the rack such that one full turn of the pinion moves the rack by π × pitch-diameter of the pinion. So pinion design directly sets steering ratio (turns lock-to-lock).

Power steering (notebook IMG 002146)

Notebook (top half, dated 24/7/2013): "Power steering" with hydraulic circuit drawing — fluid line, rotary valve (direction control), piston, pinion, steering column, "to reservoir", "from pump".

How hydraulic power steering works: - An engine-driven pump (belt off the crankshaft) supplies pressurised hydraulic fluid to a rotary valve at the base of the steering column. - The rotary valve sits between the steering column and the rack pinion. - When the driver turns the wheel, a torsion bar inside the column twists slightly → opens the rotary valve in the appropriate direction → high-pressure fluid is routed to one side of a piston integrated into the rack. - The piston pushes the rack → adds force to the driver's steering input → assist. - When the wheel is centred, fluid flow recirculates back to the reservoir.

Notebook bullets: - Adv: "It is self-dependant. No need of battery current." - Disadv: "Pump is driven by engine." (continuous parasitic load even when no assist is needed)

Electronic power steering (EPS)

Notebook (bottom half of IMG 002146): "In case of electronic power steering: (1) There is extra gear is provided on rack which is operated by electric motor. (2) There is sensor connected on that gear which calculates the steering angle & sends the value to the motor. And according to that the amount of power to be given by motor is decided."

"Adv: Less components, weight reduced." "Disadv: Current is consumed from external source." (i.e. battery → alternator → engine, but only when assisting)

How EPS works: - An electric motor is geared to the rack (or to the column on column-EPS). - A steering-angle / torque sensor measures both how far the wheel is turned AND how hard the driver is twisting it. - The motor delivers proportional assist — only when needed.

Benefits: - Lower fuel consumption (no continuous pump drag). - Variable assist by speed (heavier feel at high speed, lighter at parking). - Lighter and simpler (no pump, no hoses, no reservoir, no belt). - Foundation for lane-keep / park-assist (motor can steer the car on its own).

The trainer's "current consumed from external source" disadvantage is the only real downside — but it's negligible vs the pump-drag savings.

Detailed Component Notes

Universal joint — why it's needed

A driver's steering wheel sits at an ergonomic angle (about 25° from vertical, leaning toward the driver). The rack sits below the engine, often offset to one side. A single straight column can't connect these without going through the firewall at a punishing angle. The universal joint lets the column bend at one or two points to navigate around the engine, exhaust manifold, and firewall structures.

Modern cars often use two U-joints in series (intermediate shaft) for smoother torque transmission and easier packaging.

Steering ratio (not explicitly on the page)

  • Manual steering — about 18–20:1 (turns wheel 18° for 1° at front wheels). Heavy on parking.
  • Hydraulic PS — about 14–16:1 (faster ratio because assist makes it OK to need more steering wheel angle for the same torque).
  • EPS / Active steering — variable, sometimes 8–10:1 at low speed (quick parking), 18–20:1 at high speed (stability).

Why a torsion bar in the hydraulic-PS rotary valve

The driver's input torque needs to be detected without the valve opening from vibration. A torsion bar inside the column twists by a few degrees in proportion to driver torque → the rotary valve detects this twist and opens proportionally → the assist scales smoothly with driver effort. Self-centring when the driver lets go.

Why "self-dependant" is an advantage of hydraulic

Notebook: "Adv: self-dependant. No need of battery current."

Hydraulic PS keeps working as long as the engine runs — independent of the electrical system. Useful in old days when battery / alternator issues were common. Less relevant now (modern cars depend on electrics for everything anyway).

Why EPS won

  • ~3% fuel saving vs hydraulic (no continuous pump drag).
  • No fluid leaks ever.
  • Software-tunable feel.
  • Enables lane-keep, park-assist, traffic-jam-assist, eventually Level-2 autonomy.
  • All BMWs since the F-chassis era (2010+) use EPS.

Key Facts

  • Steering chain: wheel → column → U-joint → rack & pinion → tie rod → steering arm → ball joint → knuckle → wheel.
  • Rack & pinion converts rotary motion to linear motion at the heart of the steering box.
  • Universal joint kinks the column to navigate around engine/firewall.
  • Hydraulic power steering: engine-driven pump + rotary valve + rack piston. Always-on assist; pump drag is the cost.
  • Electronic power steering (EPS): electric motor + angle/torque sensor. Variable assist; lower fuel use; enables lane-keep / park-assist.
  • EPS won — all modern BMWs use it.
  • Steering ratio typically 14:1 (PS) to 8–20:1 variable (EPS / active steering).
  • Hydraulic PS "self-dependant" — works without electrics; EPS needs ECU + 12 V supply.
