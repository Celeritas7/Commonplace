Steering, Wheels & Tyres

Active Steering & Hydraulic Pump (with ECO valve)

Source images: IMG20250515003636, IMG20250515003644

Overview

Active steering is BMW's name for a steering system in which the steering ratio is variable and the system can add or subtract steering angle independently of the driver — under control of an electronic actuator and a DSC-coordinated control unit. Combined with a modern hydraulic pump design that has an ECO (Electrically Controlled Orifice) valve to vary fluid output by demand instead of running at full flow always, this delivers two big benefits: light, quick parking + stable, accurate high-speed driving from the same steering system, with lower fuel consumption than a constant-displacement pump.

Working Principle

Active steering — what it does

Notebook (IMG 003636): "★ Active steering — controls steering independent of driver. No interference of electric motor. Task & principle: 1. Support of steering effort by power steering. 2. Overriding steering by actuating unit. 3. Dynamic driving interventions by active steering system. 4. [blank]"

Three layered functions: 1. Power-steering assist — same as a standard hydraulic PS system; reduces effort needed at the steering wheel. 2. Override steering — an additional electromechanical actuator can add or subtract steering angle between what the driver inputs and what the front wheels actually do. Variable-ratio behaviour. 3. Dynamic driving interventions — the system can apply small steering corrections on its own, coordinated with DSC (stability control) to counter understeer / oversteer.

Power-steering layer (3 details)

Notebook (IMG 003636, middle): "Power steering: 1. Steering angle supplied by driver is overridden by active steering. 2. Adjusting force on wheel angle applied by conventional power steering. 3. Power steering adapts steering effort perfectly to driving conditions."

The hydraulic power steering still provides the muscle to actually move the rack — what's new in active steering is that the angle going into the rack is the sum of: - The driver's steering-wheel rotation (passed through a normal mechanical column). - An additional rotation added/subtracted by the actuating unit (an electric motor + planetary gear that sits in the steering column).

Both feed into the rack, which is then assisted by the conventional hydraulic pump for low effort.

Components (3)

Notebook (IMG 003636, bottom): "Components: 1. Control unit 2. Sensors 3. Communication with DSC"

The classic control-unit + sensors + comm pattern (cf. DME architecture, EGS architecture): - Control unit — runs the active-steering algorithm, decides target ratio and intervention angle. - Sensors — steering angle, vehicle speed, yaw rate (from DSC), lateral acceleration (from DSC), wheel speeds (from ABS via DSC). - Communication with DSC — bi-directional: DSC sends stability-related signals; active steering sends steering-angle / intent.

Why variable steering ratio matters

At low speed (parking) you want few steering wheel turns lock-to-lock so you can spin the wheel quickly without crossing arms or shuffling — fast ratio (e.g., 8:1, so 1 turn of wheel = 1/8 turn of front wheels which is plenty).

At high speed (autobahn) you want slow, precise steering — small wheel inputs shouldn't translate to large front-wheel angles → slow ratio (e.g., 18:1, so 1 turn = 1/18 turn).

Active steering smoothly varies the ratio from ~ 8:1 at parking to ~ 18:1 at highway speed by adding (or subtracting) the actuating unit's rotation between column and rack. Driver feels the same steering-wheel range across all speeds; car responds differently.

Hydraulic-pump improvement — the ECO valve

Notebook (IMG 003644, header): "★ Design of hydraulic pump: – Vent-type pump with ECO (Electrically Controlled Orifice) to control volumetric flow of fluid."

A conventional vane-type hydraulic power-steering pump displaces a fixed volume per revolution — output flow scales with engine rpm. At highway cruise the pump is delivering far more fluid than the steering needs → excess flow recirculates through the pressure-relief valve → wasted energy → heat → fuel waste.

The ECO valve is an electrically-controlled orifice that varies the pump's effective output: - At low speed / parking — orifice fully open → high flow → strong assist. - At high speed / straight-line — orifice closed → low flow → low assist needed, low parasitic drag.

The DME (or active-steering ECU) commands the ECO valve based on speed, steering activity, and other inputs.

Pump output curve (graph in notebook)

Notebook (IMG 003644, plot): Y-axis: volumetric flow (L/min); X-axis: engine speed (rpm). Two curves: - "Valve fully supplied with current" → high volumetric flow, climbing curve up to 800 mA (full ECO command). - "Valve not supplied with current" → less volumetric flow, much lower curve, asymptotic at 0 mA.

The graph shows how the same physical pump can have two completely different flow characteristics depending on the ECO valve command. The control unit commands the right operating point to suit current driving conditions.

Benefits of overriding steering

Typical benefits: - Variable steering ratio — fast at low speed (parking), slow at high speed (stability). - Stability assistance — counter-steers automatically during oversteer or asymmetric braking. - Lane-keeping foundation — system can steer the car on its own (with software upgrade). - Lower fuel consumption — ECO-valved pump uses less energy than always-on.

Detailed Component Notes

Why a "planetary" gear set in the actuating unit

A planetary gear can take two inputs and produce one output (the escalator superposition principle): - Input 1: driver's column rotation (one planetary element, e.g., the carrier). - Input 2: electric motor's rotation (another element, e.g., the sun gear). - Output: rack pinion (the third element, e.g., the ring gear).

When the motor is stationary, output = driver input (1:1 ratio). When the motor runs forward, output > input (faster ratio). When the motor runs backward, output < input (slower ratio). Same hardware → infinite variable ratio.

Why the pump still uses hydraulics in an "active" car

Active steering is hybrid — hydraulic muscle (still does the heavy work of moving the rack) + electronic brain (adds the ratio variation). Pure electric power steering (EPS) eliminated the hydraulics altogether on later cars. Active steering was the transitional generation in the mid-2000s BMW lineup (E60-era).

When does the actuating-unit motor run?

  • Below ~ 60 km/h — adds rotation → fast variable ratio for parking and city.
  • Above ~ 60 km/h — subtracts rotation → slow ratio for highway stability.
  • During DSC intervention — adds counter-steer at the wheels independent of driver input.

The motor is small (just adds/subtracts angle, not torque) — actual rack motion is still done by hydraulics.

ECO valve current calibration

Notebook (graph annotation): "800 mA" max → "0 mA" min.

The ECO valve solenoid is driven from 0 mA (closed, minimum flow) to about 800 mA (fully open, maximum flow). The ECU varies this continuously based on the driving situation.

Self-restraint after collision

If the active-steering system detects a sensor failure or motor fault, the actuating unit defaults to 1:1 pass-through (the planetary gear set has a mechanical fail-safe that locks the motor side, leaving the driver in direct control through a fixed ratio). The car is still drivable, just without ratio variation or stability assistance.

Key Facts

  • Active steering = variable ratio + autonomous steering intervention, layered over conventional hydraulic power steering.
  • 3 functions: support steering effort (PS), override steering (variable ratio), dynamic interventions (DSC coordination).
  • Actuating unit = electric motor + planetary gear set in the steering column. Adds/subtracts rotation between column halves.
  • Variable ratio: ~ 8:1 at parking → ~ 18:1 at highway. Driver always turns the wheel by the same amount; car responds differently.
  • Components: control unit, sensors, communication with DSC.
  • ECO valve (Electrically Controlled Orifice) modulates the hydraulic pump's effective flow → 0 mA (low flow, low assist) to ~800 mA (full flow, high assist).
  • Saves fuel vs constant-displacement pump (less drag at highway cruise).
  • BMW E60-era technology; later replaced by full EPS.
  • Fail-safe: defaults to 1:1 pass-through if active-steering system fails.
