Steering, Wheels & Tyres

Steering Geometry — Wheelbase, Track Width, Ackerman Principle

Source images: IMG20250514111458, IMG20250514111507, IMG20250514111514, IMG20250514111525

Overview

The four front-wheel-geometry fundamentals: wheelbase (distance front axle to rear axle), track width (distance between left and right wheels on the same axle), and the Ackerman principle (the geometric requirement that the inner and outer front wheels must turn through different angles during a corner so all four wheels roll without scrubbing). The notebook walks through why simple parallel-arm steering produces "wrong" cornering paths, what the "true" path looks like, and how a trapezoidal steering linkage (steering arms angled inward toward the rear axle) implements the Ackerman principle in real hardware.

Working Principle

Wheelbase and track width — definitions

Notebook: "Wheelbase: Distance b/w centre of [front] axle & centre of rear axle. OR distance b/w contact points of wheel with ground which are on same side of vehicle.

Track width: Distance b/w [two] contact points of wheels on same axle."

Measurement Definition
Wheelbase Longitudinal distance — front contact patch to rear contact patch, same side. Or front axle centreline to rear axle centreline.
Track width Lateral distance — left wheel contact patch to right wheel contact patch, same axle.

Both numbers shape vehicle dynamics: - Longer wheelbase → straighter-line stability, smoother ride, larger turning circle. - Shorter wheelbase → tighter turning, more nimble, more susceptible to pitching. - Wider track → less body roll, better cornering grip, less prone to rollover. - Narrower track → easier to fit in narrow lanes, more agile feel.

The Ackerman problem — why parallel steering is wrong

Notebook (IMG 111507): "The rotation axes of four wheels intersect at connecting centre point during cornering. So angle should get reduced [for inner wheel vs outer]."

When a car turns, all four wheels trace circular arcs around a common centre point (the turn centre). The inner wheel traces a smaller-radius arc than the outer wheel. For both front wheels to roll cleanly without scrubbing, each must point along its own arc's tangent — meaning the inner wheel must turn through a larger angle than the outer wheel.

If the two front wheels stay parallel (point in the same direction) during a turn, one or both will scrub sideways — tyre wear, lost grip, and a horrible squeal.

Wrong vs True cornering paths

The "wrong" sketch shows what happens with bad Ackerman — the car follows a sweeping outside line. The "true" sketch shows correct Ackerman — the rear inside wheel cuts a tighter arc that matches the inner front wheel's path. Ackerman geometry — the trapezoidal solution

Notebook (IMG 111525): "★ Trapezoidal geometry. Ackerman. STATEMENT — The steering Ackerman should be in such a way that: ① The inclined link of [the] steering arm should be designed in such a way that they should form a triangle whose sides are parallel to lines shown in dia.

Ackerman principle — Extended arms run parallel to the line of front wheel contact points to the centre of the rear axle. So car can negotiate the bend."

The Ackerman geometry is implemented by angling the steering arms inward so an imaginary line extended from each kingpin axis through the corresponding steering-arm-to-tie-rod ball joint, passes through the centre point of the rear axle.

In construction terms: - Front wheels at the corners of a steering trapezoid. - Steering arms (the protrusions from each front knuckle) angled inward toward the centre of the rear axle. - Tie rod between the two arm ends. - When the rack moves the tie rod sideways, the two steering arms swing through different angles because of their angled mounting → inner wheel turns more than outer wheel automatically.

Why the rear-axle-centre target

For pure rolling, the turn-centre must lie on the extended line of the rear axle (because rear wheels don't steer — they always roll along their own axis). Front wheels must point at the same turn centre. The Ackerman geometry ensures the kingpin-to-tie-rod arms naturally direct each front wheel toward this rear-axle-line target.

Components

Track width Left-to-right wheel distance (lateral, same axle). Sets cornering vs roll.
Steering arm Protrusion from each front knuckle. Angled inward for Ackerman.
Steering trapezoid The 4-sided shape formed by front axle + tie rod + two steering arms.
Tie rod Connecting bar between the two steering arms (or rack ends + arms).

Kingpin axis Each front wheel's steering pivot axis.

Inner front wheel Turns through a LARGER angle than the outer (because smaller arc radius).
Outer front wheel Turns through a SMALLER angle than the inner.

Detailed Component Notes

How "trapezoidal" geometry self-corrects

Picture the steering arms as the two non-parallel sides of a trapezoid, with the front axle as the top and the tie rod as the bottom. When the tie rod moves sideways, the two steering arms — being angled inward — pivot through different swept angles. The geometry is tuned so the inner wheel (which has more angular leverage on its arm) turns more than the outer wheel in the right proportion to match the Ackerman requirement.

Exact 100% Ackerman compliance only happens at one specific steering angle — but the trapezoid is designed so that across the whole useful steering range, the angles are very close to correct. The small residual error is acceptable (front tyres can scrub a few % during hard cornering without trouble).

Pro-Ackerman vs Anti-Ackerman

Not in the notebook but worth knowing:

  • Pro-Ackerman (standard) — inner wheel turns more than outer. Optimised for low-speed corners (parking, slow turns) where tyres can't generate enough slip angle to compensate for parallel-steering scrub. Standard on all road cars.
  • Anti-Ackerman (race cars) — outer wheel turns more than inner. Used in high-speed open-wheel racing where the loaded outer tyre needs more steering angle to generate slip angle and the inside tyre is unloaded anyway.

Implication for tyre wear

Notebook implication: car can "negotiate the bend" cleanly.

Bad Ackerman → permanent tyre scrubbing on every turn → uneven front-tyre wear (especially toe shoulders) → shorter tyre life. Good Ackerman = front tyres wear evenly.

Key Facts

  • Wheelbase = distance between front and rear axles (longitudinal).
  • Track width = distance between left and right wheels on the same axle (lateral).
  • Ackerman principle: the inner front wheel must turn through a larger angle than the outer during a corner, so all four wheels roll without scrubbing.
  • The turn centre must lie on the extended rear-axle line (because rear wheels can't steer).
  • Trapezoidal steering geometry implements Ackerman: steering arms angled inward toward the rear axle centre.
  • Without Ackerman → tyre scrubbing → uneven wear + lost cornering grip.
  • 100% Ackerman compliance happens at one specific angle; trapezoid tuned for the practical range.
  • Pro-Ackerman = road cars (low-speed scrubbing prevention); anti-Ackerman = racecars (high-speed slip-angle tuning).
