Latent Heat & The Pressure-Boiling Relationship

Source images: IMG20250514181206, IMG20250514181214

Overview

Two pages that set up the two thermodynamic tricks that make a refrigeration cycle work. First, latent heat — when a substance changes phase (solid → liquid, or liquid → gas) it absorbs (or releases) a large amount of heat without changing temperature. The page demonstrates this with an ice-cube experiment: a glass of hot water cools dramatically, but the ice bath surrounding it doesn't heat up — because the heat is being consumed by the phase change (ice → water), not by warming the liquid. Second, the pressure-boiling relationship — the temperature at which a liquid boils depends on the pressure above it. Raising the pressure raises the boiling point; lowering the pressure lowers it. By controlling pressure with a compressor and expansion valve, an AC system can force the refrigerant to evaporate at very low temperatures inside the evaporator (absorbing cabin heat) and to condense at high temperatures inside the condenser (rejecting heat to the outside air).

Observations

① Water in glass gets colder. ② Water in bowl didn't get hotter. — IMG 181206.

The asymmetry is the point. The hot glass is losing heat (cooling down), and the heat has to be going somewhere — but the cold bowl's temperature is not rising. Where did the heat go?

Explanation — ice cubes need heat energy to melt

"Ice cubes need heat energy to melt." — IMG 181206.

The heat that left the hot glass was absorbed by the phase change of ice → water, not by warming the resulting water. Specifically:

  • Melting 1 g of ice at 0 °C consumes ~334 J (the latent heat of fusion of water).
  • That same 334 J would otherwise have warmed 1 g of water from 0 °C to +80 °C (using specific heat ≈ 4.18 J/g·°C).

So a single gram of ice melting absorbs as much heat as it would take to warm an equal mass of water across the entire range 0 → 80 °C — without the ice's own temperature changing at all (it stays at 0 °C until all of it has melted).

Absorption of heat energy — the two state-change rules

The page underlines two cases where heat is absorbed without temperature change:

   ★ Solid → Liquid       (melting / fusion)
                          Absorbs the "latent heat of fusion"
                          e.g., ice at 0 °C absorbing heat to become water at 0 °C

   ★ Liquid → Gas         (evaporation / vaporisation)
                          Absorbs the "latent heat of vaporisation"
                          e.g., water at 100 °C absorbing heat to become steam at 100 °C

                          Refrigerant equivalent: R134a evaporating at ~5 °C
                          in the evaporator, absorbing cabin heat without
                          warming itself.

The AC system exploits the second rule. Inside the evaporator, the refrigerant boils (changes from liquid to gas) at a temperature well below cabin temperature. The latent heat of that phase change is supplied by the cabin air flowing across the evaporator fins — which is exactly how the cabin air gets cold.

Pressure controls the boiling point

The third page (IMG 181214) introduces the second key thermodynamic relationship:

   Pressure    Boiling point of water
   ─────────   ─────────────────────────
   1 bar       100 °C   ← standard atmospheric pressure
   2 bar       above 100 °C   ← higher pressure → higher boiling point
   < 1 bar     below 100 °C   ← lower pressure → lower boiling point

The page's exact words: "If pressure on water is 2 bar, water will start boiling at temp above [100 °C]. If pressure on water is less than 1 bar, water start boiling at temp below 100 °C."

This relationship is not unique to water — every liquid has a pressure-vs-boiling-point curve, and the AC system uses that curve for its working fluid (the refrigerant R134a or R1234yf) to engineer two very specific boiling/condensing temperatures:

  • Low pressure side (after the expansion valve, inside the evaporator): ~1.5–3 bar → refrigerant boils at ~0–5 °C → cold enough to chill cabin air.
  • High pressure side (after the compressor, inside the condenser): ~12–20 bar → refrigerant condenses at ~50–70 °C → hot enough to reject heat to outside ambient air (typically 30–45 °C).

The compressor and expansion valve are essentially the two pressure-control devices that switch the refrigerant between those two pressure regimes.

Summary — utilisation of evaporation in air conditioning

"Summary: Utilization of evaporation in air conditioning system." — IMG 181214.

The whole AC cycle in one sentence: deliberately force the refrigerant to evaporate at a low pressure (and therefore low temperature) inside the cabin's evaporator, so that the latent heat of evaporation comes from the cabin air — cooling it.

Components

Component Function
Refrigerant (R134a / R1234yf) Working fluid; chosen for its boiling temperature being just-below-cabin at AC-system low pressure
Evaporator Low-pressure heat exchanger where refrigerant evaporates, absorbing cabin heat
Condenser High-pressure heat exchanger where refrigerant condenses, rejecting heat to outside air
Compressor Raises pressure on the gas side, lifting the boiling point above ambient
Expansion valve (TXV / thermostatic expansion valve) Drops pressure on the liquid side, lowering the boiling point below cabin temperature

Key Facts

  • Latent heat is the heat absorbed / released during a phase change at constant temperature.
  • Melting 1 g of ice absorbs ~334 J — the same energy that would warm 1 g of water from 0 °C to ~80 °C.
  • An AC system exploits the liquid → gas latent heat (evaporation) inside the evaporator to absorb cabin heat.
  • Boiling point depends on pressure: higher P → higher boiling point; lower P → lower boiling point.
  • AC uses this relationship to make the refrigerant boil at ~0–5 °C inside the evaporator (low pressure 1.5–3 bar) and condense at ~50–70 °C in the condenser (high pressure 12–20 bar).
  • The compressor and expansion valve are the pressure-control devices that switch the refrigerant between the two regimes.
