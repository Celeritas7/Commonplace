AC System

Temperature Control & The Reheat Process

Source images: IMG20250514181252, IMG20250514181255

Overview

Two pages that answer a question file 03 left open: if the evaporator always chills incoming air to ~3 °C, how does the cabin ever come out at a comfortable 22 °C? The answer is the reheat process — after the evaporator has cooled (and dehumidified) the air, a fraction of that cold air is routed through the heater core and mixed back with the rest of the cold air to hit the cabin setpoint. The page draws two competing implementations: water-side control (modulates how much hot coolant flows through the heater core via valves) and air-side control (a blend door, called a mixing flap, splits the airflow between a cold path and a heater path). The second page covers the pollen / cabin air filter and recirculating mode, then revisits the full refrigerant cycle with annotations on each component (compressor as "heart", condenser as radiator-equivalent, receiver-drier's silica gel role).

Working Principle

The two principles of AC

"Principles of AC: — Air temp control — Clean air intake." — IMG 181252.

The page reframes the AC system as having two jobs:

  1. Air temperature control — the cooling and reheating mechanics that hit the cabin setpoint.
  2. Clean air intake — the filtering and recirculation strategy that keeps pollutants and particulates out of the cabin.

The rest of this file's content is structured around these two principles.

The reheat process

"★ Reheat process: — routed through heat exchanger — water side temp control — air side temp control." — IMG 181252.

The naïve AC design would just modulate the evaporator temperature between cold (for hot days) and warm (for cool days) to hit cabin setpoint. But the evaporator does two jobs at once — cool the air and dehumidify it (cabin moisture condenses on the cold fins). If you let the evaporator warm up to (say) 15 °C, you lose the dehumidification — windows mist up, cabin feels muggy.

Reheat keeps the evaporator chilling everything to ~3 °C (full dehumidification, every time), and then adds a controlled amount of heat back to the airflow to hit the cabin temperature setpoint. The result: every cabin temperature setting gets the same crisp dehumidification, regardless of how warm or cool the actual cabin air ends up.

How it works: The cabin blower pushes air across the evaporator (chilled), then through the heater core (which is always in the airflow). The heater control valve modulates how much hot engine coolant flows through the heater core. Less coolant flow → less heat delivered → cabin air stays cooler. More flow → more heat → cabin air ends up warmer.

Downsides: Slow response (the heater core's thermal mass takes seconds to minutes to warm up or cool down). Valve seizes are a common failure mode. Coolant leaks inside the dashboard are extremely expensive to fix.

This was the standard design until the mid-1990s and is still used on some lower-cost vehicles.

How it works: The heater core has hot engine coolant flowing through it continuously (no control valve). All cold air from the evaporator hits a mixing flap that splits the flow into two paths:

  • Bypass path: air goes around the heater core. Stays cold.
  • Heater path: air goes through the heater core. Comes out hot.

The two streams recombine downstream of the heater. The flap's position controls the split:

  • Full cold setting: flap blocks the heater path entirely → all air bypasses → cabin gets fully chilled, dehumidified evaporator air.
  • Full hot setting: flap blocks the bypass entirely → all air goes through the heater → cabin gets fully heated air.
  • Intermediate: flap splits the airflow proportionally → output is the blend of cold + hot → any setpoint in between.

Why this is better than water-side control:

Attribute Water-side Air-side (blend door)
Response time Seconds to minutes (heater core's thermal mass) Instantaneous (the flap moves; no thermal lag)
Dehumidification at warm settings Lost (evaporator goes warm) Preserved (evaporator stays cold)
Failure modes Valve seizes, coolant leak in dashboard Flap motor fails (cabin stuck at one temp; cheaper repair)
Complexity Simpler mechanically Needs a precision-positioned flap and motor

Every modern BMW (and almost every car since 2000) uses air-side / blend-door control.

Filtering — the cabin air filter

"Filtering: keeps pollutants concentration less." — IMG 181255.

The cabin air inlet has a pollen / cabin air filter — a pleated paper or fabric element that removes particulates, pollen, and (with activated-carbon versions) odours from inflowing air. It sits in the HVAC airflow path between the inlet duct and the blower.

Standard service life: ~15,000–25,000 km in Indian conditions (less in dusty / polluted environments). Replaced as part of routine service.

Recirculating mode

"Recirculating mode: when there is high refrigerant demand." — IMG 181255.

In fresh-air mode, the HVAC pulls outside air in through the cabin filter, cools it via the evaporator, and exhausts it through the cabin vents. On a 45 °C day, that's a continuous stream of 45 °C air arriving at the evaporator — a large heat load.

In recirculating mode, a flap blocks the outside-air inlet and instead pulls air from inside the cabin back into the HVAC. Once the cabin has cooled to (say) 25 °C, the evaporator is now chilling 25 °C air instead of 45 °C air — much less work for the compressor.

Use cases: - Hot day, after initial cool-down: switching to recirc reduces the compressor load and improves fuel economy. - Driving through a polluted area (tunnel, behind a smoking truck): recirc keeps outside air out of the cabin.

The system automatically returns to fresh-air mode after some minutes to prevent CO₂ buildup from the occupants' breath (a real concern on long trips — recirc-only cabins can hit 2,000–3,000 ppm CO₂, which causes drowsiness).

Silicon gel in the receiver-drier deserves a callout: it's a desiccant identical to the small packets in shoe boxes and electronics packaging. Its job inside the drier is to chemically absorb any trace water that gets into the refrigerant during assembly or service. The page warns specifically why water is dangerous:

  1. Icing inside the evaporator — water freezes at the expansion valve outlet, blocking refrigerant flow.
  2. Damage to pipes — water + refrigerant → hydrofluoric acid traces → corrosion of aluminium tubes from the inside.

Both failure modes are catastrophic for the AC system. The silica-gel drier prevents them.

Components

Component Function
Heater core Small radiator carrying engine coolant; warms cabin air
Heater control valve (water-side systems only) Modulates coolant flow through the heater core; older design
Temperature mixing flap (blend door) Splits airflow between heater-bypass and heater-path; modern temp-control mechanism
Cabin air filter (pollen filter) Removes particulates and pollen from inflowing air; service item
Recirculation flap Switches air inlet between outside air and cabin air
Compressor The "heart" — pumps refrigerant through the loop
Condenser Radiator-shape; rejects heat to outside air; gas → liquid
Receiver & drier (with silica gel) Filters dirt and absorbs moisture from refrigerant

Key Facts

  • AC has two principles: air temp control and clean air intake.
  • The reheat process keeps the evaporator at full cold (full dehumidification) and adds heat back to hit the cabin setpoint.
  • Water-side control: modulates coolant flow to the heater core. Older, slower, leak-prone.
  • Air-side / blend-door control: a mixing flap splits airflow between cold and heated paths. Modern, instant response, robust.
  • A pollen filter at the air inlet removes particulates and pollen; service item.
  • Recirculating mode: blocks outside-air inlet, recirculates cabin air. Used for fast initial cool-down on hot days and for polluted-air avoidance.
  • The compressor is the "heart" of the AC system.
  • The condenser is similar to a radiator and is mounted behind the engine radiator at the front of the car.
  • The receiver-drier contains silica gel to absorb moisture from the refrigerant — moisture causes evaporator icing and pipe corrosion.
