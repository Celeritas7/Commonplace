AC System

Compressor, Auxiliary Fan & Pressure Sensor

Source images: IMG20250514181240, IMG20250514181243

Overview

Two pages that detail the hardware components of the AC system's high-pressure side. The first page focuses on the compressor — specifically the 7-piston swash-plate type that BMW uses, and the blocking protection that replaces a traditional magnetic clutch with a moulded rubber element. The same page also introduces the auxiliary fan (also called the condenser cooling fan): its task is to push outside air across the condenser when the car isn't moving fast enough to do so naturally, and its speed is regulated by three independent inputs (coolant temperature, transmission oil temperature, and AC high-side pressure). The second page covers the pressure sensor that measures the high-pressure-side reading and is the primary input driving auxiliary-fan speed, and gives a cross-section of the thermostatic expansion valve (TXV) showing its sensing bulb, diaphragm, and metering orifice.

Working Principle

Swash-plate compressor — 7 reciprocating pistons

The compressor is described on the page as: "7 reciprocating piston swash plate compressor."

A swash-plate compressor has 7 pistons arranged in a ring around a central shaft. The shaft carries an angled disk (the swash plate); as the shaft rotates, each piston is pushed back and forth axially because the swash plate's surface is at an angle to the shaft. Seven pistons spaced 360°/7 = ~51° apart means there are always several pistons in different parts of their stroke, giving the compressor a near-continuous output flow with low pulsation.

                  │   Crankshaft (from belt) │
                  │           │              │
                  │           ▼              │
                  │     ┌────────┐           │
                  │     │ Swash  │  ← angled │
                  │     │  plate │    disk   │
                  │     └────────┘           │
                  │      ╱  │  ╲             │
                  │    ╱    │    ╲           │

                  │  └───┘ └─┘  └───┘        │    arranged in a ring

Extent (output) control — alteration of swash-plate position

"Extent: works on alteration of swash plate position." — IMG 181240.

This is the page's introduction to the variable-displacement mechanism mentioned in file 03. The swash plate's angle is not fixed — a control mechanism inside the compressor can tilt the plate to a steeper or shallower angle. Steeper angle = longer piston stroke = more refrigerant pumped per revolution = more cooling capacity. Shallower angle = shorter stroke = less capacity, less load on the engine.

This is the variable-displacement compressor that file 03's margin annotation introduced. The same compressor body can pump anywhere from ~5 % to 100 % of its rated displacement without ever cycling on/off via the magnetic clutch — which is exactly the "compressor working controlled" fuel-economy benefit the previous file mentioned.

Blocking protection — no magnetic clutch

"Blocking protection: No magnetic clutch. Blocking protection by means of moulded rubber element." — IMG 181240.

A traditional fixed-displacement compressor must use a magnetic clutch on its drive pulley so it can be disengaged when cooling isn't needed (and so the compressor can be detached if it seizes). A variable-displacement compressor doesn't need that — it can just be modulated down to ~zero displacement. So BMW removes the clutch entirely and direct-drives the compressor from the engine belt.

But what happens if the compressor's internal mechanism seizes (refrigerant slugging, oil starvation, bearing failure)? Without a clutch, the seized compressor would either snap the belt or stall the engine. The solution is a moulded rubber element in the drive coupling — a sacrificial part designed to shear or stretch when the compressor draws too much torque, decoupling the engine from the seized unit before damage propagates.

   Engine belt pulley ─── rubber coupling ─── compressor input shaft
                          ↑
                          │
                  Shears under torque overload
                  → compressor isolated
                  → engine keeps running
                  → belt stays intact

Auxiliary fan — the condenser cooling fan

The condenser (high-pressure side, in front of the engine radiator) needs forced airflow across its fins to reject heat. When the car is moving, ram air from the grille is sufficient. When the car is stationary or moving slowly (traffic jam, drive-through, idle at lights), no ram air → the condenser overheats → high-side pressure climbs → AC efficiency drops.

The auxiliary fan sits behind the condenser and pushes air through it whenever ram air is inadequate.

Attribute Detail
Task Dissipation of heat to outside air
Function Ensures sufficient flow of air across the condenser

Auxiliary fan speed — three regulating inputs

"Regulation of fan speed: — coolant temperature — also gear oil / transmission oil temperature — high pressure in [AC] circuit." — IMG 181240.

The auxiliary fan is shared between three different cooling needs:

  1. Coolant temperature: when engine coolant approaches its thermostat-open temperature, the fan helps the main radiator reject heat.
  2. Transmission oil temperature: many BMWs route transmission oil through a small heat-exchanger in or near the radiator. When the trans-oil temperature climbs, the fan helps cool it too.
  3. AC high-side pressure: when refrigerant pressure climbs above the design range (sign that the condenser isn't rejecting enough heat), the fan speeds up to push more air through the condenser.

Whichever of the three demands is highest at any moment commands the fan speed.

Pressure sensor — auxiliary fan controller

"Pressure sensor: Controls auxiliary fan. Placed before receiver & dryer. ★ Measures pressure in high pressure section." — IMG 181243.

The pressure sensor lives on the high-pressure side of the AC circuit, between the condenser outlet and the receiver/drier inlet. Its reading is:

  • The dominant input to the auxiliary fan speed controller (input #3 in the list above).
  • A protective input that can disengage the compressor if pressure climbs dangerously high (refrigerant overcharge, blocked condenser, failed auxiliary fan, etc.).

A margin annotation reads: "Damage to receiver & dryer is cheaper than condenser amount [replacement cost]." The sensor is placed before the drier (rather than directly on the condenser) so that if there's an overpressure event, the drier acts as a sacrificial buffer — the drier is a much cheaper part to replace than the condenser, which is plumbed into the front of the engine bay behind the radiator and is labour-intensive to replace.

Thermostatic expansion valve (TXV) — cross-section

The page sketches the TXV's internal mechanism:

                ┌──────────────────────┐
                │   Sensing bulb       │  ← clamped to evaporator
                │   (refrigerant-      │     outlet pipe; reads
                │    charged           │     evaporator outlet temp
                │    capillary)        │
                └─────────┬────────────┘
                          │
                          ▼ pressure
                  ┌─────────────────┐
                  │   Diaphragm     │
                  │   ┌─────────┐   │
                  │   │  ───────│───│──── push rod
                  │   └─────────┘   │
                  └────────┬────────┘
                           ▼
                   ┌──────────────┐
                   │  Valve seat  │   ← opens/closes metering
                   │   ↓ pin ↓    │     orifice based on
                   │              │     diaphragm position
                   └──┬───────┬───┘
                      │       │
        liquid in ────┘       └──── liquid + vapour out
        (high-P, warm)              (low-P, cold,
                                     to evaporator)

Working principle:

  1. The sensing bulb is clamped to the evaporator outlet pipe and is partially filled with the same refrigerant as the system.
  2. When the evaporator outlet is warm, the refrigerant inside the bulb evaporates → pressure in the bulb's capillary line rises → pushes the diaphragm down → opens the valve wider → more refrigerant flows into the evaporator → cools it more.
  3. When the evaporator outlet is cold, refrigerant in the bulb condenses → bulb pressure drops → diaphragm rises → closes the valve → less refrigerant flows → evaporator warms back up.

The TXV is a self-regulating mechanical controller — no electrical signals, no ECU involvement. It maintains a roughly constant superheat at the evaporator outlet (typically 5–8 °C above the saturation temperature), which is the optimum operating point for both cooling capacity and compressor protection (preventing liquid slugging).

Components

Component Function
7-piston swash-plate compressor Pumps refrigerant from low to high pressure; near-continuous flow with low pulsation
Swash-plate angle controller Varies effective piston stroke → varies compressor displacement
Moulded rubber drive coupling Sacrificial element that shears under torque overload; isolates a seized compressor without damaging belt/engine
Auxiliary fan Forces outside air across the condenser when ram air is insufficient
Pressure sensor Reads high-side pressure between condenser and drier; commands fan speed and compressor cut-off
Receiver & drier Filters and dries refrigerant; sacrificial buffer for overpressure events
Thermostatic expansion valve (TXV) Self-regulating mechanical metering valve; uses a sensing bulb on the evaporator outlet to adjust refrigerant flow
TXV sensing bulb Refrigerant-filled bulb clamped to evaporator outlet; drives the TXV diaphragm via thermal-pressure feedback

Key Facts

  • The compressor is a 7-piston swash-plate type — multiple pistons phased around a central angled disk produce low-pulsation flow.
  • The swash plate's angle is variable — this is the variable-displacement mechanism that file 03 introduced.
  • BMW's variable compressors do not have a magnetic clutch — they direct-drive from the belt.
  • A moulded rubber coupling acts as a sacrificial shear element if the compressor seizes.
  • The auxiliary fan speed is regulated by three independent demands: engine coolant temp, transmission oil temp, and AC high-side pressure — whichever is highest wins.
  • The pressure sensor is placed before the receiver/drier so the drier acts as a sacrificial buffer (cheaper than condenser replacement).
  • The TXV is a self-regulating mechanical valve — sensing bulb pressure pushes a diaphragm that opens/closes the metering orifice based on evaporator outlet temperature.
