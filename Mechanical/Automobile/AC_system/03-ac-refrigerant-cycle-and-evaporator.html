AC Refrigerant Cycle & Evaporator

Source images: IMG20250514181223, IMG20250514181230

Overview

The first page draws the complete refrigerant cycle — the closed loop of four components (compressor, condenser, fluid reservoir & drier, expansion valve, evaporator) that the refrigerant flows through indefinitely while the AC is on, with the pressure and temperature annotated at every leg of the loop so the student can trace the phase changes. The second page details the evaporator's four components and its three control mechanisms: a temperature sensor that shuts off the compressor below 3 °C to prevent icing, a variable-displacement compressor that modulates capacity instead of cycling on/off, and the varying evaporator temperature strategy that improves both fuel economy and dehumidification quality.

Pressure and temperature at each stage

Stage Pressure Temperature Refrigerant state
Compressor inlet ~1.5–3 bar low (just above evaporator outlet temp) Low-pressure gas
Compressor outlet → Condenser inlet 12–20 bar 50–70 °C High-pressure superheated gas
Condenser outlet 12–20 bar 50–70 °C High-pressure liquid (after condensation)
Fluid reservoir / drier 12–20 bar 50–70 °C High-pressure liquid (filtered & dried)
Expansion valve inlet 12–20 bar warm liquid High-pressure liquid
Expansion valve outlet → Evaporator inlet 1.5–3 bar drops sharply Liquid + vapour mix (cold)
Evaporator outlet → Compressor inlet 1.5–3 bar ≈ 0–5 °C (slightly superheated) Low-pressure gas

Role of each component

  • Compressor: takes low-pressure refrigerant gas, raises its pressure to 12–20 bar. The compression heats it (gas-law work-into-heat conversion). Driven by an engine-belt-driven magnetic clutch — engaged only when the AC is requested.
  • Condenser: a radiator-shaped finned coil mounted in front of the engine radiator. Hot high-pressure gas flows through it, ambient air flows across it, and the gas condenses to a liquid at constant pressure. The heat of condensation is rejected to the outside air.
  • Fluid reservoir & drier: a small can downstream of the condenser. Holds excess liquid refrigerant as a buffer, removes any moisture (with a desiccant) and traps dirt particles. Moisture in the system is catastrophic — it freezes in the expansion valve, blocking refrigerant flow.
  • Expansion valve (TXV): a precision metering orifice. Drops the refrigerant pressure abruptly from 12–20 bar down to 1.5–3 bar. The pressure drop causes a portion of the liquid to flash-evaporate, and the boiling point of the remaining liquid drops to ~0–5 °C (per file 02-latent-heat-and-pressure-boiling-relationship).
  • Evaporator: where the cooling actually happens. Cold liquid + vapour mix enters; cabin air blown across the fins gives up its heat to the refrigerant as the refrigerant evaporates. Cabin air leaves cold; refrigerant leaves as low-pressure gas, returning to the compressor.

Evaporator — 4 components and the icing failure mode

"① Evaporator: Absorbs heat energy." — IMG 181223.

The evaporator subsystem has four components beyond the refrigerant-side hardware:

# Component Function
① Evaporator (heat exchanger itself) Where refrigerant boils, absorbing cabin air heat
② Positively-activated fan (cabin blower) Forces cabin air across the evaporator fins
③ Condensate drain Channels water dripping off the evaporator (from cabin-air humidity) out under the car
④ Temperature sensor Measures air temperature at evaporator outlet; prevents icing

Icing failure mode

"Ice formation on evaporator is because of 'Expansion valve' is faulty. If flow of air is less & flow of refrigerator is high because of faulty expansion valve, evaporator becomes too much cold." — IMG 181223.

The failure chain:

   Faulty expansion valve (stuck open)
         │
         ▼
   Too much refrigerant pushes into the evaporator
         │
         ▼
   Evaporator gets colder than 0 °C
         │
         ▼
   Cabin air's water vapour FREEZES on the evaporator fins instead
   of dripping off as condensate
         │
         ▼
   Ice blocks airflow → blower can't pass air across the coil
         │
         ▼
   AC output drops to almost nothing while compressor still runs
   (eventually liquid refrigerant slugs back into the compressor —
   the next page warns about this)

The temperature sensor in the evaporator outlet exists specifically to prevent this. When it detects air temperature below 3 °C, it commands the compressor's magnetic clutch to disengage, halting refrigerant flow until the evaporator warms back up.

The temperature-sensor / compressor-shutoff loop

The second page (IMG 181230) opens mid-sentence (continuing from the previous topic):

"(...) the temp sensor [in the] filler bulb senses the temp & it can 'shut off the compressor.' Also the blower speed is increased to pass more & more air through evaporator. If this will not happen, liquid refrigerator enters inside compressor." — IMG 181230.

So the system has two protective actions when the evaporator gets too cold:

  1. Shut off the compressor (temperature sensor commands the magnetic clutch to disengage).
  2. Increase blower speed to push more warm cabin air across the evaporator, warming it back up.

The reason both matter is in the third sentence: if neither happens, liquid refrigerant reaches the compressor inlet. The compressor is designed to compress gas — liquid is incompressible, so liquid arriving at the compressor's inlet can hydraulically shock the pistons and destroy the unit. This is called slugging in HVAC parlance.

Temperature sensor — what and where

"Temp sensor — Prevents icing. Measures air temp. At air temp below 3 °C & operate compressor by magnetic clutches." — IMG 181230.

Specifically: - Measures: air temperature at the evaporator outlet (not refrigerant temperature). - Threshold: 3 °C — slightly above freezing, gives a safety margin. - Action: switches the magnetic clutch on the compressor pulley. The clutch couples the engine belt to the compressor shaft only when energised; when de-energised, the belt spins the pulley but the compressor stays stationary.

The variable-displacement compressor — modern alternative

A margin annotation reads: "The variable [displacement] compressor is used."

A variable-displacement compressor doesn't need to cycle on and off via the magnetic clutch. Instead, its internal swashplate angle changes continuously, varying the compressor's pumping volume from near-zero to maximum without disengaging from the belt. Benefits:

  • No clutch-engagement jolt felt through the engine.
  • No on/off cabin temperature swings.
  • Smoother engine load — the alternator and fuel system don't have to react to the AC compressor stepping in and out.

Varying evaporator temperature — the modern control strategy

"Varying evaporator temp: — low outside temp, higher evaporator temp. — high outside temp, lower evaporator temp." — IMG 181230.

A simple fixed-evaporator-temperature design always tries to chill the evaporator to ~3 °C regardless of outside conditions. A varying-evaporator-temp design adjusts the target up or down depending on what the cabin actually needs:

  • Cool day (outside 20 °C): evaporator target raised to ~8–10 °C. The cabin still gets dehumidified air, but the compressor works much less.
  • Hot day (outside 40 °C): evaporator target dropped to ~3 °C. Full cooling capacity available.

Two advantages

"Adv: ① Fuel economy (compressor working controlled). ② Avoidance of excessive dehumidification." — IMG 181230.

  1. Fuel economy: the compressor is one of the engine's bigger parasitic loads (up to 3–5 kW at full output). Running it at half capacity on a cool day saves a measurable amount of fuel.
  2. Avoidance of excessive dehumidification: a fixed-3 °C evaporator dehumidifies the cabin aggressively — sometimes too much, leaving the cabin air feeling dry and irritating to the eyes / sinuses. The variable strategy keeps humidity at a more comfortable level on mild days.

Key Facts

  • The refrigerant cycle has 4 active components plus a fluid reservoir & drier: compressor → condenser → reservoir/drier → expansion valve → evaporator → back to compressor.
  • High-pressure side: 12–20 bar, 50–70 °C — between compressor outlet and expansion valve.
  • Low-pressure side: 1.5–3 bar, near 0 °C — between expansion valve and compressor inlet.
  • The fluid reservoir & drier removes moisture (which would freeze in the expansion valve) and dirt particles.
  • Evaporator icing is caused by a faulty expansion valve flooding the evaporator with too much refrigerant.
  • The temperature sensor at the evaporator outlet prevents icing by shutting off the compressor below 3 °C (and increasing blower speed).
  • Without that protective shut-off, liquid refrigerant slugs the compressor and destroys it.
  • The magnetic clutch on the compressor pulley is what physically engages/disengages compressor operation.
  • A variable-displacement compressor is the modern alternative: it modulates pumping volume continuously instead of cycling on/off.
  • Varying evaporator temperature strategy improves fuel economy and prevents excessive dehumidification.

Open Questions / Uncertainty

  • [?] "Filler bulb" in "the temp sensor in [the] filler bulb senses the temp" (IMG 181230) is an unusual phrasing. The standard term is sensing bulb — the small refrigerant-filled bulb clamped to the evaporator outlet line, used in older mechanically-modulated TXV systems. Modern AC systems use an electrical NTC thermistor at the evaporator outlet instead. The trainer may have been describing the older mechanical TXV setup, the modern electrical setup, or conflating the two. Preserved as "temperature sensor" generically in the body text.
  • [?] "Operates compressor by magnetic clutches" — plural "clutches" is the source wording. There is only one magnetic clutch on a normal AC compressor. The plural may be a transcription slip or a Hinglish convention. Treated as singular in interpretation.
  • [?] The exact pressures at each stage (15 bar, 12-20 bar, 1.5-3 bar, etc.) are 2013-era R134a system figures. Modern R1234yf systems run slightly different pressures (typically 10-15 % lower on the high side), but the overall layout is identical.
  • [?] Compressor power draw of "3-5 kW" in the Variable Compressor section is my engineering context, not on the source page. Real automotive AC compressors at full load are typically 2-4 kW for passenger cars, higher for SUVs. The order of magnitude is correct.
