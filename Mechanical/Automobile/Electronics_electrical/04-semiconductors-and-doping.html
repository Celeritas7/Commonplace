Semiconductors — Basics, N-type & P-type Doping

Overview

A semiconductor is a material whose conductivity sits between insulator and conductor — and crucially, can be switched between insulating and conducting states by light, heat, electric field, or chemistry. Doping (adding controlled impurities) makes a semiconductor either N-type (excess electrons → carries current via electrons) or P-type (excess "holes" → carries current via missing electrons moving the other way). Combining the two creates a PN junction = diode — the foundation of every transistor, every ECU, every electronic ignition coil. This page covers the manufacturing basics and the two doping types.

Working Principle

What a semiconductor is

Notebook: "Semiconductor: Insulator is converted into conductor: energy of light releases charge carriers → current flow in outer circuit."

In pure form, silicon (Si) and germanium (Ge) are insulators — all valence electrons are tied up in covalent bonds. But: - Energy input (light, heat, electric field) can knock electrons free from their bonds → these become charge carriers. - Once free, electrons can flow through an external circuit → current.

This switchable conductivity is the magic property: a semiconductor can be made to conduct or not conduct on demand.

Notebook (use case): "Rain/light sensor" — uses this principle: incident light frees electrons in a semiconductor → measurable current → presence/intensity of light detected.

Manufacture (basic materials)

Notebook: "Basic: Silicon, Germanium, Compounds such as Gallium arsenide; Metal oxides. Process: Silica sand → Reduction → Smelter → Crystal."

Step Process
Raw material Silica sand (SiO₂) — extremely abundant
Reduction Heat sand with carbon → frees Si from oxide → impure silicon
Smelter Repeated zone-refining → 99.99999% pure (7-nines) silicon
Crystal growth Czochralski or float-zone process → single-crystal Si ingot

Other semiconductors: - Germanium (Ge) — used historically; mostly obsolete (high leakage at temperature). - Gallium arsenide (GaAs) — high speed, used in RF + LEDs. - Gallium phosphide (GaP) — used in LEDs. - Metal oxides — used in thermistors (PTC/NTC).

N-type semiconductor — doped with PHOSPHORUS

Notebook (IMG 002303, bottom): "N-type semiconductor: – Phosphorus atom is doped. – So one extra electron is available for conduction of current."

Silicon has 4 valence electrons (covalent-bonds with 4 neighbours). Phosphorus has 5 valence electrons.

When P is substituted for an Si atom in the crystal lattice: - 4 of P's electrons form bonds with neighbours. - The 5th electron has nowhere to go → it's loose → free to conduct.

Each P atom donates one free electron → "N-type" because the charge carriers are negative.

The notebook draws the lattice with phosphorus atom showing one extra dot (electron) that isn't paired.

P-type semiconductor — doped with ALUMINIUM

Notebook (IMG 002308): "P-type semiconductor: – Al atom is doped. – Freely moving hole allows 'current' to flow."

Aluminium has 3 valence electrons.

When Al is substituted for an Si atom: - 3 bonds formed; one bond position is missing an electron → that absence is called a "hole." - An electron from a neighbouring Si bond can hop into the hole → leaves a new hole where it came from. - Effectively, the hole moves through the lattice → carries current (in the opposite direction of electron motion).

Each Al atom creates one free hole → "P-type" because the (apparent) charge carriers are positive.

Doping summary

Notebook (lower IMG 002308): "★ Semiconductor crystal (Si) if doped with another material like Phosphorus (P) then one excess electron is available for current flow. ★ Semiconductor crystal (Si) if doped with another material with one [electron] less, like Aluminium (Al), then one hole [available]."

Detailed Component Notes

"Hole" — physical reality vs convenience

A "hole" is a conceptual charge carrier — it's actually electrons in adjacent atoms hopping into the missing bond position, which appears (from outside) as a positive charge moving the other way. The mathematics is the same as if a real positive particle were moving — easier to analyse the circuit using the hole abstraction.

Why doping levels are so small

Typical doping concentrations are 1 dopant atom per 10⁵–10⁸ silicon atoms. Despite the tiny ratio, this is enough to change conductivity by 8+ orders of magnitude.

Manufacturing — from sand to chip

  1. Mine silica sand (SiO₂).
  2. Reduce to MGS (metallurgical-grade silicon, ~99% pure).
  3. Convert MGS to trichlorosilane → distil → reduce to electronic-grade silicon (EGS, 99.9999999% pure).
  4. Grow into a single-crystal ingot (Czochralski method — silicon "pulled" out of a melt as a single crystal).
  5. Slice into wafers (~0.7 mm thick discs).
  6. Pattern the wafer with photolithography → dope specific regions N- or P-type → build up the device structure.
  7. Cut, package, test.

A modern chip has billions of doped regions on one wafer.

Key Facts

  • Semiconductor = switchable conductor; insulator → conductor on demand.
  • Materials: Si, Ge, GaAs, GaP, metal oxides.
  • Manufacturing: silica sand → reduction → smelter → crystal.
  • N-type = doped with phosphorus (or arsenic) — 5 valence e⁻ → donates extra electron.
  • P-type = doped with aluminium (or boron) — 3 valence e⁻ → creates a hole (missing electron).
  • Conduction in N-type: by free electrons (negative carriers).
  • Conduction in P-type: by holes (positive carriers; really electrons hopping the other way).
  • PN junction (combination of both) = diode → the foundational electronic component.
