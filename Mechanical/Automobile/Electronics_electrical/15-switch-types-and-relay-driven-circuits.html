Overview

A reference page cataloguing the nine kinds of switch a BMW technician will see across the car (push-button, latching, key, pull, pressure, rotary, toggle, pedal, combination) with their schematic symbols and actuating method, followed by two practical circuits that show how those switches appear in real wiring: a wiper circuit with a parking-position cam (so the wiper always stops in the off-screen rest position regardless of when the driver turns the switch off), and a headlight relay that uses a 200 mA control current to switch 10 A of load current.

Wiper circuit with parking cam

"If wiper is on & if we turn it off, it stays [parks] at parking position." — IMG 113332.

The wiper motor must always end its stroke off the windshield even if the driver turns the dash switch off at a random moment. The mechanism that makes this happen is a second, hidden switch mounted on the wiper-arm gearbox shaft — a cam-actuated bypass switch that's closed for the majority of the wiper rotation and only opens when the wiper has reached the parked position.

              ┌──────────────────────────────┐
              │   Dash wiper switch          │
   Battery+ ──┤ (driver controls)            │
              │                              │
              │   ┌──── parked-position ─────┐
              │   │     cam switch           │
              │   │  (on wiper shaft)        │
              │   └──────────────────────────┘
              │                              │
              └──────► Wiper motor M ────────┘
                          │
                          ▼
                       Ground (chassis)

Operation:

  • Dash switch ON: current reaches the motor through the dash switch directly. Wiper sweeps continuously.
  • Dash switch OFF, wiper mid-stroke: the cam switch is still closed (it's only open at the parked position), so current keeps flowing through the bypass path → motor keeps running.
  • Dash switch OFF, wiper reaches parked position: cam opens → current cuts → motor stops at the bottom of the windshield, hidden behind the trim.

The two switches together form an OR gate: motor runs if either path is closed. The cam switch's job is to override the user's "off" command just long enough to complete the current stroke.

Pressure switch detail

A pressure switch is a mechanical pressure transducer with a digital (open/closed) output rather than an analog reading. Inside the housing, fluid pressure pushes against a diaphragm that's spring-loaded against a contact:

                        contact opens when
                        pressure < spring force
                              │
                              ▼
       fluid    │   ░░░░░░░░  ▲
       pressure │   diaphragm │  spring
       →        │   ──────────┤  ↑
                │             │
                                ⨯  contact (closed when
                                    pressure pushes
                                    diaphragm up)

The page describes it simply: "non pressure spring at certain level switch let open" — meaning the spring keeps the contact in one state until pressure exceeds the spring force, at which point the contact switches.

Common automotive uses (drawn from the symbol drawn on the page — the "P" with a contact and a pressure arrow): - Oil pressure warning lamp on the dashboard - Brake-light switch (older mechanical version actuated by hydraulic line pressure) - AC high/low-pressure cutout - Power-steering pressure switch (raises idle when steering loads the pump)

Relay — the 200 mA / 10 A example

The headlight circuit at the bottom of IMG 113335 shows the canonical relay arrangement: a small control current in the dashboard switch operates a large load current in the headlight wiring.

   ┌──── Control side (200 mA) ────┐  ┌─── Load side (10 A) ───┐
   │                               │  │                         │
   │   ┌──── relay coil ────┐     │  │     ┌── relay contacts ──┐
   │   │                    │     │  │     │                    │
   │   ▼                    │     │  │     ▼                    │
   ─┴─┤├──[ 200 mA ]──•───•─┘     │  ├──•─/ ─•──[ 10 A ]── (X) ─┘
    Battery+         switch       │  Battery+   contacts    Headlight bulb
    (control rail)                │  (load rail)            ("More current")
                                  │
                                ground                       ground

The trade-off — separating dashboard wiring from headlight wiring:

If the headlight were wired directly to the dash switch With the relay arrangement
10 A must travel from battery → through the cabin → through the dash switch → back out to the headlight 10 A stays in a short heavy cable in the engine bay
Dash switch contacts must be rated for 10 A → bulky, expensive Dash switch only carries 200 mA → small, cheap
Long thin wire = high I²R loss = headlight dim Short heavy wire = low loss = full brightness
Voltage drop across switch = brightness loss Voltage drop minimised
Switch arcs when opening 10 A inductive load → contact erosion Relay contacts erode but they're cheap to replace

This is why every high-current load on the car — headlights, fuel pump, fan, horn, rear demister, starter motor — has a relay between the user-facing switch and the load. The user-facing switch carries only the relay coil current.

Key Facts

  • Nine switch families appear in a modern BMW; their schematic symbols are standardised.
  • Combination switches (column stalks) bundle indicators, wash/wipe, beam, and cruise into one assembly.
  • Wiper parking is enforced by a cam-actuated bypass switch on the wiper shaft that keeps the motor running past the user's "off" command until the wiper reaches the rest position.
  • Pressure switches are mechanical pressure-sensing devices with digital output — spring vs fluid force determines the contact state.
  • Relays separate control wiring from load wiring: small switch + thin wire on the dashboard side, large contact + thick wire on the load side.
  • A typical automotive relay uses ~200 mA coil current to switch ~10 A load current — a 50× current-handling multiplier.
  • The relay arrangement is mandatory on every high-current load (headlights, fuel pump, fan, horn, starter motor) for both ergonomic (small switches) and electrical (low voltage drop) reasons.
