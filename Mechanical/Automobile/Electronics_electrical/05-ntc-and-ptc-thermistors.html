NTC & PTC Thermistors

Source images: IMG20250515002320, IMG20250515002326, IMG20250515002339, IMG20250515002344

Overview

Thermistors are temperature-sensitive semiconductor resistors. NTC (Negative Temperature Coefficient) has resistance fall with rising temperature — used as temperature sensors (coolant temp, oil temp, intake air temp). PTC (Positive Temperature Coefficient) has resistance rise sharply at a switch-on temperature — used as self-resetting overcurrent protection and as resistive heaters (auxiliary heat, heated mirrors, seat heaters). The page covers both characteristic curves, the physics behind each, and the typical automotive applications.

Working Principle

NTC (Negative Temperature Coefficient)

Notebook (IMG 002320): "NTC thermistor (Negative temperature coefficient): Resistance which is sensitive to heat. ★ Non-conductors become conductor because of thermal energy. NTC is Negative Temperature Coefficient. Resistance ∝ 1/Temperature → R ∝ 1/T No. of electrons increased."

In an NTC material (typically a metal-oxide ceramic): - At low temperature — few free electrons → high resistance. - At high temperature — thermal energy frees more electrons from the lattice → more carriers → lower resistance.

The R-vs-T curve drops exponentially as temperature rises.

Notebook (margin): "Value of resistance becomes less when we apply heat."

NTC application

Notebook: "★ Application: Resistance highly dependant on temp. Suitable as temperature sensor. Coolant temp sensor, oil temp sensor, intake air temp sensor."

Standard automotive uses: - Coolant temperature sensor — DME reads engine temp. - Oil temperature sensor — for transmission oil or engine oil. - Intake air temperature sensor — completes the air-density calculation alongside MAP. - Outside air temperature — feeds climate control + dashboard display.

The ECU measures the voltage drop across the NTC in a voltage divider → calculates resistance → looks up the corresponding temperature on a stored table.

PTC (Positive Temperature Coefficient)

Notebook (IMG 002326): "PTC (Positive temperature coefficient): BaTiO₃ (Barium Titanate). R ∝ T. When temp is increased beyond certain limit: i) Sudden increase in resistance ii) Insulating grain boundaries. Formation of artificial boundaries."

Unlike NTC's smooth curve, PTC has a sharp transition: - Below a certain temperature (the "Curie point" of the ceramic) → low resistance, current flows freely. - Above the Curie point → resistance jumps sharply (orders of magnitude) → current essentially stops.

The mechanism in barium titanate (BaTiO₃): - Below Curie point → ferroelectric domains aligned → conductive. - Above Curie point → domains scramble → insulating grain boundaries form between crystallites → current path interrupted.

Notebook (margin example): "Motor: As load increases, temp of motor increases, so PTC is required to control [current/temperature]."

Surge control behaviour (IMG 002344 graph)

Notebook draws: current vs time graph showing "sudden increase" then "sudden decrease," labelled "Time in milliseconds" — header "Surge control"

When current spikes (e.g., motor stall): 1. PTC starts cool → low resistance → high current flows. 2. The high current self-heats the PTC. 3. PTC crosses Curie point → resistance jumps → current chokes off. 4. As current drops, PTC cools → returns to low resistance → current restored.

The cycle takes milliseconds and self-protects the circuit without an external fuse or controller.

PTC advantages (IMG 002339)

Notebook: "Advantages: ① High current flow → tide to load ② Self-heating ③ Reduces the current ④ Protects the overload"

PTC applications — as heating element

Notebook: "Application: ① Auxiliary heater ② Heated side mirror ③ Seat heater"

PTCs are ideal as self-regulating resistive heaters because: - They warm up fast (low cold resistance = high initial heating power). - They reach their target temperature and then automatically reduce power (resistance rises with temp → less power dissipation). - No overheating possible — physics-limited. - Intrinsically safe — no need for a thermostat or external protection.

Notebook (Adv list — bottom): "① No-glowing-hot components (limit of heating is auto-limited) ② Easy to regulate ③ Intrinsic safe (built-in — provides safety internally — no need of external device)"

Components

Type Material Behaviour Use
NTC Metal-oxide ceramic (e.g., Mn-Ni-Co spinel) R drops smoothly with T Temperature sensors

Ni-RTD (for completeness, not on page) Pure nickel wire R rises linearly with T High-accuracy temperature measurement

Detailed Component Notes

Why NTC for sensing instead of NI-RTD

  • NTC has much larger ΔR/ΔT → easier to measure accurately with a simple voltage divider.
  • NTC is cheap to mass-produce as a ceramic bead.
  • The trade-off: NTC is non-linear → needs a lookup table; RTD is linear but produces smaller signal.

Why BaTiO₃ specifically

Barium titanate is a ferroelectric ceramic with a sharp Curie transition around 120 °C (can be tuned by doping). Below 120 °C it's conductive; above, insulating. Other ceramics give different switch points; the manufacturer picks the right composition for the target temperature.

"No glowing hot components" — why PTCs are intrinsically safe

A nichrome wire heater can keep getting hotter and hotter until it glows red, melts, or sets fire to its surroundings if the thermostat fails. A PTC heater physically cannot exceed its design temperature — the resistance jump shuts off current automatically. This makes PTCs perfect for safety-critical applications where overheating is unacceptable (seat heaters, auxiliary cabin heaters where you can't see the element).

Auxiliary heaters in diesel cars

Note: Diesel engines warm up slowly (high efficiency = less waste heat). Cold-start cabin heating is a customer comfort issue in winter. Solution: a PTC auxiliary heater in the HVAC air path — kicks in immediately at cold start to warm cabin air while waiting for engine coolant to come up to temp. Self-regulating, no risk of overheating.

Key Facts

  • Thermistor = temperature-sensitive semiconductor resistor.
  • NTC = R drops smoothly with temperature. Used as temperature sensors (coolant, oil, intake air).
  • PTC = R jumps sharply at Curie point. Used as self-limiting heaters + self-resetting overcurrent protection.
  • NTC formula: R ∝ 1/T (approximately exponential drop).
  • PTC formula: R ∝ T (with sharp transition).
  • PTC material: doped barium titanate (BaTiO₃).
  • PTC applications: auxiliary heater, heated mirrors, seat heaters, motor protection.
  • Intrinsically safe: PTCs can't overheat (resistance rise auto-limits current).
  • No external thermostat needed for PTC heaters.
