Battery Diagnostic Measurements & Current Fundamentals

Overview

The dealer-technician's measurement toolkit for the electrical system: voltage scales (μV to kV range — from sensor signals to ignition-coil HV), digital vs analog multimeter comparison, the standard sequence of 12 voltage measurements that a BMW Maruti-Ritz example walks through (open circuit, key-on with various loads, vehicle running at multiple rpms — confirming the alternator is charging correctly), the causes and symptoms of overcharging, the discharge-over-time curve that shows how a parked battery loses capacity (with vs without disconnect switch), and the four effects of electric current (heat, magnetic, chemical, atomic) — the fundamental phenomena that everything from a starter motor to a battery to an LED relies on.

The 12-step battery + alternator voltage check

Notebook (IMG 112900, top-right corner): "Maruti Ritz ZXR" — using this car as the example

Procedure (12 measurements):

# Test condition Reading
1 Open circuit reading 12.65 V
2 Key on, headlights OFF (all loads off) 12.38 V
3 With parking light on, reading (blinking) 12.26 V
4 Headlights ON 11.95 V
5 All loads ON (parking + hazard + radio + roof light + AC blower) 11.74 V
6 Reading with vehicle idling at cold start, at rpm is max (i.e., starting battery refill) 14.29 V (charging voltage, not actual battery V)
7 Vehicle idling (with all loads on) 11.92 V
8 Vehicle at 1500 rpm 13.54 V
9 At 3000 rpm 14.35 V
10 Vehicle at max rpm (~6000 rpm) 14.35 V (regulator caps charging voltage)
11 Max rpm with all loads ON (IMG 112908 top) 14.35 V (regulator clamps even under load)
12 Reading with vehicle OFF (just after running) 13.66 V → slowly will reach 12.6 V (surface charge dissipating)

Margin notes: "charging voltage / Not actual" and "regulator doesn't allow alternator to go above 14.35 — it cap[s] at 14.35 - constant rpm"

Interpretation: - Test 1 is the resting voltage (true SoC indicator). - Tests 2-5 show how each load drops the battery voltage while engine OFF. - Tests 6-10 track the alternator output as rpm climbs; voltage regulator clamps at ~ 14.35 V to prevent overcharging. - Test 12 shows surface charge that takes ~ 1 hour to dissipate back to true resting voltage.

This is a complete electrical-system health check done with just a multimeter — battery condition, alternator output, voltage-regulator behaviour all in one session.

Causes of overcharging

Notebook (IMG 112908, middle): "Consequences of overcharging: ① Corrosion of electrodes ② Lowering the fluid level ③ Formation of oxy-hydrogen gas

Causes: ① Faulty voltage regulator ② Incorrect charging"

When charging voltage exceeds ~ 14.5-14.7 V continuously: - Electrode corrosion — positive grids oxidise → mechanical failure. - Water loss — electrolysis boils off water → electrolyte concentration rises → SG too high → plate damage. - Oxy-hydrogen — explosion risk (see chapter 02).

Causes are almost always: - Bad voltage regulator (mechanical type stuck closed, or solid-state failed). - Bad charging set-up (jumper cables connected wrong, generic charger left on too long).

Charging behaviour graph

Notebook (IMG 112908, bottom graph): Terminal voltage on left Y-axis (0 to 14.2 V); time on X-axis (1-8 hours). Curves: ① charging current constant until V reaches 14.2 V; ② voltage held constant 14.2 V → current decreases exponentially; ③ battery fully charged → current drops to zero; ④ no current after full charge.

This is the classic 2-stage CV/CC charging profile: - Stage 1 (Constant Current): charger pushes max current; voltage rises gradually. - Stage 2 (Constant Voltage): voltage clamped at 14.2 V (a "boost charge" level); current tapers down as battery saturates. - Stage 3 (Float): current drops near zero; battery is full.

The alternator-regulator system in a car is a simplified version of this — runs at fixed regulator voltage and lets the battery draw what it needs.

Discharge over time (with vs without disconnect switch)

Notebook (IMG 112921, top graph): Y-axis: battery voltage (12.0 to 12.5 V); X-axis: time (6 weeks to 12 weeks) Two curves: – Without disconnect switch: drops fast (1.0% per week — represents parasitic draw) – With disconnect switch: drops slowly (self-discharge only, ~ 0.5% per month)

Margin: "Recharge"

For a parked car: - Without battery isolator → parasitic draw (alarm, comfort modules, clock) drains battery → may need recharging after ~ 6 weeks. - With battery isolator → only self-discharge → 3-6 months before recharge needed.

Modern cars with energy management (chapter 12) hold this margin without manual isolators by progressively shutting down comfort consumers.

Current — the four effects

Notebook (IMG 112921, bottom): "Current — 1 Ampere = 2.42 × 10¹⁵ electrons per second (should be 6.24 × 10¹⁸ — trainer slip)

Effects: ① Heat effect — Heater ② Magnetic field — Electric motor, generator, speaker ③ Chemical effect — Battery (stores current in it) & LED (light-emitting) ④ Atomic effect — LED (light-emitting diode)"

All four physical effects of electric current — every electrical device in the car exploits at least one:

Effect Mechanism Where used
Heat (resistive) Current through resistance → I²R heating Heater elements, cigarette lighter, defrost grid, fuses (when they blow), incandescent bulbs
Magnetic Current through wire → magnetic field; field through coil → induced current Starter motor, alternator, solenoids, relays, speakers, ignition coil
Chemical Current drives redox reactions Battery charging, electrolysis, electroplating
Atomic / electronic Direct electron-hole recombination → photons LED, fluorescent tube, semiconductor light sensors

Note: The proper number of electrons in 1 A is 6.24 × 10¹⁸ per second (= 1 C of charge / 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C per electron). The trainer's "2.42 × 10¹⁵" is incorrect; preserved verbatim with this correction noted in Open Questions.

Detailed Component Notes

Why high input impedance (10+ MΩ) matters

A multimeter with low impedance (analog ~ 20 kΩ/V) loads the circuit being measured — pulls some current through itself → distorts the reading. Modern digital meters with 10 MΩ impedance pull negligible current → measurement is the actual voltage, not a perturbed one.

This matters when reading high-impedance sensor signals (O₂ sensor, knock sensor) — a load-induced voltage drop on a low-impedance meter can give a 50% wrong reading.

"Body in hospital — 1 mV"

The trainer's example: ECG (electrocardiogram) signal is in the millivolt range. Body electricity is tiny — hospital equipment must amplify by 1000× to read it cleanly. Same principle on a car: sensor signals are often millivolts; the ECU's analog front-end amplifies before A/D conversion.

Why surface charge at 13.66 V settles over an hour

Notebook test 12: "Reading with vehicle off: 13.66 V → slowly will reach 12.6 V"

Right after charging, the outermost layers of the plates have higher concentration of active material than the bulk → terminal voltage reads slightly higher than the "true" rest voltage. As the chemistry equilibrates internally (diffusion), the surface charge dissipates and voltage settles to the actual SoC voltage of ~ 12.6 V over 30 minutes to an hour.

For accurate SoC reading, wait at least 30 minutes after charging or after a drive before reading resting voltage.

Why disconnect switch helps long storage

Some cars in long-term storage (vintage, race, RV) have a mechanical knife-switch on the battery negative cable. Turning it OFF disconnects the entire car from the battery → no parasitic load → battery only loses charge to internal self-discharge (which is much slower).

Penalty: all clocks, radio presets, ECU adaptation values reset when reconnected. Modern cars with computer-controlled fuelling really don't like being fully disconnected — best avoided unless storing > 1 month.

Key Facts

  • Voltage scales the technician meets: 1 μV (faint sensors) to 25 kV (Xenon strike pulse).
  • Digital multimeter beats analog: better display, auto-polarity, high input impedance (≥ 10 MΩ), shock-resistant.
  • Probe colour codes: red = power/signal, brown (or black) = ground.
  • 12-step battery + alternator voltage check sweeps from open circuit through loads up to max rpm.
  • Healthy resting V: 12.65 V.
  • Charging V at idle: ~ 14 V; at speed: ~ 14.35 V (regulator clamps).
  • Overcharging causes: electrode corrosion, water loss, oxy-hydrogen formation.
  • Overcharging cause: faulty voltage regulator or wrong charger.
  • Discharge over time: faster with parasitic load; slower with battery isolator.
  • 4 effects of current: heat, magnetic, chemical, atomic.
  • Examples by effect: heater (heat), motor/alternator (magnetic), battery (chemical), LED (atomic).
