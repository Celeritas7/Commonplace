Battery — Specifications, Ratings & Failure Modes

Source images: IMG20250514112826, IMG20250514112831

Overview

How to tell whether the battery in your hand is fresh (manufactured recently) vs an old shelf-warmer that's already starting to sulfate. The page covers the A–M month-code system for reading the manufacture date, the sulfation failure mode (irreversible lead-sulfate crystal growth on the plates of an aged or deep-discharged battery), the exploding-battery scenario (overcharging → oxy-hydrogen gas → spark → boom), and the three rating systems every battery ships with: Ampere-hours (Ah) for total energy storage, Cranking Amps (CA) for current capability, and Cold Cranking Amps (CCA) for cold-weather starting performance.

Working Principle

Freshness — the month code

Notebook (IMG 112826): "Freshness — 6 months Not to buy more than 6 months [old] A B C D E F G H J K L M ① neglected"

Most battery manufacturers print a letter+digit code on the case showing manufacture date: - First character (letter) = month of manufacture using A=January, B=February, ... skipping I, so:

Letter Month
A January
B February
C March
D April
E May
F June
G July
H August
J September (skipping I to avoid confusion with 1)
K October
L November
M December
  • Following digit = year (or last digit of year).

E.g., a code starting "B3" = February 2023.

Notebook: "Not to buy more than 6 months [old]"

A battery sitting on the shelf for 6+ months is already losing capacity through self-discharge and soft sulfation (light PbSO₄ deposits that have started crystallising). Don't pay full price for old stock.

Sulfation

Notebook: "Sulfation occurs if battery is old."

Sulfation is the slow build-up of hard lead-sulfate crystals on the plates. Two scenarios: - Soft sulfation — happens during normal discharge; reversible by charging. - Hard sulfation — happens after prolonged deep discharge or long unused storage; PbSO₄ crystals grow large and stable → can no longer be broken down by charging → permanent capacity loss.

Causes of hard sulfation: - Leaving the battery deeply discharged for weeks (e.g., garage queen, forgotten boat). - Operating at chronic low charge (e.g., short urban trips that never fully recharge). - High temperature (accelerates crystal growth).

Hard-sulfated batteries can sometimes be partially revived by equalisation charging (high-voltage controlled charge that breaks down some crystals), but capacity rarely returns to original.

Exploding batteries

Notebook (IMG 112826): "Exploding batteries: – Voltage regulator to avoid overcharging – Overcharging produce oxy-hydrogen film – Even small spark can ignite oxy-hydrogen mixture."

The exploding-battery scenario: 1. Voltage regulator fails → alternator overcharges battery beyond 14.5 V. 2. Excess voltage electrolyses the water in the electrolyte: $$2\text{H}_2\text{O} \;\xrightarrow{\text{electricity}}\; 2\text{H}_2 + \text{O}_2$$ 3. Hydrogen gas accumulates above the electrolyte, mixed with oxygen → oxy-hydrogen gas (a stoichiometric explosive mixture, fires below the lower flammability limit of 4% H₂ in air). 4. Any spark — disconnecting jumper cables, hitting the case with a wrench, even static — can ignite it → battery case explodes, sulphuric acid sprays everywhere.

Why voltage regulator is the critical safety device: it limits charging voltage to ~14.4 V → minimises gassing → less hydrogen accumulation.

Three battery ratings

Ampere-hours (Ah)

Notebook: "Ampere-hours (A·h): Product for how much time battery can provide certain amount of current. This is total amount of energy that battery can store."

Total energy capacity. A 70 Ah battery can theoretically deliver: - 70 A for 1 hour, OR - 35 A for 2 hours, OR - 7 A for 10 hours, OR - 3.5 A for 20 hours (commonly the rated discharge condition — known as C/20).

In practice, Peukert's Law means high-current discharge gives less than rated Ah; low-current gives close to rated.

Cranking Amp (CA) / Marine Cranking Amp (MCA)

Notebook: "Cranking Amp (CA) / Marine Cranking Amp (MCA): Amount of current battery can provide at 0 °C [error — actually at 0 °F for CCA, but CA is at 0 °C, see below] 1.2 volts per cell for 30 sec."

Rating Test temperature Hold for
CA (Cranking Amps) 0 °C (32 °F) 30 sec at ≥ 1.2 V/cell (= ≥ 7.2 V total)
MCA (Marine Cranking Amps) 0 °C (32 °F) Same as CA; used for marine/RV applications

The test reproduces a moderate winter cold-start scenario: how much current can the battery sustain at the freezing point while maintaining a useful voltage.

Cold Cranking Amp (CCA)

Notebook (IMG 112831): "Cold cranking amp (CCA): – Amount of current battery can provide at 0 °F (−18 °C) – At least 1.2 volts per cell for 30 seconds."

The standardised severe-cold test: - Temperature: 0 °F (−18 °C) — proper winter cold. - Duration: 30 seconds. - Voltage floor: 1.2 V/cell × 6 = 7.2 V minimum at terminals. - Result: the current the battery delivered = its CCA rating.

A typical car battery is rated 500-900 CCA. Cold winters need higher CCA because cold thickens engine oil (more cranking torque needed) AND cold reduces battery output (chemistry slows down).

Hot Cranking Amp (HCA)

Notebook: "Hot cranking amp (HCA): – Amount of current supplied at 80 °F (26 °C) – Can provide 1.2 volts for 30 seconds."

The warm-weather test: - Temperature: 80 °F (26 °C) — moderate room temperature. - Same 30 sec / 1.2 V/cell criterion. - HCA > CCA always (warm chemistry works better).

"Power is workdone"

Notebook (bottom of IMG 112831, circled): "Power is workdone"

The trainer's reminder that: $$\text{Power (W)} = \text{Work done (J)} / \text{Time (s)}$$

In battery context: a battery's power rating depends on both how much energy (Ah) AND how fast you can extract it (CCA/CA limits the rate). A high-Ah battery with low CCA can deliver lots of total energy but won't crank a cold engine.

Approximate relationships (rules of thumb): - HCA ≈ 1.4 × CCA (battery delivers ~ 40% more at warm temp) - CA ≈ 1.25 × CCA (50% drop at 0°F vs 0°C is roughly 25%)

Detailed Component Notes

Why "I" is skipped in the month-letter code

I (capital eye) and 1 (one) look identical in many handwritings and printed labels → potential for service-shop confusion (technician reading "I3" as "13" → adds a year, or reverse). Skipping I means the alphabet runs A-H, then jumps to J for September.

Some manufacturers also skip Q (looks like 0) — full safe alphabet: A B C D E F G H J K L M N P R S T U V W X Y Z (24 letters). The notebook shows only up to M = December, sufficient for 12 months.

Why 6 months is the freshness limit

A flooded lead-acid battery self-discharges at ~ 5% per month at room temperature (more in heat). Six months on the shelf = ~ 30% state of charge loss → battery is now sitting partially discharged → sulfation begins → cycle life drops permanently.

Modern AGM batteries are much better (1-2% self-discharge per month) but the 6-month rule still applies in dealer training as a safe limit.

Why CCA matters in cold climates

At −18 °C: - Engine oil viscosity ~ 3× thicker than at 20 °C → starter needs more torque → more current. - Battery internal chemistry slows → output current drops 30–40% vs warm. - Combined: a battery that cranks fine at 20 °C may fail at −18 °C.

So specifying a battery with adequate CCA for your climate is critical. India / tropical climates can use lower CCA; Europe / Canada / Russia need higher CCA (often 700+ for diesels in winter).

Why voltage regulator prevents explosions

The regulator's setpoint of ~ 14.4 V is just below the gassing voltage of a lead-acid battery (~ 14.7 V at room temp). Above that, water electrolysis accelerates → hydrogen gas accumulates → explosion risk.

Modern alternators have an ECU-controlled charging scheme that: - Sets 14.4 V in normal driving. - Drops to ~ 13.8 V at high battery temperatures (reduces gassing further). - Boosts to ~ 14.6 V briefly during cold cranking (helps recover state of charge).

See energy management system chapter for the BPM vs APM details.

Key Facts

  • Freshness check: month letter code (A=Jan, B=Feb, ..., skipping I → J=Sep, ..., M=Dec) + year digit.
  • Don't buy a battery older than 6 months off the shelf.
  • Sulfation = hard PbSO₄ crystal growth on plates. Soft (reversible) vs hard (permanent capacity loss).
  • Exploding battery scenario: overcharging → water electrolysis → H₂ accumulates → spark ignites → boom.
  • Voltage regulator prevents overcharging — kept at ~ 14.4 V to limit gassing.
  • 3 main ratings:
  • Ah = total energy capacity (~ 60–80 Ah for cars)
  • CCA = cold-cranking amps at −18 °C / 0 °F, 30 sec, ≥ 7.2 V
  • HCA = hot-cranking amps at +26 °C / 80 °F
  • HCA ≈ 1.4 × CCA approximately.
  • Cold climate → spec higher CCA; tropical → lower CCA OK.
  • "Power is work done" — P = W/t reminder.
