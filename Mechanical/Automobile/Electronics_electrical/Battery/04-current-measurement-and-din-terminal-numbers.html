Battery

Current Measurement & DIN Terminal Numbers

Source images: IMG20250514112926, IMG20250514112933, IMG20250514112937

Overview

Two interlocking foundations for any electrical diagnostic work on a BMW. First, how to measure current correctly — the ammeter must sit in series with the load, not in parallel (a common beginner mistake that effectively shorts the circuit and damages the meter). For currents larger than the multimeter's ~20 A range, a clamp meter is used instead because it senses the magnetic field around the conductor without breaking the circuit. Second, the DIN 72552 terminal-number convention — the small numbers stamped next to every wire on a German-spec vehicle (30, 15, 50, 31, R, etc.) that tell the technician what each rail does without looking at a wiring diagram. The page also touches on diagnostic-system terminology (DIS / DME software language) and transport mode — the dealer-only state in which non-essential consumers are inhibited to keep the battery alive during shipping, parking-lot storage, or extended service.

Working Principle

Measuring current (the right way and the wrong way)

To read the current flowing through a load, the ammeter must become part of the conductor — current cannot enter the meter unless the wire is opened and the meter inserted into the gap.

WRONG (parallel — top diagram in notes):

       +-----------+
   +---|   (A)     |---+
   |   +-----------+   |       The ammeter is across the bulb. Almost
   |                   |       all current short-circuits through the
[ Bat ]            [ Bulb ]    meter's ~0 Ω shunt → blown fuse / meter.
   |                   |
   +-------------------+

RIGHT (series — corrected note: "Ammeter should be connected in series"):

   +-----(A)-----+
   |             |
[ Bat ]      [ Bulb ]          All load current flows through the
   |             |             ammeter, which reads it on its scale.
   +-------------+

Multimeter vs clamp meter

Tool Range How it connects When to use
Multimeter (ammeter mode) typically up to 20 A (10 A fused on most DMMs) Series — circuit must be broken Sensor draws, parasitic drain, control-circuit currents
Clamp meter Tens to hundreds of amps Jaw clamped around the conductor — circuit stays intact Starter cranking current, alternator output, headlight harness

"Clamp meter — use to check current more than 20 A. Large current can't be calculated by multimeter." — IMG 112933.

The clamp meter works on the magnetic effect of current (one of the four effects listed in file 03-battery-diagnostic-measurements-and-current-fundamentals) — a current-carrying wire generates a magnetic field, and the meter's split-core transformer converts that field back into a readable amperage.

DIN 72552 terminal numbering

Every wire on a BMW (and almost every German-spec vehicle since the 1960s) carries a stamped two-digit number that identifies what the rail does. The notes give the five most-used terminal codes:

Terminal Name Voltage state Used for
30 Battery+ (B+) Always live (direct from battery) Memory loads, alarm, ECU keep-alive, hazard switch
15 Ignition ON Live with key in "ON" / engine running Injection, ignition coils, instrument cluster, most lighting
50 Starter trigger Live only while key is in "START" Starter solenoid pull-in
R Radio Live with key in "Radio" / accessory position Audio, accessory sockets
31 Ground / Earth Chassis return (0 V) Every load's return path

Relay control current (the 2 mA / 2.3 A pair)

The page notes that a typical automotive relay needs:

  • Maintaining current: ≈ 2 mA to keep the coil energised once the magnetic circuit has closed.
  • Operating (pull-in) current: ≈ 2.2 – 2.5 A — the larger inrush needed to pull the armature against its spring at the moment of switching.

This is why relays exist at all: the ignition switch carries only the 2 mA – 2.5 A coil current, while the relay contacts carry the much larger load current (10–30 A for a horn or fuel pump, 80 + A for a starter motor). The switch stays small and cool; the high current never passes through the dashboard.

Process Flow — A normal start sequence using these terminals

1. Key OFF        → only Term 30 alive (BCM keep-alive,
                    alarm, clock, central locking ECU)

2. Key to R       → Term 30 + R alive (radio, accessory)

3. Key to ON (Ign)→ Term 30 + 15 alive (DME wakes up,
                    fuel pump primes, instrument cluster lights)

4. Key to START   → Term 30 + 15 + 50 alive
                    50 energises starter solenoid via relay
                    → solenoid pulls pinion in & closes
                    high-current contacts → starter cranks

5. Key released   → Term 50 drops, ignition stays at 15
                    Starter de-energises, engine self-sustains

Detailed Component Notes

Why "connected in series" is non-negotiable

A voltmeter has very high internal resistance (MΩ); an ammeter has near-zero internal resistance (a precision shunt of mΩ scale). If you treat them the same and put an ammeter across the battery, you've effectively closed a dead short through the meter — the multimeter's internal fuse blows instantly (best case) or the meter itself catches fire (worst case). The page underlines this with the comment "Ammeter should be connected in series" next to a crossed-out wrong-connection sketch.

Diagnostic-system terminology (top-of-page note)

  • Diagnostic system: "software which gives command" — the DIS (Diagnostic Information System) / later ISTA tester that BMW dealers plug into the OBD socket. It doesn't just read — it can command the DME and other modules to run actuator tests (open injector 3, spool turbo VGT, cycle ABS valves).
  • Transport mode: a one-bit flag that the DIS sets which puts the car into "shipping" state — interior lights, locking confirmation, drowsy-battery-warning chimes, etc. are all suppressed. The page calls out three example consumers that transport mode silences: stereo system, AC system, "unwanted systems during servicing."
  • "24-car problem": likely a Navnit-internal training scenario about a fleet of 24 dealer-stock cars whose batteries went flat because transport mode had been disabled. Preserved verbatim; meaning is contextual to the trainer.

Self-discharge — first introduction

The page begins the self-discharge topic that file 05 will cover in detail with graphs. The first mechanism listed:

  • Impurities in the dilute sulphuric acid — trace Fe (iron) and Sb (antimony) from manufacturing.
  • Alloy elements in the metal plates — the lead grids are not pure Pb (pure lead is too soft); they contain antimony or calcium for mechanical strength.

These trace metals create tiny internal galvanic cells across the plate surface that slowly bleed charge even when no load is connected. At 20 °C, after 3 months, a 12.85 V battery drops to about 12.65 V purely from self-discharge — the trigger value for the trickle-charger graph on the next page.

Key Facts

  • Ammeter goes in series; voltmeter goes in parallel. Swap them and you blow something.
  • A multimeter in current mode handles up to 20 A. Above that, use a clamp meter.
  • DIN 72552: 30 = always-live B+, 15 = ignition ON, 50 = starter momentary, 31 = ground, R = radio/accessory.
  • A relay needs ~2.2–2.5 A to pull in but only ~2 mA to hold. This is why a small dashboard switch can control a 100 A starter.
  • Transport mode silences non-essential consumers (stereo, AC, etc.) to protect the battery during dealer storage.
  • At 20 °C a fully-charged battery falls from 12.85 V to ≈ 12.65 V over 3 months purely from self-discharge.
