Charging System & Alternator

Source images: IMG20250514113312, IMG20250514113322, IMG20250514113342, IMG20250514113346, IMG20250514113354

Overview

Five pages that move from "what the battery does" to "what the charging system does to keep the battery alive." First, the battery-defect pie chart — the dealer-floor statistic that 45% of all returned batteries fail because of incorrect charging, not because of any flaw in the battery itself. Second, the state-of-charge measurement triplet — voltage, current, and electrolyte temperature — and the temperature-compensated charging-voltage curve that smart regulators follow to push acceptance higher in cold weather and avoid gassing in hot. Third, the past / today / future evolution of vehicle electrical demand and the dual-battery architecture that splits the engine-start load from the cabin-load. Fourth, the alternator construction itself — a 3-phase claw-pole AC machine with a six-diode rectifier bridge that converts the AC to DC. Fifth, the complete charging-circuit block diagram and the seven-step alternator startup sequence that explains how the alternator initially borrows excitation current from the battery (via the dashboard "charge" lamp) and then becomes self-exciting once the engine is spinning.

Working Principle

Why most batteries fail — the 45% number

The notebook's pie chart of returned battery defects:

   Reason                            Share
   ────────────────────────────────  ─────
   ① Incorrect charging               45 %     ←─── the big one
   ② Total discharge                  20 %     ←─── deep discharge → sulfation
   ③ Manufacturing fault               8 %
   ④ Total discharge (second mode)    20 %?    ←─── see [?] note at bottom
   ⑤ Battery is OK (no fault found)    5 %
   ⑥ Other                              2 %

The single most important fact in this chart: nearly half of all field failures are caused by the charging system, not by the battery. A battery that's chronically undercharged (alternator dropping out at idle, voltage regulator stuck low, parasitic drain at night) sulfates within months. A battery that's chronically overcharged (regulator stuck high, customer left a cheap aftermarket charger on for weeks) loses water and corrodes its grid within a year. The dealer's job when a battery dies young is not to replace and move on — it's to find the charging-system fault first, otherwise the new battery will die the same way.

The three measurement quantities for state of charge

To know whether the battery is being charged correctly, the charging system continuously measures three quantities:

# Quantity How measured Why
① Battery voltage Sense wire from BCM to battery + post Direct read of cell terminal voltage
② Discharge & charge current Hall-effect or shunt sensor at battery -ve post Knows the net Ah balance over time (Coulomb counting)
③ Electrolyte temperature Direct temp sensor on the battery, or calculated from ambient temp + coolant temp + vehicle speed Determines correct charging voltage target (see curve below)

"It can shut off voltage regulator i.e. can increase voltage above 14.6 V" — IMG 113312.

The system can override the default 14.4 V regulator setpoint when its model says the battery needs more (cold, partly discharged) or less (hot, full).

Temperature-compensated charging voltage

A 12 V Pb-acid battery's correct charging voltage is not a constant — it depends on electrolyte temperature. The notebook plots this curve directly:

The rule of thumb the curve embeds: charging voltage drops roughly −4 mV per °C per cell above 20 °C reference. For a 6-cell 12 V battery that's −24 mV per °C at the pack level. Over a 60 °C swing from −30 °C to +30 °C, that's a 1.4 V difference in the correct setpoint.

Why both directions matter: - Cold: the chemistry slows down. Without pushing the voltage higher, the battery would never accept enough charge. - Hot: the chemistry runs away. Without pulling the voltage lower, the battery gases and loses water rapidly.

A regulator that doesn't temperature-compensate is the single biggest cause of the "45% incorrect charging" failure mode. Modern BMW (E60 onwards) does this in the DDE / DME via a network-broadcast temperature value; older systems used a temperature compensation built into the alternator's mechanical regulator.

Past, present, future of vehicle electrical load

The page captures the evolution of electrical demand in three lines:

   PAST    — Battery starts engine & gets recharged. End of story.
              Single battery, basic alternator, ~500 W load.

   TODAY   — "Electric" — vehicle is now a rolling computer.
              Power steering, ABS, ESC, ECU, infotainment, HUD,
              electric seats, heated seats, AC blower, headlights,
              alternator boosted to 1.5–2.5 kW.

   FUTURE  — Alternator Management System (Hyundai magnetic clutch).
              Smart alternator that disengages from the engine when
              the battery is full → saves fuel by removing parasitic
              drag from the crankshaft. (BMW's equivalent is
              "Brake Energy Regeneration" — only charges during
              braking / coasting to recover otherwise-wasted energy.)

Energy vs power — why two batteries make sense

The page reframes the engine-start and accessory-load problem in energy / power terms:

   ENGINE START (cranking)        WHILE DRIVING (cruise)
   ───────────────────────         ──────────────────────
   P = V × I  (power = V × A)       E = P × t  (energy = W·s)
   Need high power                  Need less power, lots of energy
   So → large plate surface         So → more number of plates
        (thin plates, low impedance)     (thick plates, more material)

       ★ 1st preference                ★ 2nd preference
       in dual-battery layout          in dual-battery layout
       = the starter battery           = the auxiliary battery
                                            (AGM, deep-cycle capable)

This is exactly the power-optimised vs energy-optimised split that file 08 introduced — applied here as the architectural answer to: "no single battery is good at both jobs at once, so use two."

The dual-battery architecture

                          ┌─── BMS (battery management system) ───┐
                          │                                       │
   ┌──────────┐           │                                       │
   │  G (alt) ├──┬──[diode]──┬──[loads]──[ Starter battery ]──[ Starter motor ]
   └──────────┘  │           │              (power-optimised)
                 │           │
            [Aux battery]    │
            (energy-          │
            optimised,        │
            AGM)              │
                 └───────────[Diode / DC-DC converter]

The architecture: 1. Alternator + main starter battery lives at the engine bay, handles the violent 200–600 A cranking demand. 2. Auxiliary AGM battery lives elsewhere (boot, under rear seat), handles the steady accessory load, recovers brake-regen energy. 3. A diode or smart DC-DC converter routes alternator output to whichever battery needs charge most. Critically, it prevents the starter battery from being drained by accessories left running with engine off. 4. The BMS monitors voltage / current / temperature on both batteries and tells the alternator how much to charge.

This is the architecture used on every modern BMW with start-stop, and on every hybrid.

The alternator — a 3-phase AC generator with a rectifier

The notes set out the alternator as two electrical machines stacked together: an AC generator (rotor + stator) and a DC rectifier.

Modern vehicles need 2 kW of alternator output (cars), trucks more. The alternator is the source of power when the engine is running — it powers every load (headlights, AC, ECU) and simultaneously recharges the battery for the next start.

The star (Y) connection in the stator

The three stator windings are connected in star (also called wye / Y): each winding's "phase end" comes out to a stator terminal, and the "neutral ends" all join at a single internal point.

                   Phase A out
                       │
                   ─── L_A ────┐
                                ├──── neutral point
                   ─── L_B ────┤    (sometimes brought out
                       │        │     to the rectifier as
                   Phase B      │     a 7th tap)
                                │
                   ─── L_C ────┘
                       │
                   Phase C out

Why star, not delta: for the same phase-winding voltage, star gives a higher line voltage at the rectifier (√3 × phase) for the same diode current. Star is also fault-tolerant — if one diode fails, the alternator still produces ~50% output. Delta would lose output entirely on a single diode failure.

The 6-diode bridge rectifier

The three AC phases are converted to DC by a 3-phase full-wave bridge:

                 +DC out (to battery +)
                     ▲
                     │
       ┌─────►├──────┼──────►├─────┐         ← 3 upper diodes
       │             │              │           (anodes from each phase,
       │             │              │            cathodes joined at +DC)
       │             │              │
   Phase A       Phase B        Phase C
       │             │              │
       │             │              │
       └◄──────┤├────┼──────◄┤├─────┘          ← 3 lower diodes
                     │                            (cathodes from each phase,
                     ▼                             anodes joined at -DC)
                  -DC out (to battery -)

The 6 diodes ensure unidirectional flow to the battery and catch the positive peak of each phase so the output is six pulses per electrical cycle — far smoother than single-phase rectification.

The 7-step alternator startup & self-excitation sequence

This is the heart of why a charge warning lamp exists on every dashboard. The page lays it out as 7 numbered steps:

   ① Ignition key turned ON.
      Current flows: Battery → "charge" warning bulb → carbon brush → slip ring.

   ② Rotor still stationary. The small current from step ①
      magnetises the rotor field coil → claws acquire N-S polarity
      alternately.

   ③ Engine starts. Belt from crankshaft spins the rotor.

   ④ Rotating magnetic field induces AC in the stator windings.
      AC fed to the 6-diode rectifier → pulsating DC.

   ⑤ DC fed to voltage regulator, which limits output to ~14.4 V.

   ⑥ Voltage regulator now supplies:
        • Battery (charges it back up)
        • All vehicle loads

   ⑦ It ALSO supplies the carbon brushes
      → alternator is now self-exciting (no longer needs the battery).

      At this moment, the dashboard "charge" lamp goes out:
      both ends of the lamp are at the same potential
      (14.4 V from regulator on one side, 14.4 V from battery+ on the other)
      → no current flows through it → lamp dark.

   If the lamp stays on after start:
      • Alternator output dead → no 14.4 V on the regulator side
      → both ends not equal → current still flows → lamp lit
      → "CHARGE" warning to driver.

This is why the warning lamp is in series between the battery and the field winding, not in parallel with anything else. It serves two functions at once: (a) provides the initial excitation current when the engine starts, (b) acts as a charge-system failure indicator the moment the alternator stops outputting.

Detailed Component Notes

Why "claw-pole" instead of separate magnets

A claw-pole rotor has one field coil and two stamped-steel finger-pole halves that interleave around it. This is brutally cheap to manufacture compared to wound salient-pole rotors (one coil per pole). One coil drives every claw to opposite polarity — N on one finger, S on the next, alternating. The downside: claw poles are less efficient than salient-pole machines at low rpm. Modern hybrid alternators (Hyundai's "MGU", Toyota's "MG1/MG2") use higher-efficiency designs.

Why the dashboard lamp goes out when the alternator works

Series-connected, voltage-difference indicator. The lamp has battery+ on one terminal and alternator+ on the other. - Engine off: alternator+ = 0 V, battery+ = 12 V → lamp lit (current flows from battery through lamp to ground via rotor circuit). - Engine running, alt OK: alternator+ = 14.4 V, battery+ = 14.4 V (battery now matches alternator) → no voltage across lamp → lamp dark. - Engine running, alt failed: alternator+ = 0 V, battery+ = 12 V (battery now discharging through loads) → lamp lit again.

The lamp tells you "alternator output ≠ battery voltage" — i.e., the charging system is not doing its job.

Why a 2 kW alternator on a "small" car

A modern small car at idle with all loads on (headlights 250 W, AC blower 200 W, heated rear screen 200 W, infotainment 100 W, ECU + sensors 150 W, fuel pump 100 W, ignition coils 50 W, miscellaneous 200 W) draws ≈ 1.25 kW continuously. Add the cushion to charge the battery back up after start (200–400 W for 10 minutes), plus margin, and 2 kW continuous is the minimum spec. Trucks with refrigerated bodies, trailer power, hydraulic lifts can need 4–6 kW.

Star vs delta — what the page's Y means in practice

A 12 V alternator producing 100 A continuous in star configuration has each phase winding rated for ≈ 33 A and ≈ 8.3 V (phase voltage). In delta, the same alternator would need each phase winding rated for ≈ 58 A but only 14.4 V — different copper choices, different optimisation. BMW uses star almost universally for ease of diagnostic (one neutral point, six rectifier connections).

Process Flow — A complete charging cycle

KEY OFF
   • Battery alone holds the rail at 12.6–12.8 V.
   • All bus modules at sleep current (~50 mA).

KEY TO IGN
   • Battery → fuse → "charge" lamp → field circuit → slip rings → rotor coil → ground.
   • Lamp lit (battery side 12 V, alternator side 0 V).
   • Rotor field is now energised (~2 A of excitation).

ENGINE STARTS
   • Starter motor draws 200–400 A from battery, voltage sags to 9–10 V briefly.
   • Crankshaft → belt → rotor spins (typically 3,000–6,000 rpm at idle).

ALTERNATOR ONLINE (within 1 s of running)
   • Stator generates 3-phase AC.
   • Rectifier outputs ~14 V pulsating DC.
   • Voltage regulator clamps the DC at the temperature-compensated setpoint.
   • "Charge" lamp dark (both sides now ≈ 14.4 V).
   • Battery accepts charge to replace what the starter took.

CRUISE (warm engine)
   • Battery is at full SoC after ~5–10 minutes.
   • Charging current tapers from ~30 A (initial) to ~2 A (float).
   • Loads run entirely off the alternator; battery is just a buffer.

ENGINE STOPS
   • Alternator coasts down, stops generating within seconds.
   • Battery instantly takes over as the sole supply.
   • All wake-on-CAN modules go to sleep within 20 minutes.
   • Quiescent current settles to ~30–60 mA (from file 06).

Key Facts

  • 45 % of returned batteries fail because of incorrect charging — fix the alternator/regulator first, then replace the battery.
  • The regulator measures three quantities: battery V, charge/discharge current, electrolyte temperature.
  • Temperature-compensated charging: ≈ −24 mV/°C pack-level. Cold = push to 16 V; hot = drop to 13 V.
  • An alternator is a 3-phase AC generator (claw-pole rotor + star-connected stator) + 6-diode bridge rectifier.
  • Star (Y) connection is fault-tolerant; a single diode failure halves output but doesn't kill it.
  • The charge warning lamp serves two roles: initial excitation source and failure indicator.
  • Modern cars need a 2 kW alternator to support today's electrical loads (BMW E60: 180 A × 14.4 V ≈ 2.6 kW).
  • The 7-step start sequence: battery excites field → engine spins rotor → AC induced in stator → rectified to DC → regulator clamps → output charges battery + loads → field draws from regulator (self-exciting).
  • Dual-battery architecture = power-optimised SLI for cranking + energy-optimised AGM for accessories.
