Self-Discharge, Resistance & Circuit Calculations

Source images: IMG20250514112945, IMG20250514112949, IMG20250514113000, IMG20250514113004

Overview

Four pages that step out of the battery itself and into the circuit theory the dealer-tech needs to interpret a multimeter reading. The block opens with the self-discharge-vs-temperature graph — the killer chart of any showroom-floor storage discussion, because it shows that a battery sitting in 40 °C Mumbai summer goes flat roughly twice as fast as the same battery in a 20 °C garage, and a 60 °C engine-bay parked battery is dead in three to four months. From there the notes move into electrical fundamentals: power (P = VI), the three factors that change a resistor's value (cross-section, length, temperature), variable resistance (rheostat), and the series and parallel rules every workshop calculation eventually reduces to. The block closes with a practical resistance-measurement procedure (ammeter + voltmeter, R = V / I) and a worked parallel-resistor example (43 Ω ∥ 51 Ω → ≈ 23.33 Ω).

Working Principle

Self-discharge vs temperature

Even with the key off and every consumer unplugged, a lead-acid battery slowly loses charge because of the trace impurities introduced in the previous page (Fe and Sb in the electrolyte and plate alloy). The rate of those internal galvanic micro-reactions roughly doubles every 10 °C — the same Arrhenius rule that governs corrosion and tyre-rubber ageing.

Trickle charger

The bottom half of IMG 112945 sketches the trickle-charger current waveform — a continuous low-current top-up (typically 100–500 mA at 13.5–13.8 V) that exactly cancels the self-discharge current. Used in dealer storage yards and museum cars to keep a battery indefinitely at full charge without overcharging. Modern equivalents are CTEK, Optimate, BMW's own "battery sustainer" sold as an accessory. The waveform irregularities in the sketch correspond to the charger cycling between bulk, absorb, and float stages — though the page doesn't label them.

Power and Ohm's law (revisited)

   P = V × I
   V = P / I
   I = P / V

With units V (volts), P (watts), I (amps) — already covered in 03-battery-diagnostic-measurements-and-current-fundamentals.

Resistance — the three controlling factors

A conductor's resistance $R$ depends on what it's made of, how thick it is, how long it is, and how hot it is. Formally:

$$R = \rho \cdot \frac{L}{A} \cdot [1 + \alpha (T - T_0)]$$

The notebook captures this as a clean three-row summary:

Variable Relationship Effect on R
Cross-section $A$ (mm²) Inversely proportional Thicker wire ↓ R
Length $L$ (m) Directly proportional Longer wire ↑ R
Temperature $T$ (°C) Directly proportional (for metals) Hotter wire ↑ R

The page also names the cold-temperature corollary: - Cryogenics: "study of materials at less than 0 °C." Listed because the temperature → resistance relationship inverts for some materials at cryogenic temperatures (superconductors), and this matters for very high-power magnet and sensor work — though it's tangential to a dealer technician.

Heating effect of current

"Area reduction → heat effect (increases resistance)" — a worn or pinched wire has less cross-section at the damaged point, so its local resistance rises, so its $I^2R$ heat dissipation rises, so it gets hotter, so its resistance rises further. This positive-feedback loop is how harness fires start.

Variable resistance — the rheostat

        Wiper (movable contact)
            ↓
        ┌───┴───┐
   ─────┤▒▒▒▒▒▒▒├──── (zigzag = fixed resistor symbol)
        └───────┘            ↕
                          (also drawn as)
                          ──/\/\/\/\──     ← potentiometer

A rheostat is a wire-wound resistor with a sliding contact (wiper). Rotating the wiper changes the effective length of wire in circuit, so $R$ goes from ~0 to its maximum. Used in older dashboard rheostat dimmers and HVAC fan-speed selectors before PWM modules took over.

Series circuits

   +─[ R₁ ]─[ R₂ ]─−        I (same through both) →
        V₁     V₂

The current is the same through every component; the voltages add:

$$V_{\text{total}} = V_1 + V_2 \quad,\quad R_{\text{total}} = R_1 + R_2$$

$$\frac{V_1}{V_{\text{total}}} = \frac{R_1}{R_{\text{total}}}$$

(The voltage-divider ratio — the same rule that lets a sensor put a clean signal voltage into the DME from a much wider supply.)

Parallel circuits

        ┌─[ R₁ ]─┐
   +────┤        ├────−       I_total = I₁ + I₂
        └─[ R₂ ]─┘

The voltage is the same across every branch; the currents add:

$$I_{\text{total}} = I_1 + I_2$$

Combined with Ohm's law ($I = V/R$), this gives:

$$\frac{V}{R_{\text{total}}} = \frac{V}{R_1} + \frac{V}{R_2} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \frac{1}{R_{\text{total}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2}$$

Practical resistance measurement (R = V / I)

The page draws two measurement circuits — both built on the rule R = V / I:

1.  Direct method — measure V across R, I through R:

    +─[Battery]──(A)──┬──[ R ]──┬──−
                     │         │
                    (V) ──────(V)
                              R = V/I

2.  Substitution method — known Rm, unknown Rx, swap-balance:

    +─[Battery]──┬──[Rm]──┬──−
                │         │
                (V)──────(V)         compare to:

    +─[Battery]──┬──[Rx]──┬──−
                │         │
                (V)──────(V)
                              same voltage at the same current → Rx = Rm

The page also notes: - R = V / I = 0.008 Ω — a worked example of a very low resistance (the kind a starter cable or battery shunt would show). - "Logarithmic scale" — for plotting resistance values that span six or more decades (mΩ shunts to MΩ insulation tests) on one axis. - "Bulb resistance increases with temperature" — a tungsten filament bulb shows ~10× lower resistance cold than hot, which is why an old incandescent's inrush current is roughly 10× the steady-state current. This is the classic example of a strongly temperature-dependent resistor.

Worked example — parallel resistance

The notebook works through one numerical case at the bottom of IMG 113004:

$$\frac{1}{R_{\text{total}}} = \frac{1}{43} + \frac{1}{51} = \frac{51 + 43}{43 \times 51} = \frac{94}{2193}$$

$$R_{\text{total}} = \frac{2193}{94} \approx 23.33 \;\Omega$$

(Result lower than either input resistor, as a parallel combination always is.)

Components

Component Function
Trickle charger Maintains a stored battery at full charge by supplying current equal to its self-discharge rate
Rheostat / potentiometer Continuously variable resistor; mechanical wiper changes effective wire length
Resistor (fixed) Drops a defined voltage at a defined current — for biasing, current-limiting, voltage-dividing
Bulb (incandescent filament) Acts as a strongly temperature-dependent resistor — low R cold, high R hot

Detailed Component Notes

Why the self-discharge curve matters in practice

Mumbai garages and Indian dealer storage yards often sit at 35–42 °C ambient for half the year. A car parked for 3 months in such conditions can drop into the 12.4–12.5 V range, which by itself is fine — but if even a small parasitic drain (a courtesy light left on, a misbehaving infotainment head unit) is layered on top, the battery falls below 12.0 V and starts to sulfate. This is the single biggest reason dealer-stock batteries fail before sale, and the trickle-charger system above is the standard remedy.

Cryogenics aside

The notebook flags cryogenics because of one practical link: the resistance of pure copper drops roughly 1% per °C as it cools. A starter cable in a −20 °C Himalayan parking lot has about 5% lower resistance than the same cable in a +30 °C Mumbai monsoon — but the engine oil is so much more viscous that the net cranking current is higher in the cold, not lower. The next file (06) will return to this in the cold-cranking discussion.

Bulb-resistance temperature dependence

A 12 V, 21 W brake-light bulb has a steady-state hot resistance of ≈ 12² / 21 ≈ 6.9 Ω. Its cold resistance is around 0.7–0.9 Ω. So the inrush current is ≈ 12 / 0.8 ≈ 15 A — for a few milliseconds — before the filament heats up and the current settles to ≈ 1.75 A. This matters because: - It is why filament bulbs fail when first switched on, not during steady operation. - It explains why LED retrofits, with their nearly constant low load, can fool BMW's bulb-check resistors and trigger a Check-Control bulb-failure warning.

Voltage-divider rule in dealer diagnostics

The series-circuit equation $V_1 / V_{\text{total}} = R_1 / R_{\text{total}}$ is the same rule the DME uses to read a NTC coolant sensor. The DME supplies 5 V through a fixed pull-up resistor $R_{\text{pull-up}}$; the sensor's resistance $R_{\text{NTC}}$ forms the other half of the divider; the DME reads the midpoint voltage. As coolant warms, $R_{\text{NTC}}$ falls, the midpoint voltage falls, and the DME looks up "midpoint voltage → coolant temp" in a calibration table.

Key Facts

  • Self-discharge rate roughly doubles per +10 °C above 20 °C. Cool storage is free battery life.
  • At 20 °C, a healthy battery sits at ~12.65 V after 3 months of idle storage.
  • A trickle charger replaces the self-discharge current without overcharging — standard dealer-yard practice.
  • R depends on: cross-section (inversely), length (directly), temperature (directly).
  • Series: $R_{\text{total}} = R_1 + R_2$. Parallel: $1/R_{\text{total}} = 1/R_1 + 1/R_2$.
  • Worked parallel example from notes: $43 \;\Omega \parallel 51 \;\Omega \approx 23.33 \;\Omega$.
  • R = V / I is the workshop's universal resistance test — ammeter and voltmeter, that's it.
  • A bulb filament's resistance is ~10× lower when cold than hot; this is why the inrush current is so large.
