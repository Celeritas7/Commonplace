Transistors — Bipolar & MOSFET

Source images: IMG20250515002514, IMG20250515002523, IMG20250515002536, IMG20250515002545, IMG20250515002558, IMG20250515002606

Overview

A transistor is a 3-terminal semiconductor device that uses a small input signal at one terminal to control a much larger current/voltage flowing between the other two. The page covers the two main families: bipolar junction transistor (BJT) — controlled by input current at the base — and MOSFET (Metal Oxide Semiconductor Field-Effect Transistor) — controlled by input voltage at the gate. Plus the transistor as switch (the foundation of every digital ECU), pulse-width modulation (PWM) for power control without resistive loss, and a quantitative comparison table showing why MOSFETs eventually replaced bipolar transistors in most automotive power-switching applications.

Working Principle

Bipolar transistor (BJT)

Notebook (IMG 002514, lower): "★ Transistor: – Collector (less doped) – Base – Emitter (highly doped) – Bipolar transistor (as it has two depletion regions)"

A BJT has three regions sandwiched together: NPN or PNP. The base is very thin so charge carriers (electrons in NPN, holes in PNP) can flow through it from emitter to collector when properly biased.

The trainer's "two depletion regions" refers to the two PN junctions in a BJT: one between emitter-base, one between base-collector. Hence "bi-polar" — uses both electron and hole conduction.

BJT use cases

Notebook (IMG 002523): "Use: ① Amplifying signal current ② Amplifying signal voltage ③ Amplifying both current & voltage

Emitter: Reference point for both input & output."

Operation What's amplified
Common-emitter Both current and voltage (most common — high gain)
Common-base Voltage only
Common-collector Current only (emitter follower)

BJT current relationships

Notebook (IMG 002523 middle): "I_C ≈ 100 · I_B"

For a typical BJT: collector current = current gain × base current. β (hFE) ≈ 100 for small-signal devices; lower (≈ 20–50) for power transistors. So a 10 mA base current can switch a 1 A collector current — the amplification that makes BJTs useful.

Transistor as a SWITCH (saturation vs cutoff)

Notebook (IMG 002536): "Switch: No wear [+12 V] → motor → BJT → ground sun rays → photo sensor → base

– Loss 1: at full current → P = 0.7 × 40 = 28 W (Vce(sat) × I) – Loss 2: at medium current → P = 6.5 × 20 = 130 W (linear-region operation)"

A BJT can be: - Fully ON (saturation) — Vce ≈ 0.7 V (the "saturation voltage"). Low loss. - Fully OFF (cutoff) — Vce ≈ supply voltage, no current. No loss. - Linear region (in between) — high loss because both V and I are non-zero simultaneously. Use as audio amplifier; avoid as switch.

The "no wear" advantage: unlike a mechanical relay (which arcs at every switch event and eventually fails), a transistor switches instantly with no physical contacts → essentially infinite cycles.

Pulse Width Modulation (PWM) — the power-control trick

Notebook (IMG 002536, bottom): "Pulse width modulation: – Switching between 'ON' & 'OFF' – Not visible to human – P_avg = P_ON × (t_on / (t_on + t_off))"

Instead of running the transistor in lossy linear mode to vary power, switch it fully ON and fully OFF many times per second — vary the ratio of ON-time to OFF-time to control average power. Examples: - 50% duty (ON half the time) → 50% of max power. - 25% duty → 25% power. - 10% duty → 10% power.

The transistor only loses energy at the brief switching transitions; in either ON or OFF state it loses almost nothing → much more efficient than linear control.

Where used: headlight dimming, fan-speed control, fuel-injector pulse-width (the basis of modern fuel injection), EPS motor current, valvetronic motor, dashboard LED brightness, etc.

MOSFET — different operating principle

Notebook (IMG 002545): "MOSFET (Metal oxide semiconductor field-effect transistor): Gate / Source / Drain / Substrate

① Electrons flow from drain → source ② Depletion region increases due to flow of electrons from gate to source ③ Electrons from drain are transferred to SOURCE so depletion region increased at DRAIN ④ Depletion region between source & drain became less ⑤ At threshold current starts flowing"

A MOSFET has a structure like a sandwich: - Source and Drain = N+ regions on a P substrate. - Gate = a metal electrode separated from the substrate by an insulating layer (silicon dioxide / quartz).

Apply positive voltage to the gate → electric field pulls electrons toward the substrate surface under the gate → a thin conductive channel forms between source and drain → current flows.

Crucially: gate is insulated — no current flows into the gate. The gate behaves like a capacitor that you charge and discharge. This is the "field effect" — the gate's electric field, not current, controls conduction.

MOSFET equivalent circuit

Notebook (IMG 002558): "Bipolar Transistor | Field-effect transistor ① Transistor controlled by control current | ① Transistor controlled by control voltage"

Demonstration in the notebook: - Bipolar driving a 2 A load: needs 2 mA × 100 = 0.2 A base current to control (significant!). - MOSFET driving same load: needs only 5 V at the gate, ~ 0 mA → "powerless control, voltage-controlled switching, simpler circuitry, sensitive to electrostatic discharge."

For the same 10 A load and 13 V battery: - Bipolar: drops 0.7 V across Vce(sat) → P_loss = 0.7 × 10 = 7 W heat. - MOSFET: drops Rds(on) × I = 0.01 × 10 = 0.1 V → P_loss = 0.1 × 10 = 1 W heat. - Mechanical: ~ 0 drop when closed, but contacts arc every switch event → eventual wear.

Modern automotive power switching = MOSFET (low loss, no wear, voltage-control = simple drive circuitry).

Detailed Component Notes

BJT base = thin and lightly doped

For β to be high (good amplification), the base must be physically thin (microns) and lightly doped so most emitter-injected carriers don't recombine in the base — they cross through to the collector. This is why BJTs were originally hard to make: the thin-base requirement.

Why MOSFETs dominate power switching now

  1. Voltage control → simple drive (5 V logic directly drives the gate).
  2. No base current → no power lost driving it.
  3. Rds(on) keeps dropping with manufacturing improvements → now < 1 mΩ available → very low conduction loss.
  4. Fast switching → low switching loss.
  5. Inherent body diode → free-wheeling diode built-in (saves a part).

The era of bipolar power switching ended in automotive around 2000; only specific high-voltage applications (e.g., IGBTs in hybrid traction inverters) still use bipolar-flavour devices.

Sensitivity to ESD

MOSFET's gate is just a few oxide molecules thick (5–10 nm). Static-electricity discharge from your finger can punch through this oxide → MOSFET destroyed. Service technicians wear antistatic wrist straps when handling MOSFETs. Production MOSFETs include internal gate-source clamping Zeners to absorb ESD spikes.

PWM frequency choice

  • Below 100 Hz → human eye sees flicker (bad for LEDs).
  • 100 Hz – 5 kHz → no flicker but audible whine from inductive loads (motors, fans).
  • 20 kHz – 100 kHz → ultrasonic (no whine) but switching losses grow → trade-off region.
  • > 100 kHz → low ripple but high switching losses, requires good MOSFETs.

Typical car PWM: headlight dimming ~ 200 Hz; EPS motor control 16–20 kHz (above audible).

Key Facts

  • BJT (Bipolar Junction Transistor): 3 regions (NPN/PNP), 3 terminals (C/B/E), current-controlled.
  • MOSFET: gate / source / drain / substrate, voltage-controlled via insulated gate (field effect).
  • β (hFE) ≈ 100 for typical small-signal BJT → I_C = 100 × I_B.
  • Vce(sat) ≈ 0.7 V — BJT's unavoidable saturation drop.
  • Rds(on) ≈ 10 mΩ — modern MOSFET on-resistance; much lower loss than BJT.
  • PWM = switch fully on/off at high frequency; duty cycle controls average power. Efficient, almost lossless.
  • Transistor as switch beats mechanical relay: no wear, fast, infinite cycles.
  • MOSFETs dominate modern automotive power switching (lower loss, simpler drive, no base current).
  • MOSFETs are ESD-sensitive — gate oxide is microscopic; handle with antistatic precautions.
