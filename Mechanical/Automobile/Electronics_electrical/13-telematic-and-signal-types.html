Overview

Three loosely-related electronics-area topics. The Telematic Control Unit (TCU) is the in-car hub that connects mobile phone, navigation, and service-provider data — illustrated on the page with a call-routing example. The signal types table is the dealer-technician reference for analogue vs digital signal types (LF voltage, HF voltage, binary, frequency, PWM, bus, optical bus) with example uses and waveform shapes. The frequency & wavelength physics page gives the basic equation relating wavelength to frequency (λ = c/f) with worked examples for sound vs electromagnetic waves.

Working Principle

Telematic Control Unit (TCU)

Notebook (IMG 003605): "Mr SMITH → [Hands-free microphone] → [TCU = Telematic Control Unit] ← → [Telephone antenna] → Service provider Also connected to: Central Information Display (CID), Navigation in-car communication computer (CCC)"

The telematic control unit is the central node for all wireless and audio communications: - Hands-free microphone + speaker → driver's voice input/output for calls. - Mobile phone (Bluetooth pairing or embedded SIM) → cellular link. - Telephone antenna → radio frequency to/from the cell tower. - Service provider → BMW Assist / ConnectedDrive / equivalent. - CID (Central Information Display) → screen for caller info, navigation, etc. - CCC (Car Communication Computer) → handles MOST-bus audio/video integration.

So an incoming call routes: cell tower → antenna → TCU → mic/speakers + CID display. An outgoing voice command: mic → TCU → cellular link → service.

Transmission paths (the bigger picture)

Notebook (IMG 003605, bottom): "Transmission path: ├── Cable-based │ ├── Electrical (via cable) │ └── Optical (light via fibre-optic cable) └── Non-cable-based ├── Electromagnetic (radio waves) ├── Optical (infrared) └── Acoustic (sound, ultrasound)"

A taxonomy of all signal-transmission methods:

Path type Sub-type Examples
Cable-based — electrical Wires CAN, LIN, BSD, conventional 12V signals
Cable-based — optical Fibre cable MOST, byteflight (see chapter 11)
Non-cable — electromagnetic Radio waves Cellular (3G/4G), GPS, Bluetooth, WiFi, FM/AM radio, key fob
Non-cable — optical Infrared Old remote keys, some IR proximity sensors
Non-cable — acoustic Sound, ultrasound Parking sensors (ultrasonic ping + echo), some gesture controls

Signal types table

The complete reference:

Signal type Sub-type Examples Waveform

Analogue High-Frequency alternating voltage (HF) Mobile, radio control, video/TV signal control Sine wave (very short period)

Voltage range axis at the bottom of the table: 1 μV — 1 mV — 1 V.

Why so many signal types

Each is optimised for a different job: - LF analogue — perfectly captures the smooth amplitude of voice/music (audio reproduction). - HF analogue — used as the carrier for radio/TV transmission (frequency far above audio frequencies, modulated by the actual signal). - Binary — simplest digital: 0 or 1; on or off. - Frequency — encodes a value as a pulse rate (e.g., wheel-speed sensor outputs more pulses per second when wheel turns faster). - PWM — encodes a value as duty cycle (% on-time); used for power control (see transistor chapter). - Bus — packetised; multiple devices share one wire (see bus chapter). - Optical bus — bus packets encoded in light (fibre).

Frequency and wavelength

Notebook (IMG 003621): "Frequency & wavelength: Sound: 332 m/s Electromagnetic waves: 300,000,000 m/s

wavelength → λ = c/f

Where: c = light speed, f = frequency

λ = c / f = 3 × 10⁸ m/s / 100 MHz = 3 × 10⁸ / 100 × 10⁶ = 3 m"

The fundamental relation:

$$\lambda = \frac{c}{f}$$

Where: - $\lambda$ = wavelength (metres) - $c$ = wave propagation speed (m/s) — 332 m/s for sound, 3 × 10⁸ m/s for electromagnetic - $f$ = frequency (Hz)

Worked example: at 100 MHz (FM radio centre frequency): $$\lambda = \frac{3 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}}{100 \times 10^6 \text{ Hz}} = 3 \text{ m}$$

So an FM radio antenna designed for resonance is ~ 3/4 of a wavelength = ~ 75 cm — matches the typical car FM whip antenna length.

Sound speed vs light speed

Wave type Speed Example
Sound (air, 20 °C) 332 m/s Parking ultrasonic sensors (40 kHz → λ = 8.3 mm in air)
Electromagnetic (vacuum/air) 3 × 10⁸ m/s Radio, GPS, light (~ a million times faster than sound)

The huge speed difference is why acoustic methods are slow (parking sensor: 1 ms ping, 5 m round-trip in air = 14 ms response — limits update rate) and electromagnetic methods are fast (cellular round-trip in milliseconds across continents).

Detailed Component Notes

Why "Mr Smith" example

The notebook uses a worked example — Mr Smith calls the service provider via the car's TCU. This illustrates the routing chain rather than introducing new equations:

Mr Smith voice → mic → TCU → cellular antenna → cell tower → service provider
                                                              │
                                                              ▼
Service provider response → cell tower → antenna → TCU → speaker → Mr Smith hears

Why both "cable-based optical" AND "non-cable optical" are listed

  • Cable optical = light inside a fibre (MOST, byteflight); the light is confined by total internal reflection.
  • Non-cable optical = light through free space (infrared remote, optical sensors).

The mechanism is the same (photons), but the medium is different.

LF vs HF terminology

In communications: - LF (Low Frequency) generally means audio (20 Hz – 20 kHz). - HF (High Frequency) in the table likely means the carrier frequencies used for radio (MHz–GHz).

In radio terminology, "HF" specifically means 3–30 MHz, "VHF" 30–300 MHz, "UHF" 300 MHz – 3 GHz. Trainer is using "HF" loosely.

Why 100 MHz wavelength is 3 m

$\lambda \times f = c$. For light/radio waves $c = 3 \times 10^8$ m/s is constant. So higher frequency = shorter wavelength:

Frequency Wavelength
1 kHz 300 km (extremely long-wave AM)
1 MHz 300 m (medium-wave AM)
100 MHz 3 m (FM radio band)
1 GHz 30 cm (mobile, GPS, Bluetooth band)
24 GHz 1.25 cm (automotive radar band)
600 THz 500 nm (visible light, green)

This is why antennas are smaller at higher frequencies — the antenna size scales with wavelength.

Key Facts

  • TCU (Telematic Control Unit) = in-car hub for cellular + Bluetooth + audio + navigation routing.
  • Transmission paths: cable-based (electrical / optical) or non-cable (EM / IR / acoustic).
  • 7 signal types: LF analogue (audio), HF analogue (carrier), binary (digital on/off), frequency (pulse rate), PWM (duty cycle), bus (packetised), optical bus (light packets).
  • Voltage range on car signal lines: μV (sensors) to V (control voltages).
  • λ = c/f — the fundamental wave equation.
  • Sound speed = 332 m/s (air); EM speed = 3 × 10⁸ m/s.
  • FM radio at 100 MHz = wavelength 3 m → typical antenna length ~ 75 cm.
  • Higher frequency = shorter wavelength = smaller antenna.
