CAN, LIN, MOST, byteflight & BSD

Source images: IMG20250515002732, IMG20250515002739, IMG20250515002754, IMG20250515002800

Notebook date: 2/8/2013

Overview

The specific bus protocols a BMW uses. CAN (Controller Area Network) is the workhorse — multi-master, twisted-pair, used in two flavours (PT-CAN fast, K-CAN slow). LIN is a single-wire sub-bus driven by a master ECU — used for slow, local communication within a single function (HVAC stepper motors, AHL master/slave). MOST uses fibre-optic cables for high-speed audio/video. byteflight is the star-topology, fibre-optic bus developed specifically for airbag/SRS safety. BSD (Bit Serial Data) and F-CAN are smaller specialised buses. The page also covers arbitration (how multi-master priorities are resolved), identifiers, and optical fibre advantages.

Working Principle

CAN — Controller Area Network

Notebook (IMG 002732): "CAN (Controller Area Network): – Linear-shaped bus structure – Developed by Bosch & Intel in 1981 – It's faster because of two-way transfer of data – Used in: i) PT-CAN (fast) ii) K-CAN (slow) – The speed is varied for communication (speed depends on processor) – We can add & remove control units (like RAM chips) – Transmission rates vary from 10 kbit/s to 1 Mbit/s – (Comfort) K-bus: 100 kbit/sec – (Safety) PT-bus: 500 kbit/sec"

CAN spec key points: - Bosch + Intel, 1981 — Bosch invented the protocol; Intel made the first chips. - Linear topology — single twisted pair runs the length of the car, every ECU taps in. - Two-way (bidirectional) — both wires used for differential signalling. - Multi-master — any node can initiate a message any time. - Bit-rate: 10 kbit/s (very slow comfort) up to 1 Mbit/s (max for high-speed CAN). - BMW splits into fast PT-CAN (500 kbit/s, powertrain) and slow K-CAN (100 kbit/s, body/comfort).

Multi-master arbitration

Notebook (IMG 002732 bottom + IMG 002739 top): "Multi-master bus: – Anyone can be master – Subscriber reads transmitter's message

Arbitration: – Priority is set for different messages – Messages sent by master as per priorities

Identifier: – Identifier code is given in message"

Arbitration mechanism (CAN's clever trick): - Each message starts with its identifier (a number) — lower number = higher priority. - If two nodes start sending at the same time, they bitwise-compare their identifiers. - The node with the lower identifier wins automatically (dominant bit overrides recessive) → the loser sees its bit overwritten and immediately stops. - The winner finishes the message; the loser retries when the bus is idle.

So priority is baked into the message ID, no separate arbitration round needed. Critical messages (airbag, engine emergency) get low IDs; non-critical (radio, infotainment) get high IDs.

F-CAN

Notebook (IMG 002739): "F-CAN: Sensors – One-way communication – Just provide I/P"

A simplified CAN variant where sensors only send (one-way), reducing complexity. Used for some sensor sub-networks.

LIN — Local Interconnect Network

Notebook (IMG 002739 bottom): "Local interconnect Network (LIN): – Sub-bus system – Adaptive headlights (AHL master) → [Xenon (slave), Xenon (slave)] – Steering – Transmission rate 19.2 kbit/s"

LIN characteristics: - Single-wire (uses chassis as ground reference) — even cheaper than CAN. - Slow — 19.2 kbit/s max. - Master-slave — one ECU is master, up to 15 slaves. - Sub-bus — bridges into a bigger CAN bus via the master.

Where LIN is used: - HVAC stepper motors — flap actuators, blower control. - AHL (Adaptive Headlights) — main module controls slave Xenon modules at each headlight. - Steering column — wiper switch, stalks, multifunction wheel buttons. - Window control — door modules. - Any application where comm speed isn't critical and the device is local to one car area.

BSD — Bit Serial Data

Notebook (IMG 002754 top): "BSD (Bit Serial Data Interface): – Speed of transmission 9.6 kbit/s – Generator [GNN] → DME → IBS (Intelligent battery sensor)"

Even slower than LIN. Used for the alternator → DME → battery sensor communication chain. Very low data rate but enough for charging-system status messages.

MOST and optic-fibre communication

Notebook (IMG 002754 middle): "Optic fibre communication: – Signal is transferred at the speed of light – Data transmission in the form of light waves

Advantages: ① High speed of data transmission ② Insensitive [to] electromagnetic influences ③ Lighter than copper ④ Less space

[Diagram: LED → glass fibre core → photoelectric cell] – Total internal reflection – Fibre coating – Fibre core (made up of special fibre)

Light's loss of power — attenuation: Light intensity becomes less if length of wire is more. So there could be loss of communication."

MOST (Media Oriented Systems Transport) is BMW's high-speed fibre-optic bus, used for in-car audio/video: - LED transmitter + photoelectric receiver at each node. - Plastic optical fibre (POF) cable — about the diameter of a pencil lead. - Total internal reflection keeps light bouncing inside the core. - Lossless against EM interference (ignition coils, alternator, etc.) because light isn't affected. - Lighter than copper bundles — important on a 7-series-size car with lots of audio gear.

Speed: 25 Mbit/s (MOST25) for early versions; later MOST50 and MOST150 with much higher bandwidth.

byteflight — safety-grade star bus

Notebook (IMG 002800): "In byteflight bus system: star bus system – Plastic optic fibres cable used for transmission of signal – Data transmission: 10 Mbit/sec"

byteflight is the safety-grade fibre-optic bus that BMW developed specifically for airbag/SRS systems: - Star topology — every node has its own dedicated fibre to the central gateway → no single-point failure on a shared bus. - Time-triggered — slots are pre-allocated, so airbag firing commands always arrive deterministically (no waiting for bus availability). - Fibre-optic — immune to EMI; very fast. - Standard speed: 10 Mbit/s.

This was the predecessor to FlexRay, which became the safety bus standard for later BMWs and other manufacturers.

   ECU B:  0   0   1   0   1   0   1   1   1   1   1   ← ID 0x059

                          │
                  At bit 6, A sends 1 (recessive) but B sends 0 (dominant)

Detailed Component Notes

Why CAN's dominant/recessive trick works

The CAN bus has an idle state (both wires at the same voltage) called "recessive" (binary 1). When any node pulls the wires apart, it's "dominant" (binary 0). Dominant always wins over recessive — if any node sends a 0, the bus reads 0 regardless of how many nodes send 1.

This wired-AND behaviour is what makes arbitration automatic: the node with the lower ID has more 0s in its identifier → wins automatically when both transmit.

Why LIN is so slow

LIN runs at 19.2 kbit/s because: - Single-wire signalling needs more bit time for the receiver to lock onto the signal cleanly. - Used for slow applications (HVAC flaps move once a second; window switches once a button-press). - Cheap silicon — small/inexpensive transceiver IC.

Why MOST uses fibre instead of copper

  1. EMI immunity — alternator + ignition + EPS motors throw off massive EM noise. Light is unaffected.
  2. Bandwidth — 25-150 Mbit/s for audio/video; copper Ethernet only just caught up.
  3. Weight — POF is much lighter than coax or twisted pair for the same bandwidth.
  4. No ground loops — fibre electrically isolates nodes → no chassis-ground noise.

Why byteflight uses star topology

For airbag safety, a single bus failure must NOT take down all the airbag modules. Star topology means each module has its own dedicated fibre to the central control — one fibre cut only kills one module, not the whole system. The redundancy is structural, not just functional.

Modern context — FlexRay and CAN-FD

After ~ 2010, BMW (and other premium manufacturers) moved safety-critical buses from byteflight to FlexRay (10 Mbit/s, time-triggered, star or bus topology). For high-bandwidth applications, CAN-FD (CAN Flexible Data, up to 5 Mbit/s) extended classic CAN. Automotive Ethernet (100 Mbit/s, 1 Gbit/s) is the current state of the art.

Key Facts

  • CAN = Bosch+Intel 1981; multi-master, twisted-pair, linear topology. 10 kbit/s – 1 Mbit/s.
  • PT-CAN (fast, 500 kbit/s, powertrain) and K-CAN (slow, 100 kbit/s, body) — two BMW CAN buses.
  • Arbitration via message identifier — lower ID = higher priority; dominant 0 always wins over recessive 1.
  • F-CAN = simplified one-way CAN for sensors.
  • LIN = single-wire master-slave sub-bus, 19.2 kbit/s. Used for HVAC, AHL, steering column.
  • BSD = Bit Serial Data, 9.6 kbit/s. Connects alternator-DME-IBS.
  • MOST = fibre-optic bus, 25 Mbit/s, for audio/video.
  • byteflight = fibre-optic, star topology, 10 Mbit/s, for airbag SRS safety.
  • Fibre advantages: high speed, EMI-immune, lighter, less space.
  • Fibre limitation: light attenuation with distance.
  • Modern replacements: FlexRay (safety), CAN-FD (general), automotive Ethernet (high-bandwidth).
