Airbags

Gas Generators & Airbag Control Units

Source images: IMG20250514181747 *(bottom portion — pyrotechnic gas generator)*, IMG20250514181751, IMG20250514181803, IMG20250514181807

Overview

Four pages that cover the hardware that makes the airbag actually deploy. There are two gas-generation technologies: the older pyrotechnic gas generator (a small explosive charge that burns a solid propellant, generating 30–50 litres of nitrogen gas in milliseconds) and the newer cold gas generator (a sealed cylinder of pressurised argon + helium, released by a small pyrotechnic valve). The trainer then walks through the airbag control unit (ACSM / MRS) — its inputs (seat occupancy, belt buckle status, side-impact sensors), its outputs (airbag igniters, belt tensioners), and the BMW evolution from MRS1 → MRS2 → MRS3 that progressively added more sensors, more airbag channels, and integration with the DME and Safety Battery Terminal. The final note compares two control-unit suppliers — Bosch and Temic — which differ in how they handle the seat-occupancy interlock for side airbags.

Working Principle

6-step ignition sequence (numbered on the source page):

# Step What happens
① Electric pulse ACSM sends 1–2 A pulse to the squib igniter (electrical "match")
② Ignition of propellant chain Squib heats the initiator powder, which ignites the main propellant
③ Burn-up of propellant Solid propellant rapidly decomposes — produces N₂ gas
④ Generation of propellant gas Hot nitrogen rushes outward from the combustion chamber
⑤ Purification of metal little [particles in filter] Filter/mesh catches solid combustion residue (mainly Na₂O and metal oxides) before they reach the bag
⑥ Escape of gas into airbag Clean, cooled gas exits through holes in the housing and inflates the bag

The whole sequence takes 20–30 milliseconds end-to-end — fast enough that the bag is fully inflated by the time the occupant has moved ~30 cm forward.

Why filter the gas?

Two reasons: 1. Particulate removal — burning sodium azide (the traditional propellant) leaves behind sodium oxide ash, which would tear the nylon bag fabric and damage the occupant's lungs if inhaled. 2. Cooling — the combustion gas is initially over 1000 °C. The filter mesh has significant thermal mass that absorbs heat as the gas passes through, cooling the gas to a few hundred °C before it enters the bag.

Cold gas generator

The page draws a separate diagram of the cold gas generator — a pressurised metal cylinder filled with inert gas mixture (95 % argon, 5 % helium):

Key features: - No combustion, no heat, no particulates. Just compressed inert gas released by a small pyro charge that bursts a sealing disk. - Argon 95 % / Helium 5 % — the helium adds molecular diffusivity (escapes the bag through the vent holes faster, giving the right deflation curve). - Cleaner than pyrotechnic — no sodium-azide concerns, easier to recycle the spent unit. - More complex to manufacture — requires a pressure-rated cylinder and a precision burst-disk.

Cold gas generators are the modern standard for side airbags and curtain airbags because they deploy faster and produce less heat — important when the bag is firing close to the occupant's head.

The page lists Siemens 2A and 2B as the supplier-named control units that orchestrate the airbag system. Functions:

Input Source
Belt latch monitoring Switch in each seatbelt buckle
Belt tensioner trigger Output to the pyrotechnic belt tensioner
Airbag igniter fire Output to each airbag's squib
Side-impact sensor inputs From doors / B-pillar satellites
Seat occupancy From a pressure pad / weight sensor in each seat

Belt Control Unit (BAE) — pyrotechnic tensioner + seat-occupancy recognition

"★ Belt control unit BAE: Pyrotechnique is used to tighten belt. & also provide 'SEAT OCCUPY RECOGNITION.' — if person is not wearing seatbelt, then for safety airbag opens but lower threshold current given & airbag fills lesser than other [than usual]." — IMG 181751.

BAE is the dedicated belt-control unit. Two functions:

  1. Pyrotechnic belt tensioner — at the moment of impact, a small pyro charge pulls the seatbelt taut by ~10 cm in a few ms. This eliminates any slack between the occupant and the belt, so the belt loads up immediately on the body instead of allowing 20+ cm of free fall toward the dashboard before tightening.

  2. Seat-occupancy recognition — a sensor in the seat detects whether someone is sitting there. If no one is in the seat, the corresponding airbag doesn't fire. If someone is in the seat but not belted: - The airbag still fires (for some safety), - But at lower power — the ACSM commands a lower igniter current → gentler deployment → less risk of injuring an out-of-position occupant who's already moving toward the dashboard.

This adaptive airbag deployment is mandated by US regulations (FMVSS 208) and is standard in modern BMWs.

ZAE2 — post-crash response

"★ ZAE2: ★ It activates hazard lights, car internal lights [after crash]." — IMG 181803.

ZAE2 is the airbag control unit's post-crash output module. Once the airbags have fired and the impact event is over, ZAE2:

  • Turns on the hazard lights (warns other drivers).
  • Turns on the cabin interior lights (helps occupants find seatbelt releases and helps rescuers see inside).
  • May also unlock the doors (in modern variants) — many ACSM designs do this automatically so rescuers don't have to break windows.

Side annotation on the page: "Control unit: 30 [pin] / 50 pin plug." The ZAE2's main wiring connector has up to 50 pins — one of the largest connectors in the car after the DME.

MRS2 bundled all the front + side + head airbag channels into one ECU, and added integration with the DME (fuel pump cut-off on impact) and the SBK (Safety Battery Terminal pyrotechnic disconnect — see file 10-power-supply-architecture-and-safety-battery-terminal). Two-stage front airbags (called "2-lever" on the page) became standard — a smaller first inflation pulse for moderate impacts, a larger second pulse for severe impacts.

MRS3 added severity-graded ignition — the same impact event might fire only the seatbelt tensioner (mild), or seatbelt + front airbag (moderate), or seatbelt + front + side + head airbags + SBK + fuel pump cut-off (severe). The DME provides input data (engine speed, vehicle speed, brake state) so the MRS3 can distinguish a deliberate hard stop from an actual impact.

Bosch vs Temic — supplier-specific differences

"Bosch: ★ Plug connection 2 pin, side airbag release with seat occupied by passenger. Temic: ★ Plug connection 3 pin, side airbag release independent of seat occupied by passenger." — IMG 181807.

Two control-unit suppliers, with different design choices:

Attribute Bosch Temic
Side airbag plug 2 pin 3 pin
Side airbag fires when seat is empty? No — requires occupancy confirmation Yes — fires regardless of occupancy

The Temic logic is the more conservative ("fire anyway, just in case the occupancy sensor is wrong"). The Bosch logic is the more efficient ("save a deployment when there's no occupant to protect"). Both approaches are used in BMW vehicles depending on the year and model. The 3-pin Temic plug is the easy diagnostic giveaway in the workshop.

Key Facts

  • Two gas-generation technologies: pyrotechnic (older, combustion-based) and cold gas (newer, compressed inert gas).
  • Cold gas mixture: 95 % Argon + 5 % Helium. Helium added for the right diffusivity through the bag's vent holes.
  • The pyrotechnic generator runs a 6-step sequence in ~20-30 ms: electric pulse → ignite → burn → produce gas → filter particulates → bag inflated.
  • Sodium azide is the traditional pyrotechnic propellant; modern designs use guanidine nitrate (cleaner).
  • The BAE belt control unit handles both the pyrotechnic seatbelt tensioner and seat-occupancy-aware airbag deployment.
  • Unbelted occupants get a lower-power airbag deployment than belted occupants — gentler in case the occupant is already out of position.
  • ZAE2 is the post-crash output module — activates hazard + interior lights after airbag deployment.
  • BMW airbag control evolved MRS1 → MRS2 → MRS3: increasing channels, DME integration, severity-graded deployment.
  • Bosch (2-pin) fires side airbags only when the seat is occupied; Temic (3-pin) fires regardless. Different supplier philosophies.
