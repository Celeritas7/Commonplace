Belt Tensioners & Belt Force Limiters

Source images: IMG20250514181820, IMG20250514181826, IMG20250514181831, IMG20250514181839

Overview

Four pages covering the belt side of the restraint system — the hardware that grips and pulls the seatbelt taut at the moment of impact, plus the force limiter that deliberately lets the belt slacken in a controlled way once peak loads are reached (so the belt doesn't itself become an injury source). The page set covers two tensioner technologies — mechanical (spring-driven, single-shot, colour-indicator status) and pyrotechnic (gas-driven, only fires above a defined impact threshold) — plus a specialised convertible-only design called the Seat-Integrated Belt System (SGS) where all belt anchor points are on the seat frame instead of the B-pillar. The final concept is the pyrotechnic belt force limiter, a controlled-release mechanism that allows progressive belt slackening to spread the deceleration force over more milliseconds.

Working Principle

Why tension a seatbelt at impact?

A worn or even normally fitted seatbelt has ~5-15 cm of slack in steady-state operation — the wearer leaning forward, the spring-loaded reel paying out a few extra cm under shoulder pressure, the belt webbing's small amount of elastic stretch. In an impact, the occupant moves forward at full speed for the first ~30 ms before the belt loads up against their body.

A pretensioner eliminates that delay. The instant the airbag control unit detects an impact, the pretensioner pulls the belt taut by ~10 cm in 5-10 ms, well before the occupant has begun moving significantly. The belt loads up immediately, so the occupant decelerates with the car instead of after a delay.

Mechanical belt tensioner

"Mechanical belt tensioner: — It fixes the helical spring & hence the belt is movable & get locked. — Red — deactivated — Green — primed — Should be replaced once they have been released." — IMG 181831.

The mechanical pretensioner uses a pre-loaded helical spring held in compression by a release mechanism. When the airbag control unit fires the release:

  1. The spring is released.
  2. It drives a rack-and-pinion (or pulley) that wraps onto the belt reel.
  3. The belt is yanked taut in milliseconds.

Status indicator window on the pretensioner housing:

Window colour Meaning
Green Spring primed; system ready to fire
Red Spring released; one-shot consumed

Single-shot consumable — once the spring has fired, the entire pretensioner unit must be replaced. The spring cannot be re-cocked in the field; it's loaded under factory-controlled conditions and the housing is sealed.

Pyrotechnic belt tensioner

"Pyrotechnic belt tensioner: — It works only at certain impact level. — Airbags opens only at certain impact level." — IMG 181831.

The pyrotechnic pretensioner replaces the mechanical spring with a pyrotechnic gas charge (similar to the airbag's gas generator, but smaller). Operating principle:

  1. The airbag control unit fires an electric pulse to the pretensioner's squib.
  2. The squib ignites a small propellant charge.
  3. Gas pressure drives a piston that wraps the belt reel.
  4. Belt taut in 5-10 ms.

Two differences from the mechanical version: - Higher force — pyrotechnic gas can deliver more peak pull than a spring. - Threshold-controlled — the airbag control unit fires the pretensioner only above a defined impact severity. Below that threshold, the seatbelt's normal inertia reel handles things. Above it, the pretensioner fires alongside the airbag.

The page notes: "Airbags opens only at certain impact level" — the same threshold logic applies to airbag deployment. Pretensioners and airbags are typically wired to fire together on collisions above the threshold, but with the pretensioner firing slightly earlier (so the belt loads up before the bag inflates).

Seat-Integrated Belt System (SGS)

"Seat integrated belt system SGS: [margin: Convertible car as there is no B-pillar] — All seat belt points attached to seat frame. — Belt adjusted as per size. — No submarining effect. — Seat belt gets adjusted as per seat height [as it is] adjusted." — IMG 181839.

In a standard saloon, the upper belt anchor (the shoulder loop the diagonal belt feeds through) is mounted on the B-pillar. In a convertible, there is no B-pillar — so the upper anchor has to live somewhere else, typically on the rear of the seat back.

The SGS (Seat-Integrated Belt System) mounts all three belt anchor points — lap anchor, buckle anchor, and shoulder anchor — on the seat frame itself:

   Saloon (B-pillar mounted):              SGS (seat-frame mounted):
   ───────────────────────────              ────────────────────────

                B-pillar                          Headrest
                  ╲                                  │
                   ╲ shoulder                        │ shoulder
                    ╲ anchor                         ╲ anchor
                     ╲                              ──╲────
                      ╲                            ┌─ ╲──┐
                       ╲                           │   ╲ │
                        ╲                          │seat │
                        ┌─┐                        │frame│
                  ┌─────│ │                        │     │
                  │seat │ │ lap anchor          ┌──│     │── lap anchor
                  │     │ │ (floor)             │  │     │   (seat frame)
                  └─────└─┘                     │  └─────┘
                          buckle (floor)        │  buckle (seat frame)

Three benefits the page calls out:

  1. Belt adjusted as per size — the shoulder anchor is fixed relative to the seat, so it stays at the right height for the occupant no matter how the seat is positioned.
  2. No submarining effect — because the lap anchor moves with the seat, the geometry of the lap belt relative to the occupant's pelvis stays constant (unlike a B-pillar-mounted shoulder + floor-mounted lap belt, where the geometry changes with seat position).
  3. Seat belt gets adjusted as per seat height — when the driver raises or lowers the seat, the entire belt assembly moves with them. No need for a separate "belt height adjuster" sliding on the B-pillar.

Belt force limiter

"Belt force limiter: — Keep injuries low which are caused by belt itself. — Controlled slackening of belt." — IMG 181839.

A surprising fact: the seatbelt itself can cause injuries at high deceleration. Specifically:

  • Compression of the clavicle under the shoulder belt → broken collarbone.
  • Compression of the lower ribs under the diagonal belt → broken ribs.
  • Compression of the soft abdomen under the lap belt → internal organ damage.

The belt force limiter is a mechanism that deliberately lets the belt slacken in a controlled way once the peak load reaches a defined threshold. The occupant continues to be restrained, but the peak force is capped — the deceleration is spread over more milliseconds, lowering the peak force on the body.

Pyrotechnic belt force limiter

"Pyrotechnic belt force limiter: — Locking mechanism. — Gas generator: during impact the belt tensioner allows the passenger to go evenly forward & then the tensioner applies tension." — IMG 181839.

A pyrotechnic-actuated version of the force limiter. Operating sequence:

   t = 0 ms          Impact detected.
                     Pretensioner fires (pulls belt taut, ~10 cm).
                     Force limiter is in LOCKED state.

   t = 10-30 ms      Belt loads up against occupant.
                     Peak belt force rises rapidly.

   t = 30-50 ms      Belt force reaches limiter threshold.
                     Force limiter is COMMANDED TO RELEASE.
                     Pyro charge unlocks the spool clutch.
                     Spool now pays out belt at a CONTROLLED rate.

   t = 50-100 ms     Occupant continues forward by a few cm
                     under reduced (limited) belt force.
                     Airbag is now inflated and catches the head.

   t = 100+ ms       Crash event over.
                     Belt remains in extended position.
                     Spool stays unlocked until reset by service.

The result is an engineered deceleration profile — sharp initial restraint, then a controlled "give" that drops the peak force from (say) 8 kN to 4 kN, well below the threshold for clavicle fracture.

Cross-reference — relation to the airbag's job

The belt force limiter is the counterpart to the airbag. The airbag prevents the head from striking hard surfaces; the force limiter prevents the belt from doing the same. Together they form a complete decelaration system with both surfaces moving in coordinated ways: the airbag inflates into the occupant's face/chest, the belt force-limits away from the occupant's chest, and the net deceleration profile is matched to human injury tolerance curves.

Process Flow — Full belt-side restraint sequence during impact

   t = 0 ms       Pre-crash. Belt has normal slack. Reel in passive state.
                  Mechanical pretensioner GREEN (primed). Force limiter LOCKED.

   t ≈ 5-15 ms    Impact detected by MRS4. Pretensioner squib fires.
                  Belt pulled taut by ~10 cm.
                  Belt now loaded firmly against occupant's chest + lap.
                  Mechanical pretensioner status → RED.

   t ≈ 30 ms      Airbag fully inflated (separate sequence — file 02).
                  Belt + airbag now both engaged with occupant.

   t ≈ 30-50 ms   Belt force rises sharply as occupant decelerates.
                  Force limiter threshold reached → pyro release fires.
                  Spool clutch unlocks; belt pays out under controlled load.

   t ≈ 100 ms     Crash event ends.
                  Occupant has decelerated with peak belt force capped.
                  Airbag deflating; belt extended; spool unlocked.

   Post-crash     Belt force limiter and pretensioner both spent.
                  Both must be replaced as service items.
                  ZAE2 module activates hazards, interior lights, door unlock.

Key Facts

  • A normal seatbelt has ~5-15 cm slack in steady-state — the pretensioner removes this in 5-10 ms at impact.
  • Mechanical pretensioner: spring-driven, single-shot, red/green status window.
  • Pyrotechnic pretensioner: gas-driven, fires only above a defined impact threshold, higher force capability.
  • Both pretensioners are one-shot consumables — replace after any deployment.
  • SGS mounts all three belt anchors on the seat frame; required in convertibles (no B-pillar) and provides geometry-stable restraint in any seat position.
  • Belt force limiter caps peak belt force so the belt itself doesn't cause clavicle / rib / abdominal injuries.
  • Pyrotechnic belt force limiter uses a gas charge to unlock the spool clutch after the pretensioner has fired, allowing controlled belt payout.
  • The belt force limiter and the airbag work as a coordinated pair — the airbag catches the head, the belt limiter caps the chest/pelvis load.
