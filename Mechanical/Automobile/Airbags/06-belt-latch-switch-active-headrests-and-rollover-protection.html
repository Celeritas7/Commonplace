Airbags

Belt Latch Switch, Active Headrests & Roll-Over Protection

Source images: IMG20250514181843, IMG20250514181856

Overview

Two pages that close the Airbags module with the remaining restraint-system elements: the belt latch switch (a two-wire Hall sensor in each seatbelt buckle that tells the airbag control unit whether the belt is fastened), active headrests (pyrotechnically-actuated headrest cushions that jump 40–60 mm forward in milliseconds to catch the head before whiplash develops), and roll-over protection — a 5-step pyrotechnic safety bar that pops up behind the rear seats of a convertible if the car begins to tip past the angle from which it can recover.

Working Principle

Belt deflector mounting

"★ Belt deflector mounting" — IMG 181843 (heading only).

The page lists "belt deflector mounting" as a section heading with no further detail. The belt deflector is the D-shaped plastic ring at the top of the B-pillar (or seat-back, in SGS designs) that the diagonal belt feeds through. Its job is to: - Route the belt from the inertia reel below to the wearer's shoulder. - Adjust the belt height to match the occupant (some have a slider mechanism for tall-vs-short drivers). - Survive the pretensioner / force-limiter loads without tearing out of its mounting.

Mounting must be structurally rigid because the belt's full restraint force routes through this single anchor point. A torn deflector mounting is one of the failure modes that defeats the entire belt system.

Belt latch switch — the Hall sensor

"★ Belt latch switch: — Two wire hall sensor. — Detects whether driver &/or passenger have fastened seat belts. — Belt warning. — Influences airbag release." — IMG 181843.

Inside each seatbelt buckle is a small Hall-effect sensor that detects whether the belt's tongue is fully inserted and locked. The sensor type:

  • Two-wire Hall sensor — power and signal share one of the wires; ground is the other. Internal electronics modulate the supply current as the signal output.
  • Detects a small magnet embedded in the metal tongue. When the tongue is inserted, the magnet enters the sensor's detection zone → output state changes.

Three downstream consumers of the latch state:

Consumer Why it cares
Instrument cluster (belt warning lamp + chime) If the seat is occupied (OC3 says so) but the belt is not latched, the cluster shows the seatbelt-icon warning lamp and sounds the belt-not-buckled chime
MRS4 control unit (deployment decision) Influences airbag deployment: unbelted occupant → reduced-power airbag (file 03 — BAE adaptive deployment)
Energy management (some models) Some BMWs disable specific features (seat ventilation, certain comfort modes) when the seatbelt is not latched, as a soft enforcement nudge

Active headrests — anti-whiplash protection

"★ Active headrests: — During impact it adjusts the mechanism of headrests & moves it forward to reduce the whiplash movement of head. — Active headrest activated within milliseconds by means of gas generator with firing pellet. — Diff adjustments: distance, depending on the setting of headrest (40-60 mm)." — IMG 181843.

The whiplash problem

In a rear-end impact, the seat pushes the occupant's torso forward (it's directly behind them), but the head has nothing pushing it — it lags behind the torso for tens of milliseconds, hyperextending the neck backward. Then, as the torso bounces forward, the head whips forward too, hyperflexing the neck. This rapid forward-then-backward neck oscillation is "whiplash" — the most common injury in rear-end crashes.

The standard countermeasure is a headrest positioned close to the back of the head, ideally within 5 cm at the occupant's normal seating position. But two problems:

  1. Many drivers position the headrest poorly (too far back, too low).
  2. Even with good positioning, the 5-cm gap means the head still moves through 5 cm of free space before being caught.

The active-headrest solution

An active headrest uses a gas generator with a firing pellet (smaller cousin of the airbag's pyrotechnic generator) to physically push the headrest cushion forward 40–60 mm in milliseconds at the moment of rear-impact:

   Before rear impact:                  Active headrest fired:
   ──────────────────                   ──────────────────────

   Headrest fixed at         →       Cushion jumps forward
   normal driver position             40-60 mm in 5-15 ms
                                      (closes the gap before
                                       head whips backward)

       │                                  │
       │  ┌─────┐                         │   ┌─────┐
       │  │head │                         │   │head │
       │  │     │                         │   │     │
       │  │     │       gap = 5 cm        │   │     │  gap = 0
       │  │     │       ─────►            │   │  ←──│  (headrest
       │  │  ┌──┴──┐                      │   │  ┌──┴──┐ catches head)
       │  │  │head │                      │   │  │head │
       │  │  │rest │ ← cushion             │   │  │rest │ ← cushion
       │  │  └─────┘                      │   │  └─────┘   pushed forward
       │  │                               │   │

By closing the gap before the head has time to lag, the active headrest eliminates the backward hyperextension phase entirely.

Adjustable distance (40–60 mm)

The page notes: "Diff[erent] adjustments: distance, depending on the setting of headrest (40–60 mm)."

The pyrotechnic stroke is calibrated to the headrest's current position. If the driver has set the headrest higher / further back (more gap to close), the actuator delivers a larger stroke (up to 60 mm). If the headrest is already close (smaller gap), a 40 mm stroke is sufficient. The MRS4 reads the headrest's current position (from a position sensor) and selects the appropriate deployment energy.

Roll-over protection in convertibles

"Roll-over protection: In convertible: ① Two protection bars mounted behind rear seat. ② Protective bar placed into support structure. ③ Crash actuator lock is released, protective bar extends. ④ Hold-back ratchet [holds it in place]. ⑤ Released by diagnostic interface [for reset]." — IMG 181856.

The roll-over hazard

A convertible has no roof to protect occupants if the car tips over. In a roll-over event, the occupants' heads can strike the road surface directly. Hardtop cars have the A-pillar and roof skin to absorb this; convertibles need a separate solution.

The deployable roll bar

Two roll bars sit folded down behind the rear seats of the convertible at rest, invisible to occupants. Each bar is held compressed against a pre-loaded spring by a crash actuator lock.

      Bar can only move UP; it cannot retract.

Why a pop-up bar instead of a fixed bar?

A fixed roll bar (always visible) is sometimes called a "sport bar" and is fine for safety but ruins the visual line of a convertible (the whole point of a convertible is the unobstructed silhouette). The pop-up mechanism stays hidden 99.9% of the car's life and only deploys when actually needed.

Other roll-over indicators

The MRS reads multiple signals to decide on roll-over deployment:

Signal Reading
Lateral g Sustained > 1 g
Vehicle tilt angle > 50–60° from vertical
Wheel-speed asymmetry Inside wheels lifting, outside loaded
Yaw rate Rotating around the longitudinal axis

The MRS fuses these into a single roll-over confidence score. Above the threshold, the actuator fires.

Key Facts

  • The belt latch switch is a two-wire Hall sensor in each seatbelt buckle.
  • It feeds three consumers: the warning lamp + chime, the airbag control unit (deployment decision), and energy management (some models).
  • Active headrests prevent whiplash by jumping 40-60 mm forward in milliseconds at rear impact.
  • The headrest's deployment energy is calibrated to its current position — wider gap → longer stroke.
  • Roll-over protection is convertible-specific — two pop-up bars behind the rear seats, deployed by a pyrotechnic-released spring.
  • The roll bar's hold-back ratchet is one-way — bar stays up after deployment, can only be reset by dealer diagnostic interface (ISTA).
  • Roll-over deployment is triggered by a fusion of lateral g + tilt angle + wheel-speed asymmetry + yaw rate.

The trainer's specification matches the older variant; modern BMWs may use either depending on the year.

  • [?] "Released by diagnostic interface" on the roll-over protection bar — confirmed correct. Most BMW convertible roll bars cannot be retracted manually after deployment because the ratchet is intentionally designed as a one-way device; reset requires the dealer's tester.

The trainer's body text doesn't separate these.
