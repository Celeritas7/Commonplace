Airbags

Active Safety, Crash Tests & Strong Occupant Cell

Source images: IMG20250514181719, IMG20250514181725, IMG20250514181732 *(top portion only — airbag history continues in file 02)*

Overview

The Airbag module begins by framing airbags inside a larger safety system rather than treating them as a standalone gadget. BMW's umbrella term is FIRST — Full Integrated Road Safety Technology — which has three protection targets (passengers, road users, other cars) and two layers: active prevention (ABS, DSC, EDC) that try to avoid the crash, and passive safety (airbags, seatbelts) that protect occupants during the crash if prevention fails. The trainer then turns to vehicle structure — the strong occupant cell with cross-member reinforcement, the curves-and-no-flat-surfaces principle for third-party protection — and the international crash-test protocols that quantify how well a car meets each of those goals (Euro-NCAP, US-NCAP, A2T, US-LINCAP, IIHS).

Working Principle

FIRST — Full Integrated Road Safety Technology

"★ FIRST (Full Integrated Road Safety Technology): ① Passengers in cars ② Occupants on road ③ Other cars." — IMG 181719.

BMW's umbrella term for all safety features in the car, organised by whom the feature protects:

# Protection target Examples
① Passengers in cars (the car's own occupants) Seatbelts, airbags, strong occupant cell, crumple zones
② Occupants on road (pedestrians, cyclists, motorcyclists outside the car) Pedestrian airbag (active hood), curved bumper, energy-absorbing front-clip design
③ Other cars (occupants of vehicles in the collision) Compatible crash structure, defined energy-absorption profile so a heavier BMW doesn't pulverise a lighter sub-compact

This three-way framing is itself part of the dealer-training story — historically, "safety" meant only protecting the car's own occupants. The shift toward also protecting pedestrians and other vehicles' occupants is what makes the system "integrated."

Active prevention vs passive safety

"Accidents prevention: ① ABS ② DSC (Dynamic Stability Control) ③ EDC (Electronic Damping Control)

Passive safety: — Airbags — Seat belts." — IMG 181719.

Layer When it acts Goal Examples
Active prevention Before the crash Avoid the crash entirely ABS, DSC, EDC, brake assist, lane-keeping, AEB
Passive safety During and after the crash Reduce harm if the crash happens anyway Airbags, seatbelts, crumple zones, occupant cell

The page lists three active systems specifically: - ABS (Anti-lock Braking System) — prevents wheel lock-up under hard braking, preserves steering control. - DSC (Dynamic Stability Control) — BMW's term for ESP/ESC; selectively brakes individual wheels to correct understeer/oversteer. - EDC (Electronic Damping Control) — adaptive damper control; varies suspension stiffness on the fly to keep tyres planted on the road.

"★ Strong occupants cell" with a cross-member annotation pointing to the diagonal bracing across the roof and floor.

Two design principles that go with the strong cell:

  1. Strong cell — the passenger compartment uses high-strength steel pillars and cross-members that resist deformation. In a collision, the front-clip and rear-clip "crumple zones" deform predictably to absorb energy; the cell itself stays rigid, preserving survival space for occupants.

  2. Third-party protection: curves in car design & less amount of flat surfaces. A sharp-cornered, flat-fronted car (think 1970s American sedan) is brutal to anything it hits — a pedestrian struck by a flat hood goes straight up into the bonnet, then back down into the road. A curved front clip with sloped surfaces deflects pedestrians up and over the car instead, which both reduces the impact energy transferred and avoids the secondary head-strike on the windshield base.

Reference braking distance — the 50 km/h baseline

"50 km/hr — 10 m is braking distance which is harmless to occupants." — IMG 181725.

A reference number the trainer uses repeatedly across the module: at 50 km/h (urban speed limit in much of Europe and India), a 10-metre braking distance brings the car to a halt with no occupant injury. This is the design baseline for both active prevention (ABS must deliver this distance) and passive safety (airbags must protect at speeds above this — below 50 km/h, seatbelt alone is sufficient).

Crash test protocols — front impact

The page lists the three principal frontal crash-test protocols:

Protocol Test setup Used for
Euro-NCAP Car hits a fixed solid wall fully (100 % overlap) — with the wall faced in deformable aluminium honeycomb European 5-star rating
US-NCAP Car hits a solid wall fully (100 % overlap, rigid wall) US NHTSA 5-star rating
A2T test Car hits a solid wall with 40 % overlap Specifically for claim settlements — speed, repairable work assessment

The Euro-NCAP and US-NCAP differ in whether the wall is rigid or deformable, but both are full-width impacts. The A2T 40 %-overlap test is for insurance / repair-cost assessment — it produces the kind of asymmetric, partial-front damage that's typical of real-world urban collisions, not the symmetric flat-wall hit of NCAP.

Crash test protocols — side impact

"★ Side impact: — Euro-NCAP: Deformable honeycomb [hits] Al [aluminium-faced car door] — Euro-NCAP [pole side impact]: vehicle moves upright & strikes pole — US-LINCAP: 62 km/hr at angle of 27° strikes [the] car." — IMG 181725.

Three side-impact protocols:

  1. Euro-NCAP side impact — a deformable-faced trolley hits the stationary test car broadside.
  2. Euro-NCAP pole test — the car is driven sideways at low speed into a rigid pole (simulates skidding into a lamppost). The pole concentrates the impact onto a narrow vertical area of the door, the hardest case to protect against.
  3. US-LINCAP — the car is hit at 62 km/h by a moving impactor angled at 27° to the side. The angle simulates a T-bone collision where the striking vehicle isn't exactly perpendicular.

Rear impact and IIHS

"★ Rear impact: — Integrity of fuel system is checked. — IIHS for tests on passive safety system of car." — IMG 181732 (top).

Two notes about rear-impact testing:

  1. Fuel-system integrity is the dominant concern in rear-impact tests — the fuel tank is typically in or near the rear, and rear-impact crashes that rupture the tank cause fires. The test certifies that the tank remains sealed up to a defined impact severity.
  2. IIHS (Insurance Institute for Highway Safety) is the third major rating organisation alongside Euro-NCAP and US-NCAP. It's funded by the US insurance industry and its tests focus on repair cost and whiplash injury in low-speed rear-end collisions (the most common, and most expensive-per-injury, crash type in dense traffic).

Components

Component Function
High-strength steel occupant cell Rigid passenger compartment; resists deformation during crash
Cross-member reinforcement Diagonal/transverse steel bracing inside the cell
Crumple zones (front + rear) Designed to deform progressively, absorbing crash kinetic energy
Curved front clip + sloped surfaces Third-party protection geometry — deflects pedestrians up and over
Active safety ECUs (ABS / DSC / EDC) Continuously act to prevent the crash in the first place
Crash test protocols (Euro-NCAP / US-NCAP / A2T / IIHS / LINCAP) Standardised assessments that quantify how well the structure meets its safety goals

Key Facts

  • FIRST (Full Integrated Road Safety Technology) is BMW's umbrella term covering protection for ① own passengers, ② road users (pedestrians/cyclists), ③ occupants of other cars.
  • Active prevention (ABS, DSC, EDC) acts before the crash; passive safety (airbags, seatbelts) acts during it.
  • The strong occupant cell resists deformation, while front + rear crumple zones absorb energy.
  • Curves & no flat surfaces in the front-clip design is the principal third-party-protection geometry.
  • At 50 km/h, a 10 m braking distance is considered the harmless-to-occupants baseline.
  • Three frontal crash protocols: Euro-NCAP (deformable wall, 100 %), US-NCAP (rigid wall, 100 %), A2T (40 % overlap, for claim/repair assessment).
  • Three side-impact protocols: Euro-NCAP (deformable trolley), Euro-NCAP pole test, US-LINCAP (62 km/h at 27°).
  • IIHS is the US insurance-industry safety rating body; focuses on repair cost and whiplash.
