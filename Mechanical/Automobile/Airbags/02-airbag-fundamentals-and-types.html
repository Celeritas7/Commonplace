Airbags

Airbag Fundamentals & Types

Source images: IMG20250514181732 *(bottom portion — airbag history & types)*, IMG20250514181737, IMG20250514181747 *(top portion — AITS, curtain, knee airbags)*

Overview

Three pages that walk through the airbag itself — its 1953 patent, the strict timing constraints (open in 30 ms, deflate within seconds so the driver can still see the road), and the seven distinct airbag types a modern BMW carries. The trainer organises the types by where they are and what they protect: front airbags for the driver and passenger, side airbags for thorax protection, head airbags (ITS and AITS) that drop down from the headliner across the side windows, curtain airbags that fold along the roof rail, and knee airbags that prevent the "submarine" effect (occupant slipping under the lap belt). One specialised type sits outside the car: the pedestrian airbag built into the bonnet, which lifts the hood up by 50–100 mm on pedestrian-strike detection to provide a cushion of crush-space between the pedestrian's head and the engine below.

Working Principle

Brief history

"History: Walter [patented] in 1953 for airbags." — IMG 181732.

The airbag concept was patented in 1953 by American John W. Hetrick and (independently the same year) by German Walter Linderer. Both early designs used compressed-gas reservoirs with mechanical triggers — slow, unreliable, and prone to accidental deployment. It took until 1971 (Ford Mercury) and 1973 (GM Oldsmobile) for the first production airbags, and only the late 1980s for them to spread to mainstream cars.

Two timing constraints

The page lists two critical timing numbers:

"— Airbag opens only in conjunction with seat belt. — BMW airbag system in [E23 — see Open Questions about page's "F23" notation]. — Airbag release only in genuine collisions. — Airbag opens in 30 msec & should deflate within [a few] [milli]sec so that driver can see what is happening on road." — IMG 181732.

Phase Time Purpose
Inflation 30 ms (full inflation) Cushion the occupant before they reach the steering wheel / dash
Deflation A few seconds Driver must be able to see the road and resume control after the impact

Why 30 ms specifically? A car crashes from 50 km/h to 0 in roughly 100–150 ms (depending on crumple-zone profile). The occupant continues moving forward at ~14 m/s until the seatbelt and airbag stop them — they cover ~50 cm of forward distance in the first 30 ms. The airbag must be fully inflated by then so the occupant's head strikes the inflated bag, not the steering wheel.

Why deflate quickly? The bag isn't there to provide a cushion after the impact; it's there to provide one during the deceleration. After the crash, an inflated bag in the driver's face would block visibility and prevent the driver from steering away from secondary hazards (other cars, embankments, oncoming traffic).

Side annotation — "provide cushioning effect to passengers."

The simplest statement of what the airbag does: convert the abrupt deceleration onto a hard surface (steering wheel, dash) into a distributed deceleration over the bag's pressurised surface area. By spreading the same momentum change over a larger contact area and a slightly longer time, the peak force on the occupant's head/chest is reduced.

Airbag fires only with seatbelt + only in genuine collisions

Two safety interlocks:

  1. Seatbelt requirement — The airbag will not fire if the seat is occupied but the seatbelt is unfastened. (Some markets require the bag to still fire at reduced intensity — see the BAE control unit in file 03.) The reason: an unbelted occupant is already in violent forward motion by the time the bag fires — being hit by a deploying airbag is worse than no airbag at all.
  2. Genuine collisions only — The control unit's algorithms distinguish between a real impact and a minor bump, kerb-strike, or pothole jolt. False deployment is dangerous (sudden airbag, sudden noise, driver loses control) and expensive (the entire pyrotechnic system must be replaced).

The submarine effect

"Knee airbag: doesn't allow person's upper body to move forward. Submarine effect is avoided, means driver can slip downward through seatbelt fitted [from underneath the lap belt]." — IMG 181747.

In a hard frontal impact, the seatbelt's lap section is supposed to anchor the occupant's pelvis. But if the occupant's lower body slides forward and downward off the seat cushion before the lap belt loads up, the belt can ride up over the pelvis and onto the soft abdomen. The occupant ends up sliding under the belt — hence "submarining." Consequences are severe: abdominal organ damage, broken pelvis, the head still moving forward but now the belt isn't restraining it.

The knee airbag prevents this by stopping the knees from going forward in the first place. Stop the knees → the hips stop too → the lap belt loads up against the pelvis as intended. The knee airbag is not there to protect the knees themselves (it has the wrong material for that); it's there to anchor the lower body so the rest of the restraint system can work.

Airbag module construction

"★ Airbag module: ① Metal plate housing, ②[blank]." — IMG 181737.

The page lists the airbag module as having two main parts but only labels the first (metal plate housing). The standard airbag-module breakdown is:

# Sub-component Function
① Metal plate housing Steel can that bolts to the steering wheel / dash; contains the folded bag and the gas generator
② Folded bag + cover The nylon fabric bag, folded into the housing; covered by a moulded plastic lid (the steering-wheel emblem area) that splits open along scored lines when the bag inflates

Compact airbag (Driver II)

"Compact airbag (Driver II) is placed for sporty steering wheels for BMW." — IMG 181737.

Sporty steering wheels (flat-bottomed, smaller diameter, on M-sport models) have less hub space for a conventional airbag module. Driver II is BMW's compact-format airbag designed to fit in the smaller hub while still delivering full deployment volume — typically achieved by using a more energetic gas generator and a tighter-folded bag.

Pedestrian airbag — the active hood

"★ Pedestrian airbag: Specially for pedestrians which could be hit by car can be [protected]. Bonnet can move up & down to absorb shocks from material dropped on bonnet." — IMG 181737.

In a pedestrian strike, the major cause of fatal head injury is the pedestrian's head hitting the engine cylinder head through the (relatively thin) bonnet sheet metal. The engine block is hard and sits only a few cm below the bonnet surface.

The pedestrian airbag (also called active hood or deployable hood) is a different beast from a conventional airbag — it's a pyrotechnic actuator under each rear bonnet hinge that pops the rear edge of the bonnet up by 50–100 mm on pedestrian-strike detection. The lifted bonnet:

  • Creates a cushion of empty space between the bonnet and the engine.
  • The bonnet sheet metal can now deform downward by 50–100 mm before contacting the engine.
  • The pedestrian's head, when it strikes the bonnet, decelerates over that 50–100 mm of crush space instead of stopping abruptly against an immovable cylinder head.

Detection is typically via pressure sensors in the front bumper (a tube along the bumper face that measures the pressure-wave signature of a pedestrian impact vs a rigid-object collision). Sensing → actuator firing happens in tens of milliseconds.

Side impact airbag behaviour

"★ Side impact: ★ airbag opens in the direction of impact & hence reduces the intensity of impact due to relative motion of opening of airbag during impact." — IMG 181737.

A subtle detail of side-airbag operation: the bag deploys outward toward the colliding vehicle / object. Because the bag is expanding into the side-impact zone at high speed, by the time the bag reaches its full extent, the side surface of the car has already deformed inward toward the occupant — but the bag's outer surface is moving outward to meet it. The two motions partially cancel, so the relative velocity between the occupant and the airbag's inner face is much lower than the raw closure speed of the impact.

Head airbag ITS (Inflatable Tubular Structure)

"★ Head airbag ITS (Inflatable Tubular Structure): — Once used, completely change [the] entire system, even nut bolts should be fixed carefully & mountings should be proper. — It opens in [slanted] position, [so] it is applicable for persons with different [heights]." — IMG 181737.

The ITS is not a conventional flat bag but a rigid inflatable tube that pops down from the headliner and extends diagonally from the A-pillar to the B-pillar across the side window. Once inflated, it has the structural integrity of a tube (not just a sheet of fabric), so it can act as a beam protecting the head from striking the window glass or B-pillar.

Two service rules the trainer emphasises: 1. After deployment, the entire system must be replaced, including every mounting bolt. The pyrotechnic shock damages all the structural fasteners; reusing any of them risks the next deployment being misaligned. 2. The slanted deployment angle means it works for occupants of different heights — a tall driver's head strikes a different part of the tube than a short driver's, but both are protected.

Head airbag AITS (Advanced ITS)

"AITS = extended ITS. AITS 1 — front seat passengers (Pillar A → Pillar B). AITS 2 — front & rear seat passengers (Pillar A → Pillar C)." — IMG 181747.

The AITS is a longer, multi-segment version of the ITS:

Variant Coverage
AITS 1 A-pillar to B-pillar (front-seat occupants only)
AITS 2 A-pillar to C-pillar (front + rear occupants)

Curtain airbag

"★ Curtain airbag: folded up in roof rail." — IMG 181747.

The curtain airbag is folded along the entire length of the roof rail above the side windows. On deployment, it falls down like a curtain covering the full window line from A-pillar to C-pillar. Unlike the ITS/AITS (rigid tubes), the curtain airbag is a flat fabric bag — it cushions head impact rather than providing structural beam support, and it stays inflated longer to also protect during rollover.

Most modern BMWs have the curtain airbag as the primary head-protection system, with no separate ITS — the curtain bag does both jobs.

Key Facts

  • Airbags were patented in 1953 (Hetrick / Linderer), reached production in the 1970s, mainstream in the late 1980s.
  • The airbag fires only with seatbelt fastened and only in genuine collisions — two safety interlocks.
  • Timing: full inflation in 30 ms; deflation in a few seconds so the driver can see and steer.
  • Seven airbag types in a fully equipped modern car: front driver / front passenger / side / head ITS / head AITS / curtain / knee.
  • The knee airbag's job is preventing the submarine effect (occupant slipping under the lap belt) — not protecting the knees themselves.
  • The pedestrian airbag is an active hood lift mechanism, not a conventional fabric bag. It creates 50–100 mm of crush space between the bonnet and engine.
  • Compact airbag (Driver II) is BMW's smaller-format variant for sporty steering wheels.
  • ITS is a rigid inflatable tube, not a flat bag. AITS is its extended (A→C) version.
  • Curtain airbags stow in the roof rail and drop full-length across the side windows.
  • After ITS / curtain deployment, the entire system + all mounting bolts must be replaced — the pyrotechnic shock damages every fastener.

Open Questions / Uncertainty

  • [?] "BMW airbag system in F23" on IMG 181732 — F23 is the BMW 2-series convertible (mid-2010s), far too late to be the first BMW with airbags. Almost certainly the trainer meant E23 (the first BMW 7-series, 1977–1987 — driver airbag introduced in 1986 on the E23). Preserved verbatim with this correction noted.
  • [?] "Airbag should deflate within 5 millisec" — the source page reads "5 millisec" or possibly "5 sec" (the unit is unclear in the handwriting). A 5 ms deflate would be physically impossible; a 5 second deflate is right for a real airbag (typically 1–3 seconds in practice, up to ~5 s for larger curtain bags). Read as "few seconds" in the body text. Real deflation happens through small vent holes in the back of the bag.
  • [?] "Walter patented in 1953" — Walter Linderer (German engineer) was indeed one of two 1953 patent-holders; the other was John W. Hetrick in the US. The trainer captured only the German-side history; the American patent is from the same year.
  • [?] Knee airbag location — drawn on the source page as "below the dash, near knees." Modern BMW knee airbags are actually integrated into the lower steering column shroud (driver side) and into the glovebox lower trim (passenger side). The under-dash location described matches older 2010-era designs.
  • [?] Pedestrian airbag "material dropped on bonnet" phrasing is awkward — the trainer probably meant "absorbs shocks from [pedestrian's head] dropping on bonnet." Preserved verbatim with this interpretation.
