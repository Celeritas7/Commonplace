Airbags

MRS4 Architecture, Up-Front Sensors & Seat Recognition

Source images: IMG20250514181817

Overview

A single dense page that closes the BMW airbag-controller story by introducing the fourth-generation Multiple Restraint System (MRS4), the up-front sensors that give the controller advance warning of a frontal impact before the B-pillar satellites would, and the OC3 seat-recognition system that tells the controller who is sitting in each seat — child, adult, or empty — so deployment can be tuned per occupant. The page is the natural completion of the MRS1 → MRS2 → MRS3 evolution covered in 03-gas-generators-and-airbag-control-units.

Working Principle

MRS4 — software-updated platform

"MRS4: Just updated software. MRS4RD: ..." — IMG 181817.

The page positions MRS4 as primarily a software update to MRS3 rather than a fundamental hardware change. The same control-unit hardware can run MRS3 or MRS4 firmware; the difference is in the algorithms (better collision discrimination, finer severity grading, faster decision latency).

MRS4RD is a sub-designation (likely Reduced Design or Redundant Design — see Open Questions). The page describes it as:

"MRS4RD takes input from all 6 sensors & releases the restraint system as per requirement." — IMG 181817.

So MRS4RD is the architecture that integrates six sensor inputs to make a fully informed deployment decision:

Up-front sensors — the resistance-strip principle

"Up-front sensors: — Small current is flowing through it. — During deceleration the resistance increases." — IMG 181817.

The up-front sensors sit at the very front of the vehicle, typically in the radiator support or behind the front bumper. They detect the impact event before the deceleration wave has propagated back to the B-pillar satellites, giving the MRS4 several extra milliseconds to fire airbags.

The page describes a specific sensor type — a resistance-based deformation gauge:

   At rest:                              On frontal impact:
   ────────                              ──────────────────

       +─────[ resistance ]─────−             +─────[ R+ΔR ]─────−
                  │                                      │
              I = V/R  flows                       I = V/(R+ΔR)
              steady DC current                    reduced current
              through the strip                    as resistance
                                                   rises with
                                                   deformation

When the front-clip deforms in an impact, the sensor strip is stretched / crushed → its electrical resistance increases sharply → the MRS4 sees a sudden current drop → impact registered.

This is a different sensing principle from the B-pillar accelerometers (MEMS micro-machined cantilevers that detect lateral g-forces). The two principles are complementary: - Up-front sensors detect frontal-impact deformation very early. - B-pillar satellites detect side-impact acceleration very accurately.

Both feed the MRS4, which fuses their data into a single deployment decision.

Calculation — positive vs negative acceleration

"Calculates positive & negative acceleration values." — IMG 181817.

The MRS4 tracks the deceleration signed (positive for braking/forward-deceleration during head-on impact, negative for rebound/backward-acceleration when the car bounces off whatever it hit). The signed profile lets the controller distinguish:

  • Hard braking (sustained positive deceleration over hundreds of ms) → don't fire airbags.
  • Frontal impact (sharp positive deceleration spike followed by negative rebound) → fire airbags.
  • Rear-end impact (sharp negative acceleration spike from rear-mounted sensors) → fire seatbelt tensioners + active headrests.

Up-front sensors must be replaced after a head-on collision

"Up-front sensors should be replaced after head-on collision." — IMG 181817.

The resistance-based sensor uses a sacrificial deformation element — once it's been stretched/crushed in an actual impact, its resistance no longer returns to the calibrated baseline. The MRS4 doesn't trust a "used" sensor for the next event, and the dealer must replace it as part of post-crash repair. (Similar to the ITS / curtain airbag mounting bolt rule in file 02 — pyrotechnic shock makes everything around the deployment a one-shot consumable.)

B-pillar satellites — two-sensor pair

"Two sensors on B-pillars which read acceleration & convert it into digital signal & send it to MRS4 control unit." — IMG 181817.

Each B-pillar contains a satellite sensor module that: 1. Has an on-board accelerometer (typically MEMS, sensing lateral g). 2. Has local conversion electronics that digitise the analog signal at the sensor itself. 3. Sends the digital signal to the MRS4 over a short bus.

Why convert locally? Sending raw analog acceleration over the harness would pick up electrical noise — the side-impact event is fast (< 5 ms) and noise could mask the real signal. Digitising at the source means the noise immunity is much higher, and the MRS4 receives clean data even with several metres of wiring in between.

The two satellites (one per side) are independent — left B-pillar reports left-side events, right reports right-side events. The MRS4 cross-checks the two to confirm a real side impact (vs an electrical glitch on one sensor).

OC3 seat recognition system

"★ Seat recognition system: — OC3 sensor mat. — Conductors with pressure-sensible resistance element. — Evaluation electronics. — Discrimination algorithms. — No airbag release directly; input from all the sensors are analysed by ECU. — Sensors can detect person sitting on seat; child or person — it is decided according to distance between bumps." — IMG 181817.

OC3 = Occupant Classification System, generation 3. It's a mat embedded in the passenger seat cushion containing a grid of pressure-sensitive resistive elements.

The "distance between bumps" detail — the page specifically calls out that the system distinguishes child from adult by how far apart the pressure spots are on the mat. An adult sitting normally creates two large contact patches (left buttock + right buttock) spaced ~30 cm apart. A small child's contact spots are closer together. A rearward-facing child seat creates a single broad pressure pattern with no spacing at all.

Why no direct airbag control:

"No airbag release directly; input from all the sensors are analysed by ECU."

The OC3 doesn't decide whether to fire — it just classifies the occupant. The MRS4 uses that classification to decide whether to fire the passenger airbag at all (no fire for child seats), at reduced power (children), or at full power (adults). The actual decision still belongs to the MRS4's central logic.

Components

Key Facts

  • MRS4 is mostly a software update over MRS3 — same hardware, improved algorithms.
  • MRS4RD integrates 6 sensor inputs (2 up-front + 2 B-pillar + driver/passenger OC3) into the deployment decision.
  • Up-front sensors use a resistance-strip principle — front-clip deformation raises the strip's resistance, dropping the current the controller sees.
  • Up-front sensors detect frontal impacts earlier than B-pillar satellites can, gaining the controller several extra milliseconds.
  • B-pillar satellites have on-board A-to-D conversion — they send digital signals to the MRS4 for noise immunity.
  • Up-front sensors must be replaced after any head-on collision — they are sacrificial deformation gauges.
  • The OC3 system classifies the passenger as empty / child / adult / child seat using a pressure-pattern map of the seat cushion.
  • The discriminator distinguishes child vs adult by the distance between pressure bumps — adult buttocks are spaced wider than a child's.
  • The OC3 doesn't fire airbags directly — it just provides the classification; the MRS4 makes the actual deployment decision.

Open Questions / Uncertainty

  • [?] "MRS4RD" — the "RD" suffix is the page's notation without expansion. Could plausibly mean Reduced Design (a cost-optimised variant for entry-level models) or Redundant Design (with cross-checked sensors for higher safety integrity). Both interpretations exist in BMW documentation across different years. Preserved as written.
  • [?] "Distance between bumps" for child-vs-adult discrimination — this is the trainer's word for the OC3 mat's pressure-pattern spacing. The technical principle (pattern matching against stored profiles) is correct, but "bumps" is unusual wording. Modern OC3 systems also incorporate weight estimation and capacitive sensing beyond the pressure-pattern check; the page captures only the pressure-pattern dimension.
  • [?] "OC3 sensor mat" — "OC3" likely stands for Occupant Classification (gen) 3. The seat-pad version mentioned here is BMW's mid-2000s implementation; modern systems use a hybrid of mat sensors and millimetre-wave radar for more robust classification.
  • [?] Up-front sensor "Small current is flowing through it; during deceleration the resistance increases" — the resistance-strip principle described is one valid implementation, but some BMW front sensors use a piezoelectric principle instead (deformation generates a voltage spike rather than changing a resistance). The trainer described one mechanism; both are field-deployed.
  • [?] The "6 sensors" count in "MRS4RD takes input from all 6 sensors" is the source figure. The page only explicitly labels four (2 up-front + 2 B-pillar). The remaining 2 are likely driver OC3 + passenger OC3 seat mats, giving the count of 6.
