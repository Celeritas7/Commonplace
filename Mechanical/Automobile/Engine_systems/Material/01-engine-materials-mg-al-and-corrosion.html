Materials

Engine Materials — Mg/Al Alloys, Galvanic Corrosion, Thermal Expansion

Source images: IMG20250514181023, IMG20250514181032, IMG20250514181049

Overview

A material-science page covering why and where BMW uses magnesium-aluminium alloys in the engine, what Mg cannot be used for, galvanic corrosion between dissimilar metals, uncritical metal pairings that don't corrode, and thermal-expansion mismatch between Al / Mg / Fe (and why aluminium screws are preferred for Al-alloy assemblies). Built around the N62 → next-gen engine weight reduction case study.

Working Principle

Why bother with light alloys — the "Dynamics" framing

The page opens with the chassis-engineering argument for low engine weight:

Lever Effect
Power-to-weight ratio (kW/kg) Higher is better. Achieved by either ① producing more power, or ② reducing weight of vehicle.
Low fuel consumption Affected by uphills and accelerating — both proportional to vehicle mass.
Optimum axle-load distribution Target 50% front / 50% rear.
Selective weight economy Reduce weight where it matters most (top of car, ends of car) instead of uniformly.

Lighter engine = better p/w ratio, better fuel economy, better axle balance.

Crankcase construction (the case study)

Notebook: "Crankcase: Al-Silicon Inner part: Al-Si alloy (Al-Si17) External part: Mg-Al alloy (AJ62) Steel liners inside cylinder for piston movement. Nearly 10 kg of engine weight reduced as compared to N64 [?]."

A composite crankcase: Mg-Al outer skin (very light, low strength) over an Al-Si load-bearing inner core, with steel liners pressed into the bores for wear resistance. The composite takes advantage of each metal's best property while avoiding its weaknesses.

Other weight-saving substitutions on the same engine: 1. Plastic intake manifold (down from cast aluminium). 2. Oil pan in aluminium (down from cast iron). 3. Composite material crankcase (Mg + Al alloys, as above).

Limitations of magnesium — where Mg CANNOT be used

Notebook ★: "Can't use as: 1. Bearing bushes 2. Cooling ducts 3. Screw connections of cylinder head 4. Mounting of crankshaft."

"Not suited for: 1. Smooth surface materials 2. Coolant contact materials 3. Heavy load carrying parts."

Reasons (not in notes — fills the gaps): - Bearing bushes — Mg is soft, galls easily under sliding loads. - Cooling ducts / coolant contact — Mg corrodes aggressively in water/glycol (galvanic + chemical). - Cylinder-head screw connections — high clamp loads; Mg threads strip. - Crankshaft mounting — heaviest dynamic load in the engine; Mg lacks the fatigue strength.

Noble vs base metals — galvanic series (notebook layout)

Notebook orders metals by chemical stability, high → low:

HIGH stability ────────────────────────────────────────► LOW stability
   Au   ►   Cu   ►   Fe   ►   Al   ►   Mg   ►   Na
   (noble)                                       (base)

Notebook caption: "Material on right dissolves when [it] comes in contact with material on left."

In a galvanic couple, the less noble (more reactive, right-hand) metal becomes the anode and corrodes; the more noble metal is the cathode and is protected. The further apart on the series, the worse the corrosion rate.

Galvanic corrosion test pairings

The page sketches the test matrix:

Pair (notebook) Which one fails (predicted by galvanic series)
Mg ↔ Fe Mg fails (Mg far right of Fe)
Cu ↔ Fe Fe fails (Fe right of Cu)
Mg ↔ Al Mg fails (Mg right of Al)
Fe ↔ Al Al fails (Al right of Fe)

Notebook test conditions: - Salt mist produced (accelerated corrosion environment — standard ASTM B117 salt-spray test). - Duration: 240 hrs.

Uncritical metal pairing — when galvanic corrosion DOESN'T happen

Notebook: "No corrosion at: 1. Dry contact point 2. Contact point sprayed with oil (e.g. steel liners at crankcase bearing) 3. Metals separated by seals (using gaskets)."

Galvanic corrosion needs an electrolyte path (water + ions) between the two metals. Remove the electrolyte (dry, oiled, or sealed) and the cell can't complete a circuit → no corrosion.

Thermal expansion mismatch

The page draws a horizontal bar chart of thermal expansion (length change per °C):

Al  ████████████████████████████████  ◄── largest expansion
Mg  ████████████████████████          ◄── medium
Fe  ████████████                      ◄── smallest

Notebook conclusion: "So Aluminium screw is used to join or assemble [Al-alloy parts] as both have nearly same expansion & Iron screw is not used as it can come under tension at higher temperature."

If you put an iron screw in an aluminium part and heat the assembly, the aluminium expands more than the steel → the aluminium tries to grow but the steel screw doesn't → the steel ends up under tension while the aluminium is under compression. Repeated thermal cycles can yield the aluminium threads or fatigue the screw.

Using an aluminium screw, both expand together → no thermal preload buildup.

Protrusive seal at head ↔ crankcase

Notebook ★: "Protrusive seal for mounting between head & crankcase — prevents the water contact between the material & prevents accidents."

A raised-bead head gasket (the "protrusive" feature) seals the coolant passages where the cylinder head and crankcase meet — preventing coolant from reaching the Al/Mg interface where it would set up galvanic corrosion.

Components

Material Where used Why
Al-Si alloy (Al-Si17) Crankcase inner / structural core Good strength, low thermal expansion vs pure Al, good casting.
Mg-Al alloy (AJ62) Crankcase outer skin Very light; only takes secondary loads.
Steel cylinder liners Bores Wear-resistant; protects soft Al-Si from piston-ring action.
Aluminium Oil pan, intake (some) Light, recyclable.
Plastic Intake manifold Lightest option; tolerates intake temperatures.
Aluminium screws Joining Al-alloy parts Match thermal expansion → no preload drift.
Steel screws Joining steel-to-steel or steel-to-cast-iron High strength, fatigue-resistant.
Gaskets / seals Between dissimilar metals exposed to coolant or moisture Block electrolyte path → block galvanic cell.

Detailed Component Notes

Why Al-Si17 specifically (the "17" matters)

Standard Al-Si alloys are 7–12% Si. Hypereutectic Al-Si17 (17% Si) is over the eutectic point — silicon precipitates as hard primary particles in the Al matrix, giving: - Better wear resistance (could theoretically run pistons without a steel liner — Mercedes/Porsche do this on some engines). - Lower thermal expansion (close to cast iron). - Higher hot strength.

BMW still uses steel liners with Al-Si17 in this construction for piston-ring durability margin.

Why AJ62 (the "AJ62" matters)

AJ62 = Mg-6%Al-2%Sr (with strontium for creep resistance at elevated temperatures). Standard Mg-Al alloys (AZ91, AM60) creep badly above 120 °C; AJ62 holds up at 150–175 °C — adequate for a crankcase outer skin.

Why iron screws still appear on BMW engines

Aluminium screws aren't used for everything — they have lower tensile strength than steel. Critical fasteners (head bolts, main-bearing bolts) are still steel, but the joint design accounts for the thermal mismatch (e.g., torque-to-yield bolts, longer shank for elastic stretch).

Key Facts

  • BMW lightweight crankcase = Mg-Al (AJ62) outer + Al-Si17 inner + steel liners.
  • Composite design saved ~10 kg over the prior generation (N64 — possibly N62 in the notebook).
  • Mg cannot be used for: bearing bushes, cooling ducts, head bolts, crank mountings, coolant-contact parts, heavy load parts.
  • Galvanic corrosion order (notebook): Au > Cu > Fe > Al > Mg > Na (right-hand metal corrodes).
  • Salt-spray test = 240 hrs standard duration.
  • Corrosion prevented by: dry contact, oil film, or seal between dissimilar metals.
  • Thermal expansion: Al ≈ Mg >> Fe. Use Al screws in Al assemblies to avoid thermal preload buildup.
  • Protrusive head gasket seals coolant away from Al/Mg interfaces.
