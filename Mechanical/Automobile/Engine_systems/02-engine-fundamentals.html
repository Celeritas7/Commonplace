Engine Fundamentals — Power, Compression Ratio, Flash Point

Source images: IMG20250514111913

Overview

A single dense page that defines five foundational engine concepts: the three "horse powers" (indicated, frictional, brake), the compression ratio with its TDC/BDC volume sketch, the typical petrol-vs-diesel compression ratio range, and the flash point / dew point / self-ignition temperature definitions. Together these are the metrics used to talk about how much work an engine extracts from fuel and how that fuel behaves chemically.

Working Principle

Three measures of engine power

# Term Definition (notebook wording)
1 Indicated Horse Power (IHP) Power developed at the cylinder due to combustion.
2 Frictional Horse Power (FHP) Power lost due to friction in the crankcase (bearings, pistons-to-wall, oil shear, etc.).
3 Brake Horse Power (BHP) Power at the crankshaft — i.e., usable output you'd measure on a dyno brake.

Relation: $$\text{BHP} = \text{IHP} - \text{FHP}$$

Compression ratio

The ratio of total cylinder volume (with piston at BDC) to clearance volume (with piston at TDC):

$$\text{Compression Ratio} = \frac{V_s + V_c}{V_c} = \frac{\text{Total volume}}{\text{Clearance volume}}$$

where - $V_s$ = Swept volume (volume the piston sweeps between TDC and BDC) - $V_c$ = Clearance volume (volume left above the piston at TDC)

The notebook also writes this as: $$\text{Compression Ratio} = \frac{\text{Total volume}}{\text{Clearance volume}}$$

Typical compression ratios (notebook values)

Engine type Compression ratio
Petrol ~ 14 : 1 [?] (real-world range is closer to 8:1 – 12:1 for NA petrols of 2013; see Open Questions)
Diesel 21 : 1 → 25 : 1

Fuel temperature definitions

Term Definition (notebook wording) Plain meaning
Flash point Temperature at which fuel ignites. More precisely: temp at which fuel vapour can briefly flash in air when an ignition source is brought near.
Dew point Temperature at which vapours convert into dew. Condensation temperature of the vapour.
Self-ignition temperature (notebook) "self-ignition temp of petrol is less & diesel is high" This is reversed in the notes — see Open Questions. Actual: diesel ~210 °C < petrol ~280 °C, i.e. diesel auto-ignites at a lower temperature, which is why diesel engines work by compression-ignition.

Components / Definitions Reference

Symbol Name Where to picture it
TDC Top Dead Centre Piston at the highest point of its travel.
BDC Bottom Dead Centre Piston at the lowest point of its travel.
$V_s$ Swept volume Cylinder volume between TDC and BDC (= piston displacement per cylinder).

Detailed Component Notes

Why diesel needs a higher compression ratio

Compression ignition relies on the air alone being squeezed to a temperature high enough to auto-ignite the injected diesel. To reach those temperatures from ambient by compression alone, you need a high compression ratio (21:1+). Petrol engines do not want auto-ignition (that's knock), so they run lower CRs and use a spark.

Flash point vs autoignition (clarification not in notes)

  • Flash point — lowest temp at which the fuel produces enough vapour to flash once when an external flame touches it (no sustained burning).
  • Fire point — slightly higher; sustained burning.
  • Autoignition / self-ignition temperature — temperature at which the fuel will ignite without any external spark/flame.

The notebook conflates flash point with ignition temperature in its single sentence; the distinction matters.

Key Facts

  • BHP = IHP − FHP.
  • CR = (Vs + Vc) / Vc.
  • Diesel CR: 21:1 to 25:1; petrol CR (notebook): ~14:1.
  • Flash point = temp at which fuel ignites (in presence of ignition source).
  • Dew point = temp at which vapour condenses.
  • Self-ignition temp: notebook says petrol < diesel — this is incorrect. Real values: diesel ≈ 210 °C is less than petrol ≈ 280 °C; that's why diesels self-ignite under compression.
