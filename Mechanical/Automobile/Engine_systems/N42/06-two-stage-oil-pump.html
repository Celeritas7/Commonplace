N42 Engine

Two-Stage Oil Pump

Source images: IMG20250515002945

Overview

The N42 oil pump is a two-stage design driven by chain directly from the crankshaft. Under most operating conditions only stage 1 runs. Stage 2 is deactivated hydraulically once oil pressure reaches 2 bar at engine speeds above 2000 rpm — saving the parasitic drag a fully-on pump would impose. The economy gain matters because the N42 spends most of its life below 4000 rpm in typical driving — a full-capacity pump would be drag for no benefit.

Working Principle

Drive arrangement

Notebook: "Driven by chain drive directly from crankshaft. Two pairs of gearwheels are connected. Main oil pump."

The pump is chain-driven off the crank (not gear-driven and not belt-driven). Two pairs of gearwheels inside form two parallel pumping stages — each stage is essentially a small gear pump. Both stages always physically rotate (they're chained to the crank); the difference is whether stage 2 is actively pumping or dead-headed.

When both stages run

Notebook: - "Sufficient pressure for: (i) VANOS control, (ii) Deactivated hydraulically above oil pressure 2 bar & revolutions 2000 rpm" - "Both work at slow speed." - "1st [stage active] — oil temp high [?]" - "Not required above 2 bar & 2000 rpm." - "Two-stage pump for ECONOMY." - "As our engine reach above 4000 rpm hardly. So pump with high capacity not needed."

The logic: | Condition | What runs | Why | |---|---|---| | Slow speed / low rpm / cold (oil pressure below 2 bar) | Both stages — combined output | Cold thick oil needs more displacement to build pressure. Sufficient flow for VANOS actuation needed even at idle. | | Above 2 bar AND above 2000 rpm | Stage 1 only (stage 2 deactivated) | Crank-driven pump already turning fast enough that stage 1 alone makes plenty of pressure. Stage 2 would just bleed back across the relief valve — wasted work. |

Hydraulic deactivation — how stage 2 "shuts off"

Notebook key annotations on the bottom diagram: "below 2 bar / above 2 bar / pressure maintained / both (1) & (2) / at 2 bar only pump (1) operates / shut-off"

A pilot oil pressure taken from downstream of stage 1 acts on a spool valve inside the pump: - Below 2 bar pilot pressure → spool open → both stages dump into the main gallery. - At or above 2 bar pilot pressure → spool closes → stage 2 output is looped back to its own inlet (recirculated, no pressure rise, near-zero work) → only stage 1 keeps feeding the gallery.

The spool is biased by a spring; at 2 bar pilot vs spring it shifts.

Why VANOS needs the pump

Notebook: "Sufficient pressure for: (i) VANOS control ..."

The VANOS phaser is a hydraulic actuator — its rotor is moved through ±25° (or so) by oil pushed through one of two pressure channels via a solenoid valve under DME control. If oil pressure drops below the threshold (~2 bar), VANOS can't actuate → timing falls back to a default position → DTC stored.

So the N42 pump has to deliver: 1. All the usual lubrication flow. 2. Plus enough head to actuate VANOS reliably even at idle.

This is why both stages run at low rpm — you need flow + pressure margin for VANOS even when the engine isn't spinning fast.

Non-return valve

Notebook: "Non-return valve required."

Prevents oil from draining back to the sump through the pump when the engine is off — so the gallery has prime ready for next start, and VANOS holds its last-commanded position.

Maintenance note — direct replacement only

Notebook: "We can't adjust latches of 'lobe shape pump' — so we can't open it. So directly replacement required."

The pump is a sealed unit. The "lobe shape pump" wording refers to the gear-lobe geometry inside; latch (clip) adjustments aren't possible from outside. Service is whole-unit swap, not rebuild.

Components

Component Function
Chain drive (from crank) Always-on mechanical drive to both pump stages.
Stage 1 pumping element Smaller gear pair; always pumps to the main gallery.
Stage 2 pumping element Larger gear pair; only adds to gallery when needed.
Pilot spool valve Pressure-actuated spool that switches stage 2 between "pumping to gallery" and "recirculating to its own inlet."
Non-return valve Stops oil draining back through the pump when engine is off.
Pressure relief valve Standard relief — caps maximum gallery pressure even when stage 2 is shut off.
Main oil gallery Distributes oil to bearings, VANOS, spray nozzles.

Detailed Component Notes

Why both stages at slow speed

At cold idle: - Engine rpm is low → crank-driven pump speed is low → flow is low. - Oil is cold and thick → pressure builds in the gallery slower per cc of flow. - VANOS needs ~2 bar AT IDLE → flow margin needed.

→ Both stages run to make the volume.

Why stage 2 deactivates above ~2000 rpm + 2 bar

At normal driving: - Stage 1 alone already produces more than enough flow. - Stage 2 still pumping would just push oil through the pressure-relief valve back to the sump → wasted shaft power → wasted fuel.

→ Pilot spool shifts stage 2 to idle recirculation → ~ zero pumping work → fuel saved.

The trainer's economy framing: "As our engine reaches above 4000 rpm hardly. So pump with high capacity not needed." — i.e., typical N42 duty cycle is light city/highway, where stage 1 alone suffices for 95% of operation.

"Lobe shape pump" — why it can't be rebuilt

Modern gear-pump rotors have tight tolerances (sub-50-micron clearances) machined in matched sets. Once worn, you can't shim or re-tension; either the rotor pair is good or it's done. Hence dealer practice is replace, don't rebuild.

Connection to the oil-to-water heat exchanger

The text on the bottom of this page restates the oil-to-water HX role (covered in cooling & oil circuits):

"At cold phase — increases [warm-up], reduces warm-up period, also increases fuel efficiency." "Normal — increases life of oil; oil-changing period increased."

Restated for context here because pump output goes through the HX next on its way to the bearings.

Key Facts

  • N42 oil pump is two-stage, chain-driven from crank.
  • Both stages always rotate; only stage 2's output is switched.
  • Stage 2 deactivated when pilot oil pressure > 2 bar AND engine speed > 2000 rpm.
  • Stage 2 deactivation = recirculate output to inlet → near-zero pumping work.
  • Both stages active at slow speed / cold start — needed to build pressure for VANOS actuation at idle.
  • VANOS needs ~ 2 bar to actuate reliably.
  • Non-return valve keeps oil in the gallery when engine is off → prime ready for restart, VANOS holds last position.
  • Pump is a sealed unit — direct replacement only, no rebuild possible ("lobe shape pump" tolerances).
  • Two-stage design saves fuel because N42 mostly runs below 4000 rpm where a fixed-displacement full-size pump would be wasteful drag.

  • [?] "At 2 bar only pump (1) operates" — could equally read "at 2 bar threshold, only pump 1 operates" (i.e., the switch point) or "when pressure equals 2 bar, only pump 1 runs." Both readings agree on the operating logic.

  • [?] Pilot pressure source — assumed to come from downstream of stage 1 (so the spool only shifts once the gallery is loaded). Notebook diagram is consistent with this.
