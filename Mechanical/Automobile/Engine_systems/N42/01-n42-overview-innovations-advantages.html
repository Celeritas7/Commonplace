N42 Engine

N42 — Overview, Innovations, Advantages

Source images: IMG20250515002828

Overview

The N42 is BMW's "new generation" 4-cylinder petrol engine (launched 2001, used on E46 316ti / 318i / 318ti from ~2001–2007). It was the first BMW four-cylinder to combine Valvetronic (throttle-less load control) with double VANOS (variable valve timing on both cams) and a magnesium-aluminium composite construction. This page is the dealer-training opening shot: what BMW asked the engineering team for, how they delivered it, and a quick comparison vs Valvetronic and Petrol Direct Injection as alternative load-control strategies.

Comparison table — VANOS vs Valvetronic vs Petrol Direct Injection

The page draws a rating matrix comparing the three load-control technologies across four operating criteria. Rating scale: ++ very good, + good, 0 normal, − bad.

Criterion VANOS Valvetronic (W²: valve opening) Petrol Direct Injection
High-pressure efficiency + + ++
Charge changing 0 ++ ++
Exhaust pressure ++ + +
Full load + + +(+) (notebook hedges this one)

What the table is saying (reading the columns): - VANOS alone — moderate gain on efficiency, weak on charge exchange (because the throttle is still doing the load-control job), strong on exhaust pressure (timing tuned for exhaust scavenging). - Valvetronic — strong on charge exchange (throttle losses eliminated), modest on the rest. - Petrol DI — strong on efficiency (precise fuel placement) and charge exchange (no throttle pumping), but exhaust and full-load benefits are smaller.

The N42 went with VANOS + Valvetronic combined (no PDI on N42 — that came later with N53). Combining VANOS and Valvetronic gives both columns' benefits: timing-shift gains and throttle-less operation.

Detailed Component Notes

Why "throttle-free load control" is the headline

On a conventional petrol engine, the throttle plate is the load-control device — close it to reduce power. But a closed throttle creates a vacuum in the manifold that the piston has to work against on the intake stroke (pumping losses) — eats fuel for no useful output. Valvetronic varies the valve lift directly (0.3 mm to ~10 mm) so air mass is metered at the valve, not at the throttle → throttle stays nearly fully open → no pumping loss → big efficiency gain at part load (city driving, cruising).

The "Main bearing # of crankshaft" margin note

N42 reduced main-bearing diameter vs the older M43 it replaced — smaller bearings have lower sliding velocity → lower friction (FHP) → more BHP at the crank. This is one of the "friction-optimized" gains.

Weight reduction

N42 uses a magnesium-aluminium composite block (Mg-Al outer skin over Al-Si inner core with steel cylinder liners) — see engine materials chapter for the full alloy story. The composite saved roughly 10 kg over a conventional all-aluminium block of the same displacement.

Key Facts

  • N42 = BMW's "new generation" 4-cylinder petrol (E46-era, ~2001–2007).
  • 5 innovations: 4-valve heads, low-friction timing, VANOS, MPFI, DME.
  • 7 advantages: lower fuel + emissions, more power + torque, throttle-free load control, lower friction, extended oil-filter intervals.
  • Comparison table puts Valvetronic and PDI tied for charge-exchange (best), VANOS best for exhaust pressure, PDI best for high-pressure efficiency.
  • N42 combines VANOS + Valvetronic to get both timing and lift control without going to PDI.
  • Mg-Al composite block saves weight; reduced main-bearing diameter cuts friction.
  • ECU is DME (MS32 variant).
  • Two displacements: N42B18 (1.8 L), N42B20 (2.0 L — only one with DISA, see DISA chapter).
