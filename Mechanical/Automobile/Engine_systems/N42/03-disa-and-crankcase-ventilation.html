N42 Engine

DISA & Crankcase Ventilation

Source images: IMG20250515002902

Notebook date: 2/8/2013 (2 August 2013)

Overview

Two N42 systems tied to the air-routing path: DISA — the variable-geometry intake that switches runner length around 4500 rpm to broaden the torque curve, and crankcase ventilation — a cyclone-type oil separator that pulls combustion blow-by gases out of the crankcase, separates the oil mist, and returns the cleaned gases to the intake manifold instead of venting to atmosphere (the old polluting way).

Working Principle

DISA — what it does

Notebook: "Variable geometry intake system — DISA." "1. Use of sliding sleeves: so the intake path become short & long. 2. Switchover point with rising rev: 4500 rev. 3. Switchover point with falling rev: 4400 rev. 4. As engine is turned off DISA is moved to 'extreme stop' position so the intake air path get blocked."

DISA = Differenziertes Sauganlage (German: "differentiated intake system"). The intake manifold has a movable sliding sleeve that opens or closes a port, effectively giving the air two paths to choose from:

Engine speed Sleeve position Effective intake runner Why
Below ~4500 rpm One position Long intake path Long path tunes the pressure wave to arrive at the intake valve just as it opens at low rpm → bonus charging effect → more low-end torque.
Above ~4500 rpm Other position Short intake path Short path lowers intake resistance, suits the higher airflow demand at high rpm → more top-end power.
Engine off "Extreme stop" Intake blocked Stops dirt/insects/water entering when the engine isn't running.

The 100-rpm hysteresis (switch up at 4500, switch back at 4400) prevents the system from flickering back and forth right at the transition point.

Crankcase ventilation — the problem

Notebook: "Crankcase ventilation: The fuel & unburnt gases enter into crankcase due to small leak between cylinder & crankcase."

During the compression and power strokes, a small amount of combustion gas (and unburnt fuel) slips past the piston rings into the crankcase — this is blow-by. If you let it accumulate: - Crankcase pressure rises → oil leaks through gaskets and seals. - Unburnt hydrocarbons dilute and acidify the engine oil → shorter oil life. - Vented straight to atmosphere → HC emissions (the old pre-PCV days).

Crankcase ventilation — the N42 solution (cyclone separator)

Notebook diagram: - Unburnt gases & fumes → directly to exhaust manifold — actually the intake manifold - Cyclone (oil separator) - Pressure of −50 mbar of vacuum is maintained - Condensate return line (oil) - Ventilation hose Margin notes: "Earlier period gases given outside" / "Pollution ↑" / "Pressure in crankcase"

How it works: 1. Blow-by gas leaves the crankcase through a ventilation hose. 2. Passes through a cyclone separator (sometimes called "coil separator" in the notes) — spins the gas-oil aerosol so the heavier oil droplets fling outward and drop down a condensate return line back to the sump. 3. The cleaned, oil-free gas is then routed to the intake manifold to be re-burnt. 4. The system maintains roughly −50 mbar of vacuum in the crankcase so blow-by always migrates out, never in.

Notebook: "Advantage: i) Oil life increased, ii) Pollution is controlled."

Detailed Component Notes

Why intake runners tune to specific rpm

Each time the intake valve opens, a pressure wave travels up the intake runner away from the valve. The wave reflects off the open end of the runner and travels back down. The runner length is tuned so the returning pressure wave arrives just as the valve is about to close → forces extra charge into the cylinder → bonus filling (this is "acoustic supercharging" or inertia ramming).

  • Long runner → long round-trip time → tuned for low rpm (waves move at sound speed; lower rpm = longer time between valve openings = longer runner needed for the wave to come back in time).
  • Short runner → short round-trip time → tuned for high rpm.

DISA is BMW's clever way to get both tunings on the same engine.

Why −50 mbar vacuum on the crankcase

  • Too little vacuum → blow-by escapes past oil-pan gaskets → leaks + smell + emissions.
  • Too much vacuum → oil gets sucked up into the intake → blue smoke + plug fouling.

Roughly −50 mbar is the sweet spot — enough to ensure outward flow without scavenging oil.

Why the cyclone "directly to intake manifold" not exhaust

The cleaned blow-by gas goes back to the intake manifold (so it gets re-burnt by the next combustion cycle) — not the exhaust manifold. The notebook label "directly to exhaust manifold" is almost certainly a slip; the diagram and physics both point to the intake side. See Open Questions.

Why "coil separator" sometimes

The cyclone is sometimes a labyrinth of coiled passages rather than a true centrifugal cyclone — same outcome (oil drops out, gas continues) but different physical geometry. The notebook uses both terms.

Key Facts

  • DISA = differentiated intake system — sliding sleeves switch intake-runner length.
  • DISA switchover: up at 4500 rpm, down at 4400 rpm (100-rpm hysteresis).
  • Engine off → DISA goes to extreme stop position, blocking intake path.
  • DISA is on N42B20 only, not N42B18.
  • Crankcase ventilation = cyclone (or coil) separator that pulls blow-by out of the crankcase, drops the oil back to sump, returns cleaned gas to the intake manifold.
  • Crankcase held at −50 mbar vacuum.
  • Without crankcase ventilation: oil contamination, pressure buildup, HC pollution to atmosphere.
  • Advantages of cyclone ventilation: oil life increased, pollution controlled.
