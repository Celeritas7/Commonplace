N42 Engine

VANOS — Variable Valve Timing

Source images: IMG20250515003027, IMG20250515003040

Notebook date: 6/8/2013

Overview

VANOS = Variable Nockenwellen-Steuerung (German for "variable camshaft control") = BMW's variable valve timing system. It rotates the camshaft relative to the crankshaft via a hydraulic phaser — shifting valve events earlier or later in the engine cycle. Double VANOS does this on both intake and exhaust cams independently. On N42 this works alongside Valvetronic (see Valvetronic chapter) — VANOS controls valve-event timing, Valvetronic controls valve-event lift.

Working Principle

The need

Notebook: "VANOS (Variable Valve Timing). Need: ★ It can change alignment between camshaft & crankshaft."

A traditional cam-driven valvetrain has the cam locked to the crank at a fixed angular relationship (set by chain or belt). The valve always opens at the same crank angle. But the optimal valve event depends on engine load and rpm: - Idle / part load: want less overlap (intake closes earlier, exhaust closes earlier) → stable combustion. - Mid load: want moderate overlap → internal EGR effect → reduces pumping losses and NOx. - High rpm: want more overlap + later closing → uses intake-air inertia to push more charge in.

VANOS lets the DME phase the cam by ±25° or so to match valve timing to operating conditions in real time.

Individual parts (11 numbered)

Back plate — closes the back face. 7. Blade (vane on rotor) — the working surfaces that swept volumes either side of them are the two pressure chambers. 8. Springs — small return springs (locking-pin spring, etc.). 9. Pressure channels A & B — the two oil feeds to the two sides of each rotor vane. 10. Solenoid valve — DME-controlled 4-way valve that selects A or B (or balances both). 11. Hydraulic bore holes in camshaft — feed channels drilled through the cam to bring oil from the gallery into the phaser.

How the rotor moves

The rotor (item 5) sits inside the housing (item 1). The rotor's vanes (item 7) split the gap between rotor and housing into pressure chambers. Oil pressure on side A of the vane pushes the rotor one direction; pressure on side B pushes it the other. The solenoid valve (item 10) under DME control sends oil pressure to either A, B, or holds both (rotor locked at current position).

Because the housing is tied to the timing chain (and so to the crank) and the rotor is keyed to the cam, moving the rotor relative to the housing rotates the cam relative to the crank → shifts valve timing.

Why the locking pin

Notebook: "Locking pin: At excess pressure it leaves down & can again lock after."

At engine start (no oil pressure yet), the rotor would flop around. The locking pin engages a hole in the housing to hold the rotor at a known default position (typically full-retard for intake, full-advance for exhaust). When oil pressure builds (above the pin spring), the pin retracts → rotor free to move under hydraulic command. When you shut down, pin re-engages.

Torsion spring role

Notebook: "(to remove torsional stresses)"

Valvetrain creates torque pulses on the cam every time a valve closes against a spring — these pulses try to twist the rotor against the housing. The torsion spring biases the rotor against this pulsing so it doesn't oscillate at idle when oil-pressure damping is weak.

Combination with Valvetronic (middle of IMG 003040)

The middle plot shows what's possible when both systems work together: - Valvetronic sets the height of the lift curve (0.3 mm → 10 mm). - VANOS sets the position of the curve in the crank-angle window (shift left or right within roughly ±25–30°, of which the 60° marker spans a typical overlap range).

Cold-start sequence

   Engine OFF:
       Rotor locked by pin to default position (no oil pressure).

   Engine starts:
       1. Stage 1 of oil pump runs.
       2. Oil pressure builds in the gallery.
       3. Oil reaches the VANOS phaser via cam bore holes.
       4. Locking pin retracts when pressure > pin-spring force.
       5. Rotor now free to be moved by solenoid commands.
       6. DME selects A/B port — rotor slides → cam phases → valve timing shifts.

Detailed Component Notes

Why "Double VANOS" on N42

N42's double VANOS phases both intake and exhaust cams independently. This unlocks: - Internal EGR — open both valves briefly together (overlap) → some exhaust gets sucked back into the cylinder → cools combustion → cuts NOx without an external EGR system. - Atkinson-cycle effect — close the intake valve after BDC (late intake closing) → some charge gets pushed back into the manifold → effective compression ratio drops → lower NOx + better part-load efficiency. - Performance shifts — advance both cams at high rpm → max overlap + late closing → uses intake inertia for extra filling.

A single-VANOS system (intake cam only) can only do a subset of these.

Why hydraulic and not electric

Hydraulic phasers are fast (full sweep in ~ 100 ms) and need only an oil supply + a small solenoid. Electric phasers (used on some 2010s+ engines) are even faster and work without oil pressure (so VANOS works at idle and cold start), but cost more and need their own motors + power. N42 went hydraulic.

Why VANOS needs the two-stage oil pump

Cross-ref: See two-stage oil pump chapter. Cross-ref: See cooling & oil circuits chapter, oil component #8.

VANOS needs ~2 bar at idle to actuate. The two-stage pump runs both stages at low rpm specifically to guarantee that pressure → which is why pump deactivation thresholds are tied to "above 2 bar AND above 2000 rpm."

The 60° marker on the combined plot

The "60°" annotation on the central curve in IMG 003040 marks a valve-overlap window of roughly 60° — the crank-angle range over which intake and exhaust are simultaneously open. VANOS lets the DME slide this window earlier or later in the cycle to suit conditions.

Key Facts

  • VANOS = variable valve timing (BMW's hydraulic cam phaser).
  • N42 has double VANOS — both intake and exhaust cams phase independently.
  • 11 individual parts in the phaser: housing, front plate, torsion spring, locking pin, rotor, back plate, blade, springs, pressure channels A & B, solenoid valve, cam bore holes.
  • Rotor is keyed to camshaft; housing is driven by the timing chain.
  • Oil pressure pushes rotor vane → cam phases relative to crank.
  • Solenoid valve under DME control selects channel A (advance) or B (retard).
  • Locking pin holds rotor at default position when oil pressure is zero (cold start, engine off).
  • Torsion spring biases rotor against valvetrain torque pulses.
  • Needs ~2 bar oil pressure to actuate → reason for two-stage oil pump always running both stages at low rpm.
  • VANOS shifts curve, Valvetronic shifts height — together = fully variable valve event.
  • Combined valve-overlap window roughly 60° of crank angle.
  • Max lift: 10 mm; Min lift (Valvetronic): 0.3 mm.
