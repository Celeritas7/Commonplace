N42 Engine

N42 Cooling & Oil Circuits

Source images: IMG20250515002924, IMG20250515002932

Overview

The N42 runs two heat-exchanging circuits in parallel — a coolant circuit and an oil circuit — with an oil-to-water heat exchanger as the bridge between them. The thermostat is MAP-controlled (electronically heated, not just wax-pellet — DME varies engine temp by load demand). The system has split short/long coolant circuits to speed warm-up, an expansion (auxiliary) tank, and dual-purpose heating for the cabin (heater core) and oil pre-warming.

Working Principle

The two parallel circuits

Notebook: "Circuits: 1. Coolant circuit 2. Oil circuit — both circuits exchange heat in between."

The two fluids carry heat between different parts of the engine, with the oil-to-water heat exchanger at the interface either dumping oil heat to coolant (normal operation) or pulling heat from coolant to warm oil (cold-start).

The radiator stack carries cooling for the engine, AC condenser, and (on automatic transmission cars) a separate ATF cooler.

MAP-controlled thermostat

Notebook: "Thermostat valve — it is made MAP-controlled. To fix engine temp. If thermostat opening is less, engine temp is high — & vice versa."

Classic wax-pellet thermostats open at a fixed coolant temperature (e.g., 88 °C). The N42 uses a MAP-(map-)controlled thermostat — an electrically heated thermostat where the DME can intentionally heat the wax (or actuate a solenoid) to open the thermostat early based on: - High load (engine making lots of heat → want cooler running to prevent knock). - Sport mode (DME wants tighter thermal control). - Coolant-temperature target from the engine-load map.

This shifts the effective coolant set-point dynamically — typically 105 °C at part load (high temp = better fuel economy) and 95 °C at full load (cooler = denser charge, less knock).

Reading "MAP-controlled" — in BMW dealer-speak "MAP" can be either Manifold Absolute Pressure (the sensor) or "engine map" (the DME data table). For the thermostat the second reading fits best — the DME uses its load map to decide thermostat opening. See Open Questions.

Detailed Component Notes

Why "double temperature sensor"

Modern engines need a coolant-temperature signal for: 1. DME (fuel enrichment, ignition timing, thermostat control, fan switching). 2. Instrument cluster (driver display).

Putting two NTC thermistors in one housing with two separate electrical pinouts gives redundancy and avoids having two separate fittings into the coolant line.

Why spray nozzles on the outlet (exhaust) side

The exhaust valve in each combustion chamber is the hottest single point in the engine. The piston crown directly under the exhaust valve sees the most heat → that side of the piston needs the most oil cooling. The squirter is aimed there, not at the centre or intake side.

Cold-start fuel economy bonus

Notebook (oil-to-water HX): "at cold phase — increases & reduces warm-up period, also increases fuel efficiency."

Cold oil = very high viscosity = high friction = high FHP = low BHP + wasted fuel. By warming the oil quickly via the coolant (which warms faster than the oil bath), the engine reaches its low-friction operating viscosity sooner → better cold-start fuel economy by ~5–10% on a winter trip.

Oil change interval extended

Notebook: "Normal — increases life of oil; oil changing period increased."

In normal hot operation, the oil-to-water HX bleeds heat out of the oil → oil stays cooler → oxidation rate drops → additives last longer → longer drain interval. Pairs with BMW's CBS (Condition-Based Service) scheme.

Key Facts

  • N42 has two parallel circuits — coolant + oil — coupled by the oil-to-water heat exchanger.
  • MAP-controlled thermostat lets DME shift coolant set-point by load (e.g., 105 °C part-load, 95 °C full-load).
  • 8 coolant components: radiator, fan, thermostat, water pump, expansion tank, double temp sensor, heater core, oil-water HX.
  • 8 oil components: two-stage pump, filter, oil-water HX, ducts+pan, piston squirters, cam spray rails, pump drive module, VANOS control channel.
  • Piston squirters aim at the outlet/exhaust side (hottest spot).
  • Oil-water HX warms oil cold, cools oil hot — both directions are useful.
  • Cold-start warmup → oil viscosity drops fast → less friction → better cold-running fuel economy.
  • Normal hot operation → oil stays cooler → longer oil-change interval (BMW CBS).
  • Cooling pack also serves AC condenser and automatic-transmission ATF cooler.
