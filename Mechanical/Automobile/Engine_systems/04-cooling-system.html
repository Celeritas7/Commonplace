Engine Systems

Cooling System

Source images: IMG20250514112452, IMG20250514112502, IMG20250514112508, IMG20250514112516

Overview

The engine cooling system carries waste combustion heat from the cylinder walls and head out to the atmosphere via the radiator, holds the coolant at a controlled temperature with a thermostat, raises the coolant boiling point with pressurization, and uses antifreeze to keep it liquid in winter. The same loop also sources hot coolant for the cabin heater.

Working Principle

A water pump circulates coolant (≈50/50 water + ethylene-glycol antifreeze) through the water jackets cast into the cylinder block and head, where it absorbs heat. Coolant then flows to the radiator, where the fan and ram air pull heat out, then back to the engine. A thermostat valve stays closed until the engine reaches operating temperature so warm-up happens quickly; once open, it lets coolant reach the radiator. The pressure cap on the auxiliary tank holds the system above atmospheric pressure, raising water's boiling point to about 110 °C so coolant doesn't boil at hard load.

Cold-start vs warm operation

  • Cold engine — thermostat closed. Coolant only circulates inside the engine (block & head); radiator loop bypassed. Engine warms up fast.
  • At operating temp — thermostat opens. Coolant flows to radiator, gets cooled, returns. Loop balances heat in vs heat out.

Detailed Component Notes

Pressure cap and the 110 °C boiling point

At atmospheric pressure water boils at 100 °C — too close to engine operating temperature. By holding the system at roughly 1 bar gauge (≈2 bar absolute), the pressure cap raises the boiling point to about 110 °C, giving a safe margin. The cap has both a pressure-relief (max) and a vacuum-relief (min) function — if either side fails, the cap is "faulty" and the system can't maintain its boiling-point margin.

Antifreeze (ethylene-glycol mix)

Notebook: "in cold countries, water freezes at 0 °C. So if we mix 50% coolant & 50% water, its freezing temp decreases. Hence problem sorts out."

A 50/50 mix of ethylene-glycol coolant and water has a freezing point around −37 °C, so the engine block doesn't crack in winter. The same mix also raises the boiling point further.

Thermostat — why it matters (notebook bullet "imp")

The thermostat is flagged in the notes as "important" because: 1. Warming-up period — keeps coolant in the engine until it's hot, so: 2. Lubrication-oil temperature rises faster → oil viscosity drops → friction drops → frictional horse power loss decreases → BHP increases. 3. Avoids wastage of fuel (a cold engine runs rich and inefficiently). 4. A radiator flap can be added in extreme cold for the same purpose.

Heater core

The cabin heater is a small auxiliary radiator that's permanently in the coolant loop. A blower fan blows cabin air across it; a valve (or just a damper) controls how much air the driver routes through.

Key Facts

  • Operating temp held around 90–100 °C; pressurised so boiling ≈ 110 °C.
  • Below 0 °C, plain water freezes → 50/50 antifreeze mix drops freezing point well below zero.
  • Thermostat closed when cold = fast warm-up = lower frictional HP loss = higher BHP & better fuel economy.
  • Pressure cap has max (relief) and min (vacuum) settings — failure on either side = system can't hold its boiling-point margin.
  • Same coolant loop feeds the cabin heater core.
  • Components: coolant, coolant tank, radiator, fan, pump, thermostat, pressure cap, water jackets, hose pipes, radiator fins.
