Engine Systems

Lubrication System

Source images: IMG20250514112524, IMG20250514112536, IMG20250514112816

Overview

The lubrication system pumps oil from the sump, through a strainer and filter, then up to every moving interface inside the engine — bearings, camshaft, cylinder walls, valvetrain — and back down to the sump under gravity. Beyond reducing wear, oil also cools hot components and cleans combustion residues. The notebook page covers the three jobs of engine oil, the full component list, system pressure, and the detailed oil-flow schematic.

Working Principle

The oil pump (gear or rotor type, crank-driven) draws oil from the oil sump through a strainer, pushes it through the oil filter, and then up the main oil gallery. From the gallery, oil branches to: 1. Crankshaft bearings → through drillings in the crank → crank-pin and connecting-rod big-end bearings → up the rod to the gudgeon pin and piston. 2. Camshaft bearings → cam lobes / tappets. 3. Nozzles / squirters spray oil onto the cylinder walls and the underside of the piston crown.

Oil that has done its work runs by gravity down the return line back to the sump. A pressure-relief valve dumps oil back to the sump if pump output exceeds the set pressure. An optional oil cooler (a small radiator) bleeds heat from the oil — important on high-output and diesel engines.

Detailed Component Notes

Oil rating — "5W40"

The notebook flags 5W40 as the example viscosity grade. SAE multigrade: - 5W — winter rating (low number = pumps well cold). - 40 — high-temperature operating viscosity (around 100 °C).

The same page lists the unrelated brake-fluid color code as a side note: DOT 3 = clear/yellowish, DOT 4 = yellowish — kept here verbatim but really belongs in the brake-system chapter.

System pressure (notebook bullets)

"1 bar = 1 N/mm² = 14.47 psi" "Filter after pump." "Pressure is 2–3 bar pressure of oil — 0 min, 8.3 max."

Interpretation: nominal hot-idle oil pressure ~2–3 bar; cold/start can spike up to ~8.3 bar (relief valve open); minimum at idle can briefly read 0 on a worn engine (warning territory).

Strict unit note: 1 bar ≈ 14.504 psi (not 14.47 — minor trainer rounding); and 1 bar = 0.1 N/mm², not 1 N/mm². The notebook conversion line is therefore arithmetically off — see Open Questions.

Filter placement

"Filter after pump" — the filter sits downstream of the pump so the pump pressurises oil through the filter media. The strainer (coarse) is upstream of the pump to protect it from large debris.

Oil cooler

"How does it get cooled? Oil cooler → Radiator"

The oil cooler is plumbed either into the engine-coolant loop (so the engine's water cooling indirectly cools the oil) or as a stand-alone air-cooled radiator near the main radiator.

Three jobs of engine oil (page header)

The notebook leads with: lubricating, cooling, cleaning. - Lubricating — separates sliding/rolling surfaces with an oil film. - Cooling — oil sprayed at piston undersides and circulating through hot bearings carries heat back to the sump (and to the oil cooler). - Cleaning — additives suspend combustion soot and acid neutralisers; the filter captures the resulting particulates.

Key Facts

  • Three jobs of engine oil: lubricate, cool, clean.
  • Example grade: 5W40 (5W cold rating, 40 hot rating).
  • Normal hot oil pressure ≈ 2–3 bar; cold-start can reach ≈ 8.3 bar; idle floor ≈ 0 on a worn engine.
  • Pump intake protected by strainer; filter sits after the pump.
  • Oil cooled by an oil cooler (water-to-oil or air-cooled).
  • Pressure-relief valve bypasses excess oil back to the sump.
  • Same loop lubricates crank bearings, cam bearings, valvetrain, gudgeon pin, cylinder walls — return is by gravity.
