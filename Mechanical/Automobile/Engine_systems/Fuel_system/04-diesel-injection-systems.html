Diesel Injection Systems (Pre-combustion, Swirl, Direct)

Source images: IMG20250515003430, IMG20250515003451, IMG20250515003456, IMG20250515003504

Page tag: "Cyl 12[3/7] Deisel" — same lecture series as the diesel combustion pages.

Overview

Three diesel injection-chamber strategies (pre-combustion, swirl chamber, direct injection) with their advantages, disadvantages, and pressure / geometry parameters. Plus the injection pump — what makes it work, what factors influence injection, and the manufacturer-by-manufacturer assignment (MAN = direct; Benz = pre-ignition; DMG = swirl chamber). The history closes with Rudolf Diesel's contribution being "idea + implementation."

Working Principle

Why combustion-chamber design matters at all

Diesel has the after-combustion problem (see diesel combustion cycle) — burning too late in the power stroke wastes work. The combustion-chamber design controls how fast and how completely fuel-air mixing happens during the brief window of effective combustion (near TDC).

The three strategies — at a glance

Strategy Where fuel ignites first Injection pressure Used by (notebook)
Pre-combustion chamber In a small auxiliary chamber, then jets into main chamber ~ 100–250 bar Mercedes-Benz (Benz: "pre-ignition")
Swirl chamber In a side chamber that creates a swirl, jets into main 250–500 bar (notebook says 250-500 bar injection pressure, 50-60% of combustion chamber volume in side chamber) DMG (Daimler-Motoren-Gesellschaft)
Direct injection (DI) Straight into the main combustion chamber bowl up to 1600 bar MAN

Pre-combustion chamber (top half of IMG 003430)

Notebook: "Pre-ignition (1/3 volume) — 2/3 ignition in combustion chamber. Hole — fuel comes in the swirl." "Advantages: 1. Low thermal loads as combustion is distributed. 2. Lower soot emissions (soot: black smoke produced by diesel). 3. Running performance is better.

Disadvantages: 1. Flow losses in holes. 2. High development cost."

Concept: Fuel is injected into a small pre-chamber that holds about 1/3 of the total combustion volume. The pre-chamber lights off; the burning charge then shoots through small holes into the main chamber, igniting the rest of the air there. The split combustion smooths out the pressure rise → quieter, less stressed pistons. But the holes cause flow losses (pumping work going both ways), and the head is more complex / expensive.

Swirl chamber (bottom half of IMG 003430)

Notebook: "Injection pressure of fuel: 250–500 bar." "50–60% of combustion chamber [volume in swirl chamber]." "Fuel is spread in the direction of swirl." "Heating takes place inside swirl chamber so heat losses are low." "Advantage: High efficiency." "Disadvantages: High thermal stresses on piston head & cylinder head gasket; hot spot." Note: "Piston head designed by BMW."

Concept: Air is forced into a swirl chamber built into the piston crown (or cylinder head) during compression. The swirling motion vaporises and mixes the injected fuel rapidly. Most of the combustion happens inside the chamber, where heat is contained → lower heat losses → higher efficiency. The downside: the chamber walls get hot — thermal stress hot-spot at the head and a tougher job for the head gasket.

Direct injection (IMG 003451)

Notebook: "Direct injection. Problem: The mixing of fuel with air. Piston cavity (shapes): Dome-shaped, Spherical-shaped, Omega-shaped. Nozzle pressure: up to 1600 bar. ('high pressure like bullet: F = P · A, so F is more')

Advantages: 1. Highly efficient — up to 45% nearly. 2. No knocking — as no ignition delay. 3. Minimum consumption of fuel.

Disadvantages: 1. Loud engine noise. 2. Exhaust less than optimal. 3. Very high demands on injection system.

Solutions: 1. Soundproofing of engine compartment ('no sound'). 2. Exhaust gas recirculation (EGR). 3. Oxidation catalytic converter. 4. Better injection pump technology."

Concept: Fuel is sprayed at very high pressure (up to 1600 bar) directly into the main combustion chamber, where the piston-bowl shape generates the swirl that mixes fuel and air. With high enough pressure and the right bowl geometry, you get most of the swirl-chamber efficiency without the wall-heat-loss problem. Modern automotive diesels are all DI.

The trainer's F = P · A note is the engineering reason for going to such extreme pressures: a small-area injector nozzle (≈ 0.2 mm² total) needs enormous pressure to push enough fuel mass through in the milliseconds available. At 1600 bar, even a 0.2 mm² nozzle delivers high mass flow.

Multicylinder injection pump: [diagram showing piston + camshaft]"

Diesel doesn't throttle air — the air volume stays constant; engine power is set entirely by how much fuel is injected. So the injection pump is the only fuel-power lever. The "7 influencing factors" are the full design space of injection — every one of them is a tunable knob on a modern common-rail system.

Pump components (IMG 003504)

Notebook: "Pump components: 1. RPM controller 2. Injection adjuster (MAP — Manifold Air Pressure controlled) 3. Cut-off valve"

Schematic note: "Solenoid valve to cut off fuel" — to stop the vehicle. "When pressure blocks it, opens."

Three pump-side controls: - RPM controller — sets fuel quantity per rpm (governor). - Injection adjuster — adjusts injection timing based on MAP (intake manifold pressure, i.e. boost level on a turbo-diesel). - Cut-off valve — a solenoid that cuts fuel flow to shut the engine off. (Mechanical diesels keep running as long as fuel is flowing — no spark to cut. This valve is how the key-off command actually stops the engine.)

Manufacturer fingerprint (notebook line)

Notebook: "MAN: Direct injection. Benz: Pre-ignition. DMG: Swirl chamber."

Historical 1930s–40s industry split: - MAN AG (Maschinenfabrik Augsburg-Nürnberg) — pioneer of automotive direct injection. - Mercedes-Benz — pre-combustion (indirect injection) on early road-car diesels. - DMG (Daimler-Motoren-Gesellschaft, the pre-Daimler-Benz entity) — swirl chamber.

Rudolf Diesel's two contributions

Notebook: "Rudolf Diesel: Invention consists of two parts — idea & implementation."

Diesel's patent (1893) covered both the thermodynamic concept (compression-ignition cycle) and the physical implementation of an engine that ran on it. The trainer's point: engineering invention is rarely just an idea — it's idea + working hardware.

Stroke length (page bottom)

Notebook: "400 mm stroke length — ..." (rest cut off)

400 mm stroke is far longer than any car diesel — that figure refers to a large industrial / marine diesel (e.g., Sulzer / Wärtsilä two-stroke marine diesels run 2 m+ strokes; medium-speed gen-set engines run 300-500 mm). The trainer likely was contrasting passenger-car diesel (stroke ~ 90 mm) with large-engine practice as a "scale of things" aside.

Components

Component Function
Pre-combustion chamber 1/3-volume auxiliary chamber where initial ignition occurs (Benz).
Swirl chamber 50–60% volume side chamber that generates air swirl during compression (DMG).
Piston bowl Shaped cavity in the piston crown (dome / spherical / omega) for DI swirl (MAN).
Injection nozzle Spray tip; multi-hole; rated up to 1600 bar (DI).
High-pressure injection pump Cam-driven multi-piston pump; meters fuel quantity per injection event.
RPM controller Pump governor — sets fuel per engine rpm.
Injection adjuster MAP-controlled timing trimmer (advances/retards for boost).
Cut-off solenoid valve Stops fuel flow on key-off; only way to shut a diesel down.
EGR (Exhaust Gas Recirculation) Recirculates exhaust to cool combustion → cuts NOx, reduces "loud engine" pressure rise.
Oxidation catalytic converter Burns off remaining HC and CO in DI diesel exhaust.

Detailed Component Notes

Why DI is "loud"

With no ignition delay buffer (the trainer's phrase), the pressure rises faster at TDC → louder cracking combustion noise. Pre-combustion and swirl-chamber engines have a built-in pressure-rise softener (the chamber acts as a buffer). DI gets efficiency at the cost of needing extra noise-control engineering.

Why piston-bowl shape matters

The trainer lists three shapes: - Dome-shaped — round bump in the centre of the bowl; deflects spray outward. - Spherical-shaped — simple cup; the most basic. - Omega-shaped (ω) — re-entrant bowl with a narrow throat; traps and recirculates the swirling air-fuel mixture for the longest residence time. Standard on modern automotive DI diesels.

Each shape sets the swirl ratio and trapped vortex strength — both critical for finishing combustion fast.

EGR (named in notebook solutions list)

Exhaust Gas Recirculation feeds inert burnt gases back into the intake, lowering peak combustion temperature → drops NOx formation and pressure-rise rate → quieter. Trade-off: more soot. Pairs with the catalytic converter or DPF (diesel particulate filter, post-2007).

Key Facts

  • Three diesel combustion-chamber strategies: pre-combustion (Benz), swirl chamber (DMG), direct injection (MAN).
  • Pre-combustion chamber ≈ 1/3 total volume; swirl chamber ≈ 50–60% total volume.
  • Direct-injection nozzle pressure: up to 1600 bar — drives mixing in the brief combustion window.
  • DI advantages: ~45% thermal efficiency, no knock (no ignition delay), low fuel consumption.
  • DI disadvantages: loud combustion noise, sub-optimal exhaust, expensive injection system.
  • DI solutions: soundproofing, EGR, oxidation cat-converter, better pump tech.
  • Diesel power is set by fuel quantity injected — air volume is constant (no throttle).
  • 7 influencing factors for injection: start of feed, start of injection, length, progression, pressure, direction, excess air.
  • Pump components: RPM controller, MAP-controlled injection adjuster, cut-off solenoid valve.
  • Cut-off valve is how a diesel is actually turned off (no spark to cut).
  • Rudolf Diesel's invention = idea + implementation (1893 patent).
