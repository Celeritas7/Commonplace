Fuel System

Fuel System — Overview (Petrol / MPFI)

Source images: IMG20250514111958, IMG20250514112016, IMG20250514112026

Overview

The fuel system carries petrol from the tank to the cylinder under controlled pressure, meters out an exact quantity per cylinder per cycle through electronic injectors, and returns unused pressurised fuel back to the tank. The page covers the component list, two versions of the flow diagram (one with electronic-control overlays), and the five advantages of MPFI (Multi-Point Fuel Injection) over the older carburettor.

Working Principle

The fuel pump (in-tank, immersed in petrol for cooling) pressurises fuel through the fuel filter and along the fuel pipe / line to the fuel rail — a manifold that holds petrol at the regulated rail pressure. A fuel-rail pressure sensor measures actual rail pressure; a pressure relief valve dumps excess fuel back to the tank via the return line, holding rail pressure at the setpoint. From the rail, one electronic fuel injector per cylinder receives a pulsed signal from the ECM (Engine Control Module) that opens it for a precise time — injector open-time × rail pressure determines the fuel mass for that cycle. The fuel then mixes with intake air in the inlet manifold, is drawn past the intake valve on the intake stroke, and lands in the combustion chamber.

Components

Component Function
Fuel tank Petrol reservoir; vented through the tank ventilation system to manage vapour.
Tank ventilation system Carbon-canister system that traps fuel vapour from the tank and purges it into the intake — keeps tank pressure stable, prevents HC emissions.
Fuel pump In-tank electric pump; pressurises fuel out of the tank. Runs at ignition-on.
Fuel filter Removes particulates that would clog injectors.
Fuel pipe / line High-pressure line from filter to rail.
Fuel rail Manifold holding all injectors at a common regulated pressure.
Fuel-rail pressure sensor Reports rail pressure to ECU for closed-loop control.
Pressure relief valve Bleeds excess fuel back to tank to hold rail pressure at setpoint.
Fuel injector Solenoid-actuated needle valve; opens for a pulse-width set by ECU to meter fuel mass.
Fuel return line Carries relief-valve overflow back to the tank.
Intake (inlet) manifold Distributes fuelled air to each cylinder's intake port.
Intake valve Cylinder-head valve that opens during the intake stroke.
Combustion chamber Where the fuel-air mixture is ignited (in petrol, by spark from the ignition system).
ECM / ECU Engine Control Module; receives sensor inputs and commands actuators (injectors, ignition coils, etc.).

Detailed Component Notes

Why a return line?

Demand at the injectors varies wildly (idle vs full throttle). The pump delivers a constant flow; the relief valve dumps the excess back to the tank so rail pressure stays steady regardless of injector demand. Modern "returnless" systems use a variable-speed pump instead — but the dealer-training era covered here is mostly return-style.

Why an in-tank pump

  • Submerged in fuel → cool (no vapour-lock).
  • Petrol is the lubricant for the pump's bearings.
  • Quieter — fuel absorbs pump noise.

MPFI vs carburettor — the 5 advantages (page bottom of IMG 112026)

Notebook: "Advantages of MPFI: 1. Controls A/F mixture so it produces same amount of power in each cylinder. So it causes less vibration in engine. 2. No need to crank the engine twice or thrice in cold starting like in carburetor. 3. Immediate response in case of acceleration & deceleration. 4. Since engine is controlled by ECM (Engine Control Module), more accurate amount of A/F mixture utilized. So proper use of fuel takes place. 5. Mileage of vehicle improved."

Why MPFI is better, in one line: per-cylinder metering + closed-loop control + electronic actuation = exact stoichiometric A/F at any load → smoother, cleaner, more efficient than a single-throat mechanical carburettor feeding a long shared intake.

Closed-loop control summary

Notebook (bottom of IMG 112026): "ECM get signal from all sensors. Manipulate it and send commands to actuator." / "ECM should send control to all actuator. ECM should get signal from all sensor."

Standard control-system framing: - Sensors → ECM (inputs: coolant temp, RPM, MAP, MAF, lambda, knock, throttle, etc.) - ECM → control logic (load calc, fuel quantity, ignition advance, etc.) - Actuators ← ECM (outputs: injectors, ignition coils, throttle motor, IAC valve, VANOS, etc.)

Key Facts

  • Flow order: tank → pump → filter → pipe → rail (+ pressure sensor) → injector → manifold → valve → chamber.
  • Excess fuel loops back to tank via the pressure-relief valve and return line.
  • Fuel pump is in-tank (cool, lubricated by petrol, quiet).
  • One injector per cylinder (MPFI) → equal fuel per cylinder → smooth.
  • ECM logic: all sensors → ECM → all actuators. Closed loop on lambda, knock, etc.
  • MPFI vs carburettor: smoother, no cold-cranking ritual, faster transient response, more accurate A/F, better mileage.
  • Tank ventilation captures fuel vapours via carbon canister.
