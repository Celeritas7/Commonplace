Exhaust System

Pollutants & Catalytic Converter

Source images: IMG20250514112321

Overview

The three regulated automotive pollutants — CO, HC, NOx — and what the catalytic converter does to them. The page contrasts the pellet-type and monolithic (honeycomb) catalyst constructions and the two-way vs three-way converter difference. It also touches on the muffler's role (back-pressure reduction, Pt/Pd/Rh material), lead in petrol (lubricating property, now phased out), and diesel film formation.

Working Principle

The combustion equation (ideal vs real)

Notebook: "Fuel + Air → HC + O₂ + N → H₂O + CO₂ + N"

Ideal combustion of a hydrocarbon fuel: $$\text{C}_x\text{H}_y + \text{O}_2 + \text{N}_2 \;\longrightarrow\; \text{CO}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{N}_2$$

This would produce nothing but CO₂, water, and inert nitrogen. In reality, combustion is incomplete and high-temperature, so you also get:

Pollutant Source What it is
CO (carbon monoxide) Incomplete combustion (not enough O₂) Toxic; bonds to haemoglobin.
HC (unburnt hydrocarbons) Misfire, cold engine, rich mixture Smog precursor.
NOx (nitrogen oxides) High combustion temperature → N₂ + O₂ react Acid rain + smog.

Notebook: "HC & CO level decreased by modern system" — meaning closed-loop EFI + cat dropped HC and CO by an order of magnitude over carb-era cars. NOx is the harder one.

Lead in petrol (historical note)

Notebook: "Lead (Pb) is added to impart lubricating property."

Tetra-ethyl lead (TEL) was added to petrol from the 1920s to about 2000: - Boosted octane (anti-knock) cheaply. - Lubricated valve seats in soft-iron heads — the lead deposits acted as a sacrificial cushion.

Phased out globally because: - Poisons catalytic converters (the lead coats the Pt/Pd/Rh and kills activity). - Toxic to humans and ecosystems.

Modern petrol uses oxygenates (MTBE, ethanol) for octane and modern alloy heads have hardened valve seats that don't need lead lubrication.

Diesel film

Notebook: "Diesel can form film."

Diesel fuel has higher viscosity and oilier character than petrol — it can form a thin lubricant film on cylinder walls, useful in some applications (e.g., HPDI fuel injectors in older diesels self-lubricate from the diesel itself). Why it's listed here on the pollutants page is unclear — possibly a sidebar comparing fuels.

Muffler / silencer

Notebook: "Muffler: 1. Back pressure is reduced in muffler. 2. Pt, Palladium, Rhodium."

Two points conflated on one bullet: 1. Muffler design — internal baffles and Helmholtz chambers cancel sound waves while keeping flow restriction (back-pressure) as low as possible. High back-pressure = engine has to push harder against exhaust = power loss. Modern mufflers balance noise vs flow. 2. Pt/Pd/Rh — these are the catalytic converter precious-metal coatings, not muffler materials. Almost certainly a notebook layout error: the trainer wrote the catalyst metals on the muffler bullet. Kept in original order; corrected interpretation in the Components table.

Catalytic converter — pellet vs monolithic

Type Construction Notes
Pellet type Contains a bed made up of thousands of small beads coated with the catalyst. Exhaust gas passes over the beads. Older design; vibration / heat-shock breaks beads → catalyst migration & restriction. Largely obsolete.
Monolithic type A honeycomb ceramic block (cordierite) with thin walls coated with Pt, Pd, & Rh mounted in a stainless-steel container. Modern standard. Lower back-pressure than pellet; better heat shock resistance; lower precious-metal loading per cat.

Two-way vs three-way converter

Type Reduces / Oxidises Catalyst Fitment
Two-way (oxidation cat) HC + CO → CO₂ + H₂O Pt, Pd Older petrol cars (pre-1989), modern diesel engines (NOx handled separately).
Three-way HC + CO + NOx → CO₂ + H₂O + N₂ Pt + Pd + Rh (Rh does NOx reduction) All modern petrol cars. Requires λ = 1 closed-loop operation.

Notebook: "Two-way catalytic converter — HC & CO" / "Three-way converter — [also NOx via Rh]"

The rhodium is what enables NOx reduction at the same time as HC/CO oxidation; it's also the most expensive of the three (£10,000+/kg historically) — which is why used cats are stripped for precious metals.

Detailed Component Notes

Why honeycomb beats pellet

  • Lower back-pressure — straight channels through the honeycomb vs tortuous path through beads.
  • Faster light-off — thin walls heat up quickly when the cold engine first starts (catalyst is inactive below ~250 °C).
  • Mechanical robustness — honeycomb survives engine vibration better; beads pulverise over time.

Why a "three-way" cat needs λ = 1

  • The oxidation reaction (HC, CO → CO₂ + H₂O) wants excess O₂ → lean exhaust.
  • The reduction reaction (NOx → N₂) wants excess reducing agent (CO and H₂) → rich exhaust.
  • The two needs are opposite. Only at λ = 1 (exactly stoichiometric) does the exhaust carry just enough O₂ for oxidation while leaving just enough CO for reduction. Both reactions proceed at high rate in the narrow stoichiometric window.

Lead poisoning the cat

A leaded-fuel tank into a cat-equipped car will kill the converter in a few hundred kilometres — Pb deposits coat the precious metal sites. This is why introduction of unleaded fuel was a hard prerequisite for the rollout of 3-way cats in the 1970s–80s.

What about diesel particulate filter (DPF)?

Not in the notes (post-dates 2007 mandates). A DPF is a wall-flow filter that traps soot particles, then regenerates by burning them off periodically. Diesel after-treatment in the modern era is DOC (diesel oxidation cat, 2-way) → DPF → SCR / NOx storage for NOx.

Key Facts

  • 3 regulated pollutants: CO, HC, NOx. Plus CO₂ (greenhouse, not toxic but regulated separately).
  • Ideal combustion: Fuel + air → CO₂ + H₂O + N₂. Real combustion adds CO, HC, NOx.
  • Catalyst metals: Pt + Pd (oxidation), Rh (reduction).
  • Two-way cat = HC + CO only (diesel). Three-way cat = also NOx (petrol at λ = 1).
  • Pellet vs monolithic — modern cats are monolithic honeycomb (lower back-pressure, faster light-off, more robust).
  • Lead in petrol lubricated valves AND boosted octane historically — but poisons cats, so phased out.
  • Muffler reduces back-pressure while cancelling noise (the trainer accidentally tagged the cat metals onto the muffler bullet).
  • Catalyst light-off temperature ≈ 250–300 °C; cold-start emissions dominate total trip emissions because of this lag.
