Exhaust System

Oxygen Sensor & Lambda (Air–Fuel Ratio Control)

Source images: IMG20250514112313

Overview

The oxygen sensor (lambda probe) sits in the exhaust stream and reports the residual oxygen content of the burnt gas back to the ECU. From that, the ECU can infer whether the cylinders ran lean (too much air) or rich (too much fuel) and trim injection time on the next cycle. The page defines lean vs rich mixture, what each does to emissions (NOx vs HC/CO), why stoichiometric is the target, and the lambda (λ) excess-air factor.

Working Principle

Lean vs rich

Mixture A/F ratio Notebook description Emission effect
Lean A/F > 14.7 (more air than stoichiometric) "high amount of air" Provides low HC & CO; high combustion temp reached → high NOx.
Stoichiometric A/F = 14.7 : 1 implicit All fuel meets exact air needed → ideal for the 3-way catalyst.
Rich A/F < 14.7 (less air than stoichiometric) "less amount of air" Lower NOx (lower peak temp), but HC & CO higher (unburnt fuel).

The trainer's note next to "lean": "A/F ratio is higher". Next to "rich": "A/F ratio is low" — meaning the air number on the top of the ratio is higher (lean) or lower (rich) than the stoichiometric 14.7.

Why stoichiometric is the target

Notebook: "So A/F mixture should be exactly stoichiometric." "So catalyst should operate nearly 90% efficient."

A 3-way catalyst (Pt/Pd/Rh) needs to both reduce NOx (needs lean exhaust) and oxidise HC + CO (needs rich exhaust). The only window where it can do both simultaneously is the narrow ±1% band around λ = 1. Outside this window, the catalyst efficiency drops sharply. Modern engines keep dithering across this band at high frequency (the lambda "switch") so on average the cat sees both lean and rich pulses.

The lambda excess-air factor (λ)

Notebook: "So any deficiency or excess air consumed by engine is usually expressed by excess air factor called lambda."

Definition: $$\lambda = \frac{(A/F)\text{actual}}{(A/F)\text{stoichiometric}}$$

For petrol where (A/F)stoich = 14.7: - λ = 1.0 — exactly stoichiometric. - λ > 1 — lean (more air than needed). - λ < 1 — rich (less air than needed).

λ Meaning Notebook number
1.0 Stoichiometric air "for Stoichiometric air, lambda = 1.0"
0.95 5% deficient air → rich mixture "for 5% deficient air, lambda = 0.95 → Rich mixture"
1.05 5% excess air → lean mixture (implied)

Closed-loop A/F control

   └─────────────┬───────────┘
                 │ burnt gas
                 ▼
   ┌─────────────────────────┐
   │  Exhaust manifold       │
   └─────────────┬───────────┘

                 ▼
   ┌─────────────────────────┐
   │  O₂ sensor (lambda)     │  measures residual O₂ in exhaust
   └─────────────┬───────────┘
                 │  voltage signal
                 ▼
   ┌─────────────────────────┐
   │  ECU                    │  compares to target λ = 1
   └─────────────┬───────────┘

                 │
   ┌─────────────▼───────────┐
   │  Injectors              │  fire next cycle with corrected pulse-width
   └─────────────────────────┘

The control loop runs every few milliseconds. Below operating temperature (cold engine), the sensor isn't hot enough to function (~300 °C minimum), so the ECU runs open-loop using a base map until the sensor heats up — that's where modern sensors include a heater element to come live faster.

Components / Definitions

Term Meaning
Stoichiometric A/F The chemically exact air mass needed to burn a unit fuel mass with no excess of either. For petrol: 14.7 : 1 by mass.
Lean mixture A/F > 14.7 (more air than needed). λ > 1.
Rich mixture A/F < 14.7 (less air than needed). λ < 1.
Lambda (λ) Excess-air factor = (actual A/F) / (stoichiometric A/F).
NOx Nitrogen oxides (NO + NO₂). Formed at high combustion temperatures (lean burn).
HC Unburnt hydrocarbons. Formed when fuel doesn't fully burn (rich, cold, misfire).
CO Carbon monoxide. Formed when there's not enough oxygen to make CO₂ (rich).
O₂ sensor / lambda probe ZrO₂ ceramic element that generates a voltage based on the O₂ partial pressure difference between exhaust and reference air. Step response: ~0.1 V (lean) ↔ ~0.9 V (rich) across λ = 1.

Detailed Component Notes

Why high temp means high NOx

At low temperature, atmospheric N₂ is inert. Above about 1800 °C, the N≡N triple bond can break and N atoms combine with O atoms → NO, then NO₂. Lean burn = excess O₂ + high combustion temperature → ideal conditions for NOx formation. Diesel engines (always lean, high CR) are intrinsically high-NOx; that's why they need DPF + SCR after-treatment.

Why rich means high HC and CO

Not enough oxygen to complete the combustion → unburnt fuel fragments (HC) and partial-oxidation product (CO) come out the exhaust.

Honda i-tech reference

Notebook top: "Honda: i-tech."

i-tech (correctly Honda i-VTEC, or could refer to i-DSI) is mentioned as an example of an early closed-loop lean-burn engine technology. The trainer was likely pointing out that going lean intentionally (Honda's lean-burn engines of the late '90s) was a way to chase fuel economy at the cost of needing NOx after-treatment. Honda eventually abandoned lean-burn for the same reason — the NOx storage catalyst was expensive and finicky.

Key Facts

  • Lean = high air, rich = low air. Reference is stoichiometric.
  • Stoichiometric petrol A/F = 14.7 : 1 by mass.
  • λ = actual A/F ÷ stoichiometric A/F. λ = 1.0 stoich, λ < 1 rich, λ > 1 lean.
  • Notebook: λ = 0.95 → 5% deficient air → rich mixture.
  • Lean produces low HC/CO but high NOx; rich is the inverse.
  • 3-way catalyst only works in a narrow ±1% band around λ = 1 → closed-loop control via O₂ sensor.
  • Target catalyst efficiency: ≈ 90% conversion.
  • O₂ sensor needs ~ 300 °C to function → modern sensors include a heater for cold-start.
