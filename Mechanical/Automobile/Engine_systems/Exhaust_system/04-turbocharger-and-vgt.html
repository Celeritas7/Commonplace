Exhaust System

Turbocharger & VGT (Variable Geometry Turbocharger)

Source images: IMG20250515003535

Page tag: "Cyl 12[?] Diesel" — from the same diesel-engine lecture series as the Petrol_deisel chapter. Note: This image was originally misfiled in Torque_convertor_gears/ and moved here in Phase 3.

Overview

A turbocharger uses otherwise-wasted exhaust energy to spin a turbine that drives a compressor on the intake side — forcing more air into the cylinder than atmospheric pressure alone would supply. More air = more fuel can be burnt = more power per stroke from the same displacement. VGT (Variable Geometry Turbocharger) adds movable guide vanes to the turbine housing that change the gas flow angle — keeping the turbocharger "in the sweet spot" across the whole rpm range and largely eliminating "turbo-lag". The page also touches the intercooler that cools the compressed intake air before it reaches the cylinder.

Working Principle

Basic turbocharger

Notebook: "Turbocharger / VGT (Variable geometry turbocharger): – Exhaust gases rotate the turbine – Guide vanes change the angle to control the vacuum – So because of VGT 'turbo-lag' is reduced."

A turbocharger has two impellers on a common shaft: - Turbine (exhaust side) — exhaust gas rushing past the curved vanes spins the wheel up to 200,000+ rpm. - Compressor (intake side) — driven by the shared shaft → compresses ambient air → delivers higher-density charge to the engine.

The two impellers are housed in separate scrolls (snail-shell castings) but connected through the centre bearing housing.

Why turbocharging works

At wide-open throttle, an engine wastes roughly 30% of its fuel energy as exhaust heat and pressure. A turbocharger captures part of that — the pressure energy — and uses it to pre-compress the next intake charge. The engine effectively runs at higher altitude in reverse — denser air, more oxygen per stroke, more power.

Without turbo (NA) With turbo
Manifold absolute pressure = ~ 100 kPa (1 bar) at WOT Manifold pressure = 150-250+ kPa (1.5-2.5+ bar) at WOT
Power-per-litre limited by atmospheric density Power-per-litre dramatically higher
Sea-level air density Effectively denser air
Cool intake air Compressed air is HOT — needs intercooler

Variable Geometry (VGT) — solving turbo lag

Notebook: "Guide vanes change the angle to control the vacuum"

Classic problem with a fixed-geometry turbo: the turbine housing is sized for one specific rpm/load. Too small → throttles at high rpm. Too big → slow to spool at low rpm (the famous "turbo lag" — driver hits the throttle, then waits a second before boost arrives).

VGT solves this with movable guide vanes (small flaps) around the turbine inlet: - Low rpm / low exhaust flow — vanes close → small effective throat → exhaust gas accelerated → drives turbine faster than a fixed-size unit at the same flow. Boost arrives quickly. - High rpm / high exhaust flow — vanes open → large throat → low restriction → engine breathes freely without choking on exhaust back-pressure.

Result: boost available across the entire rpm range with no lag — what made modern turbo-diesels (and now turbo-petrols) drive almost as smoothly as larger naturally-aspirated engines.

Vane actuation

The vanes are moved by: - A vacuum diaphragm (older designs) controlled by the engine vacuum system + a solenoid valve. - An electric stepper motor (modern designs) controlled directly by the DME via PWM.

Position is feedback-monitored so the DME knows whether the vanes are obeying commands.

Intercooler

Notebook (lower): "Intercooler — Advantage: ① Cools the hot air"

Compressing air heats it (gas law: $PV = nRT$ → compression raises both P and T). The compressed air leaving the turbo can be 80-150 °C even with ambient air at 20 °C. Hot air is less dense → defeats some of the turbo's density benefit.

An intercooler is an air-to-air (or air-to-water) heat exchanger that sits between the turbo compressor outlet and the intake manifold. Compressed air flows through it; ambient air flows across the fins → drops intake temperature back down to 30-50 °C → density restored → more air mass per stroke.

Bonus: cooler intake = lower combustion temperature peak = lower NOx formation = better emissions.

Detailed Component Notes

Turbine shaft rpm

A modern small turbocharger can spin at 180,000-200,000 rpm. The shaft is floating on a thin oil film (hydrodynamic bearing) or on ceramic ball bearings (ball-bearing turbos — lower friction, faster spool). Oil supply from the engine's main oil gallery is critical — losing oil pressure for even a second destroys the bearings.

Why VGT was first used on diesels

Diesel exhaust is cooler than petrol exhaust (~ 700 °C vs ~ 900 °C). The VGT mechanism's stainless-steel vanes survive better at diesel temperatures. Modern VGT designs (Inconel vanes, ceramic coatings) work on petrol too — first wide BMW use was the N54 (post-notebook era) and N55.

Turbo lag — what causes it

Even with VGT, some lag remains because: 1. The turbo's rotating mass (turbine + compressor + shaft) takes time to accelerate. 2. Exhaust pressure must build up from idle level to drive-pressure level. 3. The boost takes time to fill the intake manifold volume.

VGT addresses (2) — gets useful exhaust pressure faster. Twin-scroll turbos and electric assist turbos address (1) and (3) further.

Why an intercooler matters MORE on a turbo

Ambient air at 25 °C → compressed to 2 bar → temperature rises to maybe 130 °C → density at the engine intake is only slightly higher than ambient. With an intercooler dropping to 40 °C → density is significantly higher → much more air mass per stroke → noticeably more power. The intercooler typically recovers 20-30% of the turbo's potential gain that would otherwise be lost to heat.

What "control the vacuum" means in the notebook

Notebook: "Guide vanes change the angle to control the vacuum"

The trainer's "vacuum" here is loose — it really means the pressure drop across the turbine (which drives the compressor). Closed vanes → high pressure drop → fast turbine spin → high boost. Open vanes → low pressure drop → free engine breathing. "Vacuum" is the dealer-training shorthand for "pressure differential."

Key Facts

  • Turbocharger = exhaust-driven turbine + intake-side compressor on a shared shaft.
  • Recovers wasted exhaust energy → more power per litre of engine displacement.
  • VGT (Variable Geometry Turbocharger) = movable guide vanes that change the effective turbine throat size based on rpm/load.
  • VGT closes at low rpm → fast spool, no lag. Opens at high rpm → free breathing.
  • Eliminates "turbo-lag" — boost available across the whole rpm range.
  • Intercooler sits between turbo compressor and intake manifold → cools 80-150 °C compressed air down to 30-50 °C → restores density → more air per stroke.
  • Vane actuation: vacuum diaphragm (older) or electric stepper motor (modern, DME-controlled).
  • Turbocharger rotates at 100,000-200,000 rpm; bearings depend on continuous oil supply.
  • First wide use on diesels (lower exhaust temp), now standard on petrol turbos.
  • Modern BMW turbo engines: N54/N55 (twin-turbo I6 petrol), N20/N26 (turbo I4 petrol), N47/N57 (turbo diesels) — all use VGT on the diesels.
