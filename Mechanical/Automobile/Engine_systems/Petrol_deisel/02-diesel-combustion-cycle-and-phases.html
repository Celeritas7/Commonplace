Overview

A full walkthrough of the diesel 4-stroke cycle with actual temperatures and pressures at each step, then a deeper look at the four phases of combustion that unfold inside the power stroke (ignition delay, combustion chamber 1 / 2, after-combustion) and how the combustion chamber design affects efficiency.

Working Principle

The 4 strokes — diesel-specific numbers

# Stroke Piston motion Valves Conditions (notebook)
1 Intake TDC → BDC Inlet open / outlet closed Fresh air only sucked in.
2 Compression BDC → TDC Both closed Temp ≈ 800 °C, pressure ≈ 55 bar at end of compression.
3 Power (with injection) TDC → BDC Both closed Fuel injected → combustion. Peak temp ≈ 1800 °C, peak pressure ≈ 140 bar. After expansion: temp falls back to ~800 °C, pressure reduced.
4 Exhaust BDC → TDC Outlet open Outlet valve actually starts opening 30°–50° crank angle before BDC during the power stroke (to ease blowdown).

The 30°–50° early-opening of the exhaust valve before BDC is a classic valve-overlap optimisation — gets the high-pressure burnt gas out faster so the piston doesn't have to push against it during the exhaust stroke.

Four phases of combustion (inside the power stroke)

The trainer drew a pressure-vs-crank-angle curve and divided combustion into four crank-angle intervals:

# Phase Crank-angle window What happens
1 Ignition delay −20° to −6° (before TDC) Fuel is being injected but hasn't ignited yet. The droplets need time to vaporise, mix with air, and reach autoignition.
2 Combustion chamber 1 −6° to +10° Rapid uncontrolled combustion of the fuel that accumulated during ignition delay → sharp pressure rise → max pressure.
3 Combustion chamber 2 +10° to +25° Controlled / diffusion-controlled burn of fuel still being injected. Pressure stays near peak.
4 After-combustion +25° to +180° (down to BDC) Tail burning of late fuel; pressure decays as piston moves down.

Ignition delay — why it matters

Notebook bullets: 1. Small amount of time fuel is spread → 1/2000 s (≈ 0.5 ms). 2. If there's lag, knocking of engine starts. 3. Depends on temperature, pressure, fuel grade.

Diesel knock is caused by too long an ignition delay: a large mass of fuel builds up before the first ignition, then all of it burns at once → violent pressure spike, audible knock, mechanical stress. Cetane number is the diesel-fuel property that measures resistance to long ignition delays.

Note from the trainer's margin (paraphrased):

"Because of diff. grades of fuel — fuel may burn at different temperatures, which is not same as the temp at which fuel burns as per design."

i.e., off-spec fuel shifts where in the crank-angle window combustion actually begins → knock.

After-combustion — efficiency loss

Notebook (after-combustion bullets): - Exhaust gases formed & they only present in cylinder. - It takes 50% of time of compression & power stroke. - The air induced is not fully utilized in combustion.

Half of the available expansion stroke is spent on tail-burning and exhaust, not productive work. This is why combustion-chamber design (next) is so important: the goal is to get as much of the burn done in phases 2 and 3 (near TDC where the piston has maximum mechanical advantage) and shrink phase 4.

Components / Definitions

Term What it means in this context
Ignition delay Crank-angle (or time) between start of injection and first detectable pressure rise.
Combustion chamber 1 (CC1) Premixed (uncontrolled) phase — fuel accumulated during delay burns rapidly.
Combustion chamber 2 (CC2) Diffusion (controlled) phase — fuel burns as it's being injected.
After-combustion Tail burning during the back half of the power stroke.
Knock (diesel) Audible/structural shock from oversized CC1 phase due to long delay.
BBDC Before Bottom Dead Centre — exhaust valve opens 30–50° BBDC.

Detailed Component Notes

Why early exhaust-valve opening (30–50° BBDC)

At BDC the cylinder still holds significant pressure from the power stroke. If the valve only opened at BDC, the piston would have to push that high-pressure gas out on the exhaust stroke — wasted work (pumping losses). Opening early lets pressure blow down to near-ambient before the piston starts moving up, so the exhaust stroke is much easier.

Key Facts

  • Diesel 4-stroke: intake (air only) → compression (800 °C / 55 bar) → power (peak 1800 °C / 140 bar) → exhaust.
  • Exhaust valve cracks open 30–50° before BDC to reduce pumping losses.
  • Ignition delay ≈ 0.5 ms (1/2000 s) under normal conditions.
  • Four combustion phases by crank angle: delay (−20→−6°), CC1 (−6→+10° = peak P), CC2 (+10→+25°), after-comb (+25→+180°).
  • Diesel knock = oversized CC1 phase from too-long ignition delay (off-spec fuel, low cetane, cold engine).
  • After-combustion eats ~50% of compression+power-stroke time — combustion chamber design exists to minimise it.
