Petrol & Diesel

Diesel vs Petrol — Comparison

Source images: IMG20250515003135

Overview

A side-by-side characterisation of diesel against petrol engines: thermodynamic cycle, internal energy, combustion control, why diesels are torque-heavy and more efficient, and the geometric clue (stroke length, bore aspect) that lets you spot a diesel by looking at its piston.

Working Principle

Thermodynamic cycle distinction

  • Diesel engine — constant pressure combustion (Diesel cycle). Fuel is injected progressively during the power stroke; the burn rate is paced so the chamber pressure stays roughly flat while the piston is moving down.
  • Petrol engine — constant volume combustion (Otto cycle). The whole charge ignites near TDC and burns almost instantly, so the burn happens at (nearly) fixed volume and pressure spikes sharply.

Why diesel is more efficient (notebook claim)

Notebook: "Internal energy is more in diesel; weak in petrol. Pressure in diesel engine is maintained, so diesel engine is more efficient than petrol engine."

Because diesel runs at a much higher compression ratio (15:1–22:1) and burns at near-constant pressure, more of the heat released converts into work on the piston before being dumped to exhaust. Petrol's lower CR and faster, sharper burn loses more energy as heat.

Why diesel produces more torque

Notebook: "Every time pressure on piston is same because fuel spread[s] during 'power stroke.' So diesel engines are more torque-producing." Marginal note: "Pushing the vehicle (slope, another person to help)."

The constant high pressure across most of the power stroke is felt by the piston as a sustained downward force → high torque at the crankshaft, especially at low rpm. That's why diesels haul heavy loads up hills (the trainer's "slope" metaphor).

Piston / bore geometry hint

Notebook margin: "petrol — squarish; diesel — non-squarish."

A square engine has stroke ≈ bore (oversquare = bore > stroke). Petrol engines are typically near-square or oversquare for rpm. Diesels run longer stroke than bore (undersquare) to: - Push the higher CR (longer stroke = more compression for the same clearance volume). - Generate more torque-arm leverage at the crank pin.

Detailed Component Notes

Constant-pressure vs constant-volume — what the textbook PV diagram shows

  • Otto (petrol): isentropic compression → instantaneous heat addition at TDC (constant volume vertical line) → isentropic expansion → constant volume heat rejection.
  • Diesel: isentropic compression → heat addition at constant pressure (horizontal line while piston moves) → isentropic expansion → constant volume heat rejection.

The "constant pressure" leg is a flat top on the diesel PV diagram instead of the petrol's sharp pressure spike.

Key Facts

  • Diesel = constant pressure, Otto/petrol = constant volume combustion.
  • Diesel CR 15:1 – 22:1; petrol CR ~ 14:1 (notebook number — high; real-world 8:1–12:1 NA petrol).
  • Diesel injection happens during the power stroke; petrol charge ignites at TDC.
  • Diesel makes more torque at low rpm because pressure on the piston is sustained throughout the power stroke.
  • Diesel pistons are typically undersquare (stroke > bore) to support high CR + torque arm.
  • Real self-ignition temperatures: diesel ≈ 210 °C, petrol ≈ 280 °C — diesel auto-ignites at the lower temp; the notebook statement is reversed.
