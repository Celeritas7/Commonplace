Driving Physics

Centre of Gravity and Vehicle Axes

Source images: IMG20250514181504, IMG20250514181509

Overview

A vehicle is a 3-dimensional rigid body sitting on four points of contact. How it tips, slews, and pitches in motion is governed by its centre of gravity and the three axes through that point. This file covers the stability factors (height of CoG, contact area, weight), defines the three vehicle axes with their motions (rolling, yawing, pitching), and then drills into yawing motion during cornering — including the forces in play and the condition that produces skidding.

A tall, narrow stack tips at a much smaller tilt angle than a short, broad stack of the same weight. The same applies to cars — a tall SUV with a high CoG rolls more in corners and tips at lower lateral acceleration than a low-slung sports car of equivalent mass.

Stability depends on three factors:

Factor Effect on stability
Height of centre of gravity Lower CoG → more stable. The line of action of weight stays inside the contact area at larger tilt angles.
Contact area Wider track and longer wheelbase → larger support polygon → more stable.
Weight Heavier vehicle resists overturning forces more (greater restoring moment about the tipping edge) — but is also harder to slow or steer.

In production vehicles the height of CoG is the parameter design engineers have the least freedom on (it's constrained by powertrain layout, passenger position, ground clearance), which is why sports cars use low engines, mid-engines, and dry sumps to bring it down.

Rolling — Causes

The notebook gives two situations that produce roll:

  1. Cornering — the centrifugal force at CoG and the reactive tyre forces at ground level form a couple that rolls the body outward.
  2. Crosswind — a side wind exerts a transverse force above the CoG, which rolls the body away from the wind side.

In both cases the suspension's roll stiffness (anti-roll bars, spring rates, damper settings) determines how much the body actually leans before equilibrium.

Force inventory during a bend:

Force Where it acts Direction
Inertia CoG Forward — vehicle tries to keep going straight
Centrifugal force CoG Outward (away from curve centre)
Lateral guiding force Front tyres (via steering input) Inward (toward curve centre) — pulls car around the bend
Steering/guidance force Front contact patches Inward — reaction of the road to the steered wheels

If the lateral guiding force at the front tyres is enough to counter inertia + centrifugal force, the car follows the steered path. If not — the car understeers (plows wide) or, when the rear loses grip, the car oversteers (tail slides out).

The page notes specifically: "Rear wheels can't support lateral guiding force" — meaning a normal (non-steered) rear axle can resist lateral push only up to its grip limit; once that limit is exceeded the rear slides.

The notebook splits the analysis: lateral forces on the rear axle arise as the car takes the turn (the body wants to swing — that swing is resisted at the rear); the transverse force on the front axle is the steering reaction at the front tyres pulling the car into the bend.

Skidding — The Failure Condition

The bottom of the page gives the operational definition of a skid:

Skidding occurs when these forces can no longer be absorbed by wheels.

i.e. when the lateral force demanded by the cornering manoeuvre exceeds the available tyre grip, the tyre slides sideways across the road surface rather than rolling along its intended path. This is the failure mode that DSC (Dynamic Stability Control) is designed to detect and intervene against — by individually braking wheels to generate a corrective yaw moment.

The trainer revisits the same concept geometrically using Kamm's friction circle in the next phase (Phase 7-2), where the skid threshold is drawn as a circle whose radius equals the available static friction force.

Key Facts

  • A vehicle's stability is set by height of CoG, contact area, and weight.
  • Lower CoG, wider track, longer wheelbase → more stable.
  • Three vehicle axes through CoG: longitudinal (rolling), vertical (yawing), transverse (pitching).
  • Rolling is caused mainly by cornering forces and crosswinds.
  • Yawing is the slewing of the body around the vertical axis — happens in every corner.
  • Pitching is the nose-down / nose-up motion under braking / acceleration.
  • During cornering: inertia + centrifugal force push outward; lateral guiding force at the steered (front) tyres pulls inward.
  • Rear wheels (non-steered) cannot generate lateral guiding force — they only resist it up to their grip limit.
  • Skidding = when the lateral forces exceed what the tyres can transmit to the road.
