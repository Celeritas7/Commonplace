Driving Physics

Aerodynamics and Drag Reduction

Source images: IMG20250515003724, IMG20250515003734, IMG20250515003738 (top)

Overview

At highway speeds the dominant resistance the engine has to fight isn't rolling friction or gradient — it's aerodynamic drag (introduced in 02 — Resistance Forces and Engine Drag, where it rose with the square of speed). Drag is set by the drag coefficient $C_w$ (German Widerstand) and the frontal area $A$ of the car. This page goes from the principle to BMW's design moves on the E60 5-Series: optimised front geometry, flush windows, a high rear edge, a rising luggage-compartment floor, ram-air lips at the front, and underbody panelling — each contributing a small reduction in $C_w$ that adds up to a measurable difference in fuel use, top speed, and high-speed straight-line stability.

Air-Flow Control Bar

Air flow control bar: fins, bar, etc. [?]

The "control bar" terminology in the notebook covers the family of small aerodynamic surfaces fitted around the body to steer the boundary-layer airflow where the designers want it: small fins (vortex generators on roof and rear-end), the bar itself (a chin spoiler under the front bumper), and similar elements. They don't add downforce in the racecar sense — they keep the flow attached to the body in the places where separation would otherwise widen the wake.

Influencing Factors on the E60

The page lists five design moves BMW used on the E60 platform to bring down its drag coefficient:

# Factor What it does aerodynamically
1 Optimized front Rounded but low front end with low stagnation pressure; smooths the airflow over the bonnet and into the windscreen.
2 Flat windows Glass surfaces sit close to flush with the body — less interruption of the boundary layer, smaller pressure step at the A- and C-pillars.
3 High rear edge The trunk lid ends high (Kammback principle) — abruptly truncating the rear means the air separates cleanly at that edge rather than swirling around a tapered tail; net wake is smaller for a given trunk length.
4 Rising luggage-compartment floor Underneath the trunk, the floor sweeps up to meet the rear bumper — reduces the underbody wake and lifts the diffuser effect.
5 Favourable-flow ram-air lips A lip at the front lower edge that splits the oncoming air: most goes over and under the car, a small managed stream goes through the radiator / wheel-arch ducts. Detailed below.

Each is a small change. Together they took the E60's $C_w$ down to about 0.27 — competitive for an executive saloon in 2003–2010.

Underbody Panelling

The top of page 14 continues with the underbody as a drag-reduction surface:

Measures to optimize drag coe.:- - The panelling of of [sic] underbody of car reduces coe. of drag.

The underside of a saloon car normally exposes the exhaust, fuel tank, suspension links, and floorpan reinforcements directly to the flow — each is a bluff body that trips the boundary layer. Fitting flat plastic underbody panels (BMW began doing this seriously around the E60 / E90 generation) makes the underside a smooth surface; the air slides along it with the same character as it does over the bonnet, and the rear diffuser geometry can actually do its job. Visible result: a measurable $C_w$ improvement and less low-frequency drone in the cabin at high speed.

How the Pieces Fit Together

The total drag a car makes is:

$$F_\text{drag} = \tfrac{1}{2} \rho v^2 \cdot C_w \cdot A$$

The designer has limited room on $A$ (the cabin has to fit people) and zero room on $\rho$ (air density) or $v$ (the driver's choice). Every aero move on this page is a $C_w$ move:

Move What it changes
Optimised front Lowers stagnation, smooths early boundary layer
Flat / flush windows Removes pillar-to-glass pressure steps
High rear (Kammback) edge Cleaner separation, smaller wake
Rising luggage floor Cleaner rear underbody, helps diffuser
Ram-air lips Manages the stream entering the engine bay & wheel arches
Underbody panels Makes the underside a smooth surface, not a bluff one
Air-flow control bar (fins, etc.) Locally re-attaches the boundary layer

Each is worth fractional thousandths of $C_w$. Sum: a saloon-car drag coefficient in the 0.26–0.30 band, which is where the E60-generation sits.

Key Facts

  • Aerodynamic drag scales with the square of speed; at 120 km/h on a saloon it's the dominant resistance.
  • Drag coefficient $C_w$ × frontal area $A$ is what the designer optimises (the rest of the drag formula is fixed by physics and the driver).
  • E60 design moves: optimised front, flat/flush windows, high rear edge (Kammback), rising luggage-compartment floor, ram-air lips, underbody panelling, control fins.
  • Air-flow control bar = small fins/bars that steer the boundary layer to stop separation.
  • Ram air lip = front lower-edge chin that diverts a managed stream into wheel arches / brake cooling ducts.
  • Underbody panelling smooths the underside of the car — measurable $C_w$ reduction.
  • The page's $\Delta C_w$ vs. inches graph shows the contribution one of these moves makes: ~0.01 drop over the swept range, ~4% of a 0.27 baseline.
