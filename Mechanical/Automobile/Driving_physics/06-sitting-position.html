Driving Physics

Driver Sitting Position

Source images: IMG20250514181600

Overview

Before any of the vehicle-dynamics theory matters at the wheel, the driver has to be physically positioned so their inputs reach the controls reliably and so the safety systems (seatbelt, airbag, head restraint) protect them in a crash. This page is a single illustrated procedure — a red-pen line drawing of a driver in the seat with the six numbered steps written below it. It's the dealer-school version of the ergonomic check a sales/service advisor runs through with a new vehicle owner.

The Six-Step Procedure

The notebook numbers six adjustments, in order:

# Adjustment What to do Why
1 Seat fore-aft + cushion fit Place yourself in the seat in such a way that you make full use of the seat cushion and your entire back is at the backrest. Spreads load across the whole cushion (no thigh pressure point); puts the spine in contact with the backrest so the seat can hold you against lateral G in a corner and against airbag deployment force in a crash.
2 Seat height Adjust seat height so that one "FIST" fits between head and headlining (roof). Gives enough head clearance to survive a vertical body movement in a rough impact, and brings the eye line high enough for visibility over the dash and bonnet. The "fist" is the standard dealer-school field measure.
3 Left-leg bend Left leg on clutch should always be slightly bent (approx 15°) at the knee even when the pedal is fully pressed. A fully-extended leg locks the knee and can't absorb a footwell intrusion in a frontal crash; a slightly bent leg lets the muscles, not the joint, take the load. Also gives reach margin so the driver isn't sliding off the seat to depress the pedal.
4 Backrest angle + steering reach Adjust the backrest angle so that the right hand can easily reach the steering wheel at top left (11 o'clock). Elbow always slightly bent. A slightly-bent elbow keeps the shoulder behind the seat (against the backrest), gives steering bandwidth for quick correction, and stops the chest from being thrown forward of the airbag's catch zone in a crash. A locked-elbow / arms-stretched posture loses all of that.
5 Head-restraint height Adjust the height of the head restraint so that the line from eye to ear extends around the middle of the restraint. Puts the restraint behind the rigid back of the skull (occipital bone), not behind the soft neck. This is what stops the head from snapping rearward in a rear-end shunt — the whiplash defence.
6 Head-restraint tilt Tilt the head restraint forwards so that the head touches the restraint even with a short backward movement. Closes the gap between the back of the head and the restraint. Even an inch of gap allows the head to accelerate backward before catching the restraint — every inch of that travel multiplies the neck strain. A near-zero gap stops whiplash before it starts.

Why This Page Sits in "Driving Physics"

The whole rest of this folder (forces, Kamm's circle, centre of gravity, skidding) is the vehicle's physics. This page is the driver's physics — and that's deliberate. Every safety system designed in the rest of the curriculum depends on the occupant being in its design position:

  • The front airbag is a thrown-mass cushion calibrated for an occupant whose chest is about 25 cm behind the wheel; too close and the bag's inflation impulse becomes a frontal blow to the sternum.
  • The belt tensioner + force limiter assume the belt is over the shoulder and pelvis, which it is only if the seat angle is upright enough.
  • The head restraint stops the skull only if the skull is in front of it.
  • The knee airbag (where fitted) needs the knee to be roughly where the design said it would be.

A reclined / slumped / leg-locked driver invalidates every one of those calibrations. The trainer's six-step list is the cheapest piece of passive safety in the whole vehicle — it just has to be done once.

Quick Field Checklist

If you have to recall this on the showroom floor without the notes:

  • Cushion — sit all the way back, full cushion used.
  • Roof — a fist between your head and the headlining.
  • Clutch — knee still slightly bent at the floor.
  • Wheel — 11 o'clock, elbow slightly bent.
  • Restraint height — eye-to-ear line through the middle of it.
  • Restraint gap — tilt forward until almost touching.

Key Facts

  • Sit so the entire back contacts the backrest and the full seat cushion is used.
  • Head-to-roof gap = one fist — the field-measure standard.
  • The left leg (clutch) stays slightly bent (~15°) even when the clutch is fully pressed.
  • Reach the wheel at 11 o'clock with the elbow slightly bent — never with stretched arms.
  • The head restraint height is set by the eye-to-ear line falling through its middle — behind the skull, not the neck.
  • The head restraint tilt closes the gap to the back of the head so even a short backward movement contacts it.
  • This procedure is the passive-safety prerequisite for the airbag / belt tensioner / head restraint / knee airbag systems to do their job.

Open Questions / Uncertainty

  • [?] Step 2 reads "FIST is between head & handling." Read as "head & headlining" (the inside roof lining) — the fist clearance is between the top of the head and the roof. "Handling" is a phonetic transcription slip for "headlining."
  • [?] Step 4 reads "at top left (11 o'clock)." On a left-hand-drive car the right hand reaches up to 1–2 o'clock, not 11; in an Indian/RHD car the right hand naturally reaches 10–11 o'clock. The trainer is RHD-context, so 11 o'clock is consistent with the steering hand on the right and the wheel offset to the driver's left of centre. Preserved as written.
  • [?] Step 5: "line from eye to ear extends around middle of [head restraint]." The text after "extends" is partly obscured by a strike-through ("eye to ear extends around middle of"); read as "the eye-to-ear line passes through the middle of the head restraint."
