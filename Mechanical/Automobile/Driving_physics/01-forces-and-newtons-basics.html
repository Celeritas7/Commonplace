Driving Physics

Forces and Newton's Basics

Source images: IMG20250514181441, IMG20250514181445

Overview

Driving physics is the study of how the forces acting on a moving vehicle combine, oppose, and resolve into motion. Before discussing tyres, brakes, or chassis behaviour the trainer establishes the three basic concepts used throughout the rest of the module: how forces add up (cumulate / parallelogram law), Newton's second law F = m·a, and the three primary forces that act on a moving car — drive force, centrifugal force, and inertia force.

Forces Cumulate

Forces acting on the same line of action cumulate: their magnitudes simply add (with sign for direction). The notebook gives two simple illustrations on a cylindrical can:

Two equal forces pushing inward          One force pushing down on top
from opposite sides                      → cylinder is compressed, rolling
→ cylinder is squeezed in place          resistance against the ground increases

The right-hand cylinder also flags an early hint of what's to come: a vertical load on a tyre increases its rolling resistance (more on this in Resistance Forces and Engine Drag).

Parallelogram Law

When two forces act at an angle to each other, their resultant is found by drawing them as adjacent sides of a parallelogram — the diagonal gives the resultant in magnitude and direction. The notes name this here without diagram; it is the working tool used later for combining drive force with transverse force, or static friction with drive force inside Kamm's circle.

Basics — Newton's Second Law and Free-Fall

$$F_{\text{(of inertia)}} = m \cdot a$$

Force equals mass times acceleration. The accompanying free-fall demonstration is the classical Galileo experiment:

Cylinder Contents Mass
Left Feathers 0.15 kg
Right Lead block 1 kg

Result (in vacuum): both fall with the same increasing speed and hit the ground at the same time. Gravitational acceleration is independent of mass; only air resistance separates the two in real atmospheric conditions.

Weight and the Lift Example

Weight is the force gravity exerts on a mass: W = m·g, where g ≈ 9.81 m/s².

The trainer uses a lift (elevator) thought-experiment to show that apparent weight depends on the lift's acceleration relative to gravity:

  • Gravitational acceleration on Earth: g = 9.81 m/s²
  • Lift accelerating downward at: 4 m/s²
  • Apparent (actual) acceleration felt by the passenger: 9.81 − 4 = 5.81 m/s²

You feel lighter inside a downward-accelerating lift because part of gravity is "used up" matching the lift's acceleration. Conversely, an upward-accelerating lift makes you feel heavier (g + a). The same principle is what makes a sharply braking car push you forward against the seatbelt, or a hard-accelerating car press you into the seat back.

Forces on a Vehicle

Three primary forces act on every moving car:

1. Drive Force

The motive force delivered by the engine to the road through the driven wheels. It acts in the direction of motion and is opposed by the various resistance forces (rolling, aerodynamic, gradient, inertial) covered in file 02.

        Direction of motion
       ←─────────── of car ───
          ┌─────┐
          │ tyre│  ───→ Drive force
          └─────┘
       ←─ Motive force

2. Centrifugal Force

Occurs as a result of deflection of an object. It only acts when an object is in motion AND moving around a fixed point.

A car taking a corner is constantly being deflected from its natural straight-line path; this deflection appears, from the car's frame of reference, as a force pushing the body outward from the curve's centre. The notes flag this as a "special type of inertia force" — physically it is the manifestation of inertia trying to keep the car going straight while the steering forces it to turn.

3. Inertia Force

Inertia is the property of matter to resist any change in its state of motion (Newton's first law). The notes give two situations where inertia is observable as a force:

Situation Inertia behaviour
Object at rest Inertia resists being accelerated from stationary
Accelerating on straight road Inertia opposes the change in speed (you feel pushed back into the seat)

In a corner, inertia tries to keep the vehicle moving in a straight line, which is felt as the centrifugal effect (#2 above) pushing the body outward.

Lateral Guidance Forces

When a car is being deflected around a bend, several systems together "balance" the lateral guidance forces — i.e. the sideways forces needed to keep the car following the steering input rather than skidding off:

  1. Tyre pressure
  2. Tyre grip
  3. Suspension (decides how the vehicle balances the lateral forces)

These three together determine how much lateral force the vehicle can absorb before it stops following the steered path. Skidding is what happens when the lateral guidance forces exceed what the wheels can transmit; this is developed further in file 03 — Centre of Gravity and Vehicle Axes.

Direction of Each Force (Quick Reference)

The notes summarise the directionality of the three primary on-road forces at the bottom of page 2:

Force Direction
Drive force Acts in the direction of motion
Inertia force Acts in the direction of motion (resists change) — but is not the actual lateral force in a corner
Braking force Only opposes the drive force (acts against direction of motion)
Centrifugal force Acts outward from the curve, in the direction of outside-of-bend
Counterforce / steering guidance Acts inward toward the centre of the curve, opposing centrifugal force

Key Facts

  • Forces along the same line of action cumulate (add with sign).
  • Forces at an angle resolve by the parallelogram law — diagonal gives the resultant.
  • F = m·a is Newton's second law; weight is just m·g.
  • In a vacuum a feather and a lead block fall at the same rate — gravity is mass-independent.
  • Apparent weight in an accelerating lift = m·(g ± a), sign depending on direction.
  • The three primary on-vehicle forces are drive, centrifugal, inertia.
  • Centrifugal force only exists when the body is in motion AND turning around a fixed point.
  • Lateral guidance is decided by tyre pressure, tyre grip, and suspension working together.
  • Braking force always acts opposite to drive force.
