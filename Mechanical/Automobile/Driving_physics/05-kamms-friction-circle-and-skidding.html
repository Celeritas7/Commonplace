Driving Physics

Kamm's Friction Circle and Skidding

Source images: IMG20250514181532, IMG20250514181536

Overview

Kamm's friction circle is the geometric tool used to decide whether a tyre is about to skid. It draws the total force a tyre can transmit to the road as a circle whose radius equals the static friction force at that tyre. Whatever combination of drive/brake force (longitudinal) and transverse/lateral force (cornering) the tyre is asked to deliver becomes a force vector — and as long as that vector stays inside the circle, the tyre grips. The instant it pokes outside, the tyre slides and the car skids. This file unpacks the circle itself, the three factors that change its radius, how the circle behaves on inclined roads, and the two driver-input causes of skidding (over-braking and abrupt steering during braking) from the second page.

        (static friction force on
         tyre in normal road)

The trainer states the founding rule:

Drive force < Static friction force.

So long as the drive (or brake) force demanded at the tyre stays below the static friction force the road can supply, the tyre rolls without slipping. The circle's radius is the static friction force — the maximum the contact patch can transmit in any direction at that moment.

Three Factors That Set the Circle's Radius

The notebook calls out — green-pen, top-right of the page — that Kamm's friction circle depends on:

# Factor Effect
1 Wheel load Higher normal load → larger static friction force ($F_\text{friction} = \mu F_n$) → bigger circle. Weight transfer under braking/cornering shrinks the circle at unloaded wheels and grows it at loaded ones.
2 Road condition Dry asphalt = high μ = large circle. Wet, icy, sandy, or oily roads = lower μ = smaller circle.
3 Tyre condition Fresh tread with sharp edges and full rubber depth = high μ. Worn-out tyre with rounded blocks and no drainage grooves = lower μ.

The trainer's two examples on this page:

Static friction force is reduced due to wet surface or tyre surface worn out.

When the static friction force shrinks, the circle shrinks. The same drive force that was safely inside the dry-road circle can now poke outside the wet-road or worn-tyre circle — that's when the car skids:

Drive force > static friction force → Hence vehicle skid.

Cornering — The Resultant Force on the Tyre

When a car is just driving in a straight line, the only horizontal force at the contact patch is the drive (or brake) force. When the car also corners, a transverse force (lateral, pointed toward the centre of the bend) is added at the patch. Their vector sum is the resultant:

              ●●●●●
           ●●       ●●
         ●  Transverse ●
        ●      force    ●
       ●        ↑       ●
       ●        │       ●         (tyre's
       ●        │ Resultant    available
       ●        │ ╱             grip circle)
        ●       │╱             ●
         ●      ●──── Drive ──●
           ●●       force   ●●
              ●●●●●

       Vehicle moving →

The trainer's rule:

If resultant force comes out of Kamm's circle, vehicle can skid while cornering.

This is exactly the cornering skid threshold: as long as the resultant (drive + transverse) stays inside the circle, the tyre tracks the steered path. The instant the resultant escapes the circle, the tyre slides — and the car either washes wide (front-axle skid → understeer) or swings the tail (rear-axle skid → oversteer).

Each wheel has its own circle because each wheel has its own load (weight distribution + transfer), its own road patch (might be on a different surface), and its own slip state. DSC, ABS, and traction control reason about each wheel's individual circle — that's why they can brake one wheel and let the others run free.

Kamm's Circles on an Inclined Road

The top of page 10 sketches a car going round an inclined road (a banked corner, drawn here in plan-and-section). The trainer notes:

           Inclined road
        ╲────────────────╲
         ╲                ╲
          ╲   ┌───┐ ┌───┐  ╲
           ╲  │ ◯ │ │ ◯ │   ╲    ← circles drawn at
            ╲ └───┘ └───┘    ╲     each contact patch
             ╲────────────────╲

All Kamm's friction circles extend to same extent.

Static forces on all wheels are too low.

Two observations:

  1. Same extent — on a banked / symmetrical surface, the four circles are roughly equal in radius because the load is being shared more evenly (the banking transfers some lateral demand into normal load on the inside wheels).
  2. Static forces on all wheels are too low — the page is flagging that on a slippery incline (the inclination is also reducing how hard the tyres can push down before sliding along the slope), the absolute size of every circle is small. So even an evenly-shared demand can blow out of every wheel's circle at once.

[?] The phrase "too low" is ambiguous — it likely means "low" (small) as a warning that grip is reduced on the incline, rather than literal "too low" (insufficient for the manoeuvre). Transcribed as written.

Braking Force and Causes of Skidding

The bottom of page 10 is the braking equivalent of the cornering analysis. The trainer draws one tyre's Kamm circle with a downward red arrow (drive direction) and an upward green arrow inside it (braking force in opposition):

              ●●●●●
           ●●       ●●
         ●     ↑       ●
        ●      │        ●
       ●   Braking      ●
       ●   force         ●
       ●      │          ●
        ●     ↓         ●
         ●  Drive      ●
           ●●         ●●
              ●●●●●
       (force still inside circle —
        OK for the moment)

Then he gives the two causes of skidding while braking:

# Cause What goes wrong
1 If brakes are applied so strongly that braking force exceeds static friction force The single-tyre vector (now mostly longitudinal braking) pokes outside Kamm's circle → wheel locks and slides.
2 If driver makes too abrupt steering motion during braking A large transverse force is suddenly demanded on top of an already-large braking force. The vector sum is the resultant; that resultant blows out of the circle and the car skids.

Both reduce to the same geometric statement: the demanded force vector exceeded the circle's radius. The difference is which axis ate the budget — pure braking (longitudinal) in case 1, combined brake + steer (resultant) in case 2.

This is the geometric justification for two pieces of training-school advice that often surprise new drivers:

  • Brake in a straight line, then turn — using the whole circle for braking, then the whole circle for cornering, never both at the saturation point.
  • No abrupt steering when already braking near the limit — it's a guaranteed way to send the resultant outside the circle.

ABS allows higher brake force (rule 1) by holding each wheel near its peak slip; DSC allows steering correction at the limit (rule 2) by braking individual wheels to rebalance the resultant.

Key Facts

  • Kamm's friction circle = the circle of all force vectors a tyre can transmit to the road at a given instant; its radius = static friction force at that tyre.
  • The circle's size depends on wheel load, road condition, and tyre condition.
  • The condition for no skid is: the vector sum of (drive/brake force) + (transverse force) stays inside the circle.
  • Skid happens the instant the resultant force vector pokes outside the circle.
  • Cornering skid: lateral demand + any longitudinal force exceeds the circle.
  • Braking skid case 1: brake force alone exceeds the static friction force (wheel locks).
  • Braking skid case 2: abrupt steering input added to already-strong braking pushes the resultant outside.
  • Each of the four wheels has its own Kamm's circle — sized by that wheel's load and grip.
  • On a slippery inclined road, all four circles are roughly equal and small — easy to lose all four at once.
  • This circle model is what ABS, traction control, and DSC are designed around — they keep each wheel's force vector inside its own circle.
