Driving Physics

Resistance Forces and Engine Drag

Source images: IMG20250514181452, IMG20250514181456

Overview

A driven vehicle has to overcome several resistance forces to maintain or build speed. The drive force from the engine is, in steady-state cruise, exactly balanced by the sum of all resistances. This file covers the main resistances (rolling and aerodynamic), the special case of crosswind / transverse force, and the related concepts of torque on the wheel, engine drag (engine braking), and the comparison of disk vs drum brakes for shedding kinetic energy as heat.

Road Resistance Forces

The relationship is direct:

Stronger drive  →  Higher  →  Stronger aerodynamic
force              speed       drag (and rolling
                               resistance)

A bigger drive force buys higher speed; higher speed costs more aerodynamic drag and more rolling resistance. At constant cruise, drive force = total resistance.

Rolling Resistance — What It Depends On

Rolling resistance is the energy a tyre loses through deformation as it rolls. It depends on:

Factor Effect
Tyre material Compound stiffness and hysteresis losses
Tyre pressure Lower pressure → bigger contact patch flexes more → higher rolling resistance
Road condition Rough/wet surfaces deform less of the tyre but pump more energy out of the suspension

A correctly inflated tyre on smooth tarmac has the lowest rolling resistance; a soft tyre on a rough road is the worst case.

Transverse Force — Crosswind

A side-wind hitting a vehicle exerts a transverse force (also called lateral force or, when caused by wind, crosswind force).

                       ←─────  Wind
                       ←─────
       ┌──────────────────┐
  ←──  │    LATERAL       │  ←── Transverse force
       │    FORCE         │      = Crosswind
       │ →                │  ←──
       └──────────────────┘

The transverse force tries to push the car sideways off its lane. It is resisted by:

  • Tyre grip (especially the front-axle steering tyres)
  • Steering input by the driver to counter the deflection
  • Vehicle body design — a tall, slab-sided vehicle catches more wind than a low, aerodynamic one
  • Modern aids — Crosswind Assist (electronic intervention) and Active Steering systems brake individual wheels to compensate; these are post-2013 era features but the basic crosswind problem they solve is identical to what the notes describe

Torque

Torque is the rotational equivalent of force — a force applied at a lever arm produces a turning moment about a pivot.

       Direction of motion              Lever arm
       ←───── of car                    │
                                        │
                  ┌─────────┐           │
                  │   wheel │  ────────→ Driving force
                  └─────────┘
                       │
                       │ (lever arm = wheel radius)
                       ↓
                  contact patch

The drive force pushes the contact patch backward on the road; by Newton's third law the road pushes the contact patch forward, generating a forward force on the car. The relationship is Torque = Force × lever arm, where the lever arm in this case is the wheel radius. A wheel with a bigger radius needs more torque from the engine to produce the same drive force at the road surface, which is why fitting oversized tyres reduces effective acceleration even if everything else stays the same.

Engine Drag (Engine Braking / Motoring)

When you lift off the accelerator while travelling downhill or coasting, the engine itself becomes a brake. This is engine drag (also called engine braking or motoring in BMW DME terminology).

How it works:

  1. While travelling downhill the driver stops accelerating — fuel supply to the piston is cut off (modern DMEs do this via "overrun fuel cut-off").
  2. With no fuel, the piston stops being driven by combustion.
  3. But the wheels are still rotating (the car is rolling), so they keep driving the pistons backward through the transmission.
  4. The pistons now need work to be moved against compression, valve pumping losses, and internal friction — this work is taken from the kinetic energy of the car.
  5. The piston motion provides a "counter-torque" opposing the wheel rotation → vehicle speed is reduced.
       ENGINE DRAG / MOTORING

  Centre of crankshaft
         ●
        ╱│╲
       ╱ │ ╲ ←─── Crank pin
      ╱  │  ╲
     ╱   │   ╲     The wheels keep rotating; they drive the piston UP
    ╱    │    ╲    against compression. The work needed comes from
   ────────────    the car's kinetic energy → car slows down.
       piston

This is also why a manual car will decelerate noticeably in gear but coast freely in neutral — in neutral the engine and wheels are disconnected so no engine-drag torque is fed back to the wheels.

Disk Brake vs Drum Brake — Counter-Inertia Torque

Brakes counteract the inertia of the vehicle by converting kinetic energy into heat at the wheel.

The notebook makes a comparative observation:

Disk brake generates less torque on the wheel as compared to drum brakes. So heavy vehicles still use drum brakes at rear.

This is one of the practical engineering trade-offs of brake design as of the early-2010s era these notes describe:

Property Disk brake Drum brake
Torque per unit size Lower Higher (self-energising effect at the shoe)
Heat dissipation Excellent (open to air) Poor (enclosed in drum)
Fade resistance Excellent Prone to fade after repeated heavy use
Self-energising / servo effect No Yes — wedging of the shoe amplifies pedal force
Typical use (2013 era) Front of all cars, rear of cars and light vehicles Rear of heavier vehicles, trailers, low-cost cars

The reason heavy vehicles still ran rear drums in 2013: the self-energising leading-shoe drum gives more torque for the same actuating force than an equivalent-size disk caliper. The trade-off is heat — a drum brake at the rear of a heavy vehicle that's used lightly (just trims away kinetic energy during ordinary slowing) stays cool enough; the front discs do the heavy lifting where most braking energy is shed.

Visual Diagram — Total Resistance Equation

   At constant cruise:

     Drive Force  =  Rolling Resistance + Aerodynamic Drag +
                     Gradient Resistance + (Acceleration Force if accelerating)

     →   Bigger drive  =  faster cruise  =  more drag
     →   Tyre wear, soft tyres, wet roads → more rolling resistance
     →   Crosswind → transverse force not in the line of motion
                     → must be cancelled by tyre grip + steering input

Key Facts

  • Drive force in steady-state cruise = sum of all resistance forces.
  • Rolling resistance depends on tyre material, tyre pressure, and road condition.
  • Aerodynamic drag rises with the square of speed.
  • Transverse force (crosswind) acts sideways; cancelled by tyre grip + steering correction.
  • Torque = Force × lever arm. For a wheel, the lever arm is the wheel radius.
  • Engine drag (motoring) = fuel cut + piston pumping against compression while wheels rotate → counter-torque slows the car.
  • Disk brakes produce less torque per size than drum brakes, but dissipate heat far better; hence drums survive at the rear of heavy 2013-era vehicles.
