Friction and Slip

Source images: IMG20250514181517, IMG20250514181522

Overview

Whatever a tyre does — accelerate, brake, corner — it does through friction at its contact patch. The notebook starts with a mechanical view of friction (surfaces interlocking like the teeth of a wooden cup on a wooden plank), splits it into static and sliding friction, then folds in the practical consequences for tyres (why racecars run wide tyres, why a wet film kills grip). It closes with the formal definition of slip — the speed difference between the spinning tyre and the moving vehicle — and notes that without slip a tyre can neither accelerate, brake, nor corner. The page ends introducing Kamm's friction circle, which is treated in full in file 05.

Friction — The Interlocking Model

The trainer draws a wooden cup sitting on a wooden plank. The two surfaces aren't smooth — they have microscopic roughness that meshes (interlocks) like jagged teeth:

        ┌─────┐
        │ cup │
        └──┬──┘
   ─────────────────────  ← surfaces look flat

   But zoomed in:

         ╱╲╱╲╱╲╱╲╱╲    ← bottom of cup
         ╲╱╲╱╲╱╲╱╲╱    ← top of plank
         (teeth mesh into one another)

To slide the cup, the applied force has to lift the cup's teeth out of the plank's grooves. This is why:

  • Applied force results in removal of interlocks — the surfaces are momentarily separated as the cup climbs out of mesh.
  • Once moving, the teeth can't easily lock again — they skim across the tops rather than dropping fully into the next groove. This is why sliding friction < static friction.

Two named friction forces follow:

Type When it acts Magnitude
Static friction force Body at rest; opposes the attempt to start motion Larger — full mesh of surface irregularities
Sliding friction force Body already moving; opposes continuing motion Smaller — only partial mesh as teeth skip across

Static friction "comes into play due to meshing of irregular surfaces of wood & cup." Once the cup is sliding, the meshing is broken and only sliding friction acts.

What Friction Depends On

The notebook explicitly lists the two factors that determine how much friction a block of mass develops on a surface:

The movement of block would depend on: 1. Contact surface 2. Weight of block

i.e. how the surfaces mesh (a function of the contact area and the roughness/coefficient of the two materials) and how hard they're pressed together (a function of the normal load — for a flat surface, the weight pushing down).

This sets up the friction-force formula on the next page:

$$F_\text{friction} = \mu \cdot F_n$$

where $F_n$ is the normal vertical force (the load pressing the surfaces together) and $\mu$ is the coefficient of friction (the meshing quality of the surface pair).

  • Large friction → high coefficient of friction (rough, grippy surface pair).
  • Low friction → low coefficient of friction (smooth, slippery pair).

Practical Tyre Cases

Why racecars use wide tyres

The notebook stops to address a common misconception:

Racecar use wide tyres is not useful to get max speed in race, but still they do that to provide max heat dissipation through tyres as speed is high & there is possibility of damage.

This is exactly right. For a rigid tyre on a rigid road, classical friction theory says $F_\text{friction} = \mu F_n$ — the contact area cancels out (wider tyre = lower pressure × larger area = same force). Wide tyres on a racecar exist for thermal reasons: the larger contact patch spreads the heat generated at the patch over more rubber, lowering the bulk temperature and stopping the rubber from going off (over-heating, blistering, chunking). Real tyres also have non-linear rubber behaviour that benefits from a larger patch — but the trainer's emphasis on heat is the dominant operational reason.

Why a wet film kills grip

   Dry road                        Wet road
   ────────────                    ─────~~~~──── ← water film
   ╱╲╱╲╱╲╱╲   ← rough               ╱╲╱╲╱╲╱╲
   ╲╱╲╱╲╱╲╱   asphalt               ╲╱╲╱╲╱╲╱
   meshes with tyre                 water fills the grooves,
                                    tyre rides over the top

Wet film reduces meshing of rough surfaces.

A film of water (or oil, or loose sand) sits in the surface irregularities and prevents the tyre's tread blocks from dropping into them. The effective coefficient of friction drops sharply — which is why braking distances roughly double on a wet road, and why tyre treads have drainage grooves to channel water out of the contact patch (covered in Steering_Wheels_tyres/07 — Tyre Footprint and Damage).

Slip — The Definition

A tyre on a moving car is not simply rolling like a coin down a ramp. To put a driving torque through the contact patch, the tyre must rotate a touch faster than the car's translational speed would require. To brake, it must rotate a touch slower. The difference is slip:

Slip = difference between the speed of the tyre & speed of vehicle.

Tyre moves faster than actual car speed (during accelerate, decelerate, & cornering).

Slip is usually expressed as a fraction or percentage:

$$\text{slip} = \frac{v_\text{tyre} - v_\text{vehicle}}{v_\text{tyre}} \quad \text{(driving)}$$

The notebook lists the practical consequences plainly:

  • Without slip, we can't accelerate or decelerate — a perfectly free-rolling tyre transmits zero longitudinal force.
  • Without slip on wheel, cornering is not possible — generating lateral grip also requires a small slip angle (the tyre's plane of rotation pointed a few degrees inward of the actual velocity direction).

The friction force at the contact patch rises with slip, peaks at a few percent (the peak grip point that ABS and TC aim for), and then falls off as the tyre starts to actually skid.

Kamm's Friction Circle — Introduction

The bottom of page 8 introduces Kamm's friction circle with a single line:

The max drive force is not stronger than static friction force.

And the small diagram:

              ●●●●●
           ●●       ●●
         ●             ●
        ●               ●
       ●  ←─── static ──── Drive force ───→
       ●     friction        ●
        ●    force           ●
         ●                 ●
           ●●           ●●
              ●●●●●

That circle's radius is the static-friction force available at the tyre. Any combination of longitudinal force (drive or brake) and lateral force (cornering) the tyre is asked to deliver must sit inside that circle. If the resultant pokes outside it, the tyre breaks traction and the car skids. The full geometric treatment, the per-wheel circles, and braking-induced skid causes are in file 05 — Kamm's Friction Circle and Skidding.

Key Facts

  • Friction arises from the meshing of microscopic surface irregularities, not from "smoothness" alone.
  • Static friction > sliding friction: once a body starts sliding, the irregularities can't fully re-mesh.
  • $F_\text{friction} = \mu \cdot F_n$ — depends on coefficient of friction $\mu$ and normal load $F_n$, not on apparent contact area for a rigid pair.
  • Wide racecar tyres exist mainly for heat dissipation, not extra grip from area alone.
  • Wet/oil film sits in the surface roughness and slashes the effective coefficient of friction.
  • Slip = tyre speed − vehicle speed. Necessary in every driving action: accelerating, braking, and cornering all require a small slip.
  • Slip-force curves peak at a few percent slip; ABS and traction control hunt around this peak.
  • The maximum drive (or brake) force a tyre can transmit is bounded by the static-friction force — this is the radius of Kamm's circle.
