Driving Physics

Body Shell — Aluminium Heat Treatment

Source images: IMG20250515003738 (bottom half)

Overview

A modern body shell is no longer plain mild-steel pressings — it's a mix of high-strength steels and aluminium alloy panels (bonnets, boot lids, doors, sometimes entire shells). Aluminium body parts get their strength not from the alloy composition alone but from a deliberate heat-treatment cycle applied during manufacture: solution heating, quenching, natural aging, then artificial aging. Once that cycle is finished the part is at full strength — but the same metallurgy that gave it strength also means plastic deformation is no longer permitted: you can't bend it back into shape after a knock without cracking it. This page captures the 5-stage temperature/time curve, the parallel hardness curve, and the implication for body repair.

The five marked stages:

Stage Temperature behaviour What's happening metallurgically Hardness
1 Heated up to a high hold (the "solution heat treatment" temperature, ~500 °C for common 6xxx-series alloys) Alloying elements (Mg, Si, Cu, Zn) dissolve into the aluminium matrix as a single-phase solid solution. Low — the alloy is soft and very ductile here.
2 Steep drop in temperature ("quench") Rapid cooling traps the alloying elements in solution; they don't have time to form coarse precipitates. Still low, but the structure is now metastable.
3 Small step at low temperature ("natural aging") At room temperature, tiny clusters of solute atoms start to form (Guinier–Preston zones). Begins to rise slowly.
4 Modest re-heat to an intermediate temperature ("artificial aging") The clusters grow into fine, coherent precipitates that pin dislocations — the precipitation-hardening mechanism. Rises sharply.
5 Drop back to room temperature; held there Precipitate structure is now fixed; the alloy is at peak hardness. High, plateau.

The first says: the strength comes from stage 4. The second says: once the part is at stage 5, you can't bend it any more without damaging the precipitate structure.

Why "Plastic Deformation No Longer Permitted"

Pre-treatment (stages 1–2) the aluminium is soft enough that body-shop dent-pulling and hammer-and-dolly work are physically possible. After full age-hardening (stage 5):

  • The fine precipitates that give the alloy its strength also make it brittle in cold-forming.
  • Trying to bend the panel back to shape past a small elastic range produces microcracks in the matrix, especially around the precipitates.
  • Re-heating the panel to soften it before bending (a steel-shop reflex) destroys the precipitate structure — you'd have to send the panel back through the whole 5-stage cycle to restore strength.

That's why the trainer flags it with a label on the curve. In practice it drives three workshop rules:

  1. Aluminium body parts are replaced, not straightened, in any meaningful damage case.
  2. Aluminium-rated rivet/bond joining is used for repair, not welding — welds also destroy the local age-hardened structure.
  3. Dedicated aluminium repair bays with separate tools — steel particles galvanically corrode aluminium, so the tooling can't be shared.

How This Connects to Aero Body Design

The body shell exists for crashworthiness and aerodynamics (07 — Aerodynamics and Drag Reduction). Aluminium panels are used for two reasons:

  • Weight. ~1/3 the density of steel — a lighter shell means lower fuel burn at constant aero performance.
  • Formability of the unhardened panel. Stages 1–2 give the body-press a soft, deep-drawable sheet for the complex curves needed (flush windows, optimised front, rising luggage floor — all the E60 aero moves).

Once those shapes are pressed and the panel is age-hardened to its final temper, the same metallurgy that holds the shape in service is what stops a body shop putting it back if it gets dented.

Key Facts

  • Aluminium body panels are strengthened by a 5-stage heat treatment: solution-heat → quench → natural aging → artificial aging → final state.
  • Strength comes from fine precipitates (Guinier–Preston zones grown into coherent precipitates) — the precipitation-hardening mechanism.
  • Hardness is low through stages 1–2 and rises sharply at stage 4 (artificial aging).
  • After stage 5, plastic deformation is no longer permitted — the part is too brittle in cold-forming.
  • Reheating the panel to soften it for repair destroys the precipitate structure and the strength with it.
  • Body-shop practice: aluminium panels are replaced, not straightened; joined by rivets/bonding, not welded; serviced in dedicated aluminium bays to avoid steel-particle contamination.
