Suspension — Overview, Components, Leaf Spring

Source images: IMG20250515002201

Notebook date: 24/7/2013

Overview

The suspension system supports the vehicle body on the wheels, isolates road inputs (bumps, ridges) from the cabin, keeps the wheels in contact with the road for steering/braking grip, and locates the wheels in the right geometry under load and during cornering. The page lists the standard component family — strut, helical spring, damper (oil-filled), control arms (wishbones), torsion bar (anti-roll bar) — and introduces the leaf spring as the simpler, older alternative.

Working Principle

A suspension corner needs to do three jobs simultaneously: 1. Support static weight — done by a spring (helical, leaf, air, torsion bar — pick one). 2. Damp oscillations — done by a damper (oil-filled telescopic shock absorber) that converts spring-bounce energy into heat. 3. Locate the wheel — done by control arms (upper + lower wishbones, or struts, or trailing arms) that hold the wheel in the correct position relative to the body.

A strut combines the spring, damper, and upper wheel-location into one telescopic unit (MacPherson strut). A wishbone suspension separates these jobs (two A-arms + a spring/damper).

A torsion bar acting between left and right wheels = anti-roll bar = resists body lean during cornering without affecting straight-line ride.

Component Function
Strut Telescopic assembly = spring + damper + upper wheel-locator in one piece. Used in MacPherson struts (front of most cars).
Helical (coil) spring The primary load-bearing spring on modern cars. Compressed under load.
Damping oil The hydraulic fluid inside the damper. Specially formulated (won't aerate or thicken with temperature).
Damper (shock absorber) Telescopic cylinder with a piston that forces oil through small orifices → converts motion energy to heat.
Lower control arm The lower wishbone (A-arm) — locates the bottom of the wheel hub.
Upper control arm (Where present — double-wishbone designs) — locates the top of the wheel hub.
Torsion bar A steel bar twisted in torsion — can act as a spring (transverse torsion-bar suspension) or as an anti-roll bar.
Anti-roll bar (stabiliser) Torsion bar that connects left and right wheels — resists body roll during cornering. Doesn't affect straight-line ride.

Leaf spring (alternative spring type)

Notebook draws leaf spring at the bottom with: - "Change in position" (left end — fixed pivot) - "Shackle pin" (right end — sliding-link pivot) - "Strut" label boxed at bottom

A leaf spring is a stack of curved spring-steel strips bound together. When the wheel hits a bump: - The spring flattens → length changes. - The fixed (eyed) end stays where it is. - The shackle end swings outward to accommodate the length change (the shackle pin is a short hinged link that allows this).

This means a leaf spring serves two functions in one part: it acts as the spring AND as a control arm (locating the axle longitudinally).

Used historically on rear axles of trucks, vans, and many older cars. Cheap, robust, but heavier and lower-performance than a coil-spring + multi-link setup.

Detailed Component Notes

Anti-roll bar margin note — "Prevent vehicle from rolling over"

Notebook side note (orange): "Anti-roll bar — prevent vehicle from rolling over"

Strictly the anti-roll bar reduces body roll (lean angle during cornering), not rollover (vehicle tipping onto its side). Rollover prevention is more about CoG height, track width, and DSC. But "anti-roll bar prevents rolling" is a common shorthand that conflates the two — the trainer's wording.

In practice, reducing body roll does help reduce rollover risk indirectly by keeping the centre of mass more centred over the track during cornering.

Why helical springs dominate

  • Lower unsprung weight than leaf springs.
  • Independent suspension (each wheel moves separately) is easier with a coil + control arm than with a leaf.
  • Lower friction than leaf-on-leaf interfaces.
  • Easier to package with a damper through the centre.

Leaf springs survive on heavy-duty truck rear axles where their load capacity + simplicity + axle-locating function are all advantages.

Why dampers need special oil ("damping oil")

  • Must keep stable viscosity from −40 °C to +150 °C (parking-lot cold to autobahn hot).
  • Must not aerate when the piston cycles rapidly — air bubbles in the orifice = sudden lost damping = bouncy ride spike.
  • Often a synthetic with friction modifiers + anti-foam additives. Branded oils like Showa, KYB, Sachs all have their own recipes.

Key Facts

  • Suspension does three jobs: support weight (spring), damp oscillation (damper), locate wheel (arms/struts).
  • Components: strut, helical spring, damping oil, dampers, lower/upper arm (wishbones), torsion bar (anti-roll = stabiliser).
  • Anti-roll bar resists body roll during cornering; trainer notes it "prevents rolling over."
  • Leaf spring is the older alternative — combines spring and axle-locator in one part; uses a shackle pin to accommodate length change.
  • Strut = integrated spring + damper + upper wheel-locator (MacPherson type).
  • Damping oil is purpose-formulated — stable viscosity, anti-aeration.
  • Modern cars: coil + control arms + dampers + anti-roll bar; air suspension on premium models — see air suspension chapter.
