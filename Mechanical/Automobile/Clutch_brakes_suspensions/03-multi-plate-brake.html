Clutch, Brakes & Suspension

Multi-Plate Brake / Clutch

Source images: IMG20250514181409

Overview

A multi-plate brake (or multi-plate clutch — same hardware, different role) is a stack of friction plates and steel plates that can be clamped together by hydraulic pressure to either lock a rotating element to the housing (brake duty) or lock two rotating elements together (clutch duty). Used extensively inside automatic transmissions to engage/release the planetary gear sets that change ratios. The notebook page draws the bow-shape (interleaved) plate stack and contrasts brake vs clutch operation with the key headline: "No loss of power" when the multi-plate is fully engaged.

Working Principle

Stack construction

Notebook label: "Releases pressure by moving down" — refers to how the hydraulic piston is released.

The multi-plate pack interleaves two sets of plates: - Friction plates (lined with friction material) — splined to one rotating member (e.g., a planetary ring gear or sun gear). - Steel plates (no friction lining) — splined to the other member (or to the transmission casing for brake duty).

Hydraulic pressure pushes a piston that compresses the stack — friction couples the two sets → the previously-free member locks to the other.

The "bow-shape region" labelled on the diagram refers to slight curvature in the steel plates (waved or bowed) that springs the stack apart when pressure releases — ensuring full release with no residual drag.

Brake duty vs clutch duty

Notebook bottom: "Brake [symbol with arrow into wall] ──── No loss of power Clutch [symbol with arrow between rotating elements] ──── Brake [restated]"

The same hardware does two different jobs depending on where the steel plates are splined:

Mode Steel plates splined to Friction plates splined to Result when engaged
Brake Transmission housing (fixed) A rotating member (sun gear, ring gear, planet carrier) Stops that member — locks it to the housing → grounds it for the gear ratio
Clutch One rotating member Another rotating member Locks the two rotating members together → makes them turn as one

In an automatic transmission, applying different combinations of brakes and clutches to a planetary gear set gives different gear ratios. See epicyclic planetary gears chapter for which combinations make which gears.

The "no loss of power" headline

Notebook: "No loss of power"

A single-plate clutch (the kind used in a manual transmission, see clutch chapter) slips during engagement — that's how it smoothly couples engine to gearbox without stalling. During slip, friction generates heat = wasted power.

A multi-plate inside an automatic transmission doesn't need to slip for smooth engagement (the torque converter handles the smoothing). Once the multi-plate clamps, it locks fully — torque transmission is 100% efficient, no slip, no power loss. Hence the trainer's "no loss of power" callout — once engaged, it's mechanically rigid.

Components

Component Function
Friction plates Steel core with friction lining on both faces. Splined to rotating member A.
Steel plates Plain steel (sometimes bow-spring shaped). Splined to member B or to housing.
Splines (inner) Engage the inner (friction) plates with rotating member A.
Splines (outer) Engage the outer (steel) plates with member B or housing.
Piston Hydraulic actuator. Pressure applied = stack clamped. Pressure released = stack disengaged.
Return spring (or bow-shape plates) Restores the piston to the released position when pressure drops. Bow-shape steel plates also help spring the stack open.
Backing plate The fixed end of the stack (opposite to the piston).
Apply oil port Where hydraulic fluid enters to apply pressure on the piston.
Bleed port Lets trapped air out when filling with ATF.

Detailed Component Notes

Why multi-plate vs single-plate

  • Torque capacity = friction coefficient × clamp force × radius × number of friction surfaces.
  • A single-plate clutch has 2 friction surfaces (front + back of the plate).
  • A 5-plate multi-plate has 10 friction surfaces → 5× the torque capacity at the same clamp force and disc diameter.
  • This is critical inside an automatic transmission where space is constrained but multiple gear-set elements must each be lockable.

Bow-shape (wave) steel plates

Notebook annotation: "Bow-shape region"

A standard flat steel plate would tend to stick to a friction plate via residual oil film when pressure releases → clutch drag in the released state. A bowed (wave-shaped) steel plate has small high-spots that spring it slightly away from the adjacent friction plate as pressure releases → guaranteed full disengagement.

How the same hardware does brake or clutch

The brake-vs-clutch distinction is purely about what the outer (steel) plates are splined to: - Splined to housing → outer plates are fixed → engaging the multi-plate stops the inner (rotating) member by friction-locking it to the housing = brake. - Splined to another rotating member → engaging the multi-plate locks both members to rotate together = clutch.

The trainer's "brake" and "clutch" labels next to the small comparison sketches at the bottom of the page emphasise this duality.

Cooling

Multi-plates in an automatic transmission are bathed in ATF (Automatic Transmission Fluid) — the same fluid that hydraulically applies them also flows through and around them, carrying away the small amount of slip heat at engagement.

Key Facts

  • Multi-plate brake = multi-plate clutch — same hardware, splines determine the role.
  • Brake duty: outer plates splined to transmission housing → stops a rotating member.
  • Clutch duty: outer plates splined to another rotating member → couples two rotating members.
  • Bow-shape steel plates spring the stack apart when pressure releases — prevents residual drag.
  • Once engaged, no slip → no heat → no power loss (unlike a manual single-plate clutch which slips during engagement).
  • Used extensively inside automatic transmissions to engage planetary gear-set elements for different gear ratios.
  • Torque capacity scales with number of friction surfaces — a 5-plate stack has 10 surfaces vs a single-plate's 2.
  • Cooled and actuated by ATF (Automatic Transmission Fluid).
