Clutch, Brakes & Suspension

Brakes — Drum vs Disc, Leading/Trailing Shoes

Source images: IMG20250514113415

Overview

The brake system converts the driver's pedal force into clamp/squeeze force at each wheel — first through a hydraulic chain (pedal → master cylinder → brake lines → wheel cylinders/calipers) and then through friction interfaces (drum + shoes, or disc + pads). The page contrasts drum brakes (typically rear) with disc brakes (typically front) and introduces the leading vs trailing shoe distinction inside a drum that determines self-energising behaviour.

Drum brake — leading vs trailing shoes

The notebook draws a drum with two shoes inside it, labelled: - Leading shoe (right in the sketch) — the shoe whose end the wheel cylinder pushes into the direction of drum rotation. As the drum rotates, friction tries to drag the shoe with the rotation → wedges it tighter against the drum → self-energising effect → more brake force per unit pedal effort. - Trailing shoe (left in the sketch) — the shoe whose end the wheel cylinder pushes against the drum rotation. Friction tries to drag the shoe away from the drum → reduces clamping → less brake force per unit pedal effort.

This is why drum brakes are sensitive to rotation direction — see the X-brake variant below.

X-brake configuration

Notebook: "X-brake — 50% braking. Also L-shape cylinder system."

The "X-brake" layout uses one wheel cylinder per shoe (instead of one shared cylinder for both shoes). Each shoe acts as leading-in-one-direction and trailing-in-the-other, so the brake produces balanced braking force forward and backward — each shoe contributes ~50% in either direction. Used where rear brakes must hold equally well during reverse parking (e.g., commercial vehicles, parking-brake duty).

The trainer also mentions an L-shape cylinder system — variant geometry, same principle.

Why drum brakes rear, disc brakes front

Notebook header (questions to class): "Why drum brakes are used in rear wheels?? Why disc brakes are in front wheels? Which is more efficient?"

The class questions get answered in a follow-up page (not in this transcription's source set). Standard engineering answers:

Reason Front gets disc Rear gets drum (on cost-driven cars)
Weight transfer under braking ~70% of braking force loads the front axle → discs handle higher heat better Rear sees less heat — drum is adequate
Heat dissipation Discs vent to air better than enclosed drums Lower load = lower heat = drum OK
Fade resistance Discs don't fade as fast — critical on front Less critical at rear
Cost Discs cost more (rotors, calipers, pads) Drums are cheap to make for low-load rear
Parking brake Hard to integrate parking brake into hydraulic caliper Easy to put a parking-brake cable on a drum shoe
Self-energising Disc is not self-energising (linear force/decel) — predictable for ABS Drum's self-energising effect is OK at rear (low force needed)

Modern performance cars (and all BMWs) use disc brakes all round — better fade resistance, easier ABS / DSC integration, lighter unsprung mass. Rear discs incorporate a separate small drum-in-disc parking brake.

Which is more efficient

Disc brakes are more efficient for the application that matters (high braking power, repeated stops without fade). Drums can produce more clamp force per unit pedal effort because of self-energisation, but at the cost of unpredictability, slower release, and worse heat handling.

Components

Component Function
Brake pedal Driver input. Pedal ratio (~ 4:1) amplifies foot force.
Master cylinder Hydraulic pump — pedal force pushes pistons → fluid pressure to all wheels.
Brake fluid Hydraulic medium. DOT 3 (clear/yellowish), DOT 4 (yellowish), DOT 5 (silicone purple).
Brake lines / hoses Carry pressurised fluid to each corner. Rigid steel inboard, flexible braided/rubber at suspension joints.
Disc (rotor) Rotating cast-iron (or carbon-ceramic) disc that the pads clamp onto. Most heat-dissipating brake type.
Caliper Houses the piston(s) and pads. Floating or fixed type.
Brake pads Friction blocks clamped onto the disc by the caliper piston.
Drum Rotating cast-iron cup that the shoes press outward against.
Brake shoes Curved friction segments that expand outward to grip the drum's inner surface.
Retainer spring Pulls the shoes back into the drum when pressure is released.
Wheel cylinder Small hydraulic cylinder inside the drum that pushes the shoes outward.
Leading shoe Shoe oriented to be self-energised by rotation.
Trailing shoe Shoe oriented to be de-energised by rotation.

Detailed Component Notes

Self-energisation explained

Imagine the drum rotating clockwise. The wheel cylinder pushes the shoes outward, both shoes touch the drum: - The leading shoe has its anchor (pivot) at the top. Drum friction acts at the contact patch, pulling the shoe clockwise around its pivot → that's the same direction as the wheel-cylinder push → shoe wedges harder. The friction force itself adds to the actuation. - The trailing shoe has its anchor at the bottom. Drum friction pulls the shoe clockwise around its pivot → that's the opposite direction to the wheel-cylinder push → shoe is partially lifted off. Friction force subtracts from the actuation.

Net effect: leading shoe does ~70% of the work, trailing does ~30%, and the total clamp force is amplified relative to pedal input (~ 2× of a comparable non-self-energising design). This is why drums are "stronger" for pedal effort — but it also means small changes in friction coefficient (water, fade) translate into big changes in braking force → unpredictable.

Why disc brakes are predictable

A disc brake's clamp force is proportional to caliper piston pressure with no self-energisation term. Brake performance varies smoothly with pedal force → makes ABS modulation (rapid release/clamp cycles) work cleanly. Drums' nonlinear self-energising makes ABS trickier on drums.

Why DOT 3 vs DOT 4 (the side note)

Earlier notebook page (Phase 1): "Brake oil — DOT3 = clear/yellowish, DOT4 = yellowish"

The numbers correspond to dry boiling points (DOT 3 ≈ 205 °C, DOT 4 ≈ 230 °C). Higher number = higher boiling point = less vapour-lock risk under hard braking. DOT 4 is standard on BMWs. DOT 5 (silicone) is incompatible with regular brake systems (different chemistry, doesn't absorb water).

Key Facts

  • Hydraulic chain: pedal → master cylinder → fluid lines → caliper or wheel cylinder → pads or shoes → disc or drum.
  • Disc brakes typically front; drum brakes typically rear (on cost-driven cars).
  • Modern BMWs use disc all round — better fade, easier ABS integration.
  • Leading shoe self-energises (friction adds to actuation); trailing shoe de-energises.
  • X-brake uses one cylinder per shoe → balanced braking forward + backward.
  • Drum amplifies pedal force via self-energisation but is non-linear — bad for ABS.
  • Disc force is linear with pressure — good for ABS, predictable.
  • BMWs use DOT 4 brake fluid (yellowish, ~230 °C dry boiling point).
