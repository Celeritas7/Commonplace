Air Suspension

Overview

Air suspension replaces the steel coil spring with an air spring — an inflatable bag/cylinder that supports the corner of the car via compressed air. Because the air pressure (and therefore the spring stiffness and ride height) can be adjusted on the fly by a pump, air suspension delivers two things conventional springs can't: automatic levelling (keeps ride height constant regardless of load) and continuously-variable stiffness (soft for comfort, firm for high speed or cornering). The notebook page lays out the hydraulic architecture — coil + accumulator + pump + oil tank — and the second page shows the closed-loop control system (sensor → control unit → adjuster → pressure compressor → air spring) that maintains target ride height.

Working Principle

Spring comparison (notebook line)

Notebook: "Steel spring = torsion of spring steel Air spring = compression of air"

Spring type Energy storage mechanism
Steel coil spring Twist (torsion) of the spring-steel wire as the coil compresses. Stiffness is fixed by wire diameter, coil diameter, number of turns.
Air spring Compression of trapped air inside a flexible bag/cylinder. Stiffness varies with pressure (higher pressure = stiffer spring).

The air spring's variable stiffness is what gives air suspension its unique behaviour.

Why an accumulator

Notebook: "Pump cannot support that much pressure" → so a diaphragm accumulator holds a reserve.

A small electric pump can't react fast enough to a sudden bump — by the time it cranks up pressure, the wheel is already past the bump. So the system uses a hydropneumatic accumulator (a chamber with a diaphragm separating hydraulic oil from a fixed mass of gas, usually nitrogen). When a bump compresses the air spring, the displaced oil pushes against the diaphragm → the gas compresses → pressure rises smoothly. When the bump passes, the gas expands → pushes oil back into the spring → ride height restored. The pump only needs to handle long-term levelling (loading luggage, fuel use) — fast bumps are handled by the accumulator.

"Constant gas mass in accumulator" → the gas charge is sealed in once at factory; only the oil side connects to the system.

What goes wrong without level control

Notebook (bottom panel): "When no level control: 1. Ride height of car changes 2. Level of car changes 3. Headlight level changed 4. Axle geometry changed 5. Driving characteristic are diminished"

When the car gets loaded (passengers, luggage, fuel, trailer): 1. Ride height drops at the loaded end → less ground clearance → bottoms out more easily. 2. Car tilts (one end lower) → looks ungainly + uneven weight distribution. 3. Headlights point down or up → blind oncoming drivers or light only the road right in front. 4. Suspension geometry changes → camber, caster, toe shift slightly off design → uneven tyre wear, reduced grip. 5. Driving feel degraded → softer one end vs the other, different steering response, longer stopping distance.

Air suspension keeps the body at constant ride height regardless of load → all of the above are avoided.

Regulation after load increase: - Compares actual & target ride height - Decision to move up or down - How much pressure required

When there is air suspension: 1. Ride height level virtually unchanged 2. Level of car never moved 3. Headlight level adjusted 4. Axle geometry [maintained] 5. Driving characteristics optimum"

Control loop in plain words

  1. Load changes (passengers in, luggage out, etc.) → ride height starts to deviate from target.
  2. Sensor reads the new (wrong) ride height at each corner.
  3. Control unit compares actual to target → calculates how much pressure to add or release at each corner.
  4. Compressor pumps more air (raise) or release valve vents air (lower) at each corner.
  5. New ride height read by sensor → loop closes.

Modern systems run this loop continuously and can target different ride heights for different modes (Sport = low, Off-road = high, Loading = lowered for cargo access).

Components

Component Function
Air spring Flexible rubber bag (bellows) or rolling-diaphragm cylinder filled with pressurised air. Supports the corner.
Coil spring (some designs) Helper spring around the air spring — see Open Questions about the notebook callout.
Hydropneumatic accumulator Diaphragm chamber with constant gas mass. Absorbs fast pressure spikes the pump can't follow.
Compressor / pump Electric (or engine-driven) pump that adds air to springs to raise ride height.
Release valve Vents air from a spring to lower ride height.
Oil tank (store) Hydraulic-fluid reservoir for systems that use oil as the working medium (some designs use air-over-oil).
Ride-height sensor One per corner — usually a rotary potentiometer or Hall-effect sensor on a link rod between the body and the lower control arm.
Control unit (ECU) Reads sensors, runs the control loop, commands compressor + release valves.
Air dryer Desiccant cartridge — keeps moisture out of the air system.
Manifold + valve block Routes pressurised air to the right corner.

Detailed Component Notes

Air-over-oil vs pure-air systems

The notebook diagram shows both an oil tank and pressurised air — suggesting an air-over-oil hydropneumatic system (Citroën's classic architecture). Pure-air systems (BMW, Mercedes, Audi) just have: - Compressor - Air dryer - Reservoir tank (for system pressure storage, not oil) - Air springs - Ride-height sensors - Solenoid valves at each corner

The notebook may be teaching the principle on a hydropneumatic example for clarity (the oil-side mechanical analogy is easier to draw than pure-air pressure-control).

Why "constant gas mass in accumulator"

The gas (nitrogen) is sealed in once at factory — only the oil side connects to the system pipework. The gas mass never changes; only its volume and pressure change as the diaphragm flexes. This means the accumulator behaves as a known, calibrated spring — the gas itself is the spring.

Levelling and headlight self-aim

The "Headlight level adjusted" point in the air-suspension benefits list is important on premium European cars — when ride height changes the optical axis of the headlight points the wrong way (up at oncoming traffic, or down at the road right in front of you). On steel-spring cars there are separate auto-level motors on the headlights driven by ride-height sensors; on air-suspension cars the ride height is already controlled so headlights stay correct automatically.

Behaviour modes (modern cars)

Premium air suspensions have selectable modes the ECU implements with different target heights and spring rates: - Comfort — softest, default ride height. - Sport / Auto — slightly lower (less aero drag, lower CoG, less roll). - Off-road / Lift — much higher ride height. - Loading / Access — lowered all the way (easier to enter, load cargo). - High-speed auto-drop — lowers automatically above ~ 120 km/h for aero benefit.

Key Facts

  • Air spring stiffness = compression of air; steel spring stiffness = torsion of spring steel.
  • Air-suspension hardware: air spring + accumulator + pump + ride-height sensor + control unit.
  • Accumulator = diaphragm chamber with constant gas mass (nitrogen, sealed at factory) — absorbs pressure spikes the pump can't follow.
  • Without level control, 5 things go wrong: ride height drops, car tilts, headlights mis-aimed, axle geometry shifts, driving feel degrades.
  • With level control, all 5 are kept at design values regardless of load.
  • Closed-loop control: sensor (height) → control unit (compares to target) → compressor/release valve → air spring → sensor (closes loop).
  • Modern cars use this for adaptive ride modes (comfort / sport / off-road / loading).
  • Air dryer in the system keeps moisture out (water inside air springs = corrosion + freezing).
