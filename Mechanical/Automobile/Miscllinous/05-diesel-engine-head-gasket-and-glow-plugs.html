Diesel Engine — Cylinder Head Gasket, Glow Plugs, and Fuel Supply Circuit

Source images: IMG20250514180903

Overview

This page covers three tightly-related diesel service topics: head-gasket selection (which directly sets the compression ratio of the rebuilt engine), the compression-vs-ignition-delay relationship that explains why the gasket choice matters, the glow-plug control loop that makes a cold diesel start, and the fuel-supply circuit that gets diesel from the tank to the high-pressure pump. All four are the practical knowledge a service technician needs when reassembling a diesel head after a top-end overhaul or diagnosing a hard-start complaint.

Cylinder Head Gasket — Picking the Right One

A diesel head gasket isn't a single part — it's a family of three thicknesses, and the right one is decided by measuring how far the piston rises out of the block at TDC (Top Dead Centre):

Cylinder Head Gasket affects compression ratio.

Gasket Piston protrusion above block deck Use case
1-hole gasket piston protrusion < 0.92 mm Smallest protrusion → thinnest gasket to maintain target combustion-chamber volume
2-hole gasket piston protrusion 0.92 mm to 1.03 mm Standard middle range — most engines fall here
3-hole gasket piston protrusion > 1.03 mm Largest protrusion → thickest gasket to maintain target combustion-chamber volume

The "hole" count is BMW's identification marking — small punched holes on the gasket edge let a technician identify the thickness without measuring. The rebuild procedure:

  1. Set the rotating assembly back in the block (crank, rods, pistons).
  2. Bring each piston to TDC and measure how far it sits above the block deck with a depth micrometer.
  3. Take the maximum piston protrusion across all cylinders.
  4. Look up which of the three gaskets matches that protrusion in the table above.
  5. Install that gasket; torque the head down.

Why this matters — the compression-ratio sensitivity

A diesel's compression ratio is set by:

$$\text{CR} = \frac{V_\text{cyl at BDC} + V_\text{chamber}}{V_\text{chamber}}$$

where $V_\text{chamber}$ is the volume above the piston at TDC. If the piston protrudes more, the gap above the piston (and so $V_\text{chamber}$) shrinks, which raises the compression ratio. A thicker (3-hole) gasket adds height back, putting $V_\text{chamber}$ back where it should be. Pick the wrong gasket and the compression ratio drifts off-spec — by a fraction, but enough to matter for diesel combustion.

Compression Ratio ↔ Ignition Delay

The trainer ties the gasket-selection rule into the underlying combustion physics:

Low compression: greater ignition delay.

High compression: less ignition delay.

Compression state Ignition delay Effect
Low (CR too low) Greater — air at TDC is cooler / less dense, fuel droplets take longer to reach autoignition temperature Rough running, white/blue smoke, hard cold start, potential knock when burn finally happens
High (CR too high) Less — air at TDC is hotter / denser, fuel ignites almost as it leaves the nozzle Higher peak pressure / temperature, more NOx, mechanical and thermal stress on rings/bearings

This is also why getting the gasket right matters: a wrong-thickness gasket pushes ignition delay one way or the other, and ignition delay is the parameter every diesel-emissions strategy is built around (covered in Engine_systems/Petrol_deisel/02 — Diesel Combustion Cycle and Phases).

Glow Plugs — The Cold-Start Loop

A diesel ignites by compression heat, but at low ambient temperatures the cylinder walls bleed away too much heat for reliable ignition. Glow plugs — resistive heating elements protruding into each combustion chamber — pre-heat the chamber for a few seconds before cranking, then continue briefly after start to stabilise combustion.

The trainer sketches the cold-start control loop:

   ┌─────────────┐                          ┌──────────────┐
   │ Temperature │                          │              │
   │  sensor     │  ──── temp signal ───→   │     DDE      │ ───→ ⊙ Glow plug
   │  (~< 2 °C)  │                          │ (engine ECU) │      (resistive heater)
   └─────────────┘                          └──────────────┘

When the temperature sensor reads below ~2 °C, the DDE (Digital Diesel Electronics — BMW's diesel engine ECU) commands the glow plugs on, holds them on through cold cranking, and extends the on-time briefly after start.

Three lines on the page corresponding to that diagram:

Element Function
Temperature sensor (< 2 °C threshold) Coolant or air-intake temperature reading — when below threshold, glow plugs are activated
DDE Digital Diesel Electronics — BMW's diesel-engine control unit; decides on-time, voltage, and post-start hold time
Glow plug The resistive heating element inside the combustion chamber; reaches ~900 °C surface temperature in 2–5 seconds

The threshold of ~2 °C is approximate — the actual DDE map activates glow plugs based on a curve of coolant temperature × ambient air temperature × battery voltage, not a single trigger value.

Fuel Supply Circuit (Low-Pressure Side)

The bottom of the page sketches the low-pressure fuel circuit — the path from the tank to the high-pressure injection pump:

   ┌──────────────┐
   │ Pre-supply   │ ──→ ┌──────────────┐ ──→ ┌────────────┐
   │  pump        │     │ Fuel filter  │     │ Fuel pump  │ ──→  to high-pressure rail
   │ (in-tank)    │     │              │     │ (HP pump)  │
   └──────────────┘     └──────┬───────┘     └────────────┘
                                │
                                ▼  (water separator / drain port,
                                    drawn as a small valve below
                                    the filter)
Stage What it does
Pre-supply pump Lift pump (usually inside the fuel tank) that pulls diesel from the tank and feeds it forward at low pressure (~3–5 bar)
Fuel filter Filters out particles and separates water (water content in diesel is destructive to the high-pressure pump and injectors). The drawn drain valve at the bottom of the filter is the periodic water drain.
Fuel pump The high-pressure injection pump (covered on the next page as the vane delivery pump) — takes the clean low-pressure feed and builds the 1000+ bar rail pressure

The three-stage chain is universal across modern diesels — what changes between manufacturers is which pump runs the high-pressure stage (vane pump, plunger pump, common-rail pump) and how the temperature of the returning fuel is managed (see 06).

Components

Component Function
1/2/3-hole head gaskets Three-thickness gasket family; thickness chosen to set the correct compression ratio for measured piston protrusion
Piston protrusion measurement Depth-micrometer reading at TDC that decides which gasket to install
Glow plug Resistive heater in each combustion chamber for cold-start ignition assistance
DDE (Digital Diesel Electronics) BMW's diesel-engine ECU; controls glow-plug on-time, fuel timing, injection quantity, EGR, turbo, etc.
Cold-start temperature sensor Coolant/air sensor whose reading triggers the glow-plug pre-heat cycle
Pre-supply pump In-tank low-pressure lift pump
Fuel filter (with water drain) Particulate + water separator before the high-pressure pump
Fuel pump (HP) Builds the 1000-bar rail pressure that drives injection (detailed in file 06)

Key Facts

  • The head gasket comes in three thicknesses — picked by piston protrusion at TDC, identified by 1/2/3-hole edge markings.
  • Protrusion thresholds: < 0.92 mm → 1-hole; 0.92–1.03 mm → 2-hole; > 1.03 mm → 3-hole.
  • The gasket controls compression ratio because it sets the gap between piston-at-TDC and the head deck.
  • Low compression → greater ignition delay; high compression → less ignition delay.
  • Glow plugs are resistive heaters used for cold start (below ~2 °C the DDE turns them on).
  • The cold-start control loop runs: temperature sensor → DDE → glow plug.
  • The low-pressure fuel circuit is: pre-supply pump → fuel filter (with water drain) → high-pressure fuel pump.
