Overview

This page opens a multi-page diesel-engine deep-dive that runs across the back of the Miscllinous notebook. The trainer covers, in order, air intake (two intake ports with different jobs), the valve set (five service-relevant features in a diesel head), and the cylinder head itself (aluminium construction, 4-valve-per-cylinder layout with a centrally placed glow plug). It's the layer of detail a service technician needs in order to read a workshop manual, not the layer a designer needs to size a port — but it tells you why the diesel head is built the way it is.

Air Intake in Diesel

A diesel engine breathes through two intake ports per cylinder, each with a distinct job:

# Port Function
1 Swirl port Swirls the intake air — creates a tangential vortex inside the cylinder so the fuel spray (when it arrives) mixes with the air around the whole bowl rather than piling up on one wall. Critical for low-load combustion quality and NOx control.
2 Tangential port "It's short path, to feed max. air inside cylinder." A straighter, less restrictive route into the cylinder — used to maximise volumetric efficiency at high load / high rpm when raw airflow matters more than swirl quality.

The trainer's framing — one port for swirl (mixing) and one for flow (volumetric efficiency) — is the standard two-port head design for indirect and direct-injection diesels of the M47 / M57 generation. At low load only the swirl port is fully active; at high load both port flaps open to give maximum airflow.

Valves — Five Service-Relevant Features

The trainer lists five features the diesel valvetrain has that you'd flag for a workshop tech:

# Feature Why it's there
1 Inlet & outlet are same (size) Diesel valves are sized symmetrically — both intake and exhaust valves at the same diameter. Unlike petrol engines (where intake is usually larger to maximise volumetric efficiency at high rpm), a diesel breathes through a turbocharger and runs at lower rpm, so the trade-off doesn't pay.
2 Nimonic The exhaust valve material — Nimonic alloy, a nickel-chromium superalloy. High-temperature & corrosion-resistant (exhaust valves see ~700–800 °C with high-sulphur diesel gas; ordinary steel would creep and pit).
3 Roller-type rocker arm A roller bearing between the rocker arm and the cam lobe instead of a sliding pad. Low friction → less parasitic loss, less wear, less oil shear. Standard on all modern diesels for both efficiency and service life.
4 Hydraulic Valve clearance Adjuster (HVA) Self-adjusting hydraulic lash compensators — a tiny oil-fed plunger sits between the rocker and the valve and takes up clearance automatically. No periodic valve-lash adjustment required (which is service-saving for the dealership and the customer).
5 Integrated valve stem seals The valve stem seal is part of the valve guide assembly rather than a separate top-cap. Cleaner production, less risk of mis-installed stem seals causing oil consumption at the valve guide.

These five together are the diesel-head differentiators a service advisor calls out when explaining why the head looks the way it does at first valve-cover-off inspection.

Two intake valves (top right), two exhaust valves (bottom), one glow plug at top-left, and the injector in the centre. The layout puts the injector right above the piston bowl for spray geometry, with the glow plug close enough to ignite the spray at cold start but out of the direct combustion flame path for service life.

Why aluminium

  • Weight — diesel blocks are already heavy; an aluminium head saves 12–15 kg vs. a cast-iron equivalent of similar size.
  • Heat transfer — aluminium's thermal conductivity is ~3× cast iron, so the head sheds combustion heat into the cooling jacket faster. Combined with high-pressure injection this gives better thermal margin under load.
  • Production — aluminium heads can be cast with complex internal water jackets and oil galleries that would be difficult or expensive in iron.

The trade-off is that aluminium expands more than the iron block beneath it, which is why the head bolts are torque-to-yield and why head-gasket selection (next page) is a precision job.

Why a glow plug

A diesel relies on compression heat to ignite injected fuel, not a spark plug. When the engine is cold, the cylinder walls draw enough heat out of the compressed air that ignition delays excessively or fails. A glow plug is a resistive heating element that pre-heats the combustion chamber for a few seconds before cranking (and continues briefly after start for stable combustion) — enough to bridge the cold-start gap. The glow-plug control loop (DDE-driven) is covered on the next page.

Components

Component Function
Swirl port Imparts rotational motion to incoming air for low-load mixing quality
Tangential port Maximises raw airflow for high-load volumetric efficiency
Inlet valves (×2 per cyl.) Open into the cylinder during intake stroke
Exhaust valves (×2 per cyl.) Open into the exhaust manifold during exhaust stroke; Nimonic for heat/corrosion resistance
Roller-type rocker arms Convert camshaft lift to valve motion with minimal friction
Hydraulic Valve Adjusters (HVA) Maintain zero valve-lash automatically across temperature and wear
Integrated valve stem seals Prevent oil migration down the valve stem into the combustion chamber
Aluminium cylinder head Lightweight, high-thermal-conductivity casting that houses valves, ports, glow plugs, injector
Glow plug Resistive heater that pre-heats the combustion chamber for cold start
Fuel injector (central, drawn but not labelled) High-pressure spray device that meters and atomises diesel into the bowl

Key Facts

  • A diesel cylinder breathes through two intake ports — a swirl port for mixing and a tangential port for raw flow.
  • Inlet and exhaust valves in a diesel are the same diameter — unlike petrol, where intake is larger.
  • Nimonic is the exhaust-valve alloy of choice — high-temp, corrosion-resistant nickel-chromium superalloy.
  • Roller-type rocker arms + HVA + integrated stem seals make the valvetrain low-friction, self-adjusting, and clean.
  • The cylinder head is aluminium for weight and thermal-conductivity reasons.
  • Each cylinder has four valves + a glow plug + a centrally located injector — the standard modern diesel head layout.
