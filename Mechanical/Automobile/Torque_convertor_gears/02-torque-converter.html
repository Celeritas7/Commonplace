Torque Converter & Gears

Torque Converter

Source images: IMG20250514181303, IMG20250514181316

Overview

The torque converter is the fluid coupling that sits between the engine and the automatic gearbox — replacing the manual clutch. It carries torque from engine to gearbox without any rigid mechanical connection, using ATF flow between a pump impeller (engine-driven) and a turbine impeller (gearbox-input-driven), with a stator in between that redirects fluid flow to actually multiply the input torque during acceleration. The page covers the three-element anatomy, the Pelton-wheel hydraulic analogy, why the stator needs a one-way clutch, and the drawbacks (slip, sluggishness, fuel use) that motivated the lockup clutch.

Working Principle

Three-element construction

Notebook draws cross-section + face-on views of: - Pump impeller — fixed to the housing (which is bolted to the flywheel — engine driven) - Turbine impeller — connected to the turbine output shaft (which goes to the transmission) - Stator — sits between them, mounted on a stator output shaft connected to a fixed shaft in transmission - One-way clutch given to stator (to make stator freewheeling)

The three impellers' jobs

# Element Connected to What it does
1 Pump impeller Engine flywheel (via TC housing) Slings ATF outward by centrifugal force as it spins → fluid leaves at high velocity at the outer rim.
2 Turbine impeller Transmission input shaft Catches the incoming ATF on its curved vanes → flow deflects → reaction force spins the turbine → torque to gearbox. ATF then exits the turbine inward, toward the stator.
3 Stator Fixed (via one-way clutch) Redirects the ATF leaving the turbine so it re-enters the pump in the right direction — adding to the pump's outward sling instead of opposing it. This redirection is what multiplies torque.

How the stator multiplies torque

Notebook (caption next to bottom diagram): "Stator changes flow of fluid coming out from turbine. So it avoids the oppose to the rotation of pump. Stator increases efficiency. One-way clutch is given to stator to avoid backward rotation (to make stator freewheeling)"

Without the stator, ATF leaving the turbine would hit the back of the pump vanes → fighting the pump → wasted energy. The stator's curved vanes redirect the outflowing ATF so it now enters the pump in the same direction the pump is spinning → the fluid adds momentum → the pump puts more energy into the loop than the engine alone would → output torque > input torque. Torque multiplication factor ≈ 2× at stall, dropping to 1× as turbine speed catches up to pump speed.

Why the one-way clutch

At low speed (acceleration from a stop), the ATF leaves the turbine going backward → stator vanes redirect it → the stator wants to spin in reverse of engine direction. The one-way clutch holds the stator stationary so it actually does the redirection job rather than just freely spinning. Without it, the stator would spin and accomplish nothing.

As the turbine speed catches up to the pump speed (when cruising), the direction of ATF leaving the turbine flips to point in the same direction the pump is spinning. Now the stator should NOT redirect — if held, it would obstruct flow. The one-way clutch lets the stator freewheel in this regime → stator rotates freely with the flow → low loss. This is the "freewheeling" state.

Operating regime Stator behaviour Why
Stall (full torque multiplication) Locked — held by one-way clutch Redirecting reverse-flow ATF — actively multiplying torque
Coupling phase (cruise) Freewheeling — one-way clutch overrun ATF flow direction has flipped — stator just spins along with flow, no redirection job

Centrifugal pump analogy (notebook drawing)

Notebook draws a Pelton wheel on the left and a centrifugal pump on the right with the labels "centrifugal force applied on oil", "pump rotation is same as flywheel", "rpm of turbine"

The trainer uses the Pelton wheel (impulse water turbine) as the conceptual model for the turbine impeller — fluid jets hit curved vanes, the vanes redirect the flow, and the reaction torques the wheel. Same principle inside the torque converter, but with ATF instead of water and at much higher speeds.

Drawbacks (4)

Notebook list: "Drawbacks: 1. Large slip zone 2. Greatly reduced power output 3. Sluggish driving characteristic 4. High fuel consumption"

The torque converter's drawbacks all trace to one root cause: slip. The turbine never quite matches the pump speed — there's always a small velocity difference (5–15% even at cruise) → some of the engine's mechanical energy is lost as heat in the ATF. That heat = wasted fuel.

Drawback Real-world impact
Large slip zone Significant slip across most operating range (especially at low rpm)
Greatly reduced power output At-wheels power is less than engine output by the slip fraction
Sluggish driving Throttle response lag (engine revs ahead of car) — the famous "automatic creep" sensation
High fuel consumption Slip heat = wasted fuel; old 3-speed autos lost 10–15% vs manual

Modern solution: a lockup clutch inside the converter mechanically couples pump to turbine at cruise speeds → zero slip, full efficiency. The torque converter is only "active" (with slip) during launch and shifts. Not detailed on this page but standard on all modern automatics.

Components

Component Function
Torque converter housing Outer shell. Bolted to flywheel — spins with engine. Contains all internal elements + ATF.
Pump impeller Curved vanes fixed to the inside of the housing. Spins with engine → centrifugal pump.
Turbine impeller Curved vanes on a separate disc connected to the turbine output shaft. Catches ATF flow → torques the gearbox input.
Turbine output shaft Connects turbine impeller to transmission input.
Stator Set of redirector vanes between pump and turbine. Mounted on a fixed shaft via one-way clutch.
Stator support shaft Fixed shaft in transmission housing that the stator's one-way clutch grips against.
One-way clutch (sprag / roller-spring) Lets stator hold against torque in one direction, freewheel in the other.
ATF (Automatic Transmission Fluid) Working fluid. Circulates pump → turbine → stator → pump in a closed loop inside the housing.
Lockup clutch (not drawn here) Modern addition — friction plate that mechanically couples turbine to housing at cruise, eliminating slip.

Detailed Component Notes

"Pump rotation is same as flywheel"

Notebook annotation: "pump rotation is same as flywheel"

The pump impeller is rigidly fixed to the converter housing, which is bolted to the flywheel. So pump rpm = engine rpm — always. No clutch in between, no slip in this connection.

"rpm of turbine" annotation

The turbine rpm is whatever the gearbox input shaft is doing, which depends on: - Selected gear ratio (gearbox). - Vehicle speed (wheels). - Currently between zero (stall — car stopped, engine idling, brake on) and full pump rpm (at cruise with lockup engaged).

Why fluid coupling smooths everything

A manual clutch has a binary state: slipping (engagement) or locked (driving). Slipping = friction + heat + wear. The torque converter's fluid coupling is continuously variable — any slip ratio is fine, no friction wear (only fluid shear, which the ATF handles). This is why automatic cars are smooth from a stop and why they idle in gear without stalling.

Pelton wheel reference (left half of IMG 181316)

Notebook (left of bottom diagram): "Pelton wheel" with curved cup-vanes

The Pelton wheel is an impulse turbine — water jets hit cup-shaped vanes, the cups reverse the jet direction (180°), and reaction force spins the wheel. The torque-converter turbine works the same way at the impeller-vane level, just with ATF instead of water and reaction (not impulse) flow direction.

Why "centrifugal force applied on oil" matters

The pump impeller doesn't need an external pump — it IS the pump. As it spins, centrifugal force throws the ATF outward from the centre to the rim → high velocity at the rim → kinetic energy → which the turbine then absorbs. The pump rim radius and rpm together determine the energy delivered to the fluid.

Key Facts

  • 3 elements: Pump impeller (engine-driven), Turbine impeller (gearbox-driven), Stator (held/freewheel).
  • Pump rpm = engine rpm — always rigidly connected.
  • Stator on one-way clutch — locked during torque multiplication, freewheels at cruise.
  • Stator's job: redirect ATF leaving the turbine so it re-enters the pump in the right direction → torque multiplication (~2× at stall).
  • ATF circulates in a closed toroidal loop: pump → outer → turbine → inner → stator → back to pump.
  • Pump pushes oil outward by centrifugal force; turbine catches flow on curved vanes (Pelton-wheel analogy).
  • 4 drawbacks: large slip zone, reduced power output, sluggish driving, high fuel use.
  • Modern lockup clutch mechanically locks pump-to-turbine at cruise → eliminates slip.
  • Smooth idle in gear is the fluid coupling's natural behaviour (no friction wear, just shear).
