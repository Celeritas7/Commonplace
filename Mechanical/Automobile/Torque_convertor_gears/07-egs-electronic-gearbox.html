Torque Converter & Gears

EGS — Electronic Gearbox Control

Source images: IMG20250514181421, IMG20250514181430

Overview

EGS (German: Elektronische Getriebesteuerung — Electronic Gearbox Control) is the automatic transmission's electronic brain — equivalent to what DME is for the engine. Where the older purely-hydraulic automatic decided when to shift using mechanical valves driven by oil-pressure signals, EGS reads digital inputs from many sensors, runs a shift-decision algorithm, commands the valve-body solenoids to apply the right multi-plate brakes and clutches, and handles fail-safe behaviour when something goes wrong. This page covers the 7 advantages of EGS over hydraulic-only control, the sensor and actuator list, the signal-flow pipeline (signal in → shift curve displacement → shift decision → gear shift), and the failure-mode strategies (substitute value, important-signal failure, defect memory).

Working Principle

Parking interlock (page header)

Notebook (top, partly obscured): "Parking interlock: Pin & socket mechanism"

A safety feature: a pin engages a slot on the transmission output to mechanically lock the gearbox when the lever is in P (Park). The car physically can't roll. The pin-and-socket mechanism is purely mechanical (driver lever → cable → pawl). EGS verifies the engaged position via a switch.

7 advantages of EGS over hydraulic-only

Notebook list (★ Advantages of EGS): 1. Smaller number of hydraulic components 2. Processing additional signals 3. Precise control of shift components 4. Compensation of alteration in friction surfaces 5. Flexible characteristic of shift curves 6. Detection of driving style 7. Protection against incorrect operation

# Advantage What it means in practice
1 Fewer hydraulic components Where the old valve body used dozens of mechanical spool valves for shift logic, EGS replaces most of them with electronic solenoid valves — fewer parts, smaller package, fewer wear points.
2 Processing additional signals Reads digital inputs the old hydraulic valve body couldn't use (coolant temp, throttle history, ABS wheel speeds, engine torque from DME via CAN, etc.)
3 Precise shift control Solenoids can be modulated (PWM duty cycle) → smooth pressure ramps → smoother shifts. Old valves were on/off only.
4 Compensation for friction-surface wear EGS learns adaptation values — as clutches wear, apply timing drifts; EGS adjusts pressure ramps to keep shift feel consistent over the life of the transmission.
5 Flexible shift curves Multiple stored shift maps (D, S, M, eco, sport+, etc.) — driver selects, EGS uses different shift curves immediately.
6 Detection of driving style EGS watches throttle history — aggressive throttle = sport-leaning shifts; gentle throttle = economy-leaning.
7 Protection against incorrect operation Won't allow R↔D shifts above a certain speed; won't allow downshift that would over-rev the engine; safe-mode behaviour on faults.

Failure modes (3 strategies)

Notebook (IMG 181430 middle/bottom):

★ Substitute value: Whenever any one of sensor is faulty, EGS uses a fallback value computed from other sensors.

★ Failure of important signal: e.g., engine-load valve, acceleration pedal sensor → EGS: – drives the gearbox in safe mode – oil pressure is maintained high (so clutches don't slip even if EGS can't trim pressure)

★ Defect memory: Defect memory is stored as history. (Like DME's error memory — readable by DIS / ISTA.)

This is the same fault-tolerance philosophy as DME (see N42 engine management): degrade gracefully, never strand the driver, log for diagnosis.

Detailed Component Notes

Why kickdown is electronic now (not hydraulic)

Old hydraulic automatics had a kickdown cable from the throttle pedal that mechanically activated a kickdown valve in the valve body. EGS reads a simple switch (or just the throttle-position sensor pinned at 100%) and decides whether to downshift based on current speed, rpm, and gear — much smarter than a mechanical cable.

Why ATF temperature matters

  • Cold ATF (< 50 °C) — high viscosity, slow shift response, EGS uses softer shift maps to avoid harsh shifts while ATF warms.
  • Normal (~ 80–100 °C) — design operating range, normal shift maps.
  • Hot (> 130 °C) — close to ATF breakdown point, EGS enters transmission overheat protection mode: locks up torque converter early to reduce slip-generated heat, may limit kickdown, may disengage AC compressor.
  • Very hot (> 150 °C) — limp mode, dashboard warning.

Substitute value example — input speed sensor fails

EGS knows: engine rpm (from DME via CAN), vehicle speed (from output speed sensor), and current gear. From these it can calculate what input shaft rpm should be. If the input sensor fails, EGS uses this computed value as a substitute → keeps running normally. Driver doesn't notice. DTC logged for next service visit.

Why safe mode locks high gear

"Safe mode" = limp-home mode after a critical fault. The standard strategy: lock the transmission in 4th gear (or a similar middle gear) with line pressure forced HIGH. Why: - 4th is direct drive — least clutch load. - High line pressure ensures clutches stay locked even with no electronic trim. - Driver can still get home, just without shift smoothness or sport response.

CAN bus dependence

Modern EGS reads many signals via CAN bus from other ECUs: - Engine torque from DME → EGS uses it for shift quality. - ABS wheel speeds from ABS ECU → cross-check vehicle speed. - Brake switch from instrument cluster → kickdown protection, P-shift interlock.

Loss of CAN = EGS goes to safe mode (can't trust any of those signals).

Key Facts

  • EGS = Electronic Gearbox Control = transmission's electronic brain (DME equivalent for AT).
  • 7 advantages over hydraulic-only: fewer hydraulic parts, more signals, precise control, wear compensation, flexible shift maps, driving-style detection, fail-safe protection.
  • 5 sensors: lever position switch, kickdown switch, vehicle speed, input speed (inductive), ATF temperature.
  • 4 actuators: pressure regulator, shift solenoids, lockup clutch, selector-lever interlock.
  • Pin-and-socket parking interlock mechanically locks output in P.
  • Signal flow: input → shift-curve displacement → shift decision → gear shift.
  • Failure strategies: substitute value (calculate from other sensors), safe mode (lock in 4th, line pressure high), defect memory (DTCs logged).
  • Reads many signals via CAN from DME, ABS, etc.
  • Adaptation memory learns shift-pressure trim values as clutches wear → consistent shift feel over life.
