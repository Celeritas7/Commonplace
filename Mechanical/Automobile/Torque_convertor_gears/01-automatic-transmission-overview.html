Automatic Transmission — Overview, Characteristics, Advantages

Source images: IMG20250514181307, IMG20250514181313, IMG20250514181325

Overview

An automatic transmission automatically selects the right gear ratio for the current driving conditions — no clutch pedal, no manual gear lever shifts. The notebook page opens by comparing torque-vs-rpm characteristics of different powertrains (bumper car, mountain bike, motor vehicle), explains why a motor vehicle needs gears to bridge the gap between its high-rpm power band and the low-rpm requirements of starting from rest, lists the advantages of automatic over manual, introduces the hydraulic system that operates everything, and gives the ATF (Automatic Transmission Fluid) working specs.

Working Principle

Three powertrain torque profiles compared

Notebook draws three torque (M) vs rpm (n) graphs:

Vehicle Curve shape Why
Bumper car Roughly flat — high torque from zero rpm, falls off slowly Electric motor: max torque at zero rpm. "No need of friction clutch, no need of transmission."
Mountain bike (human-powered) Hump shape — low torque at very low rpm, rising to a peak in the middle cadence (~ 80 rpm), falling at high cadence Human muscles can't deliver torque at extreme low or high rpm — that's why bikes have gears to keep cadence in the comfortable range.
Motor vehicle (ICE) Long sigmoidal hump — torque starts low, rises through 1000–3000 rpm, peaks around 4000 rpm, falls past 5000 Internal-combustion engines need to be kept in their useful rpm range. From rest the engine sits at 1000 rpm idle but the wheels need 0 rpm — a gear (or torque converter) bridges the gap.

The trainer's first conclusion: an ICE-powered vehicle MUST have either a clutch+gearbox (manual) or a torque converter+automatic gearbox to manage the mismatch between engine speed and wheel speed.

Why electric bumper cars don't need a clutch or gearbox

An electric motor produces max torque at zero rpm — exactly when you need the most torque to get the car moving. Plus motors can spin at very high rpm too. So one motor can cover the entire speed range without needing ratio changes. This is also why electric road cars (Tesla, Polestar, BMW i-series) use just a single-speed reduction gear, not a multi-speed transmission.

Why the motor vehicle curve needs multiple gear ratios

Notebook shows a multi-gear plot: "1st gear (steepest), 2nd, 3rd, 4th, 5th — each progressively flatter, with comparatively constant torque than 1st gear"

The lower gears (1st, 2nd) have high reduction → multiply engine torque massively → moving the car from rest. The higher gears (4th, 5th, 6th) have low reduction → preserve engine rpm efficiency at cruise. Plotting torque at the wheels vs road speed gives a series of overlapping curves, each gear good for one speed range:

   wheel-torque
       ▲
       │  ╱ 1st (high torque, low speed)
       │ ╱
       │╱─── 2nd
       │   ╲─── 3rd
       │     ╲─── 4th
       │       ╲─── 5th (low torque, high speed)

The automatic transmission picks which curve the car operates on based on rpm, throttle, and load.

Advantages of automatic transmission (5)

Notebook bullets (Adv.): 1. Interruption-free acceleration (no power loss between gear changes — see Open Questions) 2. Little stress (on the driver — no clutch pedal, no gear stick) 3. Automatic modification to suit driving style (sporty / economical modes) 4. Low load on drive train (smooth shifts, no shock loading) 5. High efficiency = low fuel consumption (modern multi-speed automatics actually beat manuals on fuel use)

Two more advantages of the automatic-transmission architecture (separately listed on IMG 181325): - No interruption in power flow - Compact design - Concentric design (everything rotates on the same axis) - No radial forces (loads stay axial, bearings live longer) - More complex manufacturing ← minus side

Hydraulic system — six jobs

Notebook: "(1) Hydraulic Adv: 1. Transmission of power 2. Dissipation of heat 3. Lubricates components 4. Transports debris away 5. Creates frictional coefficients 6. Carry out shift"

The hydraulic system (with ATF as the working fluid) does six things simultaneously:

# Job How
1 Power transmission Inside the torque converter — engine spins pump impeller, ATF flow drives turbine impeller → torque to gearbox
2 Heat dissipation ATF carries heat from clutches, brakes, gears → through an ATF cooler (separate radiator or coil in main radiator) → out to ambient
3 Component lubrication ATF films every gear mesh, every bearing, every bushing
4 Debris transport Wear particles + clutch material carried by ATF flow back to a filter
5 Friction-coefficient regulation ATF additives set the friction coefficient of clutch packs — too high = harsh shifts; too low = slip + burn
6 Shift actuation ATF pressure routed by the valve body to apply/release multi-plate brakes and clutches → that's how the gear change happens

This is why ATF specs are so specific — every additive blend is tuned for one of these six jobs and they interact.

ATF (Automatic Transmission Fluid) — temperature range

Notebook: "ATF (Automatic Transmission Fluid): temp range −30 °C to 180 °C"

A wide working window: - −30 °C at cold start in winter → ATF must still flow (no congealing). - +180 °C under hard use (towing, mountain descents, summer city traffic) → ATF must not break down, foam, or vaporise.

Synthetic ATFs (Dexron VI, Mercon LV, BMW LT-71141 / LA 2634, etc.) hit this range. Old mineral ATFs couldn't.

Components

Component Function
Torque converter Fluid coupling that replaces the manual clutch — see torque converter chapter.
Planetary gear sets Provide multiple ratios in a compact concentric package — see epicyclic planetary gears.
Multi-plate clutches Engage planetary elements to other planetary elements — see multi-plate brake/clutch.
Multi-plate brakes Ground planetary elements to the case — same hardware as clutches with different splining.
Valve body Hydraulic logic — routes ATF pressure to the right clutch/brake to apply each gear.
ATF (transmission fluid) Working fluid for all six jobs above. Temp range −30 °C to 180 °C.
Oil pump (in transmission) Crank-driven; supplies all hydraulic pressure — see transmission hydraulics chapter.
ATF cooler Heat exchanger — usually a section of the engine radiator or a separate small heat exchanger.
TCU / EGS The electronic control unit that decides when to shift and commands the valve body — see EGS chapter.

Detailed Component Notes

"Interruption-free acceleration" claim

Notebook Adv. #1: "Interruption-free acceleration"

Older 4-speed automatics with shift bands had a brief torque dip during shifts (one gear releases before next engages). Modern automatics with overlapping clutches (or DSG-style dual-clutch) genuinely have zero torque interruption — one clutch releases as the other engages. The trainer is teaching the modern claim.

"Automatic modification to suit driving style"

Notebook Adv. #3: "Sporty / Economical"

Modern EGS has multiple shift maps: - Economical / D — short-shifts up to high gears quickly to keep rpm low. - Sport / S — holds gears longer, downshifts more aggressively on throttle. - Manual / M — driver controls shifts via paddles or +/- stick.

The TCU/EGS detects driving style from throttle history and adapts. See EGS chapter.

"Concentric design / no radial forces" advantage

Notebook (IMG 181325): "Concentric design / No radial forces"

Manual transmissions have parallel shafts with gears meshing between them — gear-mesh forces push the shafts apart radially → loads the bearings sideways → wear point. Planetary gear sets have all elements on the same axis — no parallel-shaft side loads → bearings see only thrust (axial) loads → longer life. This is one of the structural reasons planetary automatics are inherently more durable than manuals.

Key Facts

  • ICE vehicles need a gearbox (or torque converter) to bridge engine rpm and wheel rpm.
  • Electric motors don't need multi-gear transmissions — they have full torque from zero rpm.
  • Mountain bikes need gears for the same reason cars do — human power has a narrow useful cadence range.
  • 5 advantages of automatic transmission: interruption-free accel, low driver stress, adaptive driving style, low drivetrain stress, high efficiency.
  • 4 architectural advantages: no interruption in power flow, compact, concentric, no radial forces (only complexity is the downside).
  • Hydraulic system has 6 jobs: power transmission, heat dissipation, lubrication, debris transport, friction-coefficient regulation, shift actuation.
  • ATF temperature range: −30 °C to +180 °C.
  • Modern AT shift logic is electronically controlled by the EGS / TCU.

Open Questions / Uncertainty

  • [?] "Sporty / economical" is the trainer's shorthand for what modern systems call "shift maps" or "drive modes." Notebook predates the now-standard "Drive Mode" selector concept.
  • [?] "More complex manufacturing" as the only disadvantage in the 4+/1− table — also implies higher repair cost, but the trainer didn't break that out.
  • [?] Bumper car as an example feels surprising in a car-engineering context — chosen because every dealer's customer has ridden one and intuitively understands the "instant push" feel of an electric motor.
  • [?] ATF temp 180 °C is a typical safe-operating maximum. Modern aggressive driving can push transient temps above 220 °C — at which point ATF starts to thermally break down. The notebook number is the design ceiling.
