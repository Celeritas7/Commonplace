Torque Converter & Gears

Epicyclic Planetary Gears — All Six Operating Modes

Source images: IMG20250514181328, IMG20250514181338

Overview

A planetary (epicyclic) gear set has three concentric elements: a sun gear at the centre, an internal ring gear on the outside, and one or more planet gears carried on a planet carrier that mesh between them. Because all three elements are coaxial, holding any one stationary while driving another and reading the third gives a different gear ratio every time. The notebook page walks through the six standard operating modes that produce six different ratios (including reverse) — plus the rule that "holding any two of three gives 1:1." This is the geometric foundation of every automatic transmission.

Working Principle

The three elements

Notebook: "Epicyclic gears: – Sun gear – Planet gear with carrier [Internal ring gear implied — it's the third element]"

Element Description
Sun gear Small gear at the centre. External teeth.
Planet gears 2–4 small gears that orbit the sun, carried on the planet carrier. External teeth on both sides — one set meshes with the sun, the other with the ring.
Planet carrier (yoke / spider) Holds the planet gears in their orbit. The carrier is itself a rotating element.
Internal ring gear Outer gear with internal teeth. Surrounds the planets, meshes with their outer faces.

Any two of the three (sun, carrier, ring) can be the input and output; the third must be held stationary (or "grounded") to give a fixed ratio.

The six operating modes (notebook list)

The notebook works through 6 configurations in order. The shorthand 3:1 etc. means output rpm < input rpm by that factor = torque multiplication.

# Held stationary Input (I/P) Output (O/P) Ratio (notebook) Effect
1 Internal ring gear Sun gear Planet carrier 3 : 1 Large shift to higher ratio (high reduction)
2 Internal ring gear Planet carrier Sun gear 1 : 3 Large shift to lower ratio (overdrive)
3 Sun gear Internal ring gear Planet carrier 3 : 2 Small shift to higher ratio (mild reduction)
4 Sun gear Planet carrier Internal ring gear 2 : 3 Small shift to lower ratio (mild overdrive)
5 Planet carrier Sun gear Internal ring gear 2 : 1 (with direction reversal) Reverse + higher ratio
6 Planet carrier braked Internal ring gear Sun gear 1 : 2 (with direction reversal) Reverse + lower ratio

Notebook headings: "Internal ring gear held stationary" (modes 1 & 2), "Sun gear held stationary" (modes 3 & 4), "Planetary gear carrier held" (mode 5), "Planetary gear carrier braked" (mode 6).

The "any two of three" rule

Notebook (margin): "When we hold any two of three we get 1:1 ratio."

If you lock any two of the three elements together, the whole planetary gear set rotates as one rigid unit → input rpm = output rpm = 1:1 ratio (direct drive). This is how automatic transmissions get direct drive in 3rd or 4th gear — apply two clutches simultaneously, lock two of the three planetary elements together, no relative motion inside the gear set → no friction loss → most efficient gear.

The "escalator" note

Notebook (bottom): "Driving slowly any one of the gear instead of holding stationary results in the speed of rotation being superimposed (escalator)."

If instead of fully holding one element stationary you drive it slowly, the input gets superimposed on top of the slow rotation → a slightly modified ratio. The trainer's escalator analogy: walking up an escalator → your speed (input) adds to the escalator's speed (background drive) → total speed = sum. This is the principle behind continuously variable behaviour in some compound planetary trains (Toyota's Hybrid Synergy Drive uses exactly this trick).

How the ratios actually arise — the Willis equation

The relationship between the three elements is captured by the Willis equation (not on this page, but useful for grounding the notebook's numbers):

$$\frac{\omega_R - \omega_C}{\omega_S - \omega_C} = -\frac{N_S}{N_R}$$

Where: - $\omega_S, \omega_R, \omega_C$ = sun, ring, carrier angular velocities - $N_S, N_R$ = number of teeth on sun, ring - Negative sign because sun and ring rotate in opposite directions when carrier is fixed.

The "3:1", "1:3", "3:2", "2:3" ratios on this page come from solving this with one velocity set to zero. The actual numbers depend on the tooth-count ratio $N_R / N_S$.

Force Path / Process Flow

Each of the six configurations as a flow

   MODE 1 — Ring held, Sun in, Carrier out (3:1 reduction)
   ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   Sun spinning fast       
        │       
        ▼ drives planets       
   Planets spin AND orbit  ◄── ring is held → planets must roll inside it
        │
        ▼ orbit speed = carrier output
   Carrier turns slowly  → 3:1 reduction

   MODE 2 — Ring held, Carrier in, Sun out (1:3 overdrive)
   ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   Carrier driven slowly
        │ planets forced around inside ring
        ▼
   Planets spin fast (mesh-driven by ring teeth)
        │
        ▼ spin sun
   Sun spins fast → 1:3 overdrive (output > input)

   MODE 3 — Sun held, Ring in, Carrier out (3:2 mild reduction)
   ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   Ring spinning
        │ planets roll inside ring
        ▼
   Planets spin around held sun
        │
        ▼
   Carrier orbit → 3:2 reduction

   MODE 4 — Sun held, Carrier in, Ring out (2:3 mild overdrive)
   ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   Carrier spinning
        │ planets orbit sun (which is held)
        ▼
   Planets must spin (mesh with held sun)
        │
        ▼
   Ring driven by outer planet teeth → 2:3 overdrive

   MODE 5 — Carrier held, Sun in, Ring out (2:1 reverse)
   ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   Carrier held — planets spin in place, no orbit
        │
        ▼
   Sun drives planets (which become idler gears)
        │
        ▼
   Planets drive ring in OPPOSITE direction
   → reverse + 2:1 ratio

   MODE 6 — Carrier braked, Ring in, Sun out (1:2 reverse)
   ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   Carrier held — planets spin in place
        │
        ▼
   Ring drives planets (idlers)
        │
        ▼
   Planets drive sun in OPPOSITE direction
   → reverse + 1:2 ratio

Components

Component Role
Sun gear Central gear. Often the input on first gear of automatics.
Planet gears 2–4 gears in mesh between sun and ring. Each spins on its own pin in the carrier.
Planet carrier The yoke/spider that holds the planet gears. Can be input, output, or held.
Internal ring gear Outer gear with internal teeth. Can be input, output, or held.
Multi-plate brake (held element) The clutch pack that grounds an element to the transmission case — see multi-plate brake/clutch.
Multi-plate clutch (locking element to element) The clutch pack used to lock two elements together for direct drive (any-two-of-three rule).
One-way clutch (overrunning) Often used to hold the carrier on coast — see overrunning clutch chapter.

Detailed Component Notes

Why automatics use compound planetary sets

A single planetary set gives at most ~6 different ratios depending on which element is held. Real automatic transmissions need 5–10 forward gears + reverse → they use two or more planetary sets in series, with each clutch/brake selectively engaging different combinations across both sets. This is what Simpson, Ravigneaux, and Wilson designs are — see Simpson/Ravigneaux/Wilson chapter.

Why "concentric design" is an advantage

Cross-ref: See AT advantages, IMG 181325.

All three elements share the same axis. No parallel shafts → no side-loading on bearings → only thrust (axial) loads → longer bearing life. This is the architectural reason automatics are durable.

Why "no radial forces"

The sun, planet, ring, and carrier are all symmetric around the axis. Tooth-mesh forces from the multiple planets (3 or 4 of them spaced around the sun) cancel each other out at the sun and ring — so the sun gear sees no net radial force, only torque. Compare to a manual gearbox where each gear mesh applies a one-sided radial load on the lay shaft.

"Held" vs "braked" terminology

The trainer uses both terms: - Held = element locked stationary to the case (typically by a multi-plate brake). - Braked = same thing — the multi-plate brake "brakes" the element to a stop.

In automatic transmission service literature, these are interchangeable.

Why the "any two of three → 1:1" works for direct drive

If both the sun and the carrier are locked to the input shaft (or two of three locked to each other in any combo), the whole assembly rotates rigidly → no relative motion at any gear-tooth interface → zero gear-mesh loss → most-efficient gear. 3rd or 4th gear in a 4-speed automatic is often direct drive for this exact reason.

Key Facts

  • 3 elements in a planetary: sun, planet carrier (with planets), internal ring gear.
  • All three coaxial → "concentric design" → no radial bearing loads.
  • Hold any one stationary → fixed ratio from one of the other two to the third.
  • 6 standard operating modes give 6 different ratios (including 2 reverse modes).
  • Ratios cited in notebook: 3:1, 1:3, 3:2, 2:3, 2:1, 1:2 — actual values depend on tooth count.
  • Carrier held = reverse (planets become idlers, output reverses direction).
  • Hold any TWO of three → entire assembly rotates as one → 1:1 direct drive (most efficient gear).
  • Slow-drive the held element = superimposed motion = adjustable ratio (the "escalator" analogy — basis of hybrid e-CVT).
  • Real automatics use compound planetary sets (Simpson, Ravigneaux, Wilson) to combine more ratios — see chapter 04.
