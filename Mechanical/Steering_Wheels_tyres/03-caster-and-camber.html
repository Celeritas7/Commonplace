Steering, Wheels & Tyres

Caster and Camber

Source images: IMG20250515002017, IMG20250515002032, IMG20250515002043, IMG20250515002051

Notebook date: 23/07/2013

Overview

Two of the four steering-geometry angles that define how the front wheels sit on the road. Caster is the forward/backward tilt of the steering axis as seen from the side — what makes a shopping-trolley wheel trail behind its pivot and self-centre. Camber is the inward/outward tilt of the wheel as seen from the front — what makes a tyre create a small lateral force that helps the car self-straighten and also defines its cornering behaviour. The page covers both with the trade-offs that come from changing each.

Working Principle — Caster

Shopping trolley analogy

Notebook (IMG 002017, header): "★ Caster — Shopping trolley stability" Margin note: "Wheel of trolley can't be placed front — it should be kept back for better balancing of weight."

A shopping trolley wheel mounts under a vertical post with the wheel's contact point behind that post — the wheel trails the pivot. As you push the trolley forward, the wheel naturally aligns with the direction of travel, because any sideways disturbance gets corrected by the geometry — the wheel wants to follow the pivot.

Caster does the same thing for a car's front wheels: the steering axis is tilted slightly so the wheel contact patch sits behind the point where the steering axis meets the ground.

Caster terminology

Notebook (IMG 002017): - Swivel axis: axis about which wheel rotates [for steering] - Wheel contact point (trailing) - Caster offset: distance b/w swivel axis & wheel contact point - Caster (angle): angle b/w swivel axis & the lateral/vertical centre plane - Large caster offset: better steering centring, better self-alignment - Small caster offset: Good steerability

Term Definition
Swivel axis (kingpin axis) The axis the front wheel pivots around when you steer. Tilted backward at the top from vertical.
Caster angle Angle between the swivel axis and the true vertical plane (when viewed from the side).
Caster offset (mechanical trail) Horizontal distance on the ground between where the swivel axis intersects the ground and where the wheel contact point sits. The wheel contact point is behind = positive caster.
Wheel contact point Where the tyre touches the road. Sits behind the swivel axis intersection (= trailing).

Pros and cons of caster offset

Notebook (IMG 002032): "Large caster offset: Adv: Straight-line stability, better steering centring. Disadv: But reduced steerability.

Small caster offset: Adv: Good steerability. Disadv: Poor straight-line stability."

Larger caster Smaller caster
✓ Strong straight-line stability ✓ Light, responsive steering
✓ Strong self-centring after a turn ✗ Less stable at high speed (wanders)
✓ Predictable feel ✗ Less self-centring
✗ Heavier steering effort
✗ Reduced steerability (lazy initial response)

Getting large caster ANGLE with small caster OFFSET

Notebook (IMG 002032, bottom): "Solution for getting large caster angle & less caster offset: [diagram with swivel axis tilted backward, but the steering wheel radius reduced so caster offset = 0]"

The trade-off can be circumvented by separating the caster ANGLE from the caster OFFSET: - Steeper caster angle (more tilt) → more self-centring torque per degree of steer. - But arrange the steering kingpin lateral position so the wheel contact patch sits closer to the kingpin axis projection on the ground (= less offset). - This is done by giving the kingpin axis some negative scrub radius (kingpin axis intersects ground outside the wheel centre, so the contact patch sits near the kingpin point).

Result: aggressive caster geometry for stability without the heavy-steering penalty.

Working Principle — Camber

Camber definition and effect

Notebook (IMG 002043, header): "★ Camber — angle b/w wheel centre plane & the vertical Importance: Camber creates torsion stress to create lateral force on the tyre. For straight driving ability."

Notebook (left + right diagrams): - Positive camber: wheels point AWAY from car at the top - Negative camber: wheels point TOWARDS the car at the top

Camber Wheel tilt When used
Positive (+) Top of wheel leans away from car (like /\ on the front view) Old American cars, off-road vehicles (load-bearing)
Zero Wheel is perfectly vertical Theoretical neutral — rarely used in production
Negative (−) Top of wheel leans toward car (like \// on the front view) Modern cars, especially performance — maximises cornering grip

Why camber creates a lateral force

Notebook (IMG 002043, bottom): "Wheel forms taper & attempts to roll. Inclined wheel centre plane → centrifugal force → lateral. So tie rods & wheel suspension force wheels towards straight-ahead driving."

A tilted wheel acts like the conical surface of a cup — it wants to roll in a circle (around the imaginary apex of the cone). The resulting lateral force at the contact patch: - Helps centre the steering on straights (the two wheels' lateral forces cancel each other out only when pointing dead ahead). - Provides predictable steering feel.

In cornering, negative camber keeps the outer (loaded) tyre's contact patch flat on the road even when body roll tries to lift the outer edge → maximum grip.

Cases — what happens when camber is off

Notebook (IMG 002043, Cases): "① If one tyre is provided more negative camber: – Car will be turned automatically (towards most positive angle) – Body drifted towards less negative camber"

If the left wheel has more negative camber than the right, the right wheel produces more lateral force (less tilted = more straight-rolling intent) → car drifts toward the less-negative-camber side. Used by alignment shops to correct pull caused by road crown.

No-camber case (trolley analogy)

Notebook (IMG 002051, top): "② If there is no camber: – Car will not travel properly on the road. – e.g.: 'Trolley in mall'"

"The weight of the car acting on strut force the car steering wheels to come back to normal position."

A zero-camber wheel just rolls straight along its axis with no lateral self-correcting force. Without camber, the car wanders — like a shopping trolley with totally vertical wheels that gets pushed by every floor irregularity.

The combination of camber + caster + kingpin inclination is what makes the car's steering self-straightening: when you let go of the wheel, the front wheels return to centre and stay there.

Components / Reference Values

Angle Typical road-car value Effect
Caster angle (positive) +2° to +8° (front wheels) Self-centring, straight-line stability
Caster offset (mechanical trail) 10–40 mm More = more self-centring
Camber (negative for performance) −0.5° to −2° (front), −1° to −2.5° (rear) Cornering grip
Camber (slightly positive) +0° to +1° (some heavy vehicles, off-road) Load-bearing, straight-line use
Camber (highly negative) −3° to −4° Race cars, lots of side-grip, ride-quality penalty

Detailed Component Notes

Why "caster" is named after a furniture caster

A furniture caster (wheel under chair, trolley, dolly) puts the wheel behind its pivot. Push the furniture in any direction, the wheel swings around to align with the push direction. Same principle — the trail of the contact point behind the pivot creates a self-aligning torque.

Why high-speed cars need more caster

At highway speed, even small steering disturbances can grow into a swerve if not corrected. High caster generates strong self-centring torque that automatically resists those disturbances. Sports cars and trucks both run more positive caster than economy cars for this reason.

Why race cars use heavy negative camber

The loaded outer tyre rolls onto its sidewall during cornering body roll → without negative camber, the contact patch would lift onto the outer edge of the tread → only a thin strip touches the road. Static negative camber pre-tilts the wheel so that under cornering load, the contact patch sits flat on the road = maximum grip.

Penalty: on straights, the static negative camber means only the inner edge of the tread touches → uneven wear, less braking grip.

Camber-thrust direction

A negatively-cambered wheel produces a lateral force toward the top of the lean — i.e., toward the car centreline. The two front wheels' camber thrust forces cancel when both are equally cambered → no pull on a straight road. Asymmetric camber = pull (used to correct road-crown lean intentionally on some setups).

Key Facts

  • Caster = side-view tilt of the steering axis (forward at the bottom, backward at the top = positive).
  • Caster offset / mechanical trail = horizontal distance between swivel-axis ground intersection and wheel contact patch. Bigger = more self-centring.
  • Large caster = straight-line stability + strong self-centring, but heavier steering and reduced steerability.
  • Small caster = light steering + good steerability, but poor straight-line stability.
  • Solution for both = high caster angle + low caster offset (separate the two trade-offs).
  • Camber = front-view tilt of the wheel. Negative = top tilts toward car.
  • Camber-thrust force comes from the wheel "rolling like a cone" toward the tilt direction. Helps centre the steering.
  • Negative camber is the modern standard for grip — keeps the outer tyre flat during body roll.
  • Asymmetric camber = car pulls toward the less-negative-camber side. Used to correct road-crown drift.
  • No camber = car wanders (no self-correcting lateral force) — "trolley in a mall."
