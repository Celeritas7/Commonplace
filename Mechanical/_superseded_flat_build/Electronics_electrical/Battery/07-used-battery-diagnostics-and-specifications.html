Used-Battery Diagnostics & Battery Specifications

Source images: IMG20250514113133, IMG20250514113138, IMG20250514113147, IMG20250514113223

Overview

Four pages that pivot from "how a battery works" to "how the dealer technician thinks when a customer drives in with a battery complaint." First, why a single dashboard voltage reading lies — a key-on 12.4 V display tells you almost nothing about state of charge, because the ECU, infotainment, and other parasitic loads are pulling the rail down. Second, the date-code decoder (week + year stamped on the case) and the four-step used-battery handling protocol (first recharge → test → subsequent recharges → weekly maintenance recharge). Third, the voltage-under-load test that exposes the three irreversible failure modes — sulfation, loss of active material, and grid corrosion — and the high-current test that mimics the engine-start load (200 A) on a graph of voltage vs time. Fourth, the factory specification triplet every battery ships with — nominal voltage / nominal capacity / cold test current — and the formal definition of the 20-hour rate (C20 = I20 × 20h, the standard 55 Ah rating derived from a 2.75 A discharge to a 10.5 V cut-off at 25 °C).

Working Principle

Why the dashboard 12.4 V reading lies

When the key is on but the engine is off, the displayed battery voltage is the terminal voltage under load, not the open-circuit voltage of a rested battery. The instrument cluster, the radio memory, the BCM, the comfort-access antenna and a handful of other modules are all drawing current. That load pulls the rail down by 0.3–0.6 V relative to the true open-circuit voltage.

"We can't judge actual charge level of battery when the voltage is shown 12.4 V in display. BCZ: there are different loads in car battery — like an ECU's display — that show that it's 12.4 V." — IMG 113133.

The corollary: a true SoC measurement always requires the battery to be at rest, all consumers off, ideally 30 minutes or more after the last load. This is exactly the OCV procedure from 06-electrolyte-freezing-cold-cranking-and-diagnostic-procedures.

Battery age code — the manufacture-date stamp

Every battery has a two-character (or longer) code stamped or stickered on the case identifying when it was made. The convention shown in the notebook:

   "35 / 00"  →   week 35  /  year 2000
                    │            └── two-digit year
                    └── ISO week number (1–52)

   week 35 → end of August
   2000     → year of manufacture

   Manufacturer shelf-life claim: + 10 months
   So expiry date stamped on the case = 06 / 2001

Note from the page: "It's only for new battery." A code that says "fresh until June 2001" only applies if the battery has been stored properly. A 2001-stamped battery that's sat in a 40 °C warehouse for 6 months is no longer "fresh" — the self-discharge curves in 05 tell that whole story.

The used-battery 4-step workflow

When a customer brings a car in with a flat battery, the dealer does not simply replace it. The standard protocol is:

   1. First recharge       — slow constant-current charge to bring SoC up to ~80%
                             so subsequent tests are valid

   2. Battery test         — OCV, CCC, high-current (load) test
                             (all three covered below + in file 06)

   3. Subsequent recharges — if the test reveals partial sulfation but not
                             total failure, repeated charge cycles can recover
                             some capacity

   4. Recharge battery for week — if the customer's daily use is short-trip-only,
                                   advise weekly trickle-charge to prevent
                                   chronic undercharging

The three irreversible damage modes (★ marker in notes)

If closed-circuit current and all connections check out but the car still won't start, the battery itself is damaged. The page enumerates three categorical failure modes:

   ① Self-discharge high          (because of crystal formation
                                   on the plates — sulfation)

   ② Much loss of active material (PbO₂ from +ve plates and Pb from -ve plates
                                   detaches as sediment "mud" at the case bottom)

   ③ 1 out of 6 cells got damaged (internal short, broken weld, or selective
                                   failure — one cell's 2.1 V is missing,
                                   total drops to 10.5 V at rest)

Each one is irreversible. No amount of charging recovers a plate that has lost its active material, and a single dead cell drags down the whole battery — there's no field repair.

Voltage-under-load test

The dealer-floor version: a small load (typically 5–10 A) is applied for a few seconds while the voltmeter watches. A healthy battery's terminal voltage drops a small amount and recovers quickly; an aged battery's voltage collapses to 11.0 V or below and recovers slowly.

"Just when apply load the voltage drops to 11.0 V. So battery is old & not working bcz it's discharged." — IMG 113138.

The three underlying reasons for this voltage collapse are now reachable:

Reason Mechanism Reversible?
① Lead-sulphate crystals on the plate Long-term undercharge or deep discharge converts the normal fine PbSO₄ to hard, large, low-reactivity crystals that block the plate surface ("sulfation") No
② Loss of active material Vibration and deep-cycling shake PbO₂ off the positive grid; it falls to the bottom of the case as black sediment No
③ Corrosion of grid Lead grid (especially in +ve plate) oxidises over years of service; high internal resistance No

High-current (200 A) load test

This is the proper laboratory-grade load test. A calibrated electronic load draws a current matching engine-start demand (~200 A for a typical 1.5–2.0 L petrol) for 15 seconds while a recorder traces terminal voltage vs time:

   V (terminal)
      12.75 ┤●
      12    ┤ ●────●────●─── Battery is good       (above 10 V at 15 s)
      10    ┤    
       9.5  ┤●     ╲
       7.5  ┤  ●─── ●           Battery is damaged (below 9.5 V before 15 s)
              ╲     ╲
            ●     ●
            ──┬─────┬─────┬──────► time (s)
              0     5    10    15

Procedure: 1. Battery must be fully charged before the test (else you're testing the charge state, not the capacity). 2. Battery temperature must be room temperature (≈ 25 °C — cold inflates apparent failures, hot inflates apparent health). 3. Load = current that matches engine-start draw (here noted as 200 A). 4. Healthy criterion: terminal voltage stays above 9.6 V at the 15-second mark (the SAE J537 cold-cranking pass criterion).

Safety triad (★ before working on a battery)

   ① dil. H₂SO₄  → electrolyte burns skin and clothing.
                  PPE: acid-resistant gloves + face shield.
                  Spill: flush with water and neutralise with baking soda.

   ② Oxy-hydrogen gases → during charging, water splits into 2H₂ + O₂.
                          Highly explosive (4–94% LFL window, the widest
                          of any common gas). No smoking, no sparks
                          near a charging battery.

   ③ Pb (lead toxicity) → wash hands thoroughly after handling.
                          Posts and plate sludge contain lead — neurotoxic
                          on chronic exposure. Recycle through licensed
                          dealers only.

The three factory specifications

Every dealer-floor battery is stamped with three numbers that together define its identity:

Spec Symbol Example Determined at
Nominal voltage $V_{\text{nom}}$ 12 V Open circuit, fully charged
Nominal capacity $C_{20}$ or $C_{nom}$ 55 Ah Discharge at $I_{20}$ until cell hits 10.5 V cut-off, electrolyte at 25 °C
Cold test current CCA / EN-rated 280 A The 30-second cold cranking test at −18 °C (see 02)

How the C20 number is actually measured

The 20-hour rate is a definitional discharge protocol, not a derived calculation. The battery is discharged at a constant current chosen so that the discharge takes 20 hours to reach the 10.5 V cut-off:

$$C_{nom} = I_{20} \times 20\,\text{h}$$

For a 55 Ah battery, the matching current is:

$$I_{20} = \frac{55\,\text{Ah}}{20\,\text{h}} = 2.75\,\text{A}$$

The actual measurement is taken under three conditions (the page's three numbered points):

  1. Constant drawing of current (2.75 A throughout — not pulsed, not variable).
  2. Length of test hours = 20 hours.
  3. Cut-off voltage = 10.5 V (per cell, ≈ 1.75 V — beyond this, the chemistry starts to be damaged).

Then plotted vs time the discharge curve looks like:

   V (terminal)
   12.75 ┤●
   12    ┤  ●─────●─────●─────●─────●          ← "Nominal voltage" plateau
                                       ╲          @ 2.75 A constant current
   10.5  ┤                                ●     ← cut-off (test stopped here)
   10    ┤
         ├─────┬─────┬─────┬─────┬─────┬──► time (h)
         0     5    10    15    20    25

A battery that hits 10.5 V at exactly 20 hours has C20 = 55 Ah. One that hits it at 18 hours has effectively aged into a 49 Ah battery — even though it was sold as 55 Ah.

Process Flow — Customer arrives with no-start complaint

1. Plug into OBD (DIS/ISTA) → read battery-history log
   • How many start cycles, average SoC, last full charge?

2. Note dashboard voltage at key-on            → 12.4 V is normal-with-loads.
   Note OCV after 30 min rest                  → < 12.5 V means depleted.

3. Decode date code on case                    → reject if > 4 years old,
                                                  test if < 4 years.

4. STEP 1 of protocol: First slow recharge to ~80 % SoC.

5. STEP 2: Run the battery test set:
   • OCV after rest
   • CCC parasitic-drain check (see file 06)
   • High-current load test (200 A, 15 s) ←── this page

6. Interpret:
   ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ Voltage holds > 9.6 V at 15 s? → battery PASSES         │
   │ Voltage drops to ~11 V at low load? → sulfation, age   │
   │ One cell dead (rest OCV ≈ 10.5 V)? → REPLACE           │
   └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

7. If REPLACE:
   • Check car catalogue for the correct spec.
   • Match all three numbers: V, Ah, CCA.
   • Same battery group size (BCI / DIN code) so it physically fits.
   • Note new battery's date code in the service record.

Detailed Component Notes

Why "5% other" failures matter

A small fraction of returned batteries fail diagnostic for reasons outside the big three — cracked case from accident, vented electrolyte from being mounted upside-down, contamination from someone topping up with tap water. The dealer's job is to find this fraction (and refuse warranty) before the battery is replaced under warranty.

Why hard sulfation is a one-way street

PbSO₄ formation during discharge is normal and reversible — those crystals are small, soft, and easily dissolved back into the electrolyte during recharging. But if the battery sits flat for weeks, those small crystals recrystallise as larger, hard, low-surface-area chunks that block the plate. No charging current can dissolve them. This is why the 4-step protocol step 1 is "first recharge" — get any soft sulfation back into solution before the diagnostic tests run, so you don't condemn a battery that just needed a top-up.

Why the 200 A test is non-destructive (in moderation)

A 15-second load at 200 A on a 55 Ah battery is roughly:

$$\Delta C = 200\,\text{A} \times \frac{15}{3600}\,\text{h} = 0.83\,\text{Ah}$$

That's about 1.5 % of total capacity. So the test takes one shallow chunk out of SoC, recoverable on a 10-minute charge afterwards. It's the equivalent of one cold engine start, by design.

Catalogue lookup, not customer recall

The page underlines: "Customer can probably change the battery — so we can't know" what spec is actually fitted now. The previous owner / previous workshop may have fitted the wrong battery. Always consult the car catalogue by VIN, not the battery currently in the engine bay — otherwise you propagate someone else's wrong choice.

Key Facts

  • Display voltage ≠ state of charge. A 12.4 V dashboard reading at key-on is normal even on a healthy battery — loads pull the rail down.
  • Battery date codes use week + year format. "35/00" = week 35 of 2000 (≈ end of August 2000).
  • Used-battery workflow: first recharge → test → subsequent recharges → weekly trickle.
  • Three irreversible damage modes: sulfation, active-material loss, single-cell failure. None are recoverable.
  • Voltage-under-load drop to 11.0 V at a small load = battery is old / discharged / sulfated.
  • High-current test: 200 A for 15 s, healthy battery stays > 9.6 V at the 15-second mark.
  • Three factory specs to match when replacing: V (12), Ah (e.g. 55), CCA (e.g. 280 A). Look up by VIN, not by what's in the car.
  • $C_{20} = I_{20} \times 20\text{h}$. For 55 Ah, $I_{20} = 2.75$ A. Cut-off at 10.5 V per cell-group of 6 (i.e. 1.75 V/cell).
  • Safety triad: dilute H₂SO₄ burns, oxy-hydrogen gas explodes (4–94 % LFL — widest of any common gas), Pb is neurotoxic.

Open Questions / Uncertainty

  • [?] "35 weeks = end of August" — the notes use ISO week 35 ≈ last week of August, which checks out. But there are two conventions (week 1 = the week containing 4 January, vs week 1 = first week with the majority of days in the new year). For day-of-year-style accuracy the exact convention should be confirmed with the manufacturer; the rough month is unambiguous.
  • [?] "10 months shelf life" is the trainer's figure; the OEM spec for a Pb-Sb battery is usually 12 months in cool storage, less in tropical climates. Possibly a Navnit-specific service rule for Indian climate; preserved verbatim.
  • [?] "1 out of 6 cells got damaged & checked" — the trailing "checked" word in IMG 113133 is partially obscured. Interpreted here as "checked" (i.e. verified by selective-cell measurement), but it could also read "shocked" or be a different word entirely.
  • [?] The notes use "Self discharge high (bcz of crystal formation)" but the broader meaning is sulfation — the trainer collapses self-discharge and sulfation into one line. They're physically related (a self-discharged battery sits flat → develops hard sulfation) but distinct phenomena. Preserved verbatim.
