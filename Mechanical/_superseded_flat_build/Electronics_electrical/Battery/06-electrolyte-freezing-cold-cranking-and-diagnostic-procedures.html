Electrolyte Freezing, Cold-Cranking & Battery Diagnostic Procedures

Source images: IMG20250514113013, IMG20250514113025, IMG20250514113114

Overview

Three pages that bring the chemistry, the resistance theory, and the workshop procedure together into one final cold-weather story. First, the electrolyte freeze curve — the single most important reason a discharged battery never survives a cold winter: as the battery discharges, the sulphuric acid converts to lead sulphate and water, the electrolyte's density falls from 1.28 g/cm³ toward 1.00 g/cm³ (pure water), and its freezing point rises from −60 °C all the way up to 0 °C. Second, the cold-start capacity graph — at −25 °C the battery's available power has fallen and the cold engine needs more cranking power, so the two curves cross at the "cold-start threshold" below which the engine will not start. Third, the dealer-floor battery diagnostic procedure — Open-Circuit Voltage (OCV), Closed-Circuit Current (CCC, the ~50 mA parasitic draw), the role of the magic eye in maintenance-free batteries, and the safety rule that every dealer technician must follow: always disconnect the negative terminal first.

Working Principle

Electrolyte freezing point vs state of charge

The electrolyte in a flooded lead-acid cell is dilute sulphuric acid. As the battery discharges, the reaction at the plates converts both PbO₂ and Pb to PbSO₄, consuming sulphate ions from the acid and producing water:

$$\text{PbO}_2 + \text{Pb} + 2\,\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 \;\xrightarrow{\text{discharge}}\; 2\,\text{PbSO}_4 + 2\,\text{H}_2\text{O}$$

The electrolyte literally dilutes itself as the battery flattens. Specific gravity (density) falls in lockstep with state of charge:

State of charge Density (g/cm³) Freezing point
Fully charged 1.28 −60 °C
Partially discharged 1.20 ≈ −30 °C
Half discharged 1.10 −10 °C
Fully discharged (≈ 100% water) 1.00 0 °C

Why this is dangerous: 1. Frozen electrolyte expands ~9% on phase change → case crack, plate damage, plate-to-plate shorts. 2. Even partial freezing converts liquid electrolyte to slush → internal resistance shoots up → battery can't deliver cranking current even after it thaws. 3. The battery is permanently destroyed, not merely flat. It cannot be recovered by charging.

This is the chemistry behind the warning every cold-climate dealership repeats: don't let a customer's car go on long-term storage with a half-discharged battery in winter.

Cold-start capacity — the two converging curves

The "cold-start capacity" graph on IMG 113025 plots power on the Y axis against temperature on the X axis (−25 °C → +20 °C). Two curves are drawn:

   Power
     │                    ●─── Available battery power (falls as it gets colder)
     │            ●────●─    (because internal R rises, chemistry slows)
     │       ●─                                    
     │   ●─                                          
     │  ╱                                            
     │ ╱           ▲ "Still have reserve capacity"   
     │╱                  region — battery > engine    
     │     ╱─●────●────●──── Required cranking power
     │   ╱                  (rises as it gets colder,                 
     │ ╱                    because cold oil + cold metal              
     │╱                     resists rotation)                          
     │       ◎  Cold-start threshold (≈ −25 °C)                        
     │       │  ── below this the curves cross:                        
     │       │  required > available → no start                         
     └───────┴──────────────────────────────►  Temp (°C)
            -25  -20  -10   0   10   20
  • Available battery power (blue curve, decreasing leftward): chemistry slows in cold, internal resistance rises, deliverable power falls.
  • Required cranking power (red curve, increasing leftward): cold engine oil is roughly 4–5× more viscous at −20 °C than at +20 °C, so the starter has to do more work.
  • Cold-start threshold (≈ −25 °C for a healthy battery): the temperature at which the two curves cross. The battery delivers exactly the power the engine needs — any colder, and the engine won't start.

The red ellipse on the original page labels "old battery" — for an aged battery, the available-power curve sags further (less surface area on plates, sulfation, higher internal R), so the cross-over temperature shifts to the right. An old battery that started fine last winter might not start this winter at the same temperature.

Three factors that shift the threshold the wrong way (★ in the notes):

  1. Low temperature — pulls down available power, pushes up required power.
  2. Low charge level — directly lowers available power.
  3. Age of battery — irreversibly lowers available power.

The notebook bracket-notes that these three "decide what is quality of battery" — i.e., the difference between a premium and an economy SLI battery isn't the chemistry, it's how shallow the available-power curve sags in cold, low-charge, or aged conditions.

Battery diagnosis — the two-test procedure

The third page (IMG 113114, dated 12/07/13) sets out the two-step dealer-floor diagnostic test:

Test What it measures How Healthy reading
① Open-Circuit Voltage (OCV) Resting state of charge Voltmeter across +/− terminals, engine off, all consumers off, settled ≥ 1 h 12.7–12.85 V (full charge)
② Closed-Circuit Current (CCC) Parasitic / quiescent drain Disconnect negative terminal; insert ammeter between battery − and chassis ground ≈ 50 mA for a sleeping modern car

Why ≈ 50 mA? A modern BMW with the key out has roughly a dozen modules still alive at low duty — the alarm, the comfort access antenna, the central locking, the clock, the radio memory, the DME's persistent memory, the BCM's wake-on-CAN listener. Each draws 1–5 mA. They sum to roughly 40–60 mA. If the reading is much higher (say > 100 mA after the bus has gone to sleep, typically 20 min after lockup), there's a module that won't go to sleep — a classic dealer warranty complaint.

The "magic eye" — built-in hydrometer

A maintenance-free (MF) battery has its electrolyte sealed in and no removable cell caps for top-up. To inspect its state of charge, a small transparent dome ("magic eye") is built into the top of one cell. Inside the dome is a small green plastic ball that floats only when the electrolyte's specific gravity is above ≈ 1.225:

Magic-eye colour Meaning
Green Ball floating in clear electrolyte: OK, ≥ 65% charge
Black (dark) Ball has sunk: low charge — recharge required
Clear / white Electrolyte level dropped below the dome: end of life, replace

It is a one-cell sample of state-of-charge — useful as a quick visual, but not a substitute for an OCV measurement (which sees all six cells).

Why "addition of distilled water is important"

For non-sealed (flooded) batteries, electrolyte evaporates over time, exposing the upper edge of the plates to air → those plate areas oxidise irreversibly. Topping up with distilled water only (never tap water — its calcium and chloride ions poison the plates) restores the level. Never top up with sulphuric acid: the acid doesn't evaporate, only the water does.

Process Flow — The dealer-floor battery test

1. Park, key out, all consumers off.

2. Open hood. Visually inspect:
   • Magic eye (if MF) → green / black / clear?
   • Terminal corrosion, case bulging, electrolyte leak.

3. Wait ≥ 30 min for surface charge to dissipate
   (or apply a brief 5 A load to "wash off" surface charge).

4. ① OCV test:
   • Place voltmeter on +/− posts.
   • Read voltage:
       12.7–12.85 V → full charge, healthy.
       12.4–12.6 V → 50–75%, charge before next test.
       <12.0 V    → discharged or failed cell.

5. Disconnect NEGATIVE terminal first (see safety section below).

6. ② CCC test:
   • Wait 20 min for bus to sleep (otherwise reading
     includes wake-up draw, not quiescent draw).
   • Insert DC ammeter in series:
       battery −  →  ammeter  →  chassis ground.
   • Read current:
       ≈ 30–60 mA → normal.
       > 100 mA   → parasitic drain, hunt the consumer.

7. Reconnect:
   • POSITIVE terminal first.
   • NEGATIVE terminal last.

8. If both tests pass and customer still complains
   of no-start → load test or CCA test under
   cranking-current discharge.

Detailed Component Notes

The cold start threshold in numbers

For a 60 Ah / 600 CCA battery: - At +20 °C: available power ≈ 6 kW, required ≈ 1.5 kW. Easy start. - At 0 °C: available ≈ 4 kW, required ≈ 2.5 kW. Comfortable margin. - At −18 °C (the SAE CCA test temperature): available ≈ 2.5 kW, required ≈ 2.2 kW. Margin almost gone. - At −25 °C: available ≈ 1.8 kW, required ≈ 2.0 kW. Crosses — no start.

A 70 Ah / 750 CCA premium battery in the same engine moves the cross-over down to roughly −30 °C — the practical reason cold-climate spec sheets specify CCA instead of Ah.

Why "negative first" — the spark hazard

When the battery is in the car, the negative post is electrically the same as the entire chassis (because terminal 31 grounds the body to it). The positive post sits at +12 V.

If you remove the positive first, the wrench/spanner is at +12 V the moment it touches the post. Any time the wrench's other end brushes the chassis, the strut tower, the fan shroud, a bracket — anything metal — it short-circuits the entire battery through itself. Result: hundreds of amps, sparks, possibly an explosion (the battery itself can outgas H₂ during normal use; a spark near the vent in a worst case ignites it).

If you remove the negative first, the wrench is at 0 V relative to the chassis. It can touch any chassis ground harmlessly, because both sides are already at the same potential.

Rule: negative off first, positive off second; positive on first, negative on last. The notebook puts it directly: "While operating or calculating open-circuit current we should remove negative terminal of battery as it provides earthing connection. If +ve terminal removed first and if it gets connected to −ve terminal it will spark or discharge battery."

Magic-eye caveats

The magic eye samples only one cell out of six. If a single different cell has failed (short, sulfation, broken weld), the magic eye can still read green while the battery as a whole is dead. This is why dealer technicians never trust the magic eye alone — it's the first glance, not the verdict. The verdict is OCV + CCA load test.

Key Facts

  • A fully charged battery freezes at −60 °C; a fully discharged one freezes at 0 °C. A flat battery in a cold garage is dead, full stop.
  • Frozen electrolyte expands ~9%, cracks the case, and the battery is unrecoverable — not merely flat.
  • Cold-start threshold ≈ −25 °C for a healthy 60 Ah / 600 CCA battery. Lower for premium batteries, higher for aged ones.
  • Three factors shift the threshold the wrong way: low temperature, low charge, age of battery. These define "battery quality."
  • OCV — voltmeter across posts at rest. Healthy = 12.7–12.85 V.
  • CCC (parasitic drain) — ammeter in negative cable, key out, bus asleep. Healthy = ≈ 30–60 mA on a modern BMW.
  • Magic eye: green = OK; black = low charge; clear = electrolyte gone, replace.
  • Top-up only with distilled water — never tap water, never acid.
  • Disconnect negative first, reconnect negative last. The single most important workshop safety rule when working on the battery.
