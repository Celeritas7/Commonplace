Diode Applications — Multimeter Test, Rectification, Zener, LED

Source images: IMG20250515002434, IMG20250515002441, IMG20250515002452, IMG20250515002502

Overview

Four real-world diode applications: multimeter diode-test diagnosis (intact vs defective diode), half-wave rectification (converting AC to pulsing DC), inductive-voltage protection using a free-wheeling diode (across solenoid coils to absorb the back-EMF spike when the coil is switched off), and the Zener diode (intentionally operated in reverse breakdown to give a precise voltage reference). Closes with the LED (Light Emitting Diode) — its construction, the colour spectrum from different semiconductor materials, and its use cases.

Working Principle

Multimeter diode test

Notebook (IMG 002434, top): "Multimeter: −ve terminal of multimeter on diode body +ve terminal of multimeter on body on which diode connected

Diagnosis: – Diode intact: low resistance in forward bias, high resistance in reverse bias – Diode defective: high resistance in forward bias, high resistance in reverse bias"

Test procedure (with multimeter in diode test or resistance mode): 1. Connect leads one way → record reading. 2. Reverse the leads → record again. 3. Healthy diode: one direction conducts (low resistance / ~ 0.6–0.7 V reading), other direction blocks (high resistance / OL). 4. Defective (open) diode: both directions block → likely fuse-blown or burnt junction. 5. Defective (shorted) diode: both directions conduct → junction has fused.

Half-wave rectification

Notebook (IMG 002434, middle): "Rectification: +ve half wave only – pulsating DC voltage – minus forward voltage" Schematic: AC source → diode → load → sine wave gets chopped, only positive half passes; output has 0.7 V deducted (the forward drop).

A diode in series with an AC source passes only the positive half-cycle of the AC waveform — the negative half is blocked. Result: a series of positive pulses at the source frequency. This is half-wave rectification — the simplest form of AC-to-DC conversion. Adding a capacitor smooths the pulses into a roughly steady DC voltage.

More common in modern designs: full-wave bridge rectifier (4 diodes) — converts both halves of AC into positive pulses, much smoother output. The alternator in every car uses a 6-diode bridge to rectify the 3-phase AC from its stator.

Inductive voltage spike — the "flashover" problem

Notebook (IMG 002434, bottom): "Induction voltage [Arc quenching]: – Arcing – Flashover – Burn contact – ACTS AS CAPACITOR"

When you switch off current through an inductor (solenoid, motor winding, ignition coil), the magnetic field collapses → induces a high voltage spike across the inductor terminals (Lenz's law). This back-EMF can be hundreds of volts even from a 12 V supply.

Consequences without protection: - Arcing across switch contacts → erodes the contacts → switch dies. - Flashover in nearby insulation → component failure. - Burnt contacts → intermittent operation, eventually total failure.

Free-wheeling diode

Notebook diagram + bullet points: "Free-wheeling diode ←→ X not allowed → Always direction current ① Induced current passes through it ② No induced voltage ③ Switches protected"

A free-wheeling diode (also called flyback diode or suppression diode) is wired in parallel with the inductive load, with its anode at the load's low side. Normal operation: diode is reverse biased → no effect. When switch opens: the collapsing field tries to keep current flowing → diode becomes forward biased → current circulates harmlessly through the diode-load loop until energy is dissipated → no voltage spike at the switch.

With free-wheeling diode Without
✓ Switch protected ✗ Switch arcing + burnt contacts
✓ No induced voltage at switch terminal ✗ Hundreds of volts spike
✓ Current decays smoothly ✗ Sudden cut → fast field collapse → huge spike

Used everywhere on a car: every relay coil, every solenoid (VANOS, EGR, fuel injector), every motor winding has a free-wheeling diode protecting whatever drives it.

Breakdown effects — Zener and Avalanche

Notebook (IMG 002441): "Breakdown effect: ① Very thin depletion region → Zener effect → electrons tunnel through depletion region. ② High voltage across depletion region → Avalanche breakdown."

Two distinct mechanisms when reverse voltage gets high enough:

Mechanism Trigger Used in
Zener breakdown Very thin depletion region (heavily doped) Low-voltage Zener diodes (2-6 V)
Avalanche breakdown High electric field accelerates carriers; collisions free more carriers High-voltage Zeners and TVS diodes (> 6 V)

Both produce the same external behaviour — current rises sharply at the breakdown voltage. Real "Zener" diodes above 6 V actually use avalanche; the name "Zener" is generic for any reverse-breakdown reference diode.

Zener diode — voltage reference

Notebook (IMG 002441 bottom + IMG 002452 top): Zener symbol: triangle with bent cathode bar. "± 2–300 V" — Zener diodes available across this voltage range. Curve shows: −10 to −2 V flat (breakdown voltage region — voltage held constant despite varying current). "0.7" forward bias still works normally.

A Zener diode is intentionally operated in reverse breakdown as a precision voltage reference: - Above its Zener voltage, voltage across the diode stays constant regardless of current through it. - Below the Zener voltage, behaves as a regular reverse-biased diode (no current).

Application (IMG 002452 top): voltage divider with Zener gives a stable reference voltage from a noisy supply. E.g., car's 12 V battery (which sags during cranking, jumps when alternator kicks in) is reduced to a steady 5 V for the ECU's logic by a Zener regulator → all sensors get a stable reference voltage.

LED — Light Emitting Diode

Notebook (IMG 002452 + IMG 002502): "LED (Light Emitting Diode): – High velocity electrons → Low velocity electrons – Remaining energy is dissipated: ① Silicon: heat ② Gallium arsenide / Gallium phosphide: light emitted"

In a forward-biased LED, electrons crossing the junction lose energy as they drop from the conduction band to the valence band (recombining with holes). In direct-bandgap semiconductors (GaAs, GaP, GaN), this energy becomes a photon; in indirect-bandgap (Si, Ge), it becomes heat.

Detailed Component Notes

Why "cold light" matters for LEDs

Incandescent bulb: 90%+ of energy goes to heat (infrared radiation), only 10% to visible light. The hot filament melts/breaks → 1000-hour life.

LED: 30–50% of energy becomes visible light; rest is heat dissipated through the chip. Lifespan: 50,000–100,000 hours at correct current. No filament to break, no glass to crack.

Why "single colour" is good

An incandescent bulb produces broadband light (all colours mixed) — to get a coloured light, you put a coloured filter over it which absorbs most of the energy. An LED produces monochromatic light directly at its forward-voltage wavelength → no filter needed → no energy lost.

MOST bus — fibre-optic data

Notebook (LED uses #4): "Data transfer — in fibre optic network (MOST bus)"

The MOST (Media Oriented Systems Transport) bus is BMW's high-speed fibre-optic network for audio/video data. Each node has an LED transmitter + photo-diode receiver, connected by plastic optical fibre. Cars from 2001+ use MOST for in-car audio/video distribution. See bus systems chapter.

Key Facts

  • Multimeter diode test: healthy = low R forward, high R reverse; defective = high R both ways (open) or low R both ways (shorted).
  • Half-wave rectifier: 1 diode → passes positive half-cycle only.
  • Full-wave bridge (4 diodes) → uses both halves; standard in alternators.
  • Free-wheeling diode protects switches from back-EMF spike from inductive loads. Required across every solenoid/relay/motor.
  • Breakdown mechanisms: Zener (tunnel, thin depletion, < 6 V) or Avalanche (high field, > 6 V). Both used as voltage references.
  • Zener diode = intentional reverse-breakdown operation → stable voltage reference.
  • LED = forward-biased diode emitting light. Direct-bandgap semiconductors (GaAs/GaP/GaN) only.
  • LED colours: GaAs:P → red/yellow; GaP → green; GaN → blue; blue + phosphor → white.
  • LED advantages: cold light, long life (50k+ hr), efficient, single-colour, no filter needed.
  • LED uses: displays, backlighting, exterior lights, MOST fibre-optic data.

Open Questions / Uncertainty

  • [?] LED anode/cathode labels in IMG 002502 read "Anode = N-type / Cathode = P-type" — this is REVERSED. Correct: Anode = P-type, Cathode = N-type (current flows from anode to cathode in forward bias). Trainer's slip; corrected silently in the body text.
  • [?] "Phosphorous content" axis on the LED colour spectrum — interpreted as the gallium-arsenide-phosphide composition ratio. Higher P → higher Vf → bluer light.
  • [?] "± 2-300 V" Zener voltage range — accurate. Zeners are available from 2.4 V (lowest practical) up to 200-300 V.
  • [?] "X not allowed → Always direction current" in free-wheeling diode notes — interpreted as "diode passes current in only one direction (the protective loop)."
  • [?] "ACTS AS CAPACITOR" — odd label on the free-wheeling diode section. The diode itself doesn't act as a capacitor; the coil + diode loop has equivalent damping behaviour. Trainer's wording.
  • [?] Blue LED (GaN) not mentioned — invented late (Shuji Nakamura, 1993, Nobel Prize 2014). Notebook is from 2013 era, so GaN was known but only Red/Yellow/Green/Blue are listed.
