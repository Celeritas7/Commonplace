Diodes — Fundamentals

Source images: IMG20250515002353, IMG20250515002400, IMG20250515002416, IMG20250515002422

Notebook date: 30/7/2013

Overview

A diode is the simplest semiconductor device — a PN junction that allows current to flow one way only. The page covers the PN-junction physics (depletion region, why current can't flow at zero bias), forward vs reverse biased behaviour, the forward voltage drop characteristic of different semiconductor materials (Ge 0.3 V, Si 0.7 V, GaAs 1.8 V, GaP 2.3 V), and the construction types (planar diode for rectification, point diode for high-speed switching). Finishes with the breakdown behaviour at high reverse voltage.

Working Principle

The PN junction — depletion region

Notebook (IMG 002353): "Diode — combination of P-type & N-type region. No grain boundaries — free electrons / no charge carriers / holes annihilated."

When P-type and N-type silicon are joined: - Free electrons from the N side diffuse across the junction to fill the holes on the P side. - This creates a depletion region at the junction — a thin layer (microns wide) with no free charge carriers. - The depletion region behaves like an insulator → current can't flow through it without external bias.

Notebook (labels on the diagram): "N-type region / Depletion region / P-type region"

Plus the voltage profile (V vs distance) showing the diffusion voltage drop across the depletion region.

Forward bias — current flows

Notebook (IMG 002400 top diagram): "Electrons are transferred to holes. So depletion region increases." (wait — reverse this for forward; see Open Questions)

Actually the top diagram of IMG 002400 shows reverse bias (battery +ve to N side, −ve to P side): - Electrons in N pulled away from junction toward +ve battery. - Holes in P pulled away from junction toward −ve battery. - Depletion region GROWS → resistance high → no current.

The bottom diagram shows forward bias (battery +ve to P side, −ve to N side): - Electrons in N pushed toward junction. - Holes in P pushed toward junction. - They meet in the middle → recombine → continuous current flow. - Depletion region SHRINKS / disappears → resistance drops → current flows.

Notebook (lower diagram): "N-type / P-type / Reverse biased voltage / Depletion region reduced" → actually reads as forward-biased setup with depletion region reduced.

Forward voltage drop (turn-on voltage)

Notebook (IMG 002416): "Forward voltage: Germanium: ~ 0.3 V Silicon: ~ 0.7 V Gallium arsenide: ~ 1.8 V Gallium phosphide: ~ 2.3 V"

The diode doesn't start conducting until the forward bias exceeds a minimum turn-on voltage specific to the semiconductor material:

Material Forward voltage (turn-on) Used for
Germanium (Ge) 0.3 V Low-loss signal diodes, but high leakage at warm temps
Silicon (Si) 0.7 V All general-purpose diodes, rectifiers, transistors
Gallium arsenide (GaAs) 1.8 V High-speed switching, red/infrared LEDs
Gallium phosphide (GaP) 2.3 V Visible-light LEDs (green, yellow, red)

The I-V curve sketch in the notebook shows the typical "knee" shape — almost no current until ~ 0.6 V then current rises sharply for Si.

Construction types — planar vs point

Notebook (IMG 002422): "Planar diode — Rectifier" with N + P regions and depletion region drawn flat (large junction area) "Point diode — Fast switching, Radio" with a sharp tip touching the other region (small junction area, fused point)

Type Construction Use
Planar diode Large flat P-N interface Power rectification (battery charging, AC-to-DC). Can carry amps.
Point-contact diode Sharp metal point on N-type slab. Fast switching of channel. Radio/UHF detection, fast-switching signal diodes. Low current capacity.

The trade-off: large junction = more current capacity but slow switching (large capacitance); small junction (point) = fast switching but tiny current capacity.

Reverse breakdown

Notebook (IMG 002422, bottom plot): Voltage axis from −100 to +1 V, showing the diode current characteristic: - Reverse biased region (−100 to 0 V): essentially no current except at extreme reverse voltage where breakdown happens - Forward biased region (0 to +1 V): standard turn-on curve

When reverse voltage exceeds a critical breakdown voltage (depends on the diode design — could be 10 V for a Zener, hundreds of volts for a power rectifier), current suddenly flows in reverse. Two mechanisms: - Zener breakdown — electrons tunnel through a thin depletion region. - Avalanche breakdown — high field accelerates carriers, which collide with bonds and free more carriers.

Detailed Component Notes

Why germanium isn't used much anymore

Germanium has the lowest forward voltage drop (0.3 V vs Si's 0.7 V) → less power dissipation in a rectifier circuit. BUT: - Leakage current rises rapidly with temperature → unreliable above 70 °C. - Energy gap is smaller → more sensitive to noise. - More expensive than silicon.

Modern designs use silicon Schottky diodes when low forward voltage is critical — same 0.3-0.4 V drop as Ge but with Si's temperature stability.

Why GaAs and GaP for LEDs

The forward voltage drop dissipates as energy when an electron crosses the junction. In Si, this energy emerges as heat (phonons). In GaAs and GaP, the energy emerges as photons (light) because the materials have a "direct bandgap" — electron-hole recombination directly produces light.

The wavelength of light = forward voltage × Planck's constant. Higher Vf → higher energy photon → bluer light. - Red LED: GaAs, ~ 1.8 V - Yellow/Green: GaP, ~ 2.3 V - Blue: GaN (gallium nitride, ~ 3.3 V — not on this page)

What "no grain boundaries" means

In a single-crystal silicon wafer, there's only one crystal lattice — atoms continuous from edge to edge. No grain boundaries means the depletion region exists cleanly at the P-N junction rather than being broken by defects. Modern wafers are grown as single crystals for exactly this reason.

Key Facts

  • Diode = PN junction; allows current ONE way only (anode → cathode).
  • Depletion region = charge-carrier-free zone at the junction.
  • Forward bias = shrinks depletion → current flows once Vf is exceeded.
  • Reverse bias = grows depletion → essentially no current until breakdown.
  • Forward voltage: Ge 0.3 V, Si 0.7 V, GaAs 1.8 V, GaP 2.3 V.
  • Planar diode = large junction, high current — used for rectification.
  • Point diode = small junction, fast switching — used for radio/signal.
  • Breakdown voltage (reverse): the diode suddenly conducts in reverse beyond this point.
  • Anode = P, Cathode = N.
