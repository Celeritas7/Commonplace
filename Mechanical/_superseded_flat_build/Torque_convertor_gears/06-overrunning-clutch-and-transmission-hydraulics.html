Overrunning Clutch & Transmission Hydraulics

Source images: IMG20250514181416, IMG20250514181433

Overview

Two related internal mechanisms in an automatic transmission. The overrunning (one-way) clutch transmits torque in one rotational direction only and freewheels in the other — used to ground a planetary element on one side of a shift, then automatically release it on the other side, smoothing the transition. The hydraulic distribution system uses pressurised ATF from the transmission oil pump to feed the torque converter, multi-plate brakes, and multi-plate clutches that execute every gear change.

Working Principle — Overrunning Clutch

Three types (notebook list)

Notebook: "★ Overrunning clutch — provide locking & freewheeling ★ Roller-spring overrunning clutch ★ Clamp-type overrunning clutch"

Type How it locks Used for
Roller-spring (sprag/roller) clutch Spring-loaded rollers wedge into tapered grooves between inner and outer races when rotation tries one way. Free in the other direction. Most automotive overrunning clutches — alternator pulley, stator support shaft in torque converter, sprag clutch in planetary gear set.
Clamp-type (cam-and-roller) clutch Cammed surface on the inner race wedges rollers against the outer race in one direction; pulls free in the other. Heavier-duty version of the same principle.

Both behave the same way externally: torque locked in one direction, freewheeling in the other.

How it integrates with planetary gears

Notebook (sketches on right): - Multiplate clutches - Ring clutch ("It has both sun & ring & so can change easily i/p & o/p") - For sun, planetary gear, planetary gear carrier shaft, sun gear, for clutch

The overrunning clutch is typically used inside the planetary gear set to hold an element (usually the planet carrier or the sun gear of one set) so that: - During acceleration in 1st gear — engine drives the planetary set forward → overrunning clutch locks the held element → fixed 1st gear ratio. - During coast in 1st gear or upshift to 2nd — direction of torque on the held element reverses → overrunning clutch freewheels → element released automatically → 2nd gear can engage smoothly.

This automatic release is the magic: no hydraulic timing required to release one element before applying another. The mechanical overrunning clutch handles it instantly, removing one of the trickiest parts of multi-clutch shift control.

Why "ring clutch" can change input/output easily

Notebook: "Ring clutch — It has both sun & ring & so can change easily i/p & o/p"

A ring clutch wraps around the ring gear of a planetary set and grounds it when applied. Combined with multi-plate clutches connecting input to either the sun or carrier, this lets the transmission swap which element is input between gears → flexibility for many ratios.

Working Principle — Transmission Hydraulics

Oil flow from pump to load (notebook diagram on IMG 181433)

Notebook draws: Oil pump ──► Pressure regulation ──► Pressure distribution │ │ Oil filter │ │ ├──► Torque converter Oil sump (at bottom — return) ├──► Multi-plate brakes └──► Multi-plate clutches

The flow:

Stage Component Function
1 Oil pump Driven by the transmission input shaft (so it's always running when the engine is running). Picks up ATF from the sump.
2 Oil filter Removes wear particles before they reach the valve body.
3 Pressure regulation A pressure regulator valve in the valve body keeps line pressure at a target (~5–10 bar) regardless of demand. Excess oil dumped back to sump.
4 Pressure distribution The valve body's manifold routes oil to one of three destinations based on currently-commanded gear:
5a Torque converter Constantly fed to keep it filled and circulating. Lockup clutch fed separately.
5b Multi-plate brakes Apply pressure to specific brake packs to hold planetary elements for the active gear.
5c Multi-plate clutches Apply pressure to specific clutch packs to lock planetary elements together.
6 Oil sump Return drain from everywhere — gravity-fed back to the pump pickup.

Why a transmission oil pump always runs

The pump must run the entire time the engine runs to: - Keep torque-converter pressure (~5 bar) so it doesn't run dry. - Maintain clutch/brake apply pressure to hold the current gear. - Feed the cooler circuit so ATF heat is rejected.

This is why a car with a dead transmission pump (failed) cannot move even if you push-start it — the gearbox internals dry up.

Pressure regulation

A typical valve body sets line pressure as a function of: - Throttle position (higher throttle = higher pressure → firmer shifts, more capacity). - Selected gear (some gears need more apply pressure). - Manual valve / lever position (P, R, N, D, etc.).

On modern automatics, the EGS commands a solenoid pressure regulator that varies line pressure electronically — more precise than the old mechanical-only valve body. See EGS chapter.

Detailed Component Notes

Why use an overrunning clutch (vs just a multi-plate brake)

You could just use a multi-plate brake for every held element — but then every shift becomes a timing problem: - Release brake A at exactly the right moment. - Apply brake B at exactly the right moment. - Too early = neutral (no torque, engine flares). - Too late = both held simultaneously = transmission locked → broken parts.

The overrunning clutch removes the release half of the problem: the moment the new shift element starts to grab, the overrunning clutch automatically releases. You only need to time the apply of the new element, not the release of the old one. Dramatic simplification.

Where overrunning clutches typically live

  • In the torque converter — between stator and stator support shaft (see torque converter chapter).
  • In the planetary gear set — typically on the planet carrier or one sun gear for 1st-gear-only holding.
  • In the alternator pulley — decouples alternator from belt during deceleration to reduce belt jerks.

"Locking & freewheeling" — the basic two-mode behaviour

Notebook header: "Overrunning clutch — provide locking & freewheeling"

Same hardware, two modes depending on torque direction: - Locked: torque tries to spin inner race opposite to outer → rollers wedge → solid lock. - Freewheeling: torque tries to spin inner race same direction as outer (or no torque) → rollers slide → no resistance.

Why valve bodies are so complex

A 4-speed auto's valve body has 30–50 individual valves: pressure regulator, manual valve, shift valves (1-2, 2-3, 3-4), throttle valve, kickdown valve, accumulator valves (smooth shifts), governor valve. Each is a precision spool that has to slide cleanly through millions of duty cycles. Sticky valves = "transmission acting up" complaint that often points to valve-body service.

Key Facts

  • Overrunning clutch = one-way clutch — locks in one rotation direction, freewheels in the other.
  • Two types: roller-spring (sprag) and clamp-type (cam-roller). Same behaviour.
  • Used inside planetary gear sets to auto-release held elements during shifts — removes the release-timing problem.
  • Also used in torque-converter stator (cross-ref) and modern alternator pulleys.
  • Transmission hydraulic flow: pump → filter → pressure regulator → valve body → distributes to torque converter, multi-plate brakes, multi-plate clutches → all returns to sump.
  • Pump runs whenever engine runs — gearbox dies if pump fails.
  • Line pressure typically 5–10 bar, regulated by valve body, modulated electronically on modern systems.
  • Valve body has dozens of precision spool valves; sticky valves = "transmission acting up" complaint.
