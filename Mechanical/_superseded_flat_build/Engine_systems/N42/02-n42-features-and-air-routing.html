N42 Engine

N42 — Four Key Features & Air Routing

Source images: IMG20250515002842, IMG20250515002848

Overview

Distils the N42's distinguishing engineering into four headline features and then walks through the engine's air-routing path — the components air passes through from atmosphere to combustion chamber, including the HFM (hot film air-mass sensor) and the noise-control gate / resonator. Sets up later pages on Valvetronic, DISA, and the throttle role.

Working Principle

The 4 things the N42 has

Notebook: "N42 has: (i) Double VANOS (electro-hydraulically controlled) (ii) N42 has Valvetronic (motor for lifting) (iii) Differentiated intake system (iv) Slit engine block (industrial) [?] & redesigned cooling system" + margin note "less vibration"

# Feature One-line meaning Detail location
i Double VANOS Variable valve timing on both intake and exhaust cams, hydraulically actuated via solenoid valves under DME control. VANOS chapter
ii Valvetronic Variable valve lift — DME-driven motor turns an eccentric shaft that adjusts an intermediate lever, varying lift from 0.3 mm to ~10 mm. Replaces the throttle. Valvetronic chapter
iii Differentiated intake system DISA — sliding sleeves change the effective intake-runner length depending on engine rpm. Only on N42B20 (the 2.0 L variant). DISA chapter
iv Slit engine block & redesigned cooling Cast block features cooling-jacket slits for better head-cooling; cooling system has split short/long circuits + MAP-controlled thermostat. Less vibration. Cooling & oil circuits

The "industrial" tag next to "slit engine block" appears to be a translation of an industrial-engineering term; the slit-block reference is likely BMW's split-coolant-jacket design. See Open Questions.

Margin note — "less vibration"

The composite Mg-Al block and slit cooling jacket reduce panel resonance — N42 was specifically engineered for cabin NVH (noise, vibration, harshness) improvement vs the M43 it replaced.

Components

Component Function
Intake duct Channels atmospheric air from inlet snout to airbox.
Intake silencer First stage of noise damping (absorbent material).
Air filter Removes particulates. Paper element.
HFM (Hot Film air-Mass sensor) Measures mass airflow into engine by heated-film anemometry. See HFM Principle below.
Gate / Acoustic resonator Helmholtz chambers tuned to cancel intake-noise frequencies. ("Reduce sound of intake system.")
Throttle valve Butterfly plate. On N42, not normally used for load control — that's Valvetronic's job. Held nearly open. See throttle role.
DISA (Variable geometry intake) Sliding sleeve mechanism that changes intake-runner length. N42B20 only.
Crankcase ventilation Cyclone separator that pulls blow-by gases from the crankcase back into the intake.
Exhaust system Item 6 in the air-routing list — see exhaust chapter.

Detailed Component Notes

HFM Principle (from notes)

Notebook: "HFM: 1. Heat is given in coil by passing current. current ∝ (flow of air) 2. As air passes coil get cooled & current is given to take the film hot & on that current used to calc flow of air."

How it actually works — the trainer's explanation in plain form: - A thin platinum heated film is held at a fixed temperature above intake-air temperature by passing current through it. - Air flowing past the film cools it. - The control circuit increases the heating current to bring the film back to its setpoint temperature. - The current needed to maintain temperature is directly proportional to the mass flow of air past the film. - That current value is the air-mass signal the DME uses for fuel-quantity calculation.

This is a King's-law hot-wire anemometer wrapped in a film-on-substrate package.

Gate / Resonator

Notebook: "Gates or resonator: reduce sound of intake system."

Two of these in series tuned to different frequencies kill the dominant intake-pulsation tones. Same general approach as the air-induction system ZEN reference — keep intake quiet without restricting flow.

Throttle valve — five roles

The throttle valve on N42 is not the load-control device (Valvetronic does that). But it's still there, doing five other jobs:

Notebook: "Throttle valve: 1. Initially we require vacuum for: i) Fuel ventilation system (carbon canister, purge valve) ii) Crankcase ventilation iii) Vacuum booster (brake servo) 2. Throttle valve not required because of Valvetronic — actually but it can be used during emergency (failure of Valvetronic). 3. Throttle valve operated by MAP sensor. 4. It is not used during working of Valvetronic — to reduce throttle losses. 5. [blank fifth bullet on page]"

Margin note: "As diesel engine doesn't have [a throttle], there is vacuum pump in it." Also: "Is to operate windows in Mercedes" [?]

Reading the five points: 1. Petrol engines need manifold vacuum for several auxiliary systems (carbon canister purge, crankcase breathing, brake booster). On N42, the throttle is closed just enough to generate the needed vacuum even when Valvetronic is doing load control. 2. Limp-home mode — if the Valvetronic motor or eccentric shaft fails, the throttle can take over as conventional load control to get the car home. 3. The throttle position is MAP-controlled (closes more when manifold pressure is too high). 4. Normal Valvetronic operation = throttle wide open (eliminates pumping losses, point 5 from the N42 advantages page). 5. (Blank in notes.)

Side note about diesel: diesels have no throttle (load = fuel quantity), so they have a dedicated vacuum pump to feed the brake booster. N42 keeps the throttle around partly to avoid needing that pump.

Key Facts

  • N42's four headline features: Double VANOS, Valvetronic, Differentiated intake (DISA), Slit-block + redesigned cooling.
  • Air path: filter → HFM → gate → acoustic resonator → throttle → (DISA on B20) → intake valve.
  • HFM = hot-film anemometer; heating current ∝ air mass flow.
  • Gate + acoustic resonator = Helmholtz noise damping ("AGD" tag).
  • Throttle on N42 has 5 jobs but load control is NOT one of them under normal operation — Valvetronic does that.
  • Throttle stays mostly open to supply vacuum for: carbon canister purge, crankcase ventilation, brake booster.
  • Throttle can take over load control if Valvetronic fails (limp-home).
  • Diesel engines need a dedicated vacuum pump for the brake booster (no throttle).
  • DISA is on N42B20 only — see DISA chapter.

Open Questions / Uncertainty

  • [?] "Slit engine block (industrial)" — "slit" likely refers to BMW's slotted cooling-jacket design that gives faster head warm-up. "Industrial" tag unclear — possibly indicating the cast-iron-style slit pattern in an aluminium block, or a translation slip. Kept verbatim.
  • [?] "AGD" abbreviation in the air-routing list — interpreted as German Ansauggeräusch-Dämpfung (intake-noise damping). Could also be "Air Geometry Device" or a different acronym. Not confirmed.
  • [?] "Gate cone — region between cycles" margin note on IMG 002848 — interpreted as referring to the Helmholtz tuning frequency choice. Wording obscure.
  • [?] "Is to operate windows in Mercedes" side note — likely the trainer making a quick aside about how Mercedes used manifold vacuum to operate pneumatic central locking / window pulldown on older models. Not directly about N42.
  • [?] Throttle role #5 is blank on the page — the trainer started a fifth bullet and didn't fill it in. Preserved as blank.
  • [?] N42B18 vs N42B20 — DISA is only on N42B20 per the notes. Both variants share the rest of the architecture.
