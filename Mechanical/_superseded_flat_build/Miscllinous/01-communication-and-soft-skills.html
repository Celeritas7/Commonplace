Communication and Soft Skills

Source images: IMG20250514113504, IMG20250514113510

Overview

The Navnit dealer-training curriculum isn't only mechanical — a substantial soft-skills block sits inside it because a service advisor or sales consultant has to listen to a customer's complaint, understand what's actually being said, and explain what the workshop will do about it. These two pages capture the trainer's model of communication: the inner chain that runs from wanting something through thinking and expressing it; the outer chain that runs through a medium to a receiver who has to understand it; the barriers (attenuation in nature, physical and psychological barriers, language, lack of interest); and the four steps for effective communication. The notebook also throws in a one-line definition of "obsession" alongside, which we capture as written.

The Inner Chain — Want → Think → Communication

The first page sketches a top-down flow:

   have   want
     ↓     ↑
   ┌──────┐
   │ WANT │
   └───┬──┘
       │
       ▼
   ┌───────┐         (How to think    Brain ─→ CPU
   │ THINK │          what I want)
   └───┬───┘         (Thinks how to get it)
       │
       ▼
   ┌──────────────┐
   │ COMMUNICATION │
   └──────────────┘

The trainer's analogy is explicit: Brain → CPU. The brain processes what you have vs. what you want, decides how to think about closing that gap, and only then produces communication — the externalised expression of that internal state.

Primary requirements of communication

The page lists the two preconditions for any communication act:

Communication requires sender & receiver(s).

Every communication must have an aim.

If either is missing — a soliloquy with no listener, or a message with no purpose — what's happening isn't communication, it's noise.

Steps for Effective Communication

The bottom of page 1 lists the four-step procedure:

# Step What it asks of you
1 Goal / Aim (focus) Decide exactly what outcome you want from this exchange. Without it the conversation drifts.
2 Prepare expression Choose words, examples, and tone before you start speaking. The clearer the preparation, the lower the chance of being misread.
3 Choose medium In-person vs phone vs email vs WhatsApp — each shapes what gets through. Pick the medium that fits the message (and the receiver).
4 Confirm understanding Ask the receiver back: "so what will you do next?" — close the loop. Without confirmation you've sent, not communicated.

In a service-advisor context: (1) "I want the customer to agree to the brake-pad change today"; (2) explain wear pattern in plain language; (3) face-to-face at the desk, not over the phone; (4) "so we'll keep the car for the brake change — that's what we agreed, yes?"

The chain runs: WANT → THINK → EXPRESS (sender side, ending with verbal expression in words + body language + intonation) → MEDIUM (where attenuation and physical barriers act) → RECEIVE → UNDERSTANDS → OBSESS/EXCEPTION (receiver side). At every node, a barrier can intervene.

The Barrier Inventory

Where it acts Barrier What it does
Nature (medium) Attenuation The signal physically weakens with distance (sound dies across a noisy workshop floor).
Medium Physical barrier Walls, partitions, machinery, mask — anything blocking the path.
Sender's brain Psychological barrier (on sender) Anxiety, hesitation, distraction — words come out wrong because the thinker is unsettled.
Receiver's brain Psychological barrier (on receiver) Receiver is upset / preoccupied / defensive — the message lands but doesn't get processed.
Receiver Language barrier Sender and receiver share no fluent common language.
Receiver Psychological barrier (in understanding) Receiver is biased against the sender or topic before the message lands.
Receiver Unfamiliar or unpleasant subject "I don't want to hear about brake bills."
Receiver Lack of interest Receiver isn't paying attention.

The trainer's drawing puts these barriers at exactly the places they act — sender-side barriers between THINK and EXPRESS, medium barriers in the gap between EXPRESS and RECEIVE, receiver-side barriers between RECEIVE and UNDERSTAND.

Verbal Expression — Three Components

The page calls out that verbal expression is not just words. It has three:

  • Words — vocabulary, structure, completeness.
  • Body language — posture, hands, eye contact, distance.
  • Intonation — pitch, pace, stress, pauses.

Two service advisors saying the same words can land very differently because of intonation and body language. The training point: own all three.

Obsession — A Side Definition

Underneath the diagram on page 2 the trainer drops in a one-line definition:

Obsession: Mad to get that.

It's a teaching aside on what "obsession" means in customer-care contexts — an extreme single-minded want that distorts the communication chain (the obsessive sender pushes a fixed message regardless of the receiver's state). Captured here for completeness.

Putting It All to Work — Service Advisor's Checklist

A simple recall for the workshop floor:

  • Goal: What outcome do I need from this conversation?
  • Express: Words, body language, and intonation all aligned to the goal.
  • Medium: Have I picked the right channel — desk, phone, WhatsApp?
  • Barriers: Is there noise, a language gap, an upset customer, an unfamiliar topic? Adjust.
  • Confirm: Has the customer told me back what they understood?

Key Facts

  • Communication = (Want → Think → Express) → medium → (Receive → Understand), with a sender and receiver(s) and a defined aim.
  • The two primary requirements: a sender and receiver(s), and an aim.
  • The four steps for effective communication: Goal/Aim, Prepare expression, Choose medium, Confirm understanding.
  • Verbal expression has three components: words, body language, intonation.
  • Barriers live at every node — attenuation/physical in the medium, psychological at sender and receiver, plus language/unfamiliarity/lack of interest at the receiver.
  • A sales/service advisor's job is mostly Step 4 (confirm understanding) — without it you've sent but not communicated.
